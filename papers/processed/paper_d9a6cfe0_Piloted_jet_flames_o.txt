Measurements of temperature and major species concentrations, based on the simultaneous line-imaged
Raman/Rayleigh/CO-LIF technique, are reported for piloted jet ﬂames of CH4/H2 fuel with varying
amounts of partial premixing with air (jet equivalence ratios of /j = 3.2, 2.5, 2.1 corresponding to stoichi-
ometric mixture fraction values of nst = 0.35, 0.43, 0.50, respectively) and varying degrees of localized
extinction. Each jet ﬂame is operated at a ﬁxed and relatively high exit Reynolds number (60,000 or
67,000), and the probability of localized extinction is increased in several steps by progressively decreas-
ing the ﬂow rate of the pilot ﬂame. Dimensions of the piloted burner, originally developed at Sydney Uni-
versity, are the same as for previous studies. The present measurements complement previous results
from piloted CH4/air jet ﬂames as targets for combustion model calculations by extending to higher Rey-
nolds number, including more steps in the progression of each ﬂame from a fully burning state to a ﬂame
with high probability of local extinction, and adding the degree of partial premixing as an experimental
parameter. Local extinction in these ﬂames occurs close to the nozzle near a downstream location of four
times the jet exit diameter. Consequently, these data provide the additional modeling challenge of accu-
rately representing the initial development of the reacting jet and the near-ﬁeld mixing processes.

1. Introduction
Recent progress in fundamental understanding of turbulent
combustion processes and in quantitative evaluation of computa-
tional combustion models has been accelerated by the availabil-
ity of detailed measurements on well-deﬁned ﬂames [1,2] and
by comparisons of measurements and modeled results based
upon different modeling approaches. Comparisons have been
conducted both within the collaborative framework of the Inter-
national Workshop on Measurement and Computation of Turbu-
lent
Nonpremixed
Flames
(TNF)
[1]
and
by
individual
researchers or groups. Among the various nonpremixed and par-
tially premixed ﬂames considered as modeling targets, a series of
piloted jet ﬂames of partially premixed CH4/air [3–7] has re-
ceived the greatest attention over the past decade and has been
modeled using a range of methods and submodels (e.g., [8–16]).
Parametric calculations of these piloted ﬂames by Cao and Pope
[14] have been particularly useful with respect to understanding
of the sensitivity of computational results to changes in bound-
ary conditions and submodels.
The piloted burner was originally developed at Sydney Univer-
sity [17,18] and variants on that design have been used to investi-
gate localized extinction in several ﬂames of various fuels and fuel
combinations [2,19]. Localized extinction occurs when turbulent
mixing rates between fuel and air become competitive with critical
rates of chemical reaction. The annular pilot ﬂame anchors the
main jet ﬂame at the jet exit, which has diameter d = 7.2 mm. Local
extinction can occur after hot products of the pilot ﬂame have been
entrained into the jet, and cold coﬂowing air begins to be entrained
and rapidly mixed with the jet ﬂuid. The probability of local extinc-
tion increases as the main jet velocity increases (or the pilot ﬂow
decreases) until the upper portion of the ﬂame ultimately blows
off. Accurate prediction of the probability of local extinction and
the associated trends in measured scalars is an important valida-
tion test for combustion models, and the Sandia piloted CH4/air
jet ﬂames have been used extensively for this purpose.
As useful as these ﬂames have been, they also have some limi-
tations. One limitation is that the range of Reynolds number cov-
ered by the CH4/air ﬂame series begins at a relatively low value
(Re = 13,400, 22,400, 33,600, 44,800 for ﬂames C, D, E, F, respec-
tively) [3]. Differential diffusion effects are apparent in some mea-
surements, especially near the nozzle in the lower Re ﬂames [7].
This causes some ambiguity in the interpretation of comparisons
with computations because most models assume equal diffusivi-

ties (unity Lewis number). Flames at higher Re would be desirable
but cannot be achieved with partially premixed CH4/air and
coﬂowing air, both starting at room temperature. Another limita-
tion is that ﬂame F is very close to blowoff and is therefore highly
sensitive to small changes in boundary conditions or model param-
eters. Flame D has low probability of local extinction, so only ﬂame
E has an intermediate level of extinction and additional intermedi-
ate steps would facilitate better model validation.
The present experiments were designed to address these lim-
itations by moving to higher Reynolds number and including
multiple steps in each progression from a fully connected ﬂame
to one with high probability of local extinction. Increased Re
numbers were achieved by using a fuel blend of CH4/H2 (equal
parts by volume) partially premixed with air. Augmentation of
methane by hydrogen is also of potential interest in the context
of alternative fuels and the burning of hydrogen rich fuel
streams resulting from carbon sequestration technologies. Three
different levels of partial premixing were used. Reduction of
the equivalence ratio of the central jet increased the stoichiom-
etric value of mixture fraction for the jet ﬂame, and this shifts
the location of the reaction zone inward relative to the turbulent
shear layer in the near ﬁeld of the jet. This serves as an addi-
tional test of the robustness of models. A controlled variation
of partial premixing is also of practical interest because ﬂames
in combustors are often detached from surfaces, such that they
burn as lifted ﬂames with signiﬁcant fuel–air mixing prior to
combustion [20].
The premixed pilot ﬂame burned CH4/H2 (equal parts by vol-
ume) and air at equivalence ratio 0.75, so inﬂow conditions may
be represented using a single mixture fraction deﬁnition. Flow con-
ditions were selected to produce fully connected turbulent jet
ﬂames with a relatively high pilot ﬂow rate. Then the jet ﬂow rate
(and Reynolds number) was maintained constant, while the pilot
ﬂow was successively reduced, causing the probability of local
extinction to increase. A total of 18 ﬂames were investigated, six
for each equivalence ratio in the main jet.
Lindstedt et al. [21] reported calculations of two of the ﬂame
series (/j = 3.2 and /j = 2.1) using a transported joint probability
density function (PDF) approach closed at the composition-enthal-
py level. The study focused on the impact of closure approxima-
tions for the scalar dissipation as applied to these near-ﬁeld
conditions, and it was shown that good agreement with measure-
ments could be obtained by applying an extended algebraic closure
for the scalar dissipation rate that accounts for small scale proper-
ties. The work also indicated that the current partially premixed
ﬂames may be treated in a manner that is consistent with fully pre-
mixed turbulent ﬂames [22].
The present paper provides a full description of the experimen-
tal results for all of the 18 ﬂames. Measurements within the pilot
entrainment region (x/d = 1 and x/d = 2) are documented in addi-
tion to the main results on the extinction behavior at x/d = 4, where
x is streamwise distance from the jet exit plane. The progression of
increasing local extinction in each ﬂame series is examined using
scatter plots and density-weighted conditional means of selected
scalars. The scalar structure of the fully burning ﬂames is com-
pared to calculations of laminar partially premixed ﬂames over a
range of strain rates with either multi-component molecular trans-
port or the simpliﬁcation of equal diffusivities (unity Lewis num-
ber).

2. Experimental methods
2.1. Diagnostics and ﬂame conditions
Experiments were performed using the apparatus described in
Ref. [5,6] for simultaneous line imaging of Raman scattering, Ray-
leigh scattering, and two-photon laser induced ﬂuorescence (LIF)
of CO. The combined system allowed measurement of single-shot
proﬁles of temperature and the mass fractions of all major species
(CO2, O2, CO, N2, CH4, H2O, and H2) with a spacing of 0.20 mm along
a 6 mm section of the focused laser beam. The estimated optical
resolution was 0.3 mm. Total energy in the test section for the Ra-
man/Rayleigh measurements was 1.6 J/pulse at 532 nm. Details of
the hardware conﬁguration are not repeated here.
Experiments were performed on piloted partially premixed
CH4/H2/air ﬂames having equal volume fractions of methane and
hydrogen. Three mixture equivalence ratios were investigated,
/j = 2.1
(Re = 67,000),
/j = 2.5
(Re = 67,000),
and
/j = 3.2
(Re = 60,000). Stoichiometric values of the mixture fraction for
these ﬂames are nst = 0.50, 0.43, and 0.35, respectively. The piloted
burner had the same geometry as used in previous experimental
studies of partially premixed CH4/air ﬂames [3–6,23]. The central
jet had exit diameter d = 7.2 mm. The inner and outer diameters
of the pilot annulus were 7.7 mm and 18.2 mm, respectively, and
the outer diameter of the complete burner was 18.9 mm. The pilot
ﬂame was supplied with premixed ﬂow of CH4/H2 (1:1) and air at
/ = 0.75. The pilot velocity was varied to control the degree of
localized extinction. Six different pilot ﬂows were used with each
of the three jet compositions for a total of 18 ﬂames (Table 1). A
laminar coﬂow of air at 0.9 m/s was supplied by a wind tunnel con-
traction with 5.8-to-1 area ratio and a 25 by 25 cm exit cross-sec-
tion. The burner was mounted to extend roughly 15 cm above the
exit plane of the wind tunnel.
With these ﬂame conditions extinction/reignition occurred rel-
atively close to the jet exit in a region centered near x/d = 4. In or-
der to characterize the near-ﬁeld region of each ﬂame, radial
proﬁles were obtained at x/d = 1 and 2, based on 100 shots at each
of four overlapping positions of the 6-mm probe. Longer records of
2000 shots (nearly 60,000 individual samples) were obtained in the
extinction region of each ﬂame (x/d = 4) with the probe centered at
r = 6 mm in the middle of the reacting shear layer.
2.2. Data processing, experimental limitations, and uncertainty
Processing of the Raman, Rayleigh, and CO-LIF line images was
carried out as described in Ref. [6]. After background subtraction
and normalization, the Rayleigh and CO-LIF results were mapped
onto the positions of the Raman superpixels. The signals from each
of these combined superpixel elements were then converted to
species concentrations and temperature using an iterative matrix
inversion approach. Diagonal elements of the matrix included a
calibration factor and a temperature-dependent bandwidth func-
tion for each species. Off-diagonal elements accounted for temper-
ature-dependent crosstalk among the Raman scattering spectra of
the major species, laser-excited ﬂuorescence interferences from
various higher hydrocarbons formed in the ﬂame, and broadband
ﬂame luminosity. Calibration curves for each matrix element were
expressed as polynomials in temperature, and they were based on
measurements in cold ﬂows, electrically heated ﬂows, and pre-
mixed ﬂat ﬂames. Temperatures in the ﬂat ﬂames were measured
separately using CARS, and equilibrium species concentrations
were calculated at the measured temperatures (nonadiabatic)
and laboratory pressure. The signal from the strong C2 band near
560 nm was used to monitor hydrocarbon ﬂuorescence, while sig-
nal near 695 nm was used as a measure of broadband luminosity,

This data set has some limitations that should be considered.
First, because of the large number of ﬂames and some operational
constraints, measurements were limited to the near ﬁeld. Second,
based on what we know of turbulence scales in jet ﬂames [24–
27] the spatial resolution of the experiments is not sufﬁcient to
measure scalar dissipation in the present ﬂames. Results for ‘re-
solved’ dissipation are reported as a qualitative indication of where
the high-dissipation regions occur and what the trends in dissipa-
tion are. However, no effort has been made to estimate what frac-
tion of true dissipation has been resolved, and this resolved
fraction will vary signiﬁcantly with radial location because the
mean mixture fraction gradient varies signiﬁcantly with radius
near the base of the ﬂame. Third, results indicate that scalar pro-
ﬁles are somewhat inﬂuenced by differential diffusion even though
the Reynolds number is relatively high. Finally, these ﬂames pro-
duce higher levels of chemiluminescence than the CH4/air piloted
ﬂames, presenting some problems as explained below.
The current ﬂames are characterized by lower levels of ﬂuores-
cence interference but higher levels of ﬂame luminosity than the
previously studied CH4/air ﬂames. Normally, a CH4/H2 ﬂame would
be less luminous than a corresponding CH4 ﬂame. In the present
work, however, the high Reynolds number yielded high rates of
mixing and heat release, so chemiluminescence rates were corre-
spondingly high. Crosstalk terms for ﬂuorescence interference
and luminosity cannot be calibrated directly but must be opti-
mized based upon correlations within the turbulent ﬂame data
and physical constraints (i.e., avoiding negative conditional mean
mass fractions resulting from over correction of these effects). Sin-
gle-shot corrections for the contribution of luminosity to the mea-
sured Raman signals were based on the luminous background
signal from a Raman-free portion of the spectrum near 695 nm
to the red side of the H2 vibrational Raman scattering band. How-
ever, these corrections did not work as well as hoped, because the
spectrum of luminosity can vary from shot to shot and the signal is
nonlocal (photons are collected with varying efﬁciency from all re-
gions of the ﬂame intersecting the view of the collection optics).
The temporal gate for these Raman scattering measurements was
triangular with full width of 9.2 ls at half maximum. This was
achieved using the rotating mechanical shutter described in Ref.
[28]. Faster gating of 3.9 ls has since been achieved using a new
shutter design. However, Raman scattering measurements in
highly luminous ﬂames may require intensiﬁer gating (at the ex-
pense of collection efﬁciency), a spectral ﬁtting approach to data
processing [29], or implementation of polarization separation and
subtraction [30,31].
Corrections for luminosity were tuned as a best effort, based on
the experience of the ﬁrst author. The /j = 3.2 and /j = 2.5 ﬂames
were processed with the same ‘calibration’ ﬁle. The luminosity cor-
rections were adjusted slightly for the /j = 2.1 ﬂames to eliminate
negative conditional mean H2 mass fraction values in the fully
burnt case. Uncertainty in the corrections for ﬂame luminosity
contributes to the uncertainty in the measured mass fractions,
but the precise contributions cannot be determined from the avail-
able data. These corrections may be too large or too small and can
therefore cause errors in either direction in mixture fraction and
individual species mass fractions. Because of the viewing angle of
the collection lens, measurements in stoichiometric and rich sam-
ples are more inﬂuenced by luminosity than lean samples. Overall,
we believe that uncertainties in species mass fractions and the
mixture fraction are perhaps 2% higher in these ﬂames than in
previous CH4/air ﬂames. Luminosity does not signiﬁcantly increase
uncertainty in temperature from Rayleigh scattering. This is be-
cause ﬂuctuations in luminosity tend to affects various species
similarly, such that mole fractions and the calculated Rayleigh
scattering cross-section are not strongly inﬂuenced. Table 2 lists
representative uncertainties for measured scalars. These uncer-
tainties are still small compared to mass fraction variances in the
locally extinguished ﬂames and compared to the measured
changes in temperature and species as local extinction probability
increases. Therefore, the main value of these ﬂames in testing pre-
dictions of localized extinction remains intact.
The mixture fraction was calculated from the measured mass
fractions using the Bilger formulation [32] modiﬁed as in the pre-
vious work on partially premixed ﬂames [3,5–7] to exclude the
oxygen terms. This reduces the sensitivity of mixture fraction re-
sults to measurement noise and ﬂuorescence interferences. Aver-
age values of mixture fraction obtained from the modiﬁed
expression and from the full Bilger expression are nearly identical
in the turbulent ﬂames investigated here.

3. Results and discussion
For each series of ﬂames the ﬂow rate through the central jet re-
mains constant while the ﬂow rate to the pilot ﬂame is decreased.
In order to understand the changes in the overall ﬂow ﬁeld that
lead to increased probability of localized extinction, scalar mea-
surements from the pilot entrainment region are presented ﬁrst
and followed by a discussion of results from the extinction region.
3.1. Pilot entrainment region
Measurements of scalar proﬁles at the exit plane of the jet
would be useful in deﬁning boundary conditions for calculations,
but cannot be obtained with the same completeness and accuracy
as measurements slightly downstream. Therefore, in order to pro-
vide a check on modeled inﬂow conditions and track the pilot
entrainment process upstream of the extinction region, radial pro-
ﬁles were measured at x/d = 1 and x/d = 2 in each ﬂame. Each pro-
ﬁle included a total of 400 laser shots, with 100 shots taken with
the 6-mm imaged region centered at each of four locations, r = 0,
3, 6, 9 mm, overlapping by half. Radial proﬁles of Favre average
mixture fraction and temperature are shown in Figs. 1–3 along
with corresponding density weighed conditional means for the
three different ﬂame series.
It is immediately obvious in each ﬂame series that unburnt
reactants from the pilot ﬂow are present at r  8 mm near the out-
er edge of the pilot annulus in the cases having the highest pilot
ﬂow rates (50 and 37 lpm). This was not apparent during the
experiments and was discovered only after the measurements
were completed and data fully processed. That is to say the pilot
ﬂame looked normal to the eye for all cases. We do not consider
this to be a signiﬁcant issue for modeling of these ﬂames for the
following reasons. The fraction of pilot gas affected is small, and
the unburnt fuel appears only near the outer edge of the pilot. Most
of the fuel is burnt by x/d = 2, with only a small deviation in mean
proﬁles for the 50-lpm cases remaining at that position. This burn-
out occurs well away from the region where the central jet is
entraining burnt gas from the pilot. In all the ﬂames with signiﬁ-
cant extinction, i.e., with pilot ﬂows of 25 lpm and below, there
is no indication of unburnt pilot gas at any measurement location.
This was conﬁrmed in the scatter data (not shown) where a small
percentage of CH4 mass fraction values reached 0.005 in the 37 lpm
pilot cases at x/d = 2 but dropped to zero (within measurement
uncertainty) for the 25 lpm and lower pilot cases. Even with the
50-lpm pilot ﬂames, the progression of results from x/d = 1, 2,
and 4 suggests that any pockets of pilot reactants burn out before
being entrained into the central jet. We therefore propose that
computations may neglect this detail and assume a fully burnt pi-
lot ﬂow at the exit plane.
Aside from the deviations caused by unburnt fuel near the outer
part of the pilot ﬂame, the density-weighted conditional mean pro-
ﬁles of temperature and CH4 mass fraction, YCH4, are nearly identi-
cal within each ﬂame series, especially at x/d = 1. This is because
the central jet velocity is ﬁxed in each series, and nothing signiﬁ-
cant happens until the pure products of the pilot are fully en-
trained and the jet begins to entrain gas that has been diluted by
coﬂowing air. This happens faster as the pilot ﬂow rate is reduced.
At x/d = 2 the plateau in the radial proﬁles of mixture fraction that
corresponds to the pilot condition has disappeared in all but the
highest pilot ﬂow cases.

ﬁnite rate chemistry. Localized extinction is observed for a small

3.2. Region of local extinction
The measurement location at x/d = 4 was roughly at the middle
of the visually observed region of reduced ﬂame luminosity due to
local extinction. None of the measured ﬂames exhibited global
extinction; all developed into fully burning ﬂames farther down-
stream. At this near-ﬁeld location the 6-mm probe segment was
sufﬁcient to include almost the entire mixing layer from potential
core (pure jet ﬂuid) to coﬂowing air. Consequently, all measure-
ments (2000 shots for each ﬂame condition) were acquired at just
one position of the probe, which was oriented along the ﬂame ra-
dius and centered 6 mm from the jet axis.

The selection of a speciﬁc laminar ﬂame reference for the present
jet ﬂames is somewhat arbitrary for two reasons. First, partially pre-
mixed methane ﬂames at low strain rates have a double reaction
zone,withthe mainreactionzoneat thestoichiometricmixturefrac-
tion and a fuel-rich ‘premixed’ reaction zone that is supported by the
presence of the main reaction zone. The structure of the fuel-rich
reaction zone and its separation distance from the stoichiometric
reaction zone is strongly inﬂuenced by both strain rate and radiation
[35,36]. Second, it is not clear how important the effects of differen-
tial diffusion are in the present jet ﬂames. It is common to assume
unity Lewis number (equal diffusivities for species and thermal en-
ergy) in the modeling of turbulent ﬂames. Results from studies
involving multiscalar measurements in downstream portions of
fully connected turbulent jet ﬂames have been well represented by
laminar ﬂame calculations with all species diffusivities set equal to
the thermal diffusivity (e.g., [7,37]). However, previous studies of
nonpremixed or partially premixed jet ﬂames have also shown that
differential diffusion effects can be important in the near ﬁeld of jet
ﬂames, particularly at low Reynolds number [7]. The present ﬂames
have reasonably high Re, but measurements are from the near ﬁeld,
so differential diffusion can not be discounted.
Effects of strain rate and differential diffusion on laminar ﬂame
structure are illustrated in Fig. 11, which shows results for major
species mass fractions and temperature from opposed ﬂow calcu-
lations at three strain rates (25, 100, and 400 s1) and using mul-
ti-component transport (Fig. 11a) or equal diffusivities (Fig. 11b).
Fuel-side composition corresponds to the / = 2.5 jet ﬂame. Both
25 s1 calculations (solid curves) show distinct double reaction
zones. The choice of molecular transport model affects all parts
of the ﬂame, but differences are most obvious at very rich condi-
tions where the curves of species mass fractions vs. mixture frac-
tion show signiﬁcant bending due to preferential diffusion of H2
when molecular transport is calculated in the normal way.
Measured conditional means of T, YH2O, and YCO2 are shown in
Fig. 12 for the three fully connected jet ﬂames (50 lpm pilot ﬂows)
and compared with laminar ﬂame curves for strain rates of 100 and
400 s1 using the two different transport assumptions. The turbu-
lent ﬂame measurements show no indication of a fuel-rich reaction
zone. Apparently, the high mixing rates (high scalar dissipation
rates) in the fuel-rich portion of the shear layer prevent the rela-
tively slow reactions from progressing to any signiﬁcant degree
at these conditions. However, there is some bending of the curves
of conditional mean species mass fraction vs. mixture fraction at
fuel-rich conditions, which suggests an inﬂuence of preferential
H2 diffusion. At stoichiometric and fuel-lean conditions the mea-
sured conditional means tend to fall between the laminar curves
for the two transport assumptions. This suggests that turbulent
transport is not dominate, allowing preferential molecular diffu-
sion to have some inﬂuence on the overall ﬂame structure. It
should be said, however, that uncertainty in mixture fraction and
mass fractions makes it difﬁcult to draw ﬁrm quantitative conclu-
sions on the relative importance of molecular and turbulent trans-
port in these ﬂames.

Fig. 13 compares measured and calculated results for the differ-
ential diffusion parameter, z = nH  nC, the difference between ele-
mental mixture fractions for hydrogen and carbon. Results for /
= 2.5 are qualitatively similar to those from the other two ﬂames.
If transport where completely dominated by turbulent advection,
then measured z would be zero for all values of mixture fraction.
The turbulent ﬂame measurements generally show z values of
smaller magnitude than in the laminar ﬂame calculations. Condi-
tional mean z is slightly negative for very rich conditions, indicat-
ing preferential diffusion of H2 away from the jet ﬂuid at the rich
edge of the mixing layer. Measured z values near stoichiometric
are similar to those in the laminar ﬂames. However, the variations
in z across mixture fraction space are smaller than in the calcula-
tions. As the probability of local extinction increases within the
turbulent ﬂame series, the measured z values tend to decrease in
magnitude. There are a few possible physical effects and experi-
mental artifacts that might contribute to the observed trends in
the measurements. First, as the pilot ﬂow rate decreases, mixing
rates increase across stoichiometric and lean samples (Fig. 8),
and this is generally expected to reduce the relative importance
of differential diffusion. Second, with increasing probability of
extinction the fraction of unburnt CH4 increases, and part of the
differential diffusion effect comes from the separation and subse-
quent diffusion of hydrogen away from carbon in the reaction
zone. Third, the level of ﬂame luminosity decreases as the probabil-
ity of local extinction increases, and this could cause a systematic
shift in conditional mean species mass fractions due to errors in
corrections for luminosity.
Based on the observations from Figs. 12 and 13 that differential
diffusion has some inﬂuence on scalar structure in these jet ﬂame,
the relatively low strain laminar ﬂame (25 s1) with differential
transport was selected as a reference for calculation of the burning
index for temperature. Conditional mean results for BIT from all
three ﬂame series (18 ﬂames in total) are plotted in Fig. 14. The
fully connected ﬂames at each value of the jet equivalence ratio
each show BIT near unity from lean conditions across to mixture
fraction values roughly 1.3 times stoichiometric. The modest dip
in burning index centered near 0.8 nst may well be due to system-
atic error of a few percent in mixture fraction within that range of
conditions. BIT is quite low in fuel-rich samples, even in the fully
burning jet ﬂames, because mixing rates are still sufﬁciently high
to prevent formation of the second reaction zone that occurs in
the low strain rate laminar ﬂame calculations. The effect is most
severe in the / = 2.1 series, where BIT drops below 0.2 even for
the fully connected ﬂame with the highest pilot ﬂow rate. As pilot
ﬂow rate is decreased in each ﬂame series BIT shifts downward
across a broad interval in mixture fraction. The relative changes
in BIT between ﬂames in each series should be insensitive to uncer-
tainties in mixture fraction or individual species. The shift from
ﬂame to ﬂame should also be insensitive to uncertainty in the mea-
sured indicators of differential diffusion. Therefore, prediction of
this progression should be a good test for turbulent combustion
models that include strong effects of ﬁnite rate chemistry.

gan with a fully connected ﬂame and stepped through increasing
levels of local extinction. Local extinction in these ﬂames occurred
relatively close to the jet exit in a region centered roughly four noz-
zle diameters downstream of the jet exit (x/d = 4). Measurements
were obtained at x/d = 1 and x/d = 2 in order to characterize the pi-
lot entrainment region and at x/d = 4 to investigate the region of lo-
cal extinction.
These CH4/H2/air ﬂames produced lower levels of hydrocarbon
ﬂuorescence than previously studied CH4/air ﬂames. However,
chemiluminescence was more intense due to high rates of mixing
and reaction. Corrections for this luminous background proved dif-
ﬁcult, leading to increased uncertainty in the Raman measure-
ments.
In
spite
of
this
limitation,
overall
results
on
the
progression of local extinction are considered robust, particularly
when expressed as relative changes in the burning index based
on temperature.
In each ﬂame series results show that reduction in pilot ﬂow
rate allows a more rapid entrainment of hot products from the pi-
lot ﬂame into the mixing layer at the edge of the high velocity jet.
Once the pilot products have been entrained, the scalar dissipation
rate within the reaction zone increases to a critical level, causing
local extinction. Dissipation measurements are not fully resolved
but still provide qualitative insights on turbulence–chemistry
interaction in the present ﬂames. With increasing probability of lo-
cal extinction the resolved density-weighted conditional mean sca-
lar dissipation levels off and then actually decreases. This may be
due to the inﬂuence of decreased temperature on diffusivity.
Conditional mean scalar measurements were compared with
results from laminar ﬂame calculations. These highly strained tur-
bulent ﬂames showed no indication of a double reaction zone, as
appears in partially premixed laminar ﬂames at low strain rates.
However, in spite of the relatively high Reynolds numbers of the
turbulent ﬂames, results indicate that differential diffusion has
some inﬂuence on scalar structure at the near-ﬁeld locations stud-
ied in the present work. This is a disadvantage of having the extinc-
tion region close to the jet exit.
The absence of signiﬁcant reaction progress at fuel-rich condi-
tions was apparent in results for the conditional mean burning in-
dex based on temperature, BIT. The results showed a progressive
decrease in BIT as pilot ﬂow rate was decreased, and the effect
was broadly spread across the full mixture fraction range. This
measure of the degree of local extinction should allow for conve-
nient comparison with model calculations of the present jet ﬂames,
and prediction of the progression in the burning index should be a
good test for turbulent combustion models that include strong ef-
fects of ﬁnite rate chemistry.
