ABSTRACT 
To validate 30% H2 (by volume) capability for the Siemens Energy’s 9 MW SGT-300-2S engine, 
high-pressure rig testing was conducted. The dry low emissions combustor was initially operated with 
natural gas to establish a baseline operation. Subsequently, it was fuelled with a 30% H2 (by volume) 
blend under various ambient temperatures. To evaluate operational margins, tests were extended to 35% 
and 40% H2. Finally, the combustion system’s robustness was rigorously tested under various of 
overfiring conditions. The impact of H2 concentration on combustor performance was investigated, 
focusing on factors such as burner metal temperature, NOx and CO emissions, combustion dynamics, 
and combustor wall temperatures. Test results indicate that burner metal temperatures and NOx 
emissions increase with increasing H2 concentration. The turbine entry temperature at which CO 
emissions begin to rise is lowered with increasing H2 concentration. This suggests that adding H2 to the 
fuel can lower the turndown temperature for controlling CO emissions, thereby improving the engine’s 
part load thermal efficiency. Combustion dynamic pressure amplitudes remained low for H2 
concentrations up to 40%. Combustor wall temperatures for H2 operation up to 40% were comparable 
to those for natural gas. Test results demonstrate that the combustion system can safely operate with up 
to 40% H2 by volume in the fuel. The effect of pilot split on combustion was investigated for natural 
gas and the fuel blends with 30% and 40% H2. A key finding is that different fuel compositions may 
require different pilot split and turndown control temperature for optimal operation.

1. INTRODUCTION 
Gas turbines (GTs) are vital in fulfilling the world's energy needs, serving both power generation 
and industrial applications. However, their operation with fossil fuels releases carbon dioxide (CO2) 
emissions, contributing to climate change. Utilising hydrogen (H2) as a fuel offers a potential solution 
to reduce CO2 emissions in gas turbine applications. Research and development efforts are actively 
pursuing this goal [1-3]. While transitioning to 100% H2 faces challenges related to economics, 
materials, storage, and combustion process [4-9], blending H2 with natural gas (NG) presents a viable 
pathway to gradually increase H2 usage in gas turbines [9]. This approach allows for continued progress 
towards a cleaner energy future while addressing the current limitations of H2 production and utilisation. 
H2 is a highly reactive gas that can significantly alter the combustion properties of fuels. When added 
to fuels, H2 can increase reactivity, broaden flammability limits, and raise adiabatic flame temperature 
and laminar flame speed [7, 9]. Consequently, burning fuels containing H2 generally results in higher 
NOx emissions and lower CO emissions [10-12]. These experimental investigations were conducted 
across a variety of test setups. They included a scaled-down industrial gas turbine Dry Low Emissions 
(DEL) combustor operated within a higher-pressure test rig [10], an industrial concept DLE combustor 
tested in an atmospheric pressure rig [11], and different combustor designs developed specifically for 
engine integration [12]. This diverse range of test environments provides valuable insights into the 
combustion behaviour of H2-enriched fuels across different operating conditions and combustor 
configurations. For example, observations from [10] suggest that NOx emissions may increase with 
increasing H2 content in the fuel. In some cases, NOx emissions were observed to increase by 25% with 
10% H2 addition and by 50% with 70% H2 addition. Conversely, CO emissions were observed to 
decrease by 50% with 90% H2 addition. This shift in emissions is primarily due to higher flame 
temperatures and changes in chemical reaction pathways.  
Recent research by Cole et al. [13] highlights the importance of considering CO emissions when 
reporting the emissions of non-carbon-containing fuels such as pure H2. This emphasis arises from the 
recognition that even in the combustion of pure H2, trace amounts of CO2 present in the ambient air can

Furthermore, fuels containing H2 have the potential to increase the combustion dynamic pressure

Adiabatic flame temperature and laminar flame speed calculations were conducted for methane (CH₄) 
and H₂ blends operating under conditions representative of a generic 9 MW gas turbine combustor [17]. 
Given the comparable adiabatic flame temperature and laminar flame speed of CH₄ and natural gas 
under these conditions [17], Figure 2 can also be used to represent the corresponding values for natural 
gas and H₂ blends. The fuel-air mixture was maintained at a pressure of 14 bar, a temperature of 666 K, 
and an equivalence ratio of 0.55. Adiabatic flame temperatures were calculated using NASA 
equilibrium code [18], while laminar flame speeds were computed using Ansys Chemkin Pro (Version 
2022 R1) and the GRI 3.0 chemical kinetics mechanism [19]. The results of these calculations are 
depicted in Figure 2. 
As H₂ concentration increases in the fuel blend, both flame temperature and laminar flame speed 
increase accordingly. While the increase in laminar flame speed with H₂ concentration below 60% 
remains relatively steady, an exponential increase is evident as H₂ concentration surpasses 60%. Notably, 
when operating with 100% H₂, a substantial enhancement in laminar flame speed is observed relative 
to pure CH4. The elevated flame temperature and increased laminar flame speed associated with fuel 
blends containing higher concentrations of H₂ present significant challenges for gas turbine combustor 
design. 
Siemens Energy’s 5 MW to 15 MW industrial gas turbine engines are now capable of operating on 
a H2-enriched natural gas fuel containing at least 30% H2 by volume. To prove the 30% H2 capability 
of the 9 MW SGT-300-2S engine, high-pressure rig testing was conducted at the Siemens Energy Clean 
Energy Center (CEC) in Berlin. 
After the baseline operation with natural gas, the combustor was fuelled with a 30% H2 blend fuel 
under various ambient temperature conditions, including ISO, -20 °C, and +45 °C. The focus was on 
factors such as burner metal temperature, NOx and CO emissions, combustion dynamics, and combustor 
wall metal temperature. 
To assess operational margins, tests were extended to 35% H2 and 40% H2 under ISO conditions. 
To test the robustness of the combustor, it was also tested under various overfiring conditions to 
establish transient operability with H2 in the fuel.

2. CURRENT STUDY 
The DLE combustion system of a 9 MW Siemens Energy SGT-300 engine was extensively tested in 
a high-pressure combustion rig at engine operating conditions. This part I paper reports the high-
pressure rig test results on various fuel blends under various operating conditions. Numerical 
simulations were carried out in part II for selected fuel blends: natural gas, and with 30% and 40% H2 
at ISO full load condition. Impact of pilot split was also studied for the fuel blend with 40% H2. Large 
Eddy Simulations with a Flamelet Generated Manifold combustion model and Thickened Flame Model 
were employed. The ARM22 chemical mechanism was used to generate flamelet manifolds for each 
configuration of varied H2 concentration. 
This section provides a detailed overview of the test facility, test hardware, and test conditions 
employed in the experiment. 
 
2.1 DLE Combustion System 
The test vehicle for this research was the DLE combustion system of the SGT-300 twin-shaft engine, 
a member of the versatile SGT-300 engine family. Notable for its robustness, reliability and adaptability, 
the SGT-300 engine family can be employed in various applications, including mechanical drive, power 
generation, and combined heat and power. The family offers two distinct configurations: 
▪ Single-shaft engine (SGT-300-1S): Generating a power output of 7.9 MWe. 
▪ Twin-shaft engine (SGT-300-2S): Delivering a power output of up to 9.0 MW for 
mechanical drive and 8.5 MWe for power generation.

As a standard, the engine family operates on gaseous fuels with a WI ranging from 25 MJ/m³ to 65 
MJ/m³ (not including H2 or H2 enriched fuels). It is well-regarded for its stringent emissions guarantees, 
achieving NOx and CO emissions guarantees of 15 and 10 parts per million respectively, corrected to 
15% O₂ concentration, on a volumetric and dry basis (ppmvd), with the option of sub 10 ppmvd NOx 
[21]. 
The SGT-300-2S engine demonstrates a notable capability for part load operation by adjusting fuel 
and air flow rates without requiring variable guide vane (VGV) or bleed-to-exhaust (B2E) modulation. 
This characteristic is referred to as "natural turndown" in this paper. Additionally, the engine can be 
operated at part load through B2E modulation, a technique that bypasses compressor exit air into the 
exhaust to maintain optimal CO emissions. This method is termed "turndown control" in this paper. 
Designed as a reverse-flow can-annular combustion system, the SGT-300 combustor is based on the 
proven DLE combustor technology for gaseous and liquid fuel operation. As illustrated in Figure 3, it 
consists of three main sections: 
(1) Pilot burner: Houses the pilot fuel galleries and injectors for both gaseous and liquid fuel. 
(2) Main burner: Houses the main air swirler and main gaseous and liquid fuel system. 
(3) Combustor can: Includes the premixing chamber (prechamber) and a double-skin 
impingement-cooled liner. 
Fuel is introduced in two stages, as shown in Figure 3. The main fuel is injected into the swirler air 
path for optimal premixing and reduced NOx emissions. Pilot fuel is injected at the end of the swirler 
air path to create a diffusion flame and stabilise the combustion process. The pilot split, or pilot schedule, 
is the ratio of pilot fuel usage to total fuel usage.

Downstream of the combustor can, a transition duct is used to adapt the flow from the circular 
combustor geometry to a sector of the turbine entry annulus. 
The main aerodynamic features of the combustor, such as fuel placement, and heat release location 
are highlighted in Figure 4. The main combustion air enters through a single radial swirler at the head 
of the combustor, then turns through a right angle into the prechamber, followed by a sudden expansion 
into the combustion chamber. The swirl number is sufficiently high to induce a vortex breakdown 
reverse flow zone along the axis, forming the central recirculation zone (CRZ). This CRZ remains 
attached to the pilot burner face, establishing a firm aerodynamic base for flame stabilisation. In the 
wake of the sudden expansion, an outer recirculation zone (ORZ) is established. The flame is stabilised 
in the shear layers formed by the CRZ and ORZ.

Recently the pilot burner has been optimised through optimising the pilot gaseous fuel injection 
holes design, resulting in significant reduction in the effective flow area leading to significantly lower 
pilot fuel usage while maintaining stable fuel flow, especially at part load. The benefits of the optimised 
pilot burner include reduction in pilot fuel usage, enhanced fuel-air mixing, and stabilised fuel flow at

The DLE combustion system studied in the paper include standard main burner, optimised pilot 
burner, standard combustor can and transition duct.

2.3 Test Conditions  
The combustor's performance was initially evaluated at ISO ambient conditions using natural gas as 
a baseline. Subsequently, blended fuels containing natural gas and varying H₂ concentrations of 30%, 
35%, and 40% by volume were introduced to assess the combustor's operability with different H2 blends.  
To evaluate the impact of H2 on turndown control temperature, engine operating conditions were 
systematically varied from full power to part load under natural turndown conditions. Following this, 
turndown control temperature was optimised for each H2 concentration. Additionally, the influence of 
pilot split on combustion performance was investigated using natural gas, 30% H₂, and 40% H₂ blends. 
The effect of ambient temperature on combustor performance was assessed with a 30% H₂ fuel mixture. 
Finally, the combustor was tested under various overfiring conditions to assess its robustness and 
establish transient operability with hydrogen in the fuel. 
 
3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS 
3.1 Test Results under Natural Turndown Operation 
This section outlines the rig test results conducted at ISO ambient conditions under natural turndown 
operation. 
Pilot splits across all engine load conditions tested, as a function of turbine entry temperature (TET), 
are shown in Figure 5 for each fuel blend. To minimise the impact of pilot splits on the overall test 
results, their values were kept constant at the same TET. Pilot splits and TET were normalised against 
their full-load values. At higher TET, significantly lower pilot splits were used to reduce NOx emissions. 
To enhance flame stability and reduce CO and UHC emissions, the pilot split was typically increased 
at lower TET.

TET was normalised by its full load value. The results indicate that at the same TET and similar pilot

The increased surface temperature of the pilot burner can be attributed to two primary factors. Firstly, 
fuels containing H2 exhibit higher flame temperatures, which directly impact the heat transfer to the 
burner surface. Secondly, the higher reactivity and increased flame speed of H2 containing fuels results 
in more compact flames that propagate upstream [14], bringing them closer to the pilot burner. This 
proximity intensifies the heat transfer to the burner surface, further contributing to the observed 
temperature increase.

turndown and with turndown control operation. For natural turndown operation, TET decreases as 
engine load decreases by lowering the fuel and air mass flow rates. At a certain load condition, known

to low levels. Under these conditions, pilot split can also be kept low compared to the natural turndown 
at the same load range. Consequently, NOx emissions can be effectively reduced.

4. CONCLUSIONS  
The impact of H2 concentration on the performance of the Siemens Energy SGT-300-2S 9 MW 
industrial gas turbine combustor was experimentally investigated through a high-pressure combustor 
rig test at engine operating conditions  
H2 concentrations up to 40% were tested under natural turndown operation. NOx emissions were 
increased and the CO kick-off point was delayed as H2 concentration in the fuel blend was increased. 
The surface temperature of the pilot burner and the amplitudes of combustion dynamic pressure at two 
distinct frequency ranges increased with increasing H2 concentration, but this increase was only 
noticeable at higher engine load conditions. The combustor can wall temperatures at most locations

The influence of pilot split on combustor performance at full load was also investigated. As expected, 
reducing pilot split was found to lower NOx emissions and pilot burner surface temperature for natural 
gas. A similar trend was observed for NOx emissions for the H2 blended fuels. However, lowering pilot 
split was found to increase the pilot burner surface temperature. Ambient temperature was observed to 
have limited impact on emissions and pilot burner surface temperature but increased the combustion 
dynamic pressure amplitude at colder ambient temperature.  
Finally, the effect of H2 concentration on turndown control temperature was demonstrated. With 
higher H2 concentrations, it was found that the turndown control temperature could be lowered, leading 
to enhanced part load engine thermal efficiency. The combustion system's robustness was tested under 
overfired and elevated load conditions demonstrating stable combustion and proving its reliability. 
A key observation in this study is that the optimal pilot split and turndown control temperature can 
vary significantly depending on the specific fuel composition. This finding underscores the importance 
of optimising these parameters for each fuel blend to ensure stable and efficient combustion operation. 
The test results showed that the SGT-300-2S combustion system can safely operate with up to 40% 
H2 in the fuel blend without experiencing flashback or hardware damage. When using 30% H2, NOx 
emissions of 25 ppmvd and CO emissions of 10 ppmvd can be achieved under turndown control 
operation across a wide range of engine load conditions. The effect of H2 enrichment on combustor exit 
temperature distribution is numerically studied and discussed in part II paper and will be a future 
validation study on the high-pressure combustor test rig.
