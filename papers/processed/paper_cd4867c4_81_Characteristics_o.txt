1. INTRODUCIlON 
Flames with swirl are often used in practical 
combustion 
systems because the swirl-induced 
recirculation zone stabilizes the flame. 
Numerous studies have been concerned with the 
swirling flame as reviewed in Refs. [l] and [2]. 
They are concerned with the flow without flame [3- 
5] and with flame [6-lo], flame configuration [ 1 l- 
131, flame stability [ 13,141, and noise emission [ 14, 
151. 
As for the formation 
and emissions of air 
pollutants such as NO, CO, and hydrocarbons in 
swirling flames, studies are limited [l&21] 
and the 
swirl has inconsistant effects of increasing [14,18] 
or decreasing [19, 203 NO, emission probably 
because of different flame structure. Little attention 
has been paid to the processes of the emission of 
CO and unburnt hydrocarbons in relation to the 
flame structure. Detailed measurements of gas 
species and temperature in swirling flames seem to 
be necessary to reveal the interrelation between the 
flame structure and the formation 
and emission 
characteristics. 
In the present study, detailed measurements of 
gas species concentration 
and temperature were 
made in two different types of flames formed 
around a fuel jet surrounded by swirling air flow, 
and effects of a wide range of combustion parame- 
ters on pollutant emissions were examined in order 
to interpret the characteristics and processes of 
pollutant formation and emissions as they relate to 
the flame structure.

3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS 
3.1. Typical Flame Configm-ation 
Two typical types of nonluminous flame arise when 
primary and secondary air ratio are changed 
keeping the fuel and total air flow rate constant. 
Photographs and sketches of the flame types A and 
B are shown in Fig. 2. Type B is formed for higher 
primary air ratio than type A. The flame conditions 
are noted in the figure caption. The swirling 
primary air serves to form a recirculation zone 
which is visualized by the light emission upon 
adding a small amount of NaCl to the flow, and the 
position and the size of the recirculation zone are 
observed. The recirculation zone of the reversed 
cone shape is drawn back into the primary air 
nozzle and extends downstream to a location 25 or 
35-40 mm from the primary air nozzle tip for the 
flame of type A or B, respectively. The recirculation 
zone is filled with high-temperature 
burnt gas to 
anchor the flame and the fuel flows mainly around 
the recirculation zone forming a fuel layer as shown 
schematically in the sketches of Fig. 2. This is 
confirmed by the measurements of gas spedies 
concentration and temperature in the next section. 
In the flame of type A, the fuel layer burns to form a 
ring flame around the recirculation 
zone that 
causes the sudden spread of the flame width. In the 
flame of type B, however, the ring flame is not 
observed because the fuel layer around the recircu- 
lation zone bums at the downstream section after 
dilution by the excess air. 
The mode of the burning of the fuel layer around 
the recirculation zone characterizes the typical 
flame configuration and structure.

1. The structure of the flame formed around a fuel 
jet surrounded by swirling air is characterized by 
the mixing and combustion of the fuel layer flowing 
around the high-temperature 
recirculation zone. 
Two typical flame configurations (types A and B) 
arise according to the primary air ratio. In the flame 
of type A, the fuel layer burns near stoichiometric 
condition to form a ring flame around the recircu- 
lation zone. In the flame of type B, the fuel layer is 
excessively diluted by the mixing with the sur- 
rounding 
air, and it burns in the downstream 
without forming a distinctive ring flame. The flame 
of type A occurs for lower primary air ratio than 
tyPe B. 
2. NO, formation and emission are signilicantly 
affected by the flame structure. Low NO, emission 
in the flame of type B occurs because the fuel layer 
burns at relatively low temperature as the result of 
dilution by excess air prior to the combustion, while 
the temperature decays rapidly because of the rapid 
mixing with the surrounding air even though the 
temperature 
in the recirculation 
zone is high. 
Higher NO, emission in the flame of type A occurs 
because high temperature generated in the recircu- 
lation zone and the surrounding ring flame con- 
tinues to the downstream as the result of slow 
mixing with the excess air and NO, is increasingly 
formed in the downstream.
