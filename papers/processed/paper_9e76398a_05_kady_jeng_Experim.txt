The present work studies the effect of utilizing counter swirling flow on pollutant 
emission characteristics at different equivalence ratios and inlet air temperatures. A 
multiple gas analyzer based on Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy is employed 
to measure emissions concentrations generated during combustion of Jet-A fuel in a swirl 
cup assembly. These measurements are located within the combustion chamber and at the 
exit plane. The goal of this study is to provide more insight into nitric oxide and carbon 
monoxide formation/destruction processes within the recirculation zone and throughout the 
combustor. The results illustrate that NOx production is a function of both the recirculation 
zone and the flame length. At low overall equivalence ratios, the recirculation zone is found 
to be the main producer of NOx. At near stoichiometric conditions, the post recirculation 
zone appears to be responsible for the majority of NOx produced.

I. 
Introduction 
ollutant emissions emanating from gas turbine engines has been of great concern in the last three decades. These 
emissions, including nitrogen oxides, carbon monoxide and unburned hydrocarbons, has a great impact on the 
environment. The reduction of CO, NOx and UHC simultaneously is challenging due to the fact that the basic 
feature of nearly all methods of emissions reduction is a “trade-off” between CO and UHC in one side and NOx on 
the other side.1 The introduction of swirling flow in the gas turbine engine industry proved a promising way in 
reducing pollutant emissions. Highly swirling flows create an internal flow recirculation zone that entrains and 
circulates a portion of the hot combustion products in the gas turbine combustor dome. This recirculation zone does 
not act only as a heat reservoir but it plays an active role in better mixing, improving blowout limits, reducing the 
combustor length and lowering emissions.2-4 Continuous efforts to further decrease NOx levels require an 
understanding of the physical and chemical processes that occur within the recirculation zone. It was found that 
increasing the swirl intensity (provided that the recirculation zone exists) does not significantly affect NOx levels.5,6 
The effect of the recirculation zone on the NOx formation was previously observed, but different conclusions were

at an overall equivalence ratio of 0.217. They observed that high NO concentrations were found along the centerline 
in the region thought to be within the recirculation zone. Chen ,6 concluded that for equivalence ratios ≤ 0.5, NOx is

of their respective toroidal recirculation zones. Measured NO concentrations were the highest in the toroidal 
recirculation zones.  Unfortunately, the equivalence ratios used in their study was not mentioned. Claypole et al,9 
studied the influence of swirl levels on the aerodynamics and NOx emissions at a constant load. The fuel used was 
natural gas with 10 % excess air above stoichiometry. It was concluded that the recirculation zone, despite its long 
residence time and moderate temperatures, did not contribute to NOx formation. Kenbar et al,5 studied the effect of 
swirl on the NOx formation within a gas fired furnace. It was concluded that most of the NOx formation took place 
in the reaction zone. Some further reactions occur within the recirculation zone. 
From these studies, one can predict that different modes of NOx formation happen within the recirculation zone 
based on the overall equivalence ratio.  
A great number of studies considered only exit plane measurements and the combustion processes were 
considered as an in/out process. On the other hand, only few studies within the combustor have been reported. To 
understand pollutant emissions formation/destruction processes, the need for in-situ species measurements becomes 
increasingly important. LDV and PDPA measurements have been utilized in the Combustion Diagnostics Lab 
(CDL) at the University of Cincinnati to study the flow aerodynamics of swirl stabilized flames and the recirculation 
zone interactions. In this study, species concentration measurements within the combustion chamber are employed 
in efforts to combine the chemical processes with the flow aerodynamics. Measurements of species concentrations 
including CO2, CO, H2O, O2, NO, NO2, SO2 and CH4 are taken within the recirculation zone and the chamber 
centerline at different equivalence ratios. To study the effect of the inlet air temperature at a wide range of 
equivalence ratios, exit plane measurements are also addressed. To determine the locations where the formation of 
pollutant emissions is a maximum, no dilution air was admitted to the chamber in order to isolate its effect on the 
pollutants formation/destruction processes.  
II. 
Experimental setup 
 
The experimental configuration used in the present study consists of a co-annular counter-rotating swirl cup 
assembly, a simplex nozzle and a square chamber. A multiple gas analyzer based on Fourier transform infrared 
(FTIR) spectroscopy is employed to measure the emission species concentrations generated during combustion of 
Jet-A fuel. In addition, a paramagnetic O2 analyzer is used to measure the excess oxygen within the sample. 
Platinum fine wire Pt/Pt30Rh type B thermocouple is utilized to obtain a spatially resolved temperature profile.  
 
A. Test Facility 
A schematic diagram of the test facility is shown in Fig. 1. The air system consists of a compressor, a dryer, a 0.5 
micron particle filter, a pressure regulator, and an air storage tank. An ASME orifice flow meter is utilized to 
measure airflow rate into the system. A 39 kW heater can be used to preheat the air per test requirements. The air 
passes through a honeycomb flow conditioner to the test section. The fuel system includes a fuel tank, a fuel pump, a 
fuel filter, a coriolis flowmeter, and a needle valve for controlling the fuel flow rate.  
The fuel is injected by a hollow cone simplex atomizer with 90-deg spray angle mounted in the center of the 
swirler. The swirler used in this study is designed to generate two coaxial, counter swirling airstreams. The swirler 
flare exit diameter D is 40mm. The detailed design of this swirl cup has been fully described elsewhere.10 The 
combustion chamber used in this study is made of both quartz glass and stainless steel plates with a 3.0 inch square 
cross-section and a height of 14 inches. The chamber is modified such that it can allow a gas sampling probe to 
traverse in two dimensions inside it.

stream lines. Gouldin,12 suggested that if the probe and tip diameters are smaller than 10% of any characteristic 
length scale of the flow provided that the probe is aligned with the mean flow for at least 10 diameters, interference 
effects can be kept minimum. As a result, the sampling probe is designed with as small diameter as possible such 
that flow perturbations will be minimized. Moreover, the probe configuration is based on standard designs in order

inch and a tip inlet diameter of 0.027 in. The probe consists of three concentric tubes. The inner most tube is used 
for the gas sample suction while the other two tubes are used for the circulation of the cooling fluid. Heated Teflon-
lined stainless steel tubing is utilized to deliver the sample to the gas analyzers. The temperature in the tubing was 
kept at 150°C to avoid water vapor condensation. In order to freeze the reactions in the sampling probe, water 
cooling was circulated in the probe to cool the sample and protect the probe itself from excessive heating. 
Uncertainties in the measured water vapor and nitrogen oxides concentrations in the sample were significant. 
Similar observations were reported.7,13 This might be attributed to the water vapor condensation on the sampling 
tube surface due to excessive cooling.14 In order to eliminate this problem, oil is utilized instead of water to cool the 
sample. It was suggested that cooling the sample down to a temperature of 300 °C is sufficient to freeze further 
reactions in the probe.15 The oil cooling system was designed such that the oil inlet temperature is to be kept at 225 
°F. This will be sufficient enough to cool the sample to temperatures below 300 °C and keep the water in the sample 
in its vapor phase. Hassan et al,16 suggested that the influence of the velocity appears to be small in the range of 40-
100 m/s. As a result, the flow rate of the gas sample is kept at moderate values as compared with the flow velocities. 
Repeatability of the measurements at different days for CO and NO was in agreement within 3.0%.  This agreement 
made us confident about the current configuration.  
 
C. Emission measurements 
At each measurement location, primary species of interest including carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide, nitrogen 
dioxide, nitrogen monoxide, sulfur dioxide, water vapor, and unburned hydrocarbons are measured. These 
measurements are employed using a MultiGas 2030 FTIR analyzer. Particles in the sample are removed by passing 
the sample through a Sample conditioning unit that includes two heated particle filters to remove particles larger 
than 0.6 µm as shown in Fig 1.  
The choice of a multiple gas analyzer based on Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) to be used for gas 
concentration measurements over single gas analyzers was preferred due to many reasons, among these are the 
ability of the FTIR analyzer to measure on a wet basis makes it possible to measure the species concentrations with 
minimal sample conditioning. Besides its ability to measure the species of interest like CO, CO2, NO, NO2, and 
H2O, the FTIR can also measure many other species like sulfur dioxide, methanol, ethylene and formaldehyde. 
Another benefit of the FTIR is its ability to perform data validation, since the infrared spectrum recorded is stored 
along with the concentration report. This means that any unusual data points can be reevaluated or validated long 
after the test was completed.17  
FTIR is based on the fact that every molecule (except atomics such as noble gases and homonuclear diatoms 
which are infrared inactive) absorbs infrared radiation with a characteristic, molecule-specific absorption spectrum. 
Gas species can be identified and quantified based on the location and magnitude of these absorptions. Figure 2 
represents the absorbance spectrum of different gases that can be found in a gas turbine combustor. The basis of all 
quantitative analysis in FTIR is Beer’s Law, which relates concentration to absorbance.18  
Since FTIR analyzers do not measure diatomic gases (e.g. O2 and N2), a paramagnetic O2 analyzer is employed 
to measure the excess Oxygen within the sample. 
 
D. Temperature measurement 
Mean temperatures are obtained using Pt/Pt30Rh type B bare wire thermocouple with a wire diameter of 0.03 in. 
Catalytic action is reduced by coating with a layer of silica using a flame incorporating silicone oil.19 The 
thermocouple bead diameter is estimated to be about 0.007 inch.  
 
E. Test Conditions 
The goal of this study is to understand the chemical processes along with flow aerodynamics in the primary zone 
of a model gas turbine combustor without dilution air present. Two main parameters are varied; these are the overall 
equivalence ratio and the inlet air temperature. Throughout the measurements, the pressure drop across the 
combustor is kept constant at 4.0 ±0.1% of the atmospheric pressure. Equivalence ratio variations are done by 
varying the fuel flow rate. Thus, the effect of the residence time can be isolated from the equivalence ratio 
variations. Exit plane measurements were utilized to study the effect of varying both the inlet air temperature and the 
overall equivalence ratio on pollutant emissions of interest such as carbon monoxide and nitrogen oxides. These 
measurements were executed at the center point, 13.0 in above the swirler flare exit. Inlet air was preheated to three 
different temperatures; these are 130 °F, 300 °F, and 450 °F. The equivalence ratio was varied from near lean 
blowout condition to a rich fuel state at φ = 1.2.  Two sets of species concentration measurements within a model 
combustor have been taken. In the first set, samples were taken at a total number of 84 locations to represent both

IV. 
Conclusion 
 
At very lean equivalence ratios, if the combustion temperature is less than the quenching temperature (1500 K), 
increasing either the inlet air temperature or the equivalence ratio will accelerate the oxidation of CO to CO2 
resulting in a reduction of CO emissions at the exit plane. But, if the combustion temperature is higher than the 
quenching temperature, then increasing the inlet air temperature will lead to an increase in the production of CO by 
dissociation.  
The locations of maximum UHC are found to precede that of the CO. The maximum NOx concentrations are 
found to be farther downstream of CO and UHC maximums. Thus the possibility exists that these emissions can be 
lowered simultaneously by controlling the temperature within the chamber.  
NOx emissions increase with increasing the inlet air temperature and attain its maximum on the lean side of 
stoichiometry. In the rich fuel combustion, increasing inlet air temperature will help reducing NOx production 
In swirl stabilized flames, NOx, production is believed to be a function of both the recirculation zone and the 
flame length. With equivalence ratio variations, it is observed that NOx production can be divided into three modes. 
In the first one, which is at low overall equivalence ratios up to φ = 0.6, the flame length is shorter than the 
recirculation zone height. Most of the combustion is believed to be completed within the recirculation zone. Thus, 
the recirculation zone becomes the main producer of NOx due to its higher temperature and local equivalence ratios 
as compared with the post recirculation zone. The second mode, which is a transitional one, appears at overall 
equivalence ratios 0.6 < φ < 0.9. In this mode, the local equivalence ratio in the recirculation zone is near 
stoichiometric. However, the flame is longer than the recirculation zone height. NOx production in this mode is 
shared between the recirculation and the post recirculation zones. In the last mode, at equivalence ratios φ > 0.9, the 
recirculation zone is fuel rich. The post recirculation zone appears to be the main producer of NOx.
