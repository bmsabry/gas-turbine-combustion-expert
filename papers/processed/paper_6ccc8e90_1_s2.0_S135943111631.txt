In the present study, an FDF (Flame Describing Function)-based approach is applied to numerically
investigate the attenuation characteristics of a perforated plate. In order to effectively suppress the com-
bustion instability, this study adopts an alternative approach to optimally design a passive damper using
a perforated plate. We conﬁrm that the proposed attenuation conditions of a perforated plate ensure
quite stable conditions over the entire range of velocity perturbation ratios. Moreover, the present
method using Luong model can be applied to all Strouhal number regimes in contrast to a conventional
method using the Howe model. Numerical results were also compared with experimental data in terms
of the resonant frequency and limit-cycle velocity perturbation ratio. Although slight discrepancies exist,
the present FDF-based approach yields an overall agreement with the experimental results. In addition,
the numerical results show that perforated plates with smaller holes or thinner plate thicknesses are
more effective at stabilizing the oscillation induced by combustion.

1. Introduction
In order to comply with strict regulatory requirements, the gas
turbine industry has typically adopted lean premixed combustors
as the most promising solution [1]. However, lean premixed com-
bustors are quite susceptible to combustion instability, which
leads to serious failure of the system [2]. Therefore, the combustion
instability must be technically resolved prior to system operation.
Numerical studies have been carried out in attempts to understand
the driving mechanism of the combustion instability as well as to
optimally suppress the thermoacoustic oscillations. These numeri-
cal models are largely classiﬁed by the self-excited mode strategy
and the forced mode strategy [1].
In the self-excited mode strategy, CFD calculations need to be
carried out until small ﬂuctuations are developed with a certain
resonant frequency. Thus, external forcing is not required because
the ﬂow ﬁeld has its own instability modes. In the past, unsteady
RANS (URANS) based turbulent combustion models [1,3–7] have
been employed to numerically analyze the combustion instability;
however, these approaches are limited in their ability to realisti-
cally capture the physical processes involved in the combustion
instability. To resolve these shortcomings, the large eddy simula-
tion (LES) based turbulent combustion model has frequently been
applied to predict unstable combustion dynamics [1,8–13]. It is
widely recognized that the LES-based turbulent combustion model
is the ultimate design tool for identifying the optimal stable condi-
tions of real combustors. However, the prediction capability of the
LES-based approach is quite sensitive to the acoustic boundary
conditions and heat transfer, which are often uncertain. Moreover,
this approach requires excessive CPU time [11].
In the forced mode strategy, coupling between the velocity per-
turbation and the heat release rate perturbation can be expressed
by the ﬂame transfer function (FTF) or the ﬂame describing func-
tion (FDF) [14–25]. Recently, perturbations of equivalence ratio
are also accounted for obtaining FTF or FDF [11,25]. In this strategy,
by imposing external forcing at the inlet in experiments or simula-
tions, the gain index and time delay are measured or calculated
between the reference point and the ﬂame zone. To avoid unneces-
sary reﬂected waves, a non-reﬂecting boundary condition must be
speciﬁed [1]. Compared to the self-excited mode strategy, this
method requires much less computational cost if FTF or FDF data
are available. Due to this advantage, this forced mode strategy
has gained popularity for the analysis of thermoacoustic instability
over a wide range of the operating conditions and various geomet-
ric conditions [19,21–24]. However, in highly turbulent swirling
ﬂames, it is difﬁcult to obtain accurate FTF or FDF data. Depending
upon FTF or FDF models, reference points, and perturbation ratios,
the values of FTF or FDF could be quite different, even at the same
frequency. Even if the difference in the values of FTF or FDF is

applicable for analyzing linear combustion instability. Alterna-

of the FTF-based approach. In the FDF-based approach, the eigen-
frequencies are varied by increasing the velocity perturbation
ratios [14,20,22]. This unique feature enables the FDF-based
approach to accurately predict the resonant frequencies and non-
linear combustion dynamics such as the hysteresis, frequency
shifting, mode change, or limit-cycle instability [14,18–21].
To stabilize the oscillation arising from the combustion instabil-
ity, these three kinds of damping devices are mostly used: quarter-
wave resonators, Helmholtz resonators, and perforated plates [26].
Quarter-wave resonators are often used to suppress transverse
high-frequency unstable modes while Helmholtz resonators are

However, Helmholtz resonators and quarter-wave resonators have
a limited ability to stabilize unstable modes only in a narrow band-
width of frequency. Thus, if combustion instability exist in a sev-
eral unstable modes in a system, above two kinds of dampers
should be installed with tuning to each resonant frequency. On
the other hand, perforated plates overcome this drawback of these
two kinds of dampers, so it can be a promising alternative to other
stabilization devices, especially in gas turbine combustors [26–31].
Up to now, there are not many researches in effectively designing
and using perforated plates to damp the thermoacoustic instabil-
ity. A state-of-the-art method is using the Howe model when
designing perforated plates [26,28–30], which has the shortcoming
of being unable to account for the plate thickness. In addition, dur-
ing the obtaining design parameters, several simpliﬁcation pro-
cesses are required.
In order to solve these weaknesses, the modiﬁed Howe model is
even utilized [26,30]. Nevertheless, same weaknesses still exist in
this approach because of the simpliﬁcation processes. To design
dampers with the original or modiﬁed Howe model, it is assumed
that dampers are operated only at very large or very small Strouhal
numbers [26,30]. If dampers need to be operated at some moderate
Strouhal numbers due to technical constraints, it is quite difﬁcult
to ﬁnd the optimal attenuation conditions of the dampers. Thus,
in order to overcome these drawbacks of the Howe model, the pre-
sent study has proposed the new approach which adopts the Luong
model [31] based on a modiﬁed form of Cummings’ empirical
equation. Compared to the conventional approaches [26,30], the
present method utilizing the Luong model has the basic advan-
tages which allows one to design perforated plates over the whole
range of Strouhal numbers. Moreover, in contrast to the previous
method using the Howe model, this approach can fully take
account for the effects of plate thickness and all design parameters
of the dampers can be obtained without any simpliﬁcation
processes.
In the present study, the FDF-based approach has been applied
to analyze the thermoacoustic instability in a lab-scale lean pre-
mixed combustor [22,23]. Computations are carried out for a wide
range of combustor and plenum lengths. Based on the numerical
results, instability maps of the combustor are generated. The
numerical results are compared with the experimental data in
terms of the eigenfrequency and limit-cycle velocity perturbation
ratios. Finally, the present study also attempted to ﬁnd the effec-
tive stabilization conditions for the perforated plate and numeri-
cally evaluated the damping effects of the perforated plate.

this reason, prior to the FTF-based instability analysis, the
Helmholtz equation with the spatial variation of the temperature
(without FTF) is typically solved to roughly estimate the resonant
frequencies [2].

3. Combustor geometry and operating conditions
The precise geometry of the combustor and its ﬂame images are
available in the previous studies [22,23]. Fig. 2 shows the axisym-
metric combustor geometry, the modeled thin ﬂame, and the
acoustic boundary condition at inlet and outlet. The position of
the reference point is estimated from the literature [22,23]. The
velocity perturbations evaluated at the reference point are used
to evaluate the source term of the Helmholtz equation. In compu-
tations, idealized acoustic boundary conditions are imposed at the
boundaries ðinlet : ^u ¼ 0 and outlet : ^p ¼ 0Þ. The premixed ﬂame
condition in this lean-premixed combustor is based on the
methane-air mixture with the ﬁxed equivalence ratio, 0.7 and
the thermal load, 1.94 kW. The velocity at the reference point is
2.67 m/s. The Helmholtz equation is solved by the ﬁnite element
method mainly due to its unique strength and robustness to treat
the acoustic boundary conditions.
Computations are made for twelve cases, covering a wide range
of plenum and chamber lengths, as given in Table 1. Thermody-
namic properties for the unburned and burnt gases are also listed
in Table 2. To account for the realizable acoustic boundary condi-
tion, an end correction is utilized at the outlet (l ¼ l þ 0:61R3)
[37,38]. Here, l and R3 are the length and radius of the combustion
chamber, respectively. Due to the assumption of compact ﬂames,
the combustion instability analysis in conjunction with the present
FDF approach is based on the global gain index and time delay. In
measurements [22], the FDF data were obtained for six different
velocity perturbation ratios in the given ﬂame conditions.
The FDF data of Flame A are well documented in the previous
work of Silva et al. [22]; those FDF data are available for the fre-
quency range from 0 to 400 Hz. By increasing the velocity pertur-
bation ratio, the gain index is decreased because the energy loss
in the system linearly increases over the whole regime. However,
the energy gain does not increase linearly in the nonlinear zone.
This nonlinear behavior is apparent especially for the high fre-
quency regime [14,22]. However, in terms of the phase, the
increase of the velocity perturbation ratio does not result in dis-
tinctly different trends. Experimental data indicate that, in the very
low frequency region, there exists a small bump in the phase of the
measured FDF data [22].

5. Conclusions
The present study focused on analyzing the combustion insta-
bility in a lab-scale lean-premixed burner using an FDF-based
approach. We also performed the design analysis for the attenua-
tion characteristics of a perforated plate which has the excellent
potential for stabilizing the combustion instability encountered
in industrial gas turbine combustors. Based on the numerical
results, the following summaries can be drawn:
(1) In order to overcome the drawbacks of the conventional
approaches utilizing the Howe model, the present study
has proposed the new approach employing the Luong model.
The model formulation indicates that the present approach
has the basic advantages such as the applicability to the
whole range of Strouhal numbers and capability to account
for the effects of ﬁnite plate thickness.
(2) In terms of the resonant frequency and the limit-cycle veloc-
ity perturbation ratio, the present FDF-based approach
shows an overall agreement with the experimental results
even if slight discrepancies exist.
(3) Numerical results indicate that by decreasing the hole radius
or the plate thickness, the bandwidth becomes much wider
and the applicability could be progressively widened. Even
with the same Strouhal number, the bandwidth can become
much broader by decreasing the hole radius. It is also found
that, if unstable modes appear sporadically in the higher fre-
quency regime, a perforated plate with a smaller hole radius
tuned to a much higher target frequency can be very effec-
tive at damping all of the unstable modes.
(4) Moreover, the present study also attempted to ﬁnd the effec-
tive stabilization conditions for the perforated plate and
numerically evaluated the damping effects of the perforated
plate. The perforated plates designed in this numerical study
have demonstrated the ability to drastically dampen the
oscillations for all test cases. It has been veriﬁed that the
proposed attenuation conditions of the perforated plate
ensure stable conditions over the whole range of velocity
perturbation ratios.
