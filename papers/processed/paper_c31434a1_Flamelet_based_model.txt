Hydrogen Jet Diffusion Flames 
J. P. H. SANDERS,* J.-Y. CHEN, AND I. GOKALP 
Laboratoire de Combustion et Syst~mes R~acttfs, Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique, 45071 Orldans,

The potential of using the laminar flamelet model for predictions of NO x emissions from turbulent hydrogen 
jet diffusion flames with various amounts of helium dilution is explored in this study. The flamelet approach 
treats turbulent flames as an ensemble of laminar flames subject to local fluid dynamic stretch which causes 
chemical kinetics to deviate from equilibrium. This flame stretch rate can be described as the scalar 
dissipation rate or as the strain rate. Numerical modeling is performed to assess the merits of these two 
choices by comparing predicted scaling behavior of NO x emission index with the experimental data of 
Driscoll and co-workers [1, 2]. The present study reveals that only with the scalar dissipation rate as the 
nonequilibrium parameter will the predicted NO x emission indices exhibit a clear scaling relation vs the 
Damkfihler number. The flamelet predictions are found to improve when the Damkibhler number increases. 
However, the absolute NO x levels are overpredicted, which is attributed to differential diffusion effects.

INTRODUCTION 
Regulations on emissions of nitrogen oxides, 
NOx, from combustion processes are becoming 
more stringent in many countries in efforts to 
improve urban air quality. Since most practical 
burners utilize turbulent flows to enhance the 
combustion rate, it is important to understand 
the complex interactions between the turbu- 
lent flow and the chemical reactions in order 
to better identify the controlling parameters 
that lead to reduction in NOx emissions. The 
amount of NO x emission is conventionally 
quantified by the NO x emission index EINOx, 
which is defined as the total mass of NOx 
emitted per unit mass of fuel burned. In com- 
bustion processes, most NOx is formed near 
the reaction zone in the form of nitric oxide, 
NO. The formation of NO is a relatively slow 
process compared to the principal combustion 
reactions. This implies that a large range of 
chemical time scales, and consequently Dam- 
k6hler numbers, needs to be properly treated 
by the turbulent combustion model. The Dam- 
kiShler number is the ratio between a typical

Since most turbulent combustion models are 
developed for a limited regime where the 
Damk6hler number is either large or small, 
modeling of turbulent combustion with a large 
disparity of Damk/Shler numbers is a challeng- 
ing task. Turbulence-chemistry interactions 
can be studied most conveniently when chem- 
istry is relatively simple, as in the case of 
hydrogen combustion. In hydrogen flames at 
ambient pressure, NO is formed principally by 
the thermal Zeldovich mechanism. Prompt NO 
is not generated, and the combustion reactions 
are well known [3]. 
Many experiments have been performed in 
the past to study the characteristics of NOx 
emissions from turbulent jet diffusion flames 
providing the basic understanding of NO x for- 
mation in turbulent flames; see Refs. [1, 2, 
4-10] and the review paper by Turns [11]. In 
particular, 
the experiments by Chen and 
Driscoll [1] on free turbulent hydrogen jet

which corresponds to a slope of 0.5 on a log-log 
plot. The flame residence time is defined as 
r u = Lv3/(D2Uj), 
where L v is the visible flame length, D is the 
nozzle diameter, and Uj is the jet exit velocity.

The scaling behavior of EINO x has been the 
subject of many modeling studies in the litera- 
ture. Early theoretical studies of NO formation 
in turbulent jet diffusion flames were based on 
the assumption of chemical equilibrium [6, 13, 
14, 15]. Here, a conserved scalar (e.g., the 
mixture fraction) is introduced to describe the 
mixing field. With the equal diffusivity assump- 
tion, mass fractions of chemical species and 
the flame temperature are directly related to 
the conserved scalar. Given the distribution of 
the conserved scalar, all thermochemical prop- 
erties can be determined. This fast chemistry 
approach avoids solving transport equations 
for mean mass fractions and temperature. 
These are obtained by using an assumed shape 
probability density function (PDF) for the mix- 
ture fraction. The NO mass fraction is ob- 
tained from its own transport equation in which 
the source term only depends on the mixture 
fraction. Past studies showed that the NO x 
emissions predicted by the fast chemistry model 
scale linearly with a global flame residence 
time, which implies a slope of 1. This is not 
supported by the experiments of Refs. 1, 2, 7, 
9, 10, 16, 17. 
Attempts at improving the fast chemistry 
model have been made by invoking the partial 
equilibrium assumption for the 
two-body 
"shuffle" reactions. The overall chemical pro- 
gress is controlled by the three-body recombi- 
nation steps, and it is tracked with a reaction 
progress variable [18]. The chemical source 
terms are a function of the mixture fraction 
and the reaction progress variable. This method 
generates a slope which is much smaller than 
0.5 [19]. Apparently, the method is inadequate 
for describing a large degree of nonequilib- 
rium. 
The conditional moment closure (CMC) 
method does give a slope close to 0.5 [20]. In 
the 
CMC method, mean thermochemical 
quantities conditioned on the mixture fraction 
are determined for each axial station. Subse- 
quently, the mean values are computed using 
an assumed shape PDF. The CMC method has 
been applied to flames with small departure 
from equilibrium, but it can be used with a 
detailed chemical mechanism. The CMC meth- 
od gives absolute levels of EINOx that are 
close to the experimental data, except for highly 
strained flames, where the method underpre- 
dicts the EINO x. 
A turbulent combustion model that does not 
rely on the assumption of high Damk/Shler 
numbers is the joint PDF transport equation 
method [21]. A joint PDF of all scalar variables 
can be solved using a stochastic Monte Carlo 
method, and the chemical source terms in the 
transport equations for the mean mass frac- 
tions are treated without modeling, in contrast 
to the mixture-fraction-based methods. Due to 
the large computational cost of the Monte 
Carlo method, reduced chemistry has to be 
used. Also, the term describing molecular dif- 
fusion needs modeling, and current models still 
pose difficulties when the reaction zone is thin. 
With this constraint, the PDF method is con- 
sidered more appropriate for chemistry with a 
broad reaction zone [22, 23]. Chen and Koll- 
mann [24] and Chen et al. [12] applied this 
method to the flames of Chen and DriscoU, 
and were able to reproduce the measured 0.5 
slope over the whole range of four decades of 
the DamkiShler number when a sufficiently ac- 
curate reduced mechanism was used. The ab- 
solute EINOx values are overpredicted by a 
factor of about 2 for high Damk6hler number

So far, the potential of using the laminar 
flamelet method [25, 26] for predicting the 
EINO x scaling behavior in turbulent jet flames 
has not been extensively evaluated, In the 
flamelet method, the turbulent flame is repre- 
sented by an ensemble of laminar steady-state 
stretched one-dimensional flames. The flamelet 
model is assumed to be applicable when 
Damk6hler numbers are large. Laminar flame 
calculations with a detailed chemistry are per- 
formed to generate a flamelet library in which 
mass fractions and the temperature are given 
in terms of the mixture fraction and a parame- 
ter that describes the stretch rate. The stretch 
rate quantifies the state of nonequilibrium of 
the flamelet. Two parameters currently are in 
use to quantify the stretch rate: the scalar 
dissipation rate and the strain rate. The theo- 
retically correct parameter is the scalar dissi- 
pation rate [26]. However, recent studies sug- 
gest that the strain rate appears to be more 
appropriate [27-31]. The rationale for using 
the strain rate, for instance Refs. 27, 30, was 
that only with this parameter could the lift-off 
height be predicted with the laminar diffusion 
flamelet method in turbulent diffusion flames. 
Mueller et al. [31] included partial premixing 
in their model, and successfully predicted the 
lift-off height using the scalar dissipation rate. 
However, they computed the mean mass frac- 
tions still using the strain rate as the nonequi- 
librium parameter. Consequently, the question 
of which parameter is responsible for nonequi- 
librium effects, the scalar dissipation rate or 
the strain rate, is still open, and it is addressed 
in the present study. 
To compute the mean NO mass fractions in 
the turbulent flame, the instantaneous NO 
source terms from the laminar flamelets can be 
used. In Ref. 32, it was shown semianalytically 
that using the strain rate as the nonequilib- 
rium parameter, the 0.5 slope would not be 
reproduced with the flamelet method. More- 
over, using the strain rate, the slope of the 
curve would increase with respect to the equi- 
librium value of 1. This was concluded from 
flow and temperature field similarity in high 
Reynolds and high Froude number flames in 
combination with the behavior of the maxi- 
mum NO production rate as a function of the 
strain rate in laminar flamelets. In the present 
study, complete flame calculations are pre- 
sented in which the scalar dissipation rate, the 
small eddy strain rate, and the large eddy 
strain rate are used as the mean nonequilib- 
rium parameter. Computations are performed 
for the flames of Driscoll and co-workers [1, 2], 
who investigated pure and helium diluted hy- 
drogen flames with various amounts of helium 
from 20 to 70% by volume. The fuel jet nozzle 
diameters used were d = 1.6, 2.6, and 3.75 
mm, and the calculations are done without 
gravity and radiation effects, which are be- 
lieved of secondary importance for turbulent 
hydrogen jet flames at a high Froude number. 
With these calculations, the effect of choosing 
different nonequilibrium parameters on the 
predicted slope of the EINO x curve will be 
explored. Two types of implementations of the 
flamelet method are compared: one in which 
transport equations for the mean mixture frac- 
tion and its variance are solved, and one in 
which a PDF transport equation for the mix- 
ture fraction is solved using the Monte Carlo 
method. The latter model was used by Chen 
et al. [12] with the joint PDF approach that 
reproduces the 0.5 slope, as mentioned earlier. 
Implementation of the flamelet model into the 
same stochastic model is useful for comparison 
with the traditional flamelet model. Such a 
comparison will indicate whether or not the 
predicted slope is sensitive to the implementa- 
tion of the turbulent combustion model. 
In the next section, the EINOx scaling be- 
havior in turbulent hydrogen flames is ana- 
lyzed, and it is related to the characteristics of 
NO formation in laminar flamelets. From this 
analysis, it is tentatively concluded that the 
strain rate would not be the nonequilibrium 
parameter that is able to reproduce the experi- 
mental 0.5 slope of EINO x vs Da. Subsequent 
detailed flame calculations, using the two im- 
plementations of the flamelet method, are pre- 
sented to confirm the previous analysis. The 
impact of differential diffusion effects on the 
predicted slope and on the absolute value of 
EINO x is discussed, and finally, the conclu- 
sions are presented.

Chemistry 
Despite the many differences in codes A and 
B, both models are based on second-order 
moment closure approaches. When combined 
with a joint scalar PDF approach, code B 
reproduces the observed 0.5 slope [24]. Substi- 
tution of the joint scalar PDF model in code B 
by the flamelet approach allows us to assess 
the sensitivity of the predicted NO x trend on a 
different implementation of the turbulent com- 
bustion model. To this end, the same flamelet 
library is used in both codes. The flamelet 
library is generated by calculating opposed pla- 
nar jet flames at various strain rates using a 
computer code that is a precursor of Rogg's 
RUN1DL [40]. The detailed chemical mecha- 
nism used here is given in Ref. 3. The strain 
rate is evaluated at the oxidizer side, and the 
scalar dissipation rate is determined at the 
stoichiometric point. The latter is defined as 
g = 2D(af/Oxj)(Of/(gxj) with D being the 
mixture diffusivity. Chemical equilibrium is 
used when ff approaches 0. The equilibrium 
values are obtained using the Chemkin PSR 
code [41]. Boundary conditions correspond to 
pure fuel and pure oxidizer, both at T = 300 
K. This corresponds to the classical flamelet 
approach [26]. Bish and Dahm [44] consider 
flamelets with boundary conditions on the rich 
side that are different from the pure fuel case, 
in view of the fact that in the far field of a jet 
flame, no pure fuel at 300 K exists. An alterna- 
tive would be to take equilibrium values for 
mass fractions and temperature at the rich side 
(a mix of fuel and hot products). However, this 
would be equivalent to the present approach 
using unity Lewis numbers in the fiamelets. 
When the scalar dissipation rate in the tur- 
bulent flame calculations is used as the stretch 
parameter, the value Xst, i.e., the stoichiomet- 
ric scalar dissipation rate in the laminar 
flamelets, corresponds to the instantaneous 
scalar dissipation rate in the turbulent flame. 
If, of the other hand, the strain rate is used, 
the strain rate at the oxidizer side in the lami- 
nar flamelets is associated with the instanta- 
neous strain rate in the turbulent flame.

dicted scalar variance, which is well predicted. 
Therefore, changing the scalar dissipation rate 
alone does not explain the large discrepancy 
between model predictions and measurements. 
The second possibility is thermal radiation, 
which leads to lower flame temperatures and 
consequently lower NO production rates. A 
radiation model has been used in code B which 
uses the optically thin limit approximation, and 
which therefore gives the upper limit regarding 
radiation effects. Typical reductions in EINO x 
for pure and He-diluted hydrogen flames are: 
48% for a pure flame, 31% for a 20% He 
flame, and 8% for a 40% He flame. All flames 
correspond to Barlow and Carter's experiment 
[10] at D = 3.7 mm and Uj = 297 m/s for the 
pure and 20% He flames and Uj = 257 m/s for 
the 40% He flame. According to these upper 
limits, radiation can only account for a differ- 
ence of a factor of 2 in EINOx, while a factor 
of 10 has to be accounted for. The third possi- 
ble cause is differential diffusion among species 
and heat, which is known to cause superequi- 
librium temperatures in laminar hydrogen 
flames. This is due to the high diffusivity of H 2 
molecules which leads to a higher diffusion 
flux of fuel (H 2) to the flame zone compared 
to a hypothetical case where all diffusivities 
are equal. However, experiments of turbulent 
hydrogen jet flames indicate that these differ- 
ential diffusion effects diminish with axial dis- 
tance as well as with jet exit velocity [10, 42], 
and therefore also with the strain rate. The 
effect of differential diffusion in the flamelet 
library is nearly invariant with the strain rate, 
as reported by Vranos et al. [43]. Therefore, at 
downstream regions, the flamelet model tends 
to overpredict the effect of differential diffu- 
sion which can have a profound impact on NO 
predictions as they are quite sensitive to tem- 
perature. As suggested by Vranos et al. [43], 
overprediction of differential diffusion effects

CONCLUSIONS 
The present study on flamelet modeling re- 
veals that when the nonequilibrium parameter 
is assigned by the scalar dissipation rate, the 
predicted EINO x vs Da curves collapse onto 
one curve, as observed in the experiments of 
Chen and Driscoll [1]. Although the model 
overpredicts the NO x emission levels, the pre- 
dicted slope of EINOx vs Da on a logarithmic 
plot is close to the experimental value of 0.5 at 
large Da numbers. The flamelet predictions 
start to deviate from the 0.5 slope for, small 
Damk6hler numbers, consistent with the no- 
tion that the flamelet approach is appropriate 
for high DamkiShler number turbulent flames. 
The overpredicted NO x levels are likely at- 
tributed to the overpredicted differential dif- 
fusion effect by the flamelet model. This has 
been shown by artificially setting all diffusitivi- 
ties equal to a common value based on unity 
Lewis number. This lowered the absolute 
EINOx significantly, while preserving an ap- 
proximately correct slope for large Damk~Shler 
numbers. Different dilutions, however, seem to 
generate different branches, which is not in 
agreement with the experiments. More re- 
search is needed to understand the implica- 
tions and utility of setting Le = 1 in the 
flamelets. 
Using the small-scale or the large-scale strain 
rate as the nonequilibrium parameter gives 
much too high values for the slope, and more- 
over, the predicted results do not collapse onto 
one curve. When compared to other turbulent 
combustion methods that reproduce the exper- 
imental 0.5 slope, the current flamelet model 
gives the largest deviation regarding absolute 
EINO x values. This has been shown to be 
caused by the differential diffusion effects, 
which will be investigated in more detail in 
future work.
