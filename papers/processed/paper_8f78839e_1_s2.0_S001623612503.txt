A B S T R A C T
A model of a gas cycle turbine operating under design and off-design conditions with variable fuel compositions 
for Gas-to-wire (GtW) applications is presented. The model considers the presence of different fractions of com-
bustible (i.e., CH 4 , C 2 H 6 , and C 3 H 8 ) and incombustible (i.e., CO 2 ) components, while accounting for the system’s 
power and emissions outputs. The content of CO 2 varied from 2 % to 42 %, while the contents of C 2 H 6 and 
C3
 H 8 were kept constant at 4 
 
 
%, all on a molar basis, for all compositions evaluated. To evaluate the behavior
of the turbine, its load was varied from 40 % to 100 % of the nominal power. The results obtained indicate that 
the mass flow rate of both the fuel mixture and the exhaust gases increases with the CO 2 fraction and with the 
turbine load. The destruction of exergy in the components was presented as a function of the Wobbe index. As the 
index increases, which is associated with smaller CO 2 fractions, the turbine and compressor exergy show a small 
reduction, while the destruction in the combustor increases drastically. The addition of CO 2 also significantly 
influences NO X and CO emissions. NO X emissions decrease and CO emissions increase with the reduction of CO 2 
present in the mixture. Overall, the results suggest that while more fuel is used to produce the same power with 
mixtures rich in CO 2 , more energy is required to separate CO 2 from combustible fuel, making GtW an alternative 
to oil wells that produce large amounts of CO 2 .
1. Introduction
The development of non-polluting energy sources has become a 
global driving force for research. In this context, natural gas has gained 
focus in recent years because renewable sources still face problems with 
respect to intermittency, requiring the use of fossil fuels as base energy 
vectors. Thus, natural gas is considered a strategic source, as it is a tran-
sition fuel between fossil and renewable energy sources, belonging to 
the class of cleaner fuels, because its combustion emits about 50 % less 
carbon dioxide (CO 2 ) compared to other fuels [1,2]. When extracted on 
offshore oil platforms, natural gas is often associated with oil and water
or other impurities, such as H 2 S and CO 2 , making its use unviable due to 
the high cost of purity required to meet the requirements for transport 
through pipelines. For instance, according to [3], the excess gas often 
present in oil wells is locally burned through a process called flaring, 
which is common during oil and gas processing despite its undesirable 
emissions.
Studies show that there are approximately 74 trillion cubic meters 
of associated gas – natural gas produced through oil wells – around the 
world [4] (apud [5]), although some gas reserves have not been ex-
plored yet because they do not provide an adequate financial return. In 
addition, according to [6], around 2.9 Gm 3 of natural gas are burned

might be relied on for several years to come. In order to make use of this 
energy source, Gas-to-wire (GtW) platforms are an alternative. This type 
of system uses gas to produce electricity at the extraction site, and ex-
cess electricity is then transmitted through submarine cables in offshore 
plants [7]. Gas-to-wire systems are generally more efficient and less 
costly than standard systems since there is no need to treat and separate 
the contaminants present in the gas to the levels required for commer-

with the fuel mixture used.
Not surprisingly, the use of GtW systems has been considered in the 
specialized literature, including the pre-salt fields present in Brazil. For 
instance, Relva et al. [5] presented a case study focused on the pre-salt 
layer, suggesting that the installation of GtW thermoelectric plants is 
feasible, but some changes must be made to make this possible (e.g., 
prioritized dispatch contracts). Ref. [8] reviewed eight GtW projects in 
Asia, Africa, and South America. According to the study, natural gas is 
attractive as it reduces dependence on oil and pollutes less. Thus, GtW 
systems might be more interesting in remote regions, as they are an 
efficient way to generate energy on-site.
Despite the fact that flaring is avoided, controlling CO 2 emissions in 
GtW systems is still necessary. For example, exhaust gases rich in CO 2 
might be captured by a CO 2 capture system and later reinjected into 
the well, executing the so-called Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) tech-
nique, which significantly increases well production [5]. However, it 
should be noted that even with recent research, many points need to 
be addressed in GtW systems. This is because most available studies ex-
clusively analyze the technical and economic implications of installing 
GtW systems for power generation, e.g., [6,9,10], while disregarding the 
presence of contaminants and their effects. Nevertheless, for contextu-
alization purposes, some of the key studies considering associated gas 
and/or contaminated fuel are reviewed next.
Ref. [11] focused on the use of GtW systems to take advantage of 
the associated gas, rather than the natural gas, burned in the process.
They found that neither the thermal efficiency nor the output power is 
significantly affected by the use of associated gas. However, more associ-
ated gas is consumed to produce the same amount of power. Following 
this line of research, Somehsaraei et al. [12] analyzed the flexibiliza-
tion of the fuel used in microturbines (MGTs). The results indicated that 
the use of biogas instead of natural gas causes an increase in fuel mass 
flow. At the same time, electrical efficiency decreases with increasing 
methane content. It should be mentioned that pollutant emissions are 
also influenced by the fuel composition.
Ref. [13] performed a parametric analysis evaluating the impact of 
constituents such as ethane (C 2 H 6 ), propane (C 3 H 8 ), isobutane (C 4 H 10 ), 
CO 2, and nitrogen (N 2 ) on the combustion of methane (CH 4 ). The results 
showed that by individually varying the content of constituents such as 
ethane and propane from 0 to 30 %, the adiabatic flame temperature 
increases, leading to an increase in the emission of NO X and CO. At the 
same time, a decrease in the contents of CO 2 and N 2 , decreases the adi-
abatic flame temperature and, consequently, the emissions of NO X and 
CO. Ref. [14] performed a partial load analysis, in addition to verifying 
the influence of the presence of N 2 in natural gas on the main parame-
ters of the cycle. The study concluded that by increasing the fraction of 
N 2 , the fuel flow rate drops significantly, causing a minimal increase in 
the first and second law efficiencies. Ref. [15] studied the behavior of 
a gas turbine powered by a mixture of CH 4 and H 2 . It was found that 
for the same turbine inlet temperature, the efficiencies of the combined 
cycles considered increased slightly when pure methane was used as 
fuel. On the other hand, NO and NO 2 emissions increased up to three 
times compared to pure methane. Although not detailed for brevity, 
other studies have also considered different fuel blends, which included 
combustible and non-combustible components, such as hydrogen [16], 
ammonia [17] and CO 2 [18], in the combustion process.
Ref. [19] studied not only the efficiency aspects of gas turbines, 
but also how the use of associated gas instead of natural gas affects 
their lifespan. The results indicated that for an ambient temperature of 
288.15 K, natural gas has higher efficiency compared to associated gas.

Based on the above and knowing that the variation in the content of 
CO 2 can range from 22 % to 68 % (on a molar basis) in some pre-salt 
fields according to [20], this work aims, for the first time, to understand 
how the composition of natural gas/CH 4 , which is heavily contaminated 
with CO 2 , along with C 2 H 6 and C 3 H 8 , affects the performance of a gas 
turbine aimed at GtW systems. The study proposes a thermodynamic 
model considering the design and the off-design conditions of a gas tur-
bine operated with a variable fuel composition. In addition, a parametric 
study is performed to evaluate how the content of CO 2 can affect key 
parameters such as fuel mass flow, exhaust gas mass flow, NO X and CO 
emissions, exergy destruction in the components, and energy spent for 
CO 2 separation. 
2. Methodology
The global model, which is a combination of several existing sub-
models available in the literature, was developed in Python TM [21] and 
is a sequential calculation routine used to calculate the performance of 
the GtW cycle, which is detailed in Section 2.1. First, based on the bound-
ary conditions shown in Table 1 and on the volumetric or molar fractions 
of Table 2, the model calculates the stoichiometric coefficients for the 
combustion of CH 4 , C 2 H 6 , C 3 H 8 and CO 2 , which are used to calculate 
the properties of the fuel, such as density, mass, and calorific value. The 
energy available from fuel combustion makes it possible to determine 
all thermodynamic states, mass flow rates, excess air, and partial pow-
ers throughout the GtW cycle, which is detailed in Section 2.2. Next, 
the model addresses the calculation of exergy fluxes, exergy destruc-
tion – Section 2.3 –, emission of pollutants, and the maximum energy 
required for the separation of CO 2 , as shown in Sections 2.4 and 2.5, 
respectively. In Section 2.6, an iterative analysis is performed under par-
tial load conditions, considering all mixtures and variables calculated 
previously.
2.1. Physical configuration
The representative schematic of a typical GtW Brayton cycle is shown 
in Fig. 1, which is composed of a compressor, combustion chamber, tur-
bine, generator, and, in the present case, a mixer; the latter ensures that 
the fuel enters the combustion chamber completely mixed. The opera-
tional conditions are as follows: air enters the compressor under ambient 
conditions (state 1), passes through the compressor, and enters the com-
bustion chamber (state 2); the fuel used in combustion reaches T f and
P f (state 3), the combustion gases enter the turbine (state 4) and are
expanded in the turbine generating electricity (state 5). The main as-
sumptions and boundary conditions used to build the numerical model 
are shown in Table 1.

2.5. Separation energetic cost
In a typical offshore well, the gas removed from the well is treated 
and the existing contaminants, mainly CO 2 , are reduced to acceptable 
levels for commercialization and transport. The excess CO 2 is then rein-
jected into the well to maintain pressure and reduce the emission of
CO 2 into the environment. In GtW platforms, gas with a high impurity 
content is used as fuel for the on-site energy generation cycle, thus re-
ducing separation costs. Therefore, the model in the current section aims 
to determine the energy required to separate CO 2 from CH 4 . Next, this 
energy expenditure is compared with the energy made available by the 
contaminated fuel, and a new efficiency is calculated through Eq. (31).

total exhaust flow rate are in good agreement compared to the turbine 
catalog data, therefore, also validating the partial load formulation and 
implementation.

3. Results
The results obtained with the present model of the GtW cycle will 
be presented in two distinct sub-sections: according to the Wobbe index 
(Section 3.1) and the partial load (Section 3.2).
3.1. Wobbe index
The main parameters of the cycle can be shown as functions of the 
Wobbe index. In fact, turbine manufacturers commercially use this in-
dex to disclose the variation range of the fuel used, so this is indeed 
a parameter of interest in turbine applications where there might be a 
variation in the fuel composition; in the present study the Wobbe index 
of the mixtures varied according to Table 2. However, more importantly, 
a high Wobbe index indicates a low CO 2 content, and vice versa. Also, 
high CO 2 content is related to combustion instability, decreasing flame 
temperature and speed, therefore, compromising stability. As the CO 2 
content decreases (Wobbe index increases), the flame becomes more 
stable, preventing flame extinction.

can be seen, the five values for the Wobbe index are considered, each 
indicating one of the compositions shown in Table 2, and are indicated 
at the upper end of Fig. 3; the lowest Wobbe values represent mixtures 
with a higher content of CO 2 . As expected, both variables decrease as 
the Wo increases because, for fuels with higher energy density, a lower 
flow rate is required to achieve the required power output and, conse-
quently, a lower air flow rate will be required for combustion. Since CO 2 
is an inert gas, it reduces the energy released during combustion and, 
therefore, more fuel is required to reach the temperature of 1487 K at 
the turbine inlet. The results presented in Fig. 3(b), which show the vari-
ation of excess air, ex air , confirm the behavior mentioned above, with 
an increase in the Wobbe index due to a decrease in the excess air used 
in fuel combustion.

4. Conclusions
With the growing search for energy efficiency and new energy
sources, GtW (Gas-to-wire) systems are a plausible compromise solu-
tion. In this article, the influence of fuel composition on a Brayton cycle 
was studied theoretically aiming at oil well applications. The numeri-
cal model comprises several analyses, such as the thermodynamics and 
exergy of the cycle, pollutant emissions, the energy used to separate
CO 2, and, finally, the cycle operating at partial load. The study considers
the influence of fuel on several cycle parameters, such as the mass flow 
of exhaust gases, the mass flow of fuel, excess air, the exergy destruc-
tion rate in the compressor, turbine, and combustion chamber, pollutant 
emissions, and isentropic efficiencies.
In short, the results show that both gas and fuel flow rates increase 
with increasing CO 2 content in the mixture and with increasing turbine 
workload, a behavior confirmed by data provided by the manufacturer 
of the SGT-400 turbine [22]. Excess air exhibits a slightly different be-
havior, as it increases with the CO 2 content (decrease in the Wobbe 
index) and decreases with turbine load. Regarding pollutants, NO X and 
CO emissions decrease and increase with increasing CO 2 content, respec-
tively. However, increasing the turbine load increases NO X emissions 
and decreases CO emissions.
It is worth mentioning that the objective of implementing GtW sys-
tems is to use as fuel the associated gas with a high contaminant content 
that, in standard systems, is burned to avoid CH 4 emissions into the at-
mosphere. One of the main economic advantages of this system is that 
there is no need to separate CO 2 from the extracted gas. Therefore, one 
of the results presented concerns the energy required for the separation 
process. The results presented show that, for fuels with a higher CO 2 
content, the use of up to approximately 6.5 MW can be avoided for a 
fuel flow rate equal to 2.02 kg/s. In other words, despite the reduced 
cycle efficiency due to the presence of CO 2 mixed with CH 4 , increasing 
the fuel mass flow results in a positive net energy balance, considering 
that the gas did not go through the separation process, avoiding power 
expenditures.
In summary, this study shows the implications of running GtW sys-
tems with a high level of associated gas. Future studies can further 
expand this analysis in at least two directions. First, one should improve 
the model presented by numerically simulating the combustion chamber 
and all kinetic and geometric details associated with it; this would also 
improve the accuracy of the associated emissions, which, in the present 
model, are qualitative. Second, a carbon capture system should be mod-
eled aiming to quantify technically, economically, and environmentally 
the impact of running a GtW with a clean fuel (i.e., fully separating CO 2 ) 
or associated gas (i.e., contaminated with CO 2 ).
