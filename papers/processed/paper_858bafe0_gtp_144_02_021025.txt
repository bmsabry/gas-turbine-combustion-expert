Design and Experimental
Characterization of a Swirl-
Stabilized Combustor for Low
Calorific Value Gaseous Fuels
Low caloriﬁc value (LCV) gaseous fuels are generated as by-products in many commer-
cial sectors, e.g., as mine gas or biogas. Their efﬁcient exploitation can be a considerable
source of primary energy. Typically, product gases from biomass are characterized by
low lower heating values (LHVs) due to their high concentration of inert gases and
steam. At the same time, their composition varies strongly based on the initial feedstock
and may contain unwanted components in the form of tars and ammonia. These proper-
ties make the design of appropriate combustion systems very challenging and issues such
as ignition, ﬂame stability, emission control, and combustion efﬁciency must be
accounted for. By employing a proprietary gas turbine burner at the TU Berlin, the com-
bustion of an artiﬁcial LCV gas mixture at stoichiometric conditions has been success-
fully demonstrated for a broad range of steam content in the fuel. This work presents the
stability maps and emissions measured with the swirl-stabilized burner at premixed con-
ditions. It was shown that the ﬂame location and shape primarily depend on the steam
content of the LCV gas. The steam content in the fuel was increased until ﬂame blow-out
occurred at LHVs well below the target condition of 2.87 MJ/kg (2.7 MJ/m3
N). The exhaust
gas is analyzed in terms of the pollutants NOx and CO for different fuel compositions,
moisture contents, and thermal powers. Finally, OH* measurements have been carried
out in the ﬂame. A simple reactor network simulation was used to conﬁrm the feasibility
of the experimental results. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4049642]
Introduction
Climate change is a driving force behind a restructuring of the
energy sector toward a reduction of fossil fuel exploitation. The
share of renewable energy has been increasing globally over the
past years [1]. Many commercial processes also lead to the forma-
tion or accumulation of low caloriﬁc value (LCV) gaseous fuels,
in the form of mine gas or product gas from steel manufacture.
Moreover, waste products (e.g., biowaste, waste materials from
forestry industry) can be processed to LCV gas. According to esti-
mates, biogas can potentially provide up to 15% of Germany’s
primary energy demand [2]. In 2017, 8.5% of Germany’s gross
electricity consumption was covered from biogas including land-
ﬁll gas and sewage gas [3].
In Germany, most of the biogas is exploited in combined heat
and power plants employing reciprocating engines [4]. Typically,
the latter offer efﬁciencies of around 40%, whereas the combined
heat and power generation process achieves total efﬁciencies of
around 90%. However, reciprocating engines require a minimum
methane content of around 40%, so that LCV gases with a lower
energy content cannot be utilized in many currently installed sys-
tems. The combination of gasiﬁcation and combustion of biogas
in (micro) gas turbines offers long-term potential for LCV gas
exploitation [2].
Low caloriﬁc value gas that cannot be exploited in power or
heat applications is often ﬂared, thus contributing as a greenhouse
gas and also providing no economic value [2]. The main difﬁculty
in combusting low-lower heating value (LHV) gases (with LHVs
of 3 to 20 times lower compared to methane [5]) in gas turbines is
establishing a stable ﬂame [2]. The gas turbine burner must offer
an adequate design for that particular application. Using ﬂameless
combustion concept, the DLR FLOX combustor was successfully
tested in a Turbec T100 microgas turbine with LCV gas with a

The high diluent content of noncombustible substances in LCV
gas leads to a decrease of the chemical kinetic rates, which affects
the ﬂame physics in various ways [10]. The diluent content
increases the speciﬁc heat of the fuel–air mixture, which decreases
the adiabatic ﬂame temperature [10], and as a result, the thermal
efﬁciency of the combustion system. However, the low ﬂame tem-
peratures were found to decrease the NOx emission levels, as the
thermal NOx path (typically the largest contributor to NOx in
modern gas turbines) is suppressed [8,9]. At the same time, the
low hydrocarbon content was found to reduce the prompt NOx
formation [8]. This suggests that the combustion of LCV product
gas may result in an overall low NOx formation. Product gas from
thermal gasiﬁcation of biomass comprises more lighter compo-
nents such as CO and H2 compared to the content of CH4. Further,
it has been reported that the ratio of the combustible components
has a signiﬁcant effect on the NOx emissions [2].
Nevertheless, due to a varying content of fuel-bound nitrogen in
the LCV product gas, especially ammonia (NH3), multiple groups
report signiﬁcant NOx emissions, as a substantial fraction of NH3
is converted to fuel NOx. Many studies suggest that the presence
of ammonia (NH3) in the LCV product gas leads to increased NOx
emissions, with NO as the major pollutant species [9]. A full load
simulation of the Alstom LVB combustor resulted in NOx emis-
sions <50 ppm for ammonia concentrations <400 ppm and con-
version rates of 40–80% [8]. In an experimental study, conversion
rates of approximately 30% were reported for ammonia concentra-
tions between 2500 and 3800 ppm, with the remainder of the
ammonia reacting to molecular nitrogen [9]. The same study con-
cluded that the fuel NO mechanism due to NH3 in the product gas
is the dominant NO formation mechanism. The contributions of
the thermal and prompt NO mechanisms were reported to be neg-
ligible, due to low adiabatic ﬂame temperatures and the small con-
centration of hydrocarbon radicals. The study suggests further that
the NH3 to NO conversion rates decrease at elevated pressures,
due to decreased concentrations of O and OH radicals at elevated
pressures, however this was not conﬁrmed experimentally yet.
Similar results were obtained in an experimental study in an Als-
tom combustor, where NO was also observed as the major pollu-
tant [11]. Conversion rates were expected to drop at elevated
pressures. In a more recent experimental study in the so-called
WINNOX TUD combustor, the same group reported much lower
conversion rates between 40 and 5%, depending on the NH3 con-
centration and the CH4 content in the fuel gas [12]. They observed
higher conversion rates for lower NH3 concentrations and
increased CH4 content. They also reported a minimum conversion
rate with a primary burner air number around 0.7, which translates
to an equivalence ratio of 1.43. An increased conversion rate from
NH3 to NOx for increased CH4 content was also reported earlier
by another group [13]. Additionally, the presence of CO2 was
found to increase CO formation in the exhaust gas [10].
The laminar burning velocity of the gas mixture depends on the
methane and hydrogen content in the product gas [14]. The lami-
nar burning velocity of the LCV product gas is expected to
increase substantially if hydrogen is added to the fuel, since the
laminar burning velocity of pure hydrogen–air mixtures is approx-
imately 170 cm/s [15]. An experimental study of laminar burning
velocities for two different generic biogas mixtures with normal
air yielded 28 cm/s for a 66% CH4/34% CO2 mixture and 46 cm/s
for a 33% CH4/17% CO2/40% C3H8/10% H2 mixture (in percent
volume) [16]. A dilution with CO2 led to a decrease of the laminar
ﬂame speed as reported in Refs. [17] and [18]. The laminar ﬂame
speed was observed to decrease as well when the fuel was diluted
with nitrogen, which also led to a shift of the peak ﬂame tempera-
ture and velocity closer to stoichiometry [19].
The high diluent content was observed to lead to a narrower
ﬂammability limit compared to natural gas. Adding CO2 was
observed to increase the ﬂammability limits due to an increased
radiation absorption, while adding steam had a negative effect on
the ﬂammability [17].
The above-mentioned detrimental effects on the ﬂame physics
lead to technical challenges for the combustion of LCV gas in a
gas turbine environment, related to ﬂame holding, lean ﬂame
blow out, and thermoacoustic instabilities [10]. Regarding the lat-
ter, an experimental study suggests that an increased CO2 content
in the product gas can have a stabilizing effect [18]. Multiple stud-
ies suggest that already a small content of hydrogen in the order
of 5% in volume may signiﬁcantly improve ﬂame stability
[20,21].
This work is part of a project with the purpose of establishing
commercially feasible power and heat production from LCV gas
combustion in gas turbines. Toward this goal, a generic gas tur-
bine burner is operated on a low LHV biosyngas with variable
fuel composition, moisture content, and thermal power. The
exhaust gas is analyzed in terms of emissions of CO and NOx.
The chemiluminescence of excited OH* radicals is imaged to
assess the ﬂame position and shape. Additionally, temperatures
are measured at the combustor wall and at several positions inside
the exhaust pipe.
Experimental Setup
Measurement Methods. The emissions in the exhaust ﬂow
were continuously measured. At the end of the exhaust tube, a
sample was extracted from the exhaust gas and fed through a
heated hosepipe into a cooled steam trap to remove humidity,
before measuring the concentrations of unburned hydrocarbons,
NOx, CO, O2, and CO2 on a dry basis. Optical methods based on
the pollutant’s absorption behavior were used for the CO, CO2,
and NOx measurements. The analyzer further distinguished
between NO and NO2. The measurement of O2 utilized the mole-
cule’s paramagnetic characteristic.
To image the OH* chemiluminescence of the ﬂame, an image-
intensiﬁed CCD camera was equipped with a UV-transmissive
lens (f/3.8, f ¼ 78 mm). Further, an interference ﬁlter, which was
transmissive between 295 and 340 nm, was employed. A total of
480 frames at a frequency of 5 frames per second were taken for
each operating point and the averaged OH* distribution was
calculated.
Temperatures were measured at various locations in the test rig
using K-type thermocouples. This included the temperatures of
the air and steam supply after heating, the burner inlet tempera-
ture, and the temperatures at the beginning and end of the exhaust
tube. No correction for the heat radiation of the thermocouple was
applied in the presented temperature measurements.
Atmospheric Combustion Test Rig. A sketch of the test rig is
presented in Fig. 1. The burner used in this work has previously
been developed at TU Berlin for the purpose of ﬂashback-safe
operation with high-reactivity fuels such as hydrogen under dry
and wet conditions [22–30].
The LCV fuel gas is injected into the air and steam ﬂow far
upstream of the burner to ensure good mixing. The gas mixture is
then fed to a radial swirler. The swirl number of the burner was
adjusted by controlling the mass ﬂux through the radial swirler
vanes. For the results presented here, a constant geometrical swirl

The ﬂow ﬁeld generated by the burner was thoroughly investi-
gated in previous studies. A sudden area expansion at the combus-
tion chamber inlet was used to induce a vortex breakdown and
stabilize the ﬂame in the shear layer between the central and cor-

hydrogen-air and natural gas–air with high contents of steam was
also conﬁrmed and led to extremely low emissions [23]. The
burner concept has been also tested at high pressure and the posi-
tive effect of steam injection on low NOx emissions was con-

effects on CO [29]. The combustor ﬂow ﬁeld was also explicitly
investigated in water-tunnel studies by means of particle image
velocimetry. A recent numerical simulation of the reacting ﬂow
with lean-premixed hydrogen conﬁrmed the experimental results
[30]. The thermoacoustic instability characteristics were also
investigated and burner and ﬂame transfer functions are available
[32].
For the experiments in this work, methane was substituted with
natural gas from the public gas grid. The LCV gas composition is
varied by changing the methane (natural gas), steam, nitrogen,
and CO/CO2/H2 concentrations. The relative hydrogen, carbon
monoxide, and carbon dioxide content was ﬁxed, since a bottled
mixture of CO/CO2/H2 is used. The bottled mixture is supplied at
a measured concentration of 35.6/18.9/45.5 vol % of CO/H2/CO2
(measurement uncertainty of 62% relative). An overview of the
gas supply hardware is shown in Fig. 2. Air, N2, and steam are
preheated and mixed upstream of the fuel injector port. The fuel is
premixed from CO/CO2/H2 and natural gas and injected at room
temperature. A second natural gas supply is used for ignition, but
switched off for the remainder of the experiments.

Results
The Results section has four parts. After discussing the ﬂame
shape and the blowout limits in the ﬁrst two parts, the following
two parts focus on the NOx and CO emissions.
Flame Shape. The impact of steam admixture on the ﬂame is
visualized in Fig. 3, where a natural gas ﬂame (top row) is com-
pared to two biosyngas compositions for varying contents of
steam. The biosyngas ﬂame presented in the middle row has a
higher methane content (15 vol % dry) compared to the design
case (as speciﬁed in Table 1). The latter is displayed in the bottom
row. In all cases the burner operated at stoichiometric conditions
(/ ¼ 1) and at a constant thermal power of 60 kW. As a result,
decreasing the methane content of the biosyngas is accompanied
with a rising hydrogen content to keep the ﬂame power constant.
The ﬁgure shows the time-averaged OH* chemiluminescence
inside the combustion chamber normalized by the maximum value
for each case.
The ﬂame shape is of V-type and similar for all studied fuels at
the lowest steam content of X ¼ 0:3 (left column in Fig. 3). The
ﬂame is compact and located close to the burner inlet. This is typi-
cal for a swirl-stabilized ﬂame that anchors in the shear layer
between the central and outer recirculation zones, where the local
ﬂow velocity and the turbulent burning velocity are equal [26].

When the steam content is increased, the ﬂame anchors slightly
further downstream and appears less compact. This is expected,
since an increase in steam admixture leads to a decrease of the
reaction rate and ﬂame speed [33]. This effect is stronger with
increased natural gas content, as the simultaneously reduced
hydrogen content further reduces the reactivity of the fuel. As a
consequence, the reaction zone is lengthening axially, as well as
radially. At a steam content of X ¼ 0:5, the pure natural gas ﬂame
reaches an unstable condition, where the ﬂame pulsates between
two quasi-stable points. This leads to two axial ﬂame positions in
time-averaged OH* chemiluminescence, with one being close to
the burner inlet and one being approximately half way down the
length of the combustion chamber. With decreasing natural gas
content, and increasing hydrogen content, respectively, this
behavior is less pronounced and the ﬂame appears more stable
and anchors closer to the burner inlet. At design point conditions,
the ﬂame shape is only slightly broader than for the X ¼ 0:4 case.
This indicates an increased ﬂame speed of the biosyngas mixture
containing hydrogen compared to the pure natural gas ﬂame. Pre-
vious studies revealed that even if only a small amount of hydro-
gen is added to the fuel, ﬂame speed and stability are increased
[15,20,21].
Blowout Limit. The blowout limit has been assessed for differ-
ent biosyngas compositions. Besides steam, the biosyngas compo-
sition is high in N2 and CO2, both increasing the overall dilution
and contributing to ﬂame cooling effects. The blowout limit of the
biosyngas ﬂame has been assessed by increasing the steam content
at stoichiometric conditions up to the point of ﬂame extinction,
for several thermal power values and compositions. As shown in
Fig. 3, the natural gas ﬂame reaches an unstable point at X ¼ 0:5
and Pth ¼ 60 kW. This condition was observed to precede ﬂame
extinction.
The tests revealed no signiﬁcant inﬂuence of the thermal power
on the blowout limit. Flame blowout occurred for the design fuel
case at X ¼ 0:65 for a thermal power of Pth ¼ 45 kW, at X ¼
0:68 for Pth ¼ 60 kW, and at X ¼ 0:675 for Pth ¼ 70 kW, respec-
tively. The slight decrease of the blowout limit in terms of X with
decreasing thermal power is likely due to the increased relative
inﬂuence of cooling by the combustion chamber walls.
Keeping the thermal power constant at Pth ¼ 60 kW and
decreasing the natural gas content while increasing the content of
H2/CO/CO2 pushed the blowout limit to higher values of X
(increased steam content). For the largest hydrogen content (9.3
vol % dry) ﬂame blowout only occurred for X > 0:75. This trend
supports the assumption of increased ﬂame speeds and reaction
rates at elevated hydrogen content.

CO Emissions. Figure 6 presents the CO emissions for differ-
ent contents of steam X and the same fuel compositions shown in
Fig. 4. For most compositions, the normalized CO emissions are
close to zero over a wide range of X.
For the biosyngas ﬂames, CO emissions increase toward higher
values of X. This is most pronounced for the 45 kW ﬂame, where
the ﬂame became highly unstable. This is likely due to reduced
ﬂame speeds as a result of the increased steam content, which neg-
atively affected ﬂame stability. At these points, the CO emissions
were increased and oscillated periodically. This effect was ampli-
ﬁed with increasing steam content.
As for the NOx emission, the CO emissions are compared for
different compositions at equal ﬂame temperatures in Fig. 7. The
emissions for different fuel compositions follow a similar trend.
At low values of / lower CO emissions were detected, which
increase when approaching the stoichiometric condition. It was
observed that the ﬂame became slightly unstable at high values of
/ due to high values of X. Since for a constant ﬂame power / is
adjusted via the air mass ﬂow, a low equivalence ratio is synony-
mous with high air mass ﬂows and hence low steam mass ﬂows,
and vice versa. The decreasing amount of oxygen surplus with ris-
ing / and X might be an additional driving factor for elevated CO
emissions.
Numerical Validation. Toward the goal of conﬁrming the fea-
sibility of the measured data, the equilibrium emissions of the bio-
syngas are simply computed by a reactor system comprising a
zero-dimensional plug ﬂow reactor (PFR) and a one-dimensional-
ﬂame reactor using the GRI-MECH 3.0 mechanism in Cantera [35].
The one-dimensional-ﬂame reactor contains the ﬂame front
information such as the composition of the product gas and the
adiabatic ﬂame temperature and simulates the main ﬂame reaction
zone. This information is fed to the PFR, which mimics the heat
loss during a long ﬂame residence time in the main ﬂame region.
The PFR is composed of 2000 partially stirred reactors (PSRs)
and models the postﬂame zone and the exhaust tube. The hot ﬂue
gas is brought to a new chemical equilibrium at temperatures pres-
ent at the outlet of the exhaust tube. Here, temperatures of around
1000 K are measured in the experiment. The heat loss in the PFR
is expressed as Q ¼ htotAðT  TenvÞ, where htot is the total heat
transfer coefﬁcient of the quartz glass combustion chamber and
the exhaust tube and A is the surface area of each reactor. A list of
the simulation parameters is presented in Table 3. Mass ﬂow rates
are taken from the experimental data.
As shown in Figs. 5 and 7, the simulation results show a reason-
ably well agreement with the experimental data except for a
severe CO over-prediction at an equivalence ratio of / ¼ 1. The
simulation results imply an almost complete CO burnout until the

behavior and ﬂame shape as it is shown in Fig. 3 and lead to the
severe CO over-prediction. This can also explain the slight over-
prediction of CO at other equivalence ratios. For lean combustion
the over-prediction is less signiﬁcant, since sharp V-ﬂames feature
lower residence time. At equivalence ratios close to unity, CO
emissions from simulations are known to show a sensitive behav-
ior to changes in /. Reducing / from 1 to 0.99 can lead to a
reduction in CO emission by 50%.
Conclusions
This work presents the successful realization of stable combus-
tion of artiﬁcially mixed low caloriﬁc value fuel gases employing
a proprietary swirl-stabilized gas turbine burner.
A stable biosyngas ﬂame was sustained over a wide range of
steam contents X. Increasing the hydrogen content of the mixture
improved ﬂame stability, despite increasing the fraction of N2 and
CO2 in the fuel at the same time. This is likely due to an increased
ﬂame velocity with increasing hydrogen content. However, its
impact on the ﬂame stabilization as reported in the literature still
lacks the conﬁrmation by simulations for this particular biosyngas
composition.
The biosyngas mixture exhibited a LHV of 2.87 MJ/kg (2.7 MJ/
m3
N) at its target point. However, even lower LHV mixtures were
shown to be combustible despite further increasing the steam
content.
NOx and CO emissions were analyzed for several thermal
powers and fuel compositions. The biosyngas ﬂames were emit-
ting less NOx compared to the methane ﬂame at equal steam ratio
X. This is likely due to the presence of N2 and CO2 and hence
increased ﬂame cooling. For all conﬁgurations emission values
were in the single-digit NOx range. A change in thermal power
and fuel composition did not lead to a noticeable effect on the
NOx emissions, whereas the ﬂame temperature was identiﬁed to
exert a large leverage.
CO emissions are close to zero over a wide range of X for most
operating points. Destabilization of the ﬂame through increased
steam or air dilution led to increased CO concentration in the
exhaust.
Flame blowout occurred at higher X with increasing hydrogen
content in the fuel. The ﬂame power had only a slight impact on
the blowout limit, potentially due to wall cooling effects at lower
ﬂame powers.
As a next step, pollutants commonly present in biogas as
ammonia and tars will be added to the biosyngas to assess their
impact on emission formation and combustion behavior.
Further, the development of a simulation tool toward predicting
unstable points and hence operational points exerting increased
CO emission is one of the key works in the next step.
