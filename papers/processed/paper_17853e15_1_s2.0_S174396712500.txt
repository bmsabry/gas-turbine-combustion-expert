A B S T R A C T
The fuel-staging technique, commonly employed in modern gas turbine combustors, enables emission control 
and combustion stability by redistributing fuel between pilot and main stages. This study numerically investi­
gated the influence of the pilot fuel split ratios (PSRs) on nitric oxide (NO) formation in a 5-around-1 multi- 
nozzle combustor for several PSR conditions (11 %, 13 %, 15 %, and 17 %). A distinct non-monotonic trend 
in NO levels at the combustor outlet was observed, with a minimum of 10.59 ppm at PSR13 %. This non- 
monotonic behavior was induced by the competing effects of residence time and fuel–air unmixedness. At 
higher PSRs, elevated temperature and extended residence time enhanced thermal NO formation. In contrast, at 
PSR11 %, increased unmixedness led to an intensification of NO production despite a lower temperature field. 
The numerical results showed good agreement with experimental data, capturing both the trend and the con­
dition for the minimum emission. These findings highlight the importance of managing thermal and mixing 
characteristics in staged combustion and provide practical insights into optimizing fuel-staging strategies for low- 
NOx operation in hydrogen-enriched gas turbines.
1. Introduction
For several decades, gas turbine combustors have been developed to 
increase efficiency, reduce fuel consumption, and minimize pollutant 
emissions. Among combustion byproducts, nitrogen oxides (NOx) and 
carbon oxides (COx) are of particular concern because of their impact on 
air quality, human health, and climate, leading to stringent regulations 
[1]. While carbon control has received significant attention, limiting 
NOx formation under high-temperature operating conditions remains a 
central challenge for low-emission gas turbine combustion.
Environmental concerns also require modern gas turbines to 
increasingly employ lean-premixed combustion to lower flame temper­
ature and thereby suppress thermal-NOx formation [2].
However, operating near the lean limit (e.g., ER ≈0.4) narrows 
stability margins, increasing the lean blowoff (LBO) and combustion 
instability. Near the LBO limit, pressure oscillations become more pro­
nounced, disturbing the flame shape, especially in swirled flows. As the 
equivalence ratio (ER) and swirl number decrease beyond a certain 
threshold, vortex breakdown becomes more likely, leading to decreased 
vortex stability and an increased probability of flame blowout [3]. In 
addition, flames with lower ER entrain more reactant pockets into the 
reaction zone due to the presence of the central recirculation zone 
(CRZ), compared to stable flames at stoichiometric conditions. This 
enhanced entrainment results in incomplete combustion, further 
amplifying blowout risk as the flame becomes unstable and may detach 
from the nozzle or be extinguished due to insufficient energy to sustain 
combustion [4].
Hydrogen–methane co-firing has been investigated as a strategy to 
reduce emissions, benefiting from hydrogen’s high flame speed and 
carbon-free characteristics. The high flame speed, primarily governed by 
the hydrogen fraction in CH4/H2/air mixtures, strengthens flame 
anchoring due to increased reactivity and temperature. However, this 
higher temperature also promotes thermal-NO formation.
In contrast, CH4-rich in CH4/H2/air mixtures tend to produce more 
CO because of their higher carbon concentration, as the lower hydrogen 
fraction reduces the temperature and inhibits complete oxidation [5]. 
This behavior offsets the benefit of COx reduction achieved by replacing 
methane with hydrogen compared to pure methane combustion. Thus, 
flame speed indirectly affects emissions through the hydrogen blending

Moreover, under lean operation with swirling flames, even though a 
hydrogen-methane blend (at ER = 0.4) has a similar laminar flame speed 
to a pure methane flame (at ER = 0.8), the presence of hydrogen in­
creases the propensity for flashback. Small-scale bulges and larger flame 
tongues contribute to unstable flame dynamics and more frequent 
flashback events, which are detrimental to stable combustion [6].
Numerous studies have also reported that the NOx emission trend 
with increasing hydrogen content is not always monotonic, as it depends 
strongly on local flame temperature, mixing quality, and residence time 
[7–17]. These competing effects emphasize the use of the hydrogen–­
methane co-firing. But the interactions among flame temperature, 
fuel–air mixing, and residence time make its behavior highly nonlinear. 
Therefore, achieving consistent low-NOx performance requires precise 
optimization of the hydrogen blending ratio and operating conditions.
These constrains motivate the use of staged combustion, which has 
been widely applied in gas turbines as an effective method to co- 
optimize flame temperature and mixing. Depending on the staging 
configuration, combustor hardware and operating strategies are adap­
ted—for example, by injecting additional air downstream or redis­
tributing fuel–air upstream to improve uniformity and suppress hot 
spots. In air-staged combustors, secondary air addition downstream 
cools the flame on the lean side before chemical effects dominate, con­
firming the temperature-lowering effect of downstream air injection 
[18]. In radially staged systems, redistributing fuel among circuits im­
proves upstream uniformity and reduces exit hot streaks, thereby 
lowering thermal NOx [19].
Building on these observations, several studies have investigated 
hydrogen enrichment under staged combustion configurations. In a 
centrally staged swirling combustor, Xu et al. [20] showed that 
increasing the pilot-staging ratio (PSR, 10–50 %) raised NOx because the 
pilot stream concentrated heat release and peak temperature near the 
core. Similarly, Benaissa et al. [21] found that in a can-type combustor 
fueled with biogas–hydrogen blends, hydrogen enrichment intensified 
the flame and increased NOx when mixing quality was limited, whereas 
improved staging and air distribution reduced peak temperature and 
emissions. Collectively, these results highlight advanced fuel staging as a 
practical lever to control peak temperature and unmixedness in 
hydrogen-enriched flames, enabling low- NOx operation.
Although such methods have achieved significant NOx reduction, the 
fundamental origins of NO formation in gas turbine combustors are 
consistently linked to flame temperature and fuel–air mixing [12–17]. 
Consequently, practical ultra-lean operation with H2-containing fuels is 
constrained by a trade-off among NOx reduction, blowoff risk, insta­
bility, and flashback, motivating control strategies—such as pilot fuel 
split ratio (PSR) optimization—that jointly manage mixing uniformity 
and residence-time/temperature effects.
Therefore, in this study, we performed both numerical simulations 
and experiments with a multi-nozzle combustor, employed with a 5- 
around-1 staged configuration and hydrogen-methane blended fuel. 
To address the complex trade-off among flame temperature, residence 
time, and fuel–air mixing, the pilot fuel split ratio (PSR) was varied and 
evaluated as a key control parameter for NOx optimization. By adjusting 
PSR, we aimed to regulate the local heat release distribution between 
the pilot and main zones, thereby controlling the competing effects of 
residence-time-induced NO formation and mixing-related temperature

2. Experimental and numerical methods
2.1. Experimental apparatus
The cross-sectional schematic of the can-type combustor used in the 
experiments is shown in Fig. 1. The test rig consisted of the nozzles, the 
combustor chamber, and a T-shaped exhaust duct. Air was supplied into 
the combustor casing and routed toward the nozzles. While flowing 
through the casing, the air was preheated by heat exchange with the 
combustor wall and entered the nozzles at the specified temperature. 
Within the combustor liner, the mixture was ignited and formed a sta­
bilized flame, and the combustion products exited through the exhaust 
duct. Four temperature sensors, TC1–TC4, are shown in Fig. 1 and were 
used to measure the gas temperature within the combustor chamber. 
The sensors were B-type thermocouples. NO concentration was 
measured using a high-precision ABB AO2000 Series gas analyzer 
equipped with an NDUV (Non-Dispersive Ultraviolet) module. Mea­
surement accuracy was ensured through calibration before each exper­
iment using a certified 51.6 ppm NO standard gas (N2 balance). We 
evaluated the instrumental uncertainty for each measurement device. 
The experimental uncertainty range (PSR: ±0.64 %, temperature: ±0.5 
%) for the exit temperature and (PSR: ±0.64 %, NO emission: ±1.11 %) 
for the NO emissions, respectively. The detailed experimental setup and 
method can be found in the previous literature [22].
2.2. Geometry and mesh
The computational domain used for the numerical simulations is 
based on an actual engine configuration currently under development, 
which is highlighted in red in Fig. 1 and detailed in Fig. 2. The experi­
mental data were obtained from tests using the same configuration, 
ensuring consistency between numerical and experimental setups. The 
model was divided into two sections, the nozzle assembly and the 
combustor chamber, as shown in Fig. 2a. The nozzle assembly employed 
a 5-around-1 configuration that defined the relative positions of the 
nozzles, as shown in Fig. 2b. One center nozzle, CN, was located at the 
center, and five outer nozzles, ON1–ON5, were placed evenly around the 
CN at a radius of 125.65 mm from the centerline. The combustor 
chamber was cylindrical, with a larger radius near the inlet and a smaller 
radius near the outlet. These sections were joined by a converging sec­
tion that provided a smooth flow transition.
Each outer nozzle consisted of three passages, i.e., main, pilot, and 
blank ones, whereas the center nozzle consisted of two passages, i.e., 
main and blank ones. Swirlers in the main passages provided counter- 
clockwise swirl about each nozzle axis, while the pilot passages were 
straight through. Oxidizer was supplied through inlets at the main, pilot, 
and blank passages and through the cooling plate. Fuel was injected via 
ports on the main-passage swirlers and within the pilot passages. Fig. 2c 
shows the inlet locations of ON3, where blue color denotes the main, 
pilot, and blank air inlets, and red color denotes the main and pilot fuel 
inlets.
The unstructured polyhedral meshes for numerical simulations are 
demonstrated in Fig. 3, with isometric views in Fig. 3a and cross-sections 
through the reaction zone in Fig. 3b. A finer mesh was applied in regions 
of complex geometry, including the mixing zone, reaction zone, and gas 
inlets. To reduce the computational cost, a grid-independence check was 
first conducted using a single-nozzle (SN) combustor, consisting of one 
outer nozzle and its corresponding chamber. Four different maximum 
cell sizes in the fine-mesh region were tested, ranging from 1.3 mm to 
2.5 mm. With the cell size of 1. 5 mm, the differences in temperature, 
axial velocity, and NO emission were found to be less than 7.26 % 
compared to the finest cell size of 1.3 mm, as illustrated in supple­
mentary material, SM. Therefore, unstructured polyhedral cells with a 
maximum cell size of 1.5 mm were adopted for the present simulations, 
resulting in approximately 13.6 million cells generated in the full 
domain.

2.3. Fuel-staging strategy
Fuel and oxidizer were supplied separately through the inlets and 
mixed with each other within the flow passages (including main, pilot, 
and blank passages). The operating conditions of gases inside the 
combustor were specified based on experimental conditions. Table 1
summarizes the operating conditions. The fuel is a mixture of methane 
(CH4) and hydrogen (H2) with a mole fraction of 50 % each. The oxidizer 
was treated as ideal-gas air, consisting of 21 % oxygen (O2) and 79 % 
nitrogen (N2) by volume. The temperatures of fuel and oxidizer inlets 
were 473.15 K and 718.15 K, respectively. An operating pressure of 1.5 
bar was adopted in the simulations. Total air mass flow rate was 
maintained at 4800 kg/h, while total fuel mass flow rate was modified to 
achieve a thermal power (input) of 2092 kW and an overall equivalence 
ratio of 0.539.
Due to the geometric and operational complexity of the staged 
combustor, a fuel-staging strategy was employed to control the distri­
bution of fuel among the individual inlets. In this configuration, fuel was 
supplied through four designated circuits: FC1 (indicated in yellow) 
delivered fuel to center nozzle, FCP (indicated in red) supplied fuel to 
pilot passages of five outer nozzles, and FC2 and FC3 (indicated in blue 
and green, respectively) fed fuel to main passages of five outer nozzles. 
The schematic diagram of the fuel circuit (FC) configuration is presented 
in Fig. 4 [22].

To simulate turbulent flow, Eqs. (3)–(6) were transformed into the 
Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) equations [23]. All simula­
tions were performed using a general-purpose CFD solver, ANSYS Fluent 
[24]. The shear stress transport (SST) k–ω model [25] was employed to 
close the Reynolds stresses, blending k–ω near walls with k–ε in outer 
regions to provide robust predictions for separated and swirling flows. 
The discrete ordinates radiation model and convective wall boundary 
conditions were applied to account for heat transfer at the combustor 
walls. These were adopted to ensure accurate modeling of flow–wall 
interactions.
2.5. Species transport model and combustion model
Since the primary purpose of this study is to evaluate the influence of 
fuel staging on NOx formation, especially NO, it is crucial to accurately 
predict the interaction processes of species within the combustor. To 
achieve this, the GRI 3.0 kinetic model [26] was employed, which in­
corporates all NO formation pathways as well as the key reactions 
governing methane-hydrogen dual fuel’s combustion. The selection of 
GRI 3.0 was based on a comparative evaluation of 18 detailed, reduced, 
and global mechanisms conducted in our previous work [27]. That study 
compared ignition delay time, laminar flame speed, NOx emissions, and 
computational efficiency for CH4/H2/air mixtures over wide ranges of 
equivalence ratio, hydrogen fraction, and pressure. Among the tested 
mechanisms, GRI 3.0 provided the best balance between accuracy and 
computational cost for hydrogen blending ratios below 60 % and pres­
sures below 10 bar, confirming its suitability for the present work.
For the turbulent–chemistry interaction, the Eddy Dissipation 
Concept (EDC) model with the Partially Stirred Reactor (PaSR) approach 
was adopted [28,29]. The PaSR model, a built-in model of the adopted 
solver [24], extends the standard EDC formulation by accounting for 
slow chemical kinetics and turbulent mixing interactions, enabling more 
accurate predictions in combustion regimes involving nitrogen chem­
istry. This modeling approach ensures an accurate representation of 
species transport and reaction kinetics in turbulent flows, providing 
reliable numerical results despite the inherent assumptions in the 
simulation setup.
Soret effect was neglected in this work, based on the preliminary 
tests, where it was included and then, excluded for a comparative study. 
The results showed that its inclusion altered the temperature, velocity, 
and NO emission only by 2.62 %. Details on the tests of Soret effect can 
be found in supplementary material, SM.
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Validation of numerical approach
The validation of the simulation results was carried out in a previous 
study [22], which employed the same numerical method and the grid 
size as in the present work. In that study, three primary parameters such 
as wall temperature, OH concentration, and NOx emissions were 
examined, where all the errors were less than 5 % compared to experi­
mental results.
In this study, gas temperatures in the combustion chamber were 
compared with numerical results. In the experiments, four thermocou­
ples were installed at TC1, TC2, TC3, and TC4, all located on the same 
axial plane at 1510 mm downstream of the nozzle inlets as illustrated in 
Fig. 1. The calculated and experimental results are shown in Fig. 5. The 
numerical results followed the experimental trend closely, with higher 
temperatures at TC1–TC3 and a noticeable drop at TC4. The sharp 
temperature decrease near the wall (TC4) is attributed to wall cooling 
effects.
The maximum deviation between the simulation and experiment was 
observed at TC4, with a deviation of 90.71K (9.56 %). According to our 
previous work [22], the measurement repeatability ranged from ±30 K 
to ±84 K (from TC1 to TC4). Considering this repeatability range (±84 K 
at TC4), the deviation observed in this study could potentially narrow 
further, suggesting good consistency between the experimental and 
numerical results. Therefore, the comparison confirms that the present 
numerical approach provides reasonable and reliable predictions of the 
temperature field inside the combustor.
3.2. Distribution of flow velocity
Fig. 6 illustrates the middle surface at z = 0, where the x-direction 
aligns with the flow direction. This figure provides a clear visualization 
of combustor components and shows 6 monitoring positions for flow- 
field analyses. The specific location at x = 32 mm indicates the 
connection between nozzles and combustor parts, also known as a 
nozzle exit. The nozzle inlet or entrance is positioned at x = −373 mm, 
while the combustor exit is located at x = 1679 mm.
As aforementioned for the boundary conditions, both the total and 
local mass flow rates of air were significantly higher than those of fuel. 
Consequently, minor variations in the PSR had a negligible effect on 
flow velocity within the nozzle passages and the combustor chamber. 
Therefore, only the case with PSR11 % was analyzed for velocity.

As the flow develops, the gases mixed, expanded, and formed two 
distinct zones: Inner Recirculation Zones (IRZs) and Interaction Zones 
(IZs). In Fig. 7b, the locations of these regions could be identified. The 
IRZs exhibited reverse flow rotating around the nozzle axis, resulting in 
negative axial velocity. Six IRZs were observed, each corresponding to 
one of the six nozzles, i.e., one center nozzle (CN) and five outer nozzles 
(ONs). In contrast, the IZs emerged from interactions between the CN 
and each ON, as well as between adjacent ONs, creating high-velocity 
regions. Five IZs were shown in Fig. 7b, where flow interactions inten­
sify the local axial velocity up to higher than 50 m/s.
However, the IZs gradually disappeared due to flow expansion and 
merging, and no reverse flow was observed within the IRZs, as shown in 
Fig. 7c. Then, the flow became more blended and uniformly distributed, 
approaching complete mixing. This enhanced uniformity stabilized the 
flame structure and promoted more homogeneous combustion, which 
helped to mitigate localized high-temperature regions responsible for 
NO formation.
3.3. Temperature distribution
Due to the geometric differences between the ON and the CN, 
particularly the presence or absence of pilot passages, flames exhibited 
distinct shapes and structures, which closely aligned with the flow 
patterns depicted by velocity distributions. Specifically, the flame 
formed by the CN exhibited a cylindrical shape, whereas the ON flames 
displayed a tailed structure. This tailed structure consisted of two con­
nected zones, i.e., an inner zone, formed by the pilot flow and the inner 
recirculation zones (IRZs), and an outer zone, resulting from interactions 
between the pilot and main flows.
Fig. 8 shows the distributions of equivalence ratio (ER) and flame 
temperature on the mid-plane of the combustor chamber, spanning from 
x = −100 mm to the combustor exit. The ER varied from approximately 
0.3 to 0.7, consistent with the lean-premixed combustion regime defined 
by the inlet boundary conditions. While the overall patterns of ER and 
temperature remained qualitatively similar for all the PSR conditions, 
spatial differences were evident due to variations in fuel distribution of 
each nozzle. Both evaluation of ER and temperature fields enabled a 
more comprehensive understanding of local combustion characteristics 
and NO formation zones, as each field played a distinct role in NOx 
chemistry.
In all cases, the mass flow rate of fuel supplied to the CN remained 
constant, resulting in similar flame structures and peak temperatures of 
around 2300 K near the CN core. In contrast, the ONs exhibited lower 
peak temperatures, typically ranging from about 1400 K in the inner 
zone to 2000 K in the outer zone. High-temperature regions exceeding 
1800 K, particularly around the CN and the upstream of the ON flames, 
indicated zones where thermal NO was likely to be formed significantly.
To see radial temperature profile evolving from the upstream to 
downstream depending on PSR, temperature profiles are shown in Fig. 9
along the normalized radial coordinate at various axial locations; x =
132 mm, 232 mm, 332 mm, 532 mm, 732 mm, and 932 mm. The 
normalized radial coordinate is defined as y/Rmax, where y denotes the 
vertical or radial coordinate (in the y direction) and Rmax the maximum 
chamber radius.

early stages of combustion, specifically between x = 132 mm–332 mm

3.4. Residence time in the combustor
To better understand the effects of PSR on flame temperature and NO 
formation, a parameter, the residence time of reactants in the 
combustor, τR, is introduced. This parameter is defined as the average 
time required for a fluid particle to travel from the combustor entrance 
to the exit while remaining within regions where the temperature ex­
ceeds a specified threshold value.
Fig. 10 shows the streamlines used for this analysis, with starting 
points located at the combustor entrance and endpoints at the liner exit. 
The entrance region is divided into distinct zones associated with the 
respective connections between each nozzle, i.e., either the center 
nozzle or the outer nozzle, and the liner.
As particles move through the chamber, they alternate between high- 
and low-temperature regions. These regions are separated by a reference 
temperature, Tref, designated here as 1800 K, from the previous study 
[30], where this threshold was identified as the onset temperature of 
significant thermal NO formation. In Fig. 10, the red segments of the 
streamlines present regions where the local temperature exceeds Tref, 
while blue segments indicate regions below this threshold.
The residence time is calculated as the average time a particle stays 
in higher-temperature regions than Tref along each streamline. Because 
the flow paths depend on the origin of a nozzle, PSR, and recirculation 
flow, the residence time also varies as well. Fig. 11 shows both the 
residence time and the average temperature along streamlines origi­
nating from the CN and the ONs. In general, streamlines from the CN 
exhibited higher average temperature, ranging from 1802 K to 1808 K, 
compared with those from the ON, which ranged from 1627 K to 1665 K. 
This temperature difference was consistent with the ER and flame 
temperature fields discussed in the preceding section, where the CN 
exhibited richer mixtures and more intense combustion, while the ONs 
showed relatively lower temperature and ER.
The results in Fig. 11 further show that the main source of NO 
variation for variable PSR was flame characteristics associated with 
ONs. Both the average temperature and residence time along ON 
streamlines increased gradually with PSR, which results from the effect 
of fuel redistribution into the pilot passages. As PSR increased from 11 % 
to 17 %, the ON residence time rose from 27.8 ms to 35.6 ms and also, 
the average temperature increased from 1626.95 K to 1664.97 K. It 
implies that increasing formation of thermal NO in the outer flame zones 
at higher PSRs. In contrast, path lines originating from the CN showed 
slight variation as a function of PSR. The average temperature remained 
between 1801.94 K and 1807.78 K, and the residence time varied only 
from 32.71 ms to 33.03 ms.
This trend was also reflected in the swirl number. The calculated 
swirl number corresponding to the ONs (not shown here) exhibited a 
slight increase—from 0.8007 at PSR 11 % to 0.8032 at PSR 17 %. 
Meanwhile, the CN swirl number remained nearly constant, varying 
little from 0.7082 to 0.7084 over the same PSR range.
This behavior is attributed to the constant fuel flow rate into the CN 
and its isolation from velocity-induced inner recirculation zones (IRZs). 
Although the CN contributed to a high baseline level of thermal NO 
emission due to relatively higher temperature, it could not account for 
the observed variation in NO emission as a function of PSR. It will be 
discussed later. Moreover, at PSR11 % and PSR13 %, the residence time 
of ON streamlines was much lower than that of the CN, leading to the 
difference in combustion dynamics between the two reaction zones. 
These findings imply the critical role of PSR-dependent flame behavior 
near the outer nozzles in controlling the overall NO formation in staged 
methane-hydrogen combustion.

This competing or combined effects of unmixedness and residence 
time on NO emission implies the non-monotonic behavior of NO emis­
sion as indicated by NO/C in Fig. 14 and a local minimum of NO 
emission is found at a particular value of PSR. In the present numerical 
simulations, the PSR was found to be 13 %. That is, when PSR increased 
from 11 % to 13 %, the NO-reduction effect of unmixedness was 
dominant over the enhanced effect of residence time on NO formation, 
resulting in a slight decrease in NO emission at PSR13 % compared with 
at PSR11 %. But, when PSR increased further beyond PSR13 %, the 
latter effect was dominant while the former became weak, leading to a 
moderate rise in NO emission at PSR15 %. Beyond PSR15 %, both 
unmixedness and residence time contributed to enhanced NO formation, 
resulting in abrupt increase in NO emission at PSR17 %.
This dependence of NO emission on unmixedness at PSR11 % can be 
explained as follows. For this condition, fluid dynamics characterized by 
weak recirculation zones, shorter mixing paths, and incomplete shear- 
layer development, may lead to fuel–air separation. Thus, fluid flow 
may allow unmixed fuel pockets to stay locally for longer durations 
within the reaction zone even if the overall residence time is short. And 
then, it enhances local flame intensities and thereby, NO emission is 
increased. This behavior is consistent with previous studies on NO for­
mation in partially premixed jets for low-shear and low-swirl conditions 
[16,32,33]. Accordingly, the present results confirm that unmixedness 
and residence time are competing factors affecting NO emission signif­
icantly in staged hydrogen-methane combustion with their relative in­
fluence varying depending on PSR.
As shown in Fig. 13, there is a little discrepancy in PSR, at which NO 
emission reaches minimum, between numerical and experimental re­
sults. That is, the simulated NO minimum occurs at PSR = 13 % (10.59 
ppm), while it is observed experimentally at PSR = 15 % (7.26 ppm). 
Nevertheless, this non-monotonic behavior of NO emission implies that 
optimization of PSR is useful for NO reduction.
4. Conclusions
The effects of pilot fuel split ratio (PSR) on fuel–air mixing, flame 
pattern, and NO emission have been investigated numerically in a staged 
hydrogen-methane gas turbine combustor. Simulations were performed 
for four PSRs between 11 % and 17 % with the H2 blending ratio of 50 % 
and the results were validated by experimental data.
The numerical results revealed that NO formation is governed by two 
competing factors. One is thermal NO production driven by flame 
temperature and residence time and the other is NO reduction induced 
by fuel–air mixing. The latter is quantified by the parameter of fuel–air 
unmixedness. As PSR increased, enhanced pilot flow led to higher flame 
temperatures and longer residence times, resulting in more NO emis­
sion. However, at the lowest PSR, NO emission didn’t decrease despite 
lower temperatures and short residence time, because the abrupt in­
crease in fuel–air unmixedness created locally rich zones, which inten­
sified NO production.
Flow-field analysis confirmed this behavior and it indicated weak 
recirculation and incomplete fuel-air mixing for the condition. Tem­
perature fields showed that temperature variation of flames formed by 
the outer nozzles was more sensitive to PSR variation than that by the 
center nozzle.
Residence-time analysis clarified the dominant role of outer nozzle 
flow paths in enhancing NO formation at higher PSRs. Residence time 
increased linearly with PSR while unmixedness exhibited non- 
monotonic dependence on PSR. The proposed equation for prediction 
of NO emission, which was made by a regression models combining 
unmixedness and residence time as two variables, successfully captured 
the non-monotonic behavior of NO emission as a function of PSR. It 
implies that NO emission could shift from mixing-controlled to 
temperature-controlled regime depending on PSR. As a result, the lowest 
NO emissions of 10.59 ppm and 7.26 ppm were observed at PSR of 13 % 
in simulations and at PSR of 15 % in experiment, respectively.
In conclusion, the present results confirm that flame temperature, 
unmixedness, and residence time are critical factors affecting NO 
emission in methane-hydrogen co-firing combustor. Pilot fuel staging 
proves to be an effective strategy for mitigation of NO emission, and an 
optimal combustor design should aim to balance between fuel–air 
mixing quality and reactive-flow field characterized by flame tempera­
ture and residence time. For this purpose, the PSR can be controlled in 
designing a combustor of a hydrogen–enriched gas turbine, leading to 
low NO emission. Further works for more realistic condition, e.g., high 
pressure will be considered in future works.
