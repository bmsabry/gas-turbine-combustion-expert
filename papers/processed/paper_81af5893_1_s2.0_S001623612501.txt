A B S T R A C T
This study investigated the effect of wall heat loss on the flame stability and emission characteristics of NH3/ 
CH4/air premixed flames in a model gas-turbine combustor. Increasing ammonia mole fraction (10 ~ 40 %) 
expanded the flame stability range at equivalence ratio ϕ ≥0.76, preventing flashbacks and ensuring stable 
combustion, particularly at low flow rates. For ϕ < 0.76, the stable region was narrower, approaching the blow- 
off limit. These stable regions exhibited more significant fluctuations at swirl number S = 0.78 than at S = 0.45, 
and lift-off occurred earlier at the dump plane. Detailed analyses revealed the impact of heat loss on flame 
behavior and emissions. Notably, local extinction phenomena significantly influenced flame dynamics, resulting 
in a pronounced increase in the N2O concentration while NO decreased. The chemical reactions responsible for 
NO and N2O production are highly sensitive to heat loss, particularly under unsteady flame conditions. A 
chemical reactor network model (CRN) was developed to predict and analyze emission characteristics. Although 
the model qualitatively captured the measured emission trends of NO and N2O, it exhibited significant dis­
crepancies, particularly in predicting N2O, and NO was overestimated. This is induced by the incompetence of 
the model, which does not account for local extinction phenomena, and the limitations of the chemical mech­
anism used in this study.
1. Introduction
The continued use of fossil fuels, which produce greenhouse gases, 
has considerably accelerated global warming. Therefore, the importance 
of carbon–neutral fuels like ammonia and hydrogen has become evident 
in moving toward a carbon-free era. Among them, ammonia, which can 
be easily stored as a liquid at room temperature under a pressure of 8 bar 
[1], is a promising hydrogen carrier owing to its high volumetric 
hydrogen density [2]. However, ammonia exhibits a small lower heating 
value (LHV), a narrower flammability limit, and a slower laminar flame 
speed than methane. This poor combustion performance increases the 
risk of flame blow-off [3,4], and a larger ignition energy is required to 
ignite the ammonia–air mixture [5]. In addition, ammonia undergoes 
oxidation more rapidly than nitrogen in the air, producing high NOx 
levels [6]. N2O, an intermediate species in ammonia combustion, has a 
Global Warming Potential (GWP) approximately 273 times higher than 
that of CO2 over 20 years [7]. Therefore, a comprehensive 
understanding of the ammonia combustion characteristics and its 
blended fuel is essential for its potential use as a carbon–neutral fuel.
In a swirl combustor, compared with methane, an ammonia flame 
can undergo excessive stretching commensurate with the extinction 
strain rate [8]. Although this could lead to local extinction, impacting 
flame stability and emissions, recirculation zones contribute to stabi­
lizing the ammonia flame by increasing the residence time of the re­
actants. Numerous studies have been conducted on ammonia flame 
stability [3,9–13]. Hayakawa et al. [9] compared the stability of 
ammonia flames in swirl burners using cylindrical and rectangular 
chambers. The stability limits were narrower in the rectangular chamber 
than in the cylindrical chamber, owing to the interrupted flow structures 
at the corners. Khateeb et al. [3] demonstrated that the stability limit 
expanded by up to 50 % of the ammonia fraction at the same Reynolds 
number and then narrowed. They also found that the swirl number had 
little impact on flame stability in an ammonia/methane swirl flame.

injected ammonia mole fraction exceeded 65 %, whereas the NO levels 
decreased. This finding was consistent with the results reported by 
Khateeb et al. [3]; however, the quantity of NO produced remained

The primary reaction pathways contributing to NOx formation 
include the thermal NOx, N2O, HNO, and NHi pathways [15]. The major 
radicals involved in this pathway are O, H, and OH. Particularly, OH 
radicals play a significant role in the oxidation of NH2 and NH, leading 
to NO production [16], and NO and OH exhibit a positive correlation 
[17]–[18]. Moreover, NO is concentrated in regions with high heat 
release rates and OH concentrations, and local NO formation is high in 
the vortex regions [19]. Viswamithra et al. [20], using a chemical 
reactor network model, demonstrated that NO production is primarily 
driven by the HNO pathway, while NO consumption is influenced by the 
N2O and NHi pathways. Thermal NOx emissions were found to be min­
imal. Zhang et al. [21] also reported that thermal NOx is minor at low 
ammonia mole fractions, and N2O is highly sensitive to NO and O rad­
icals through the reaction N2O + O → 2NO.
Ammonia has a lower adiabatic flame temperature than methane, 
which reduces the risk of damage to the liner owing to thermal fatigue. 
However, it may be more susceptible to flame quenching due to the liner 
cooling in a gas-turbine combustor. In addition, as the flame heat loss 
increases, NO emissions decrease, while N2O emissions increase 
[18,22,23]. Zhang et al. [22] found that cooling an ammonia swirl flame 
from a liner decreased OH radical concentration and shifted NO away 
from the wall. This resulted in a reduced combustion intensity and ef­
ficiency due to the decreased heat release rate caused by wall heat loss. 
Moreover, under fuel-lean conditions, an increase in the cooling flow 
rate leads to lower NO levels and increases N2O levels. This phenomenon 
is likely due to the inhibition of O/H radical production by the branching 
reaction H + O2 ⇌ OH + O [18], which is particularly sensitive to high 
temperatures. In a study by Okafor et al. [23], under insulated condi­
tions, NO levels were found to be higher due to lower heat loss compared 
to the uninsulated condition, whereas N2O levels were higher in the 
uninsulated condition. In a micro gas-turbine combustor using ammonia 
fuel [24], a greater quantity of N2O was produced when the liner tem­
perature was low. Mashruk et al. [25] analyzed emission characteristics 
with experiments and confirmed the effect of Reynolds number and 
thermal power with adding ammonia in NH3/H2/Air premixed swirling 
flames. NO and NO2 decreased with ammonia concentration, but N2O 
increased at the same thermal power. They also pointed out that the N2O 
emission increased at high ammonia flames, which results in increased 
heat loss and greater NH radical production. Meanwhile, the effect of 
radiation heat loss in a fuel-lean one-dimensional laminar flame simu­
lation was minor for N2O but decreased for NO [26].
The main challenges associated with ammonia flames include a 
narrow flame stabilization range, slow flame speed, and significant NO 
and N2O emissions. A potential strategy to overcome these limitations 
and enable the use of ammonia in conventional combustion systems 
with minimal system modifications is to blend ammonia with a more 
reactive mixture such as CH4. In a previous study on NH3/H2/Air swirl 
flames [25], the emission characteristics were thoroughly analyzed with 
respect to the heat load and Reynolds number. It is known that OH 
radicals have a significant effect on NO and N2O at low temperatures. In 
CH4 combustion, OH radicals are primarily produced from H radicals 
originating predominantly from CH4 [27]. Consequently, we aimed to 
study the influence of wall temperature and local flame extinction on 
combustion and emission characteristics, considering thermal power 
and Reynolds number, in premixed NH3/CH4/air flames in a model gas 
turbine combustor.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 details 
the experimental setup, conditions, and numerical validation of the 
chemical reactor network (CRN) model used in the analysis. Section 3.1
explores the flame stability with both the swirl number and ammonia 
mole fraction. Section 3.2 discusses the distribution of wall tempera­
tures, emission characteristics, and the relationship between operating 
parameters and the heat release rate. Section 3.3 shows the effects of 
wall heat loss resulting from variations in wall temperature and Rey­
nolds number through numerical analysis using the CRN model. Finally, 
Section 4 concludes the study.

2.1. Experimental setup and test matrix
Fig. 1 shows a schematic of the experimental equipment used in this 
study. The experimental process began with the CH4/air mixture passing 
through the swirler and being ignited by a spark ignitor, and ammonia 
was injected according to the experimental conditions. Each gas fraction

We used 99 % pure methane and ammonia with flow rates regulated by 
mass flow controllers (SLA5851, Brooks), ensuring a repeatability of 0.2 
% of the set point. The chamber had four sides of 70 mm × 180 mm 
quartz windows. The swirl and dump planes were aligned to capture 
transient characteristics, such as lift-off upstream. Flame visualization 
was performed using a DSLR camera (D7100, Nikon) and a High-Speed 
(HS) camera (FASTCAM Mini AX50, Photron). High-speed camera im­
ages were captured diagonally to enhance the effectiveness of capturing 
flame-wall interactions. We employed an infrared (IR) camera (T620, 
FLIR) with a spectral range of 7.5 to 15 μm to measure the wall tem­
perature. As wavelengths larger than 5 μm cannot transmit through 
quartz, this allowed us to measure the wall temperature accurately. An 
IR image is taken at a specific time when the wall temperature has 
stabilized under experimental conditions. The temperature variation is 
less than ± 1 K/min, and the accuracy of the IR camera is within ± 2 % 
of the reading. The heat release rate was quantified by using a photo­
multiplier tube (PMT) (H11461-02, Hamamatsu) equipped with a 
bandpass filter (307FS10-25, Andover) centered at 307.1 nm (FWHM =
10 ± 2 nm), corresponding to OH wavelength near 300 nm. Both the 
PMT and IR cameras were positioned in front of the chamber.

2.2. Numerical modeling
2.2.1. Chemical reactor network (CRN)
A CRN model was developed to simulate the combustion process 
within the model combustor. As shown in Fig. 4, this model incorporates 
four Perfectly Stirred Reactors (PSR), each representing different zones 
within the combustor: the flame zone, Central Recirculation Zone 
(CTRZ), and Corner Recirculation Zone (CRZ). Heat loss through the 
wall has a notable impact on NO and N2O emissions [22,23]. The heat 
loss based on the wall temperature captured by an IR camera was 
incorporated. Model parameters, such as residence time influenced by 
Re and reactor volume, were derived from additional Reynolds-averaged 
Navier-Stokes equations (RANS) CFD simulations. The total heat loss 
through heat transfer to the wall was calculated using Eq. (6). 
Qloss,total = k
(
Tflame −Twall
)/
δ
(6) 
Here, k is the thermal conductivity of the quartz, δ is the quartz 
thickness, Tflame is the temperature near the wall and it is obtained from 
CFD results, and Twall is the peak wall temperature measured through an 
IR camera. The RANS simulation was conducted using OpenFOAM 
(reactingFoam). The combustor was discretized into 3D unstructured 
mesh with 3 × 106 finite volume cells. The equations for the mass, 
momentum, and energy conservations together with the species trans­
port equations were solved using a 2nd-order accuracy numerical 
scheme. The turbulence effect was described by the k-omega SST model, 
and the well-stirred reactor model was applied for turbulent combus­
tion. At the inlet, Dirichlet boundary conditions were applied to veloc­
ity, temperature, and fuel composition based on experimental 
conditions. The mass flow rate of each reactor was determined by 
considering the recirculation zones. The reactant composition was based 
on the experimental conditions of the inlet mixture at 298 K and 1 atm. 
The CRN calculations were performed using Cantera [29].
Fig. 5 shows a comparison between the experimental data and the 
predictions from the CRN for NO and N2O emissions. The standard de­
viations for the experiments were less than 15 ppm for NO and less than 
2 ppm for N2O. Various mechanisms were used, including those from 
Kovaleva et al. [30], Arunthanayothin et al. [31], Konnov et al. [32], 
Glarborg et al. [33], Okafor et al. [34], and POLIMI [35]. In general, the 
CRN model predicted higher NO and lower N2O levels than the experi­
mental data. For Re = 12000 and Re = 13000, N2O levels were close to 
zero but showed a trend similar to the experimental results at Re =
14000. Despite these discrepancies, both the model and experimental 
data demonstrated similar trends under experimental conditions. 
Considering the uncertainties in predicting NOx and N2O with current 
state-of-the-art chemistry mechanisms, and given the model’s simplicity 
[20,21], this CRN model is suitable for identifying important chemical 
reactions and making qualitative predictions. Among six state-of-the-art 
NH3/CH4/Air combustion chemistry mechanisms, the Kovaleva mech­
anism, which comprises 59 species and 354 elementary reactions, yields 
the best prediction of the measured NO and N2O in our CRN configu­
ration. Thus, emission characteristics and reaction sensitivity were 
analyzed using this mechanism.
3. Results and discussion
3.1. 1 flame stability and characteristics
Fig. 6 shows the stable region of flame as a function of ϕ. For ϕ ≥
0.76, this region expands as α increases, up to 0.4, for both swirl 
numbers. Khateeb et al. [3] found that the stable region expands in NH3/ 
CH4/Air swirl flames when the ammonia mole fraction exceeds a certain 
value, preventing flashbacks. We noticed flame perturbation near the 
swirler under low flow rate and low ammonia mole fraction conditions, 
particularly outside the stable region for S = 0.78 (i.e. α = 0.1, ϕ = 0.85). 
It is attributed to the vortex breakdown at the center, but the flame 
stabilized at a constant height as the ammonia mole fraction increased at 
fixed ϕ and U (i.e., α = 0.4). This occurred because of the slower flame 
speed, weakening the reverse flow upstream. For ϕ < 0.76, the stable 
region contracted owing to decreased reactivity from added ammonia 
and increased flow rate, nearing the blowout limit and causing insta­
bility. The stable region is considerably broader at S = 0.78 than at S =
0.45, but at S = 0.78, the flame exhibits noticeable vibrations and noise 
in the unstable region. The acoustic perturbation observed at the flam­
mability limit is likely akin to the thermoacoustic instability mechanism 
observed in lean premixed methane combustors [36].
Fig. 7 illustrates the flame shape captured in the same area as the IR 
image under the experimental conditions of Q = 9 kW. As the mole 
fraction of ammonia increased, the white color gradually diminished, 
and the flame transitioned to orange. This change was due to the intense 
orange chemiluminescence from excited NH2 and NO2 [37], which 
became more noticeable as the NH3 concentration increased. For S =
0.78, as Re increased from 16,000 to 18000, the lift-off (LO) condition 
shifted to lower α, dropping from 0.3 to 0.1. This change is attributed to 
the reduced laminar flame speed due to the addition of ammonia. 
Interestingly, at the same Re, lift-off happens for a lower α at S = 0.78 
than at S = 0.45, which can be attributed to differences in the strain rate 
near the dump plane, and LO correlated linearly with the extinction 
strain rate and the fluid dynamic strain rate at the flame base [38]. The

3.2. Wall temperature and emissions
The temperature distributions for the two swirl numbers are largely 
similar, with the only exception being the peak wall temperature (Twall). 
Fig. 9 shows the Twall for each experimental condition. When both ϕ and 
Q were increased, Twall also increased, consistent with variations in 
adiabatic flame temperature because of changes in heat load and reac­
tant composition. Under the same Q, an increase in Re decreased ϕ, 
causing Twall to decrease. Twall is slightly higher at S = 0.78 than at S =
0.45. This can be attributed to the intensified recirculation zone and the 
contact with the wall. The flames at S = 0.78 directly impacted the wall, 
leading to an enhancement in the recirculation zone and heat transfer to 
the wall. Consequently, the flame front was positioned closer to the 
chamber wall and moved more upstream than at S = 0.45, resulting in a 
higher peak temperature. Although the temperature variation with α 
remained minor in Groups I and II, it significantly decreased in Group III. 
This reduction was attributed to localized extinction on the wall due to 
reduced flame reactivity and an extended quenching distance, resulting 
from the addition of ammonia under the low equivalence ratio condi­
tions of Group III [5].
Fig. 10 shows the flame images of Groups I, II, and III captured using 
a high-speed camera at S = 0.45. In Groups I and II, the flame intensities 
remained relatively constant. However, in Group III, the flame devel­
oped between 0 and 15 ms, after which the flame area decreased 
because of local extinction from 15 to 30 ms. In addition, local extinction 
and reignition occurred periodically, contributing to the lower wall 
temperature in Group III than in Groups I and II. In Fig. 11, the OH* 
intensity measured using the PMT is presented as a probability density 
function. OH* serves as a reliable marker for predicting the heat release

3.3. Numerical analysis for experimental results
To analyze the effects of Twall and Re on emissions, numerical ana­
lyses were performed using a CRN model, as shown in Fig. 13. Overall, 
the impact of wall temperature on emissions is greater than that of Re 
under the same heat load. Zhang et al. [22] demonstrated that aug­
menting wall heat loss in ammonia/air flames leads to decreased NO 
emissions and increased N2O emissions. Likewise, the increased heat 
loss results in decreased wall temperature, leading to a reduction in NO 
emissions. N2O levels were close to zero except when Twall = 800 K. For a 
fixed Q, a relatively higher wall temperature implies lower heat loss and 
a more stable flame with higher temperatures. Increasing Re with a 
decrease in ϕ leads to a relatively unstable flame, and the resulting 
decrease in flame temperature further enhances N2O production, as 
depicted in Fig. 10. As shown in Fig. 12, increasing the Re under the 
same heat load not only shortens the residence time required for 
adequate NO production but also decreases the production of H radicals 
because of the lower equivalence ratio. In the same group, NO decreases 
as the Re increases. This can be attributed to factors such as radiative 
heat loss and short residence times. Although the radiation heat loss

4. Conclusion
In this study, we investigated the effects of heat loss through 
combustor walls on the flame stability and emission characteristics of 
NH3/CH4/air premixed swirl flames in a gas-turbine model. We found 
that enhancing the ammonia mole fraction expands the flame’s stability 
regime at lower flow velocities (ϕ ≥0.76) by reducing reactivity and 
preventing flashback. However, at ϕ < 0.76, higher flow rates and 
ammonia addition led to flame blowout. At a swirl number (S) of 0.78, 
the recirculation zones, including both the corner and central regions, 
were more pronounced compared to those at S = 0.45. Additionally, 
acoustic perturbations and signs of flame instability began to emerge 
near the flammability limit. The flame lifted off due to the higher strain 
rates at the dump plane, which were greater than those observed at S =
0.45.
Temperature distribution along the combustor wall, mirroring 
adiabatic flame temperature trends, is shown to be modulated by the 
swirl number and the presence of recirculation zones, impacting emis­
sions. Under reduced reactivity conditions (ϕ = 0.74, α = 0.4), local 
extinction significantly lowered wall temperatures and caused notice­
able variations in N2O and NO emissions. N2O levels increased in the 
presence of local extinction, while NO concentrations dropped 
considerably.
In the numerical analysis, wall heat loss had a more pronounced 
effect on NO and N2O concentrations compared to changes in Reynolds 
number and equivalence ratio under the same heat load. NO levels were 
reduced due to shorter residence times and fewer OH radicals, while 
N2O reaction pathways, particularly N2O(+M) ⇌ N2 + O(+M), showed 
high sensitivity to heat loss. Numerical simulation shows that accurate 
prediction of N2O emissions requires a more sophisticated model 
capable of incorporating unsteady flame dynamics. By contrast, the 
trend in NO emissions can be explained by variations in wall tempera­
ture, equivalence ratio, and flow residence time using a chemical reactor 
network modeling approach.
