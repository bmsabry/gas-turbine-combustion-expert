Industrial gas turbines play a fundamental role in modern energy infrastructure, serving as 
key enablers of reliable power generation and industrial operations. With rising global 
energy demand and the imperative to reduce its environmental impact, these turbines 
are undergoing continuous innovation. This study explores major technological advance­
ments, including novel material applications, aerodynamic refinements, improved combus­
tion techniques, and the increasing role of digital technologies. Furthermore, the transition 
to alternative fuels and the integration of renewables are examined as pivotal develop­
ments. The analysis underscores the importance of ongoing enhancements in efficiency, 
emissions reduction, and operational flexibility to meet the evolving needs of the energy 
sector. In addition to technological progress, economic and regulatory factors shape the 
development and deployment of industrial gas turbines. Stricter emissions policies and 
the push for decarbonization drive research into hydrogen and synthetic fuels, while 
digital monitoring and predictive maintenance improve operational reliability and cost 
efficiency. The role of industrial gas turbines in hybrid energy systems, combining conven­
tional and renewable sources, highlights their adaptability in ensuring grid stability. These 
combined innovations reinforce their relevance as a critical component of future energy 
solutions, enabling the transition toward more sustainable and resilient power generation.

1 Introduction
Industrial gas turbines are crucial components in modern energy 
systems, playing a central role in electricity generation, mechanical 
energy provision, and various industrial processes. As global 
energy demand rises and the pressure to develop more environmen­
tally friendly and efficient energy solutions increases, industrial gas 
turbines are poised for significant transformation.
This study explores the current developments and future trends 
shaping the future of industrial gas turbines. The significance of 
industrial gas turbines extends beyond their immediate applications 
in power generation. Their efficiency, fuel flexibility, and ability to 
provide a stable power and heat supply make them indispensable in 
industrial settings, such as chemical processing, oil and gas (O&G) 
production, and district heating. Moreover, their aforementioned 
adaptability to various fuel sources, including natural gas, hydro­
gen (H2), and synthetic fuels, aligns them with ongoing efforts 
toward decarbonization and energy diversification.
Over the past century, gas turbines have evolved significantly, 
with advancements in materials science, aerodynamics, cooling 
techniques, and combustion technologies playing a pivotal role in 
enhancing their performance. This article provides a historical 
overview of these developments, highlighting key milestones that 
have shaped modern industrial gas turbines [1].
In addition to historical progress, technological advances con­
tinue to drive the efficiency and reliability of industrial gas turbines. 
These advancements include cycle improvements, such as the incor­
poration of recuperators and reheat cycles, which enhance thermal 
efficiency. Additionally, innovations in compressor and turbine 
design, including high-pressure ratios and optimized blade geome­
tries, contribute to improved performance [2]. The role of additive 
manufacturing (AM) in producing intricate cooling structures and 
lightweight components is also explored as a transformative force 
in gas turbine engineering [3,4].
One of the most pressing challenges in industrial gas turbine 
development is achieving greater fuel flexibility. As the energy 
sector moves toward a more sustainable future, the ability to 
operate on alternative fuels, such as H2 and biofuels, becomes 
increasingly important. This analysis examines recent progress in 
combustion technology that enables lower emissions and higher 
efficiency when using these alternative fuels [5].
The digitalization of industrial gas turbines has also emerged as a 
major trend, revolutionizing the way these machines are operated 
and maintained. Industry 4.0 concepts, including predictive main­
tenance [6–11] and digital twin technology [12–15], are reshaping 
maintenance strategies and enabling real-time performance optimi­
zation. By leveraging advanced data analytics and machine learn­
ing, operators can anticipate potential failures, reduce downtime, 
and optimize energy output.

Furthermore, the integration of industrial gas turbines with 
renewable energy sources is gaining traction as a means to 
enhance grid stability and increase overall energy system resil­
ience. Hybrid power plants, which combine gas turbines with 
renewable sources such as wind or solar, offer a promising 
approach to balancing intermittent generation and ensuring reliable 
power delivery [16,17]. Additionally, combined heat and power 
(CHP) systems continue to be a key application for industrial gas 
turbines, maximizing overall energy utilization by capturing and 
repurposing waste heat [18,19].
From a global perspective, industrial gas turbines play a vital 
role in meeting energy demand across diverse markets. This 
research explores regional trends in gas turbine deployment, 
including the impact of policy frameworks, market dynamics, 
and economic considerations on their adoption. The role of gas tur­
bines in emerging economies, where demand for flexible and reli­
able energy solutions is growing rapidly, is also discussed [20–23].
Looking ahead, the future of industrial gas turbines will be 
shaped by both technological and market-driven factors. Key chal­
lenges include further reducing emissions, improving efficiency, 
and ensuring long-term fuel availability. The ongoing development 
of advanced materials, hybrid energy systems, and digital control 
technologies will play a crucial role in addressing these challenges 
[20].
2 Historical Development and Significance of 
Industrial Gas Turbines
The inception of gas turbine technology can be traced back to the 
late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. The earliest concep­
tualizations of the gas turbine engine emerged from the pioneering 
works of John Barber, who patented a design for a “Gas Turbine” 
in 1791. Barber’s design, although primitive and not practical for 
power generation, laid the groundwork for future developments 
by outlining the basic principles of the gas turbine cycle [24].
In the late nineteenth century, engineers like Karl de Laval, Sir 
Charles Parsons, and George Brayton contributed significantly to 
the evolution of turbine technology. De Laval and Parsons, 
known for their development of the steam turbine, indirectly influ­
enced gas turbine technology by demonstrating the potential of 
turbine-driven machinery. Meanwhile, Brayton’s work on the 
Brayton cycle, a thermodynamic cycle that underpins gas turbine 
operation, provided a theoretical foundation that would later be 
realized in practical applications [25,26].
The early twentieth century saw significant advancements as the 
principles of gas turbines began to be translated into workable 
engines. In 1903, Norwegian engineer Ægidius Elling successfully 
built and operated the first gas turbine capable of producing more 
power than it consumed, albeit with limited efficiency. His work 
demonstrated the feasibility of gas turbines for practical use, 
marking a crucial milestone in their development [27]. Probably 
due to the fact that there was no real industrial basis in Norway 
at that time, his work went largely unnoticed, but that of the 
Armengauds in 1905 did attract attention (even though their gas 
turbine did not provide a net power output). This was also reflected 
in the literature of the time: Stodola’s seminal work on turboma­
chinery (1905) was already entitled steam and gas turbines [28]. 
The main problem in these early years was manufacturing a com­
pressor with a high enough efficiency, something that would come 
back and haunt gas turbine engineers in the decades to come. 
During the 1930s and 1940s, the pursuit of jet propulsion for air­
craft provided a substantial impetus for gas turbine development. 
Frank Whittle in the United Kingdom and Hans Joachim Pabst 
von Ohain in Germany independently developed the first opera­
tional jet engines, proving the viability of gas turbines for high- 
speed aircraft propulsion [29]. The first jet engine to enter series 
production was the Jumo 004 in 1944. The success of these early 
jet engines highlighted the potential of gas turbines for other appli­
cations, including power generation and industrial use [30].
The post-World War II era saw a rapid expansion in the applica­
tion of gas turbine technology beyond aviation. The need for effi­
cient and reliable power generation solutions spurred innovation in 
industrial gas turbines. Advances in materials science, particularly 
the development of high-temperature alloys, enabled gas turbines 
to operate at higher temperatures and pressures, significantly 
improving their efficiency and power output [31].
In the 1950s and 1960s, heavy-duty gas turbines began to be 
widely adopted for electricity generation and mechanical drive 
applications. Companies like General Electric (GE), Siemens, 
BBC, and Westinghouse played pivotal roles in the commercializa­
tion and widespread adoption of gas turbines. These turbines were 
initially used as peaking power plants due to their ability to start up 
quickly and respond to demand fluctuations. However, as technol­
ogy progressed, they increasingly became a staple for base-load 
power generation and combined cycle plants [1,32]. At the same 
time, small industrial gas turbines were developed, mainly based 
on aero-engine designs, for example, by Solar Turbines. Other 
companies manufacturing industrial turbines at the time were 
Snecma, Sulzer, and GEC Alstom. Kawasaki Heavy Industries 
(KHI) entered the market in the 1970s.
The development of combined cycle technology in the latter half 
of the twentieth century marked a significant evolution in gas 
turbine applications [33]. Combined cycle power plants (CCPPs) 
integrate gas turbines with steam turbines to utilize the waste 
heat from the gas turbine exhaust to generate steam, which in 
turn is used to operate a steam turbine and thus generate additional 
electricity. This integration greatly enhanced the overall efficiency 
of power plants, often achieving efficiencies exceeding 60% 
[25,26]. Combined cycle technology has become the dominant 
configuration for new large power plants due to its superior effi­
ciency and lower emissions compared to conventional fossil fuel 
power plants. The ability to quickly ramp up and down also 
makes these plants ideal for complementing intermittent renewable 
energy sources like wind and solar power.
The applications for industrial gas turbines are mostly heat- 
driven, i.e., their purpose is to generate heat or cooling as well as 
electricity, the focus being on the thermal aspect. Consequently, 
their efficiency is measured as a combination of both, and as 
such, it can exceed 90% [34]. Industrial gas turbines can be used 
to generate hot water, steam, cooling, or a combination of two or 
even all three forms of heat.
In recent decades, advancements in computational fluid dynam­
ics (CFD), materials engineering, and control systems have further 
propelled the performance and reliability of industrial gas turbines. 
Modern gas turbines are equipped with sophisticated control 
systems that optimize performance in real-time, reducing emis­
sions and increasing fuel efficiency. The use of advanced ceramics 
and thermal barrier coatings allows turbines to operate at unprece­
dented temperatures, pushing the boundaries of efficiency even 
further [35,36].
The push toward decarbonization and sustainability is shaping 
the future trajectory of industrial gas turbines. Research is 
ongoing into H2 combustion and carbon capture technologies, 
which hold the potential to significantly reduce the carbon footprint 
of gas turbine power generation. H2, in particular, is seen as a 
promising fuel due to its zero-emission combustion profile. Com­
panies are actively developing gas turbines capable of operating 
on 100% H2 or H2–natural gas blends, aiming to make them 
viable solutions for a low-carbon energy future [37–39].

3.1 Cycle Considerations and Advances in Compressor 
Design. This article focuses on stationary gas turbines with a 
power output up to 50 MW. Unlike large-scale heavy-duty gas tur­
bines that are nearly exclusively installed as prime movers in 
large-scale CCPP gas turbines in the considered range are mostly 
used in heat-driven CHP plants, or may provide residual services 
as flexible peakers in simple cycle operation [21]. In the considered 
power range, they may compete with gas engines in CoGen-plants 
[40]. A significant number of industrial gas turbines are moreover 
used as mechanical drives for compressors and pumps in, e.g., oil 
and gas industries [22]. This usage is not in focus here, and it is safe 
to say that technological trends identified in relation to the core 
engine are applied regardless of the usage of the engine.
Consequently, the design principles of small and medium-sized 
gas turbines differ from those of large-scale ones, in so far as 
greater flexibility with regard to fuels and a wide operating range 
are required. In addition to this, small and medium plants are 
offered to a wider range of customers stretching from local utilities, 
private companies, and the service sector to guarantee a decentral­
ized supply of heat and electricity in, e.g., hospitals or large 
housing and office districts. Therefore, the design must accommo­
date requirements of both low capital expenditures and operational 
expenditures, and meet the requirements of a robust design to guar­
antee a high level of availability at moderate maintenance efforts.
For mechanical drive applications, the preferred configuration is 
a two-shaft design, in which the power turbine is mounted as a 
separate unit from the gas generator. This setup allows flexible 
operation of both shafts, as the speed of the power turbine is decou­
pled from the compressor speed. Some original equipment manu­
facturers (OEMs) offer both single-shaft and two-shaft gas 
turbines of the same series where the gas generator is built of iden­
tical compressor and combustor sections with slight modifications 
in the high-pressure turbine stages. For the two-shaft design, the 
last turbine stages of the single-shaft engines are replaced by 
freely spinning power turbines that are only aerodynamically 
coupled with the gas generator [41,42].
The efficiency of the ideal Brayton Cycle with isentropic com­
pression, expansion, and isobaric combustion is determined 
solely by the pressure ratio. In practice, this is also coupled with 
the turbine inlet temperature to maximize the specific work of 
the gas turbine at the target value of the turbine exit temperature 
that determines the performance of the bottoming cycle.
Figure 1 provides an overview of the trends for industrial gas tur­
bines offered by the major gas turbine producers worldwide [22]. 
Overall, the pressure ratio increases with the shaft power and 
ranges between 15 and 20 for gas turbines under 20 MW, and 
reaches 30 for larger shaft powers. In addition to the traditional 
industrial gas turbines, a second group of gas turbines is added to 
the graph that is derived directly from aero-engine core engines 
with only slight modifications, which are referred to as aeroderiva­

thermal efficiencies and are only excelled by industrial gas turbines

To quantify the technology level of industrial gas turbines, the 
efficiency is plotted versus the cycle pressure ratio, and the slope 
is compared to the overall potential of the ideal Brayton cycle 
(Fig. 2). Here, a clear tendency can be observed across the wide 
range of pressure ratios in existing gas turbines. In fact, the trend 
closely follows a graph that approximately aligns with the line 
defined by 70% of the ideal pressure ratio of the Brayton cycle. 
This indicates that technological barriers common to all gas 
turbine producers are clearly identified. Overcoming these barriers 
requires considerable effort to achieve technological advance­
ments, which will be outlined in this contribution. Similarly, aero­
derivative engines achieve high efficiency levels with designs 
derived from aero-engine technologies of the late 1980s and 
1990s [43].
If gas turbines are used in CCPP and CHP plants, large values of 
the turbine outlet temperature are required to achieve the optimum 
level of fuel exploitation.
Figure 3 shows the importance of the exhaust gas enthalpy for 
CHP efficiency: some engines that have a high electrical efficiency 
(e.g., engine 2) have a fairly low overall CHP efficiency because 
the high electrical efficiency entails low exhaust gas temperatures 
(Fig. 4). On the other hand, an engine with a low electrical effi­
ciency (e.g., Engine 6), may have a very high overall efficiency. 
This is because a low electrical (thermodynamic) efficiency 
causes a high exhaust gas temperature [41,42].
If turbine exit temperatures above 500 °C are considered suitable 
for exhaust heat recovery plants, it becomes evident that most aero­
derivative turbines are less efficient compared to industrial gas tur­
bines optimized for combined applications (Fig. 5). The reason is 
that aeroderivatives combine high-pressure ratios with moderately 
high turbine inlet temperatures (TIT), leading to low turbine outlet 
temperatures. Nevertheless, aeroderivatives bear advantages in 
being more compact and having less weight, which are important 
for various installations, for example, mobile electricity units 
[22]. Smaller gas turbines have a higher cost per megawatt than 
larger engines because scaling allows OEMs to reduce manufactur­
ing costs (per kilowatt) as unit power ratings and physical size 
increase [44]. Hence, it is more difficult to incorporate advanced 
cooling and sealing technologies in small engines. On the other 
hand, smaller gas turbines perform well because the limited

Gas turbine plants embedded in energy systems dominated by 
volatile renewable energy sources need to operate more flexibly 
with respect to load changes and fuel variability. Consequently, 
compressors must be designed to ensure safe operation over a 
wider range and to be more resistant to flow separation [2]. The sta­
bility limits can be extended by application of casing treatment 
(CT). However, optimization of CT is linked to the individual 
design of the airfoils and cannot be generalized [48,49]. Moreover, 
because fouling may diminish the effect of CT after a relatively 
short operating time, it is currently not considered in industrial 
gas turbines.
In general, gas turbines in the considered range may face harsh 
operational and environmental conditions, in particular at chemical 
plants or in offshore installations of the oil and gas industry [50]. 
Though the inlet of gas turbines is protected by elaborate filter car­
tridge systems [51], the front stages of the compressor may be 
exposed to foreign object damage (FOD) [52] due to polluted 
intake air, causing either erosion or damage on the blade surfaces 
that would affect the compressor performance and stability [53]. 
Frequent washing of the compressor flow path is a common 
precaution against compressor fouling to restore the performance 
[54]. Icing is a phenomenon not only restricted to aero-engine com­
pressors but also occurring in the front stages of industrial com­
pressors under certain climate conditions, and may lead to FOD 
in the front stages. Its mechanism needs to be understood, and 
anti-ice measures are required, e.g., discussed by Assady and 
Sammak [55].
The development of active and passive surge control systems has 
gained increased attention and covers a wide variety of solutions. 
For details, see the review articles by Alsuwian et al. [56] and Blan­
chini and Giannattasio [57].
3.2 Enhanced Turbine Design. The following subsections 
examine the multifaceted advancements in turbine technology. 
First, key material innovations, including cutting-edge manufactur­
ing techniques, are explored. Subsequently, emphasis is placed on 
aerodynamic refinements and enhanced thermal management strat­
egies to optimize turbine performance.
3.2.1 Materials Science. New alloys and coatings that with­
stand higher temperatures contribute significantly to efficiency 
improvements. Examples include nickel-based superalloys [58] 
and ceramic matrix composites (CMCs), which remain stable 
under extreme temperatures while maintaining high strength.
Even though CMCs can withstand very high temperatures, their 
geometric adaptability is fundamentally limited. This means it is 
very difficult and often impossible to integrate sophisticated 
cooling schemes into CMC parts, offsetting much of their high- 
temperature capabilities and limiting their use to geometrically 
simple components. Some efforts have been made to investigate 
their suitability for film cooling [59].
The evolution of superalloys now seems to be geared toward 
novel production methods, i.e., AM and the development of 
alloys suitable for AM. Numerous AM methods exist, but by far 
the most important for gas turbines are direct energy deposition 
and laser powder bed fusion, which is also called selective laser 
melting (SLM). The former is used especially for repair, as it 
does not rely on a powder bed. This is of relevance for service 
parts, as repair is generally cheaper and quicker (for legacy 
engines). The relative simplicity of the cooling schemes makes 
the economic argument more difficult, though. The latter is primar­
ily used to manufacture new, high-temperature components [3]. 
AM via SLM essentially consists of numerous very thin welds, 
making the utilization of non-weldable materials (i.e., most nickel- 
based alloys) not suitable for AM. Although cobalt-based materials 
like MAR-M509 are suitable, their prohibitive costs have seen

3.2.2 Aerodynamic Optimization. Continuous improvements 
in turbine blade design optimize airflow and minimize losses. 
CFD and other advanced simulation methods enable engineers to 
develop designs that ensure maximum energy conversion [69]. 
As turbines are exposed to very high gas temperatures, the 
design of gas turbine blades and vanes demands for highly interdis­
ciplinary design systems covering thermal and structural design 
considerations. To accommodate internal flow paths for the 
coolant inside the airfoils, the leading edge thickness needs to be 
increased to allow for the ejection of the cooling flow (as shower- 
head or film-cooling) into the primary flow path, causing additional 
losses and exit flow angle deviations at transonic outlet Mach 
numbers (base-pressure losses, see Ref. [70]). Unlike flows in 
axial compressors, turbine flow fields are more strongly dominated 
by the occurrence of secondary flows due to high turning and stage 
pressure ratios that strongly interact with heat transfer properties 
along the side walls [71]. Moreover, secondary flow phenomena 
must be thoroughly understood, as they not only contribute to addi­
tional aerodynamic losses but also influence the local development 
of the cooling film along the wetted surfaces of film-cooled blades 
and vanes [72]. To reduce secondary losses, contoured endwalls— 
either axisymmetric or non-axisymmetric—are considered. Nor­
mally, this is achieved through an axisymmetric reduction of the 
span between the leading edge and trailing edge. This approach 
decreases flow velocity in the high-turning upstream part of the 
vane, thus mitigating the emergence of passage crossflow. Addi­
tionally, the passage contraction induces an acceleration that 
thins the endwall boundary layer in the downstream region, 
further reducing secondary flow. Non-axisymmetric contouring 
stretches from the suction to the pressure side of the profiles and 
mainly controls the secondary flow pattern locally, and the optimi­
zation of the endwall shape has been an essential matter of research 
[73–75]. Moreover, its manufacturing is more elaborate and expen­
sive, and, therefore, due to economic restrictions not the desired 
option for small and medium size industrial gas turbines. Instead, 
more focus is on profile shaping itself to reduce secondary flows 
by influencing the loading distribution in the streamwise direction 
to improve both aerodynamic performance and combustor–stator 
interaction [76–78].
With regard to the stage matching, 3D multistage-CFD has 
become a key tool since Denton first published a multistage 
Euler solver for turbomachinery flows [79]. With the development 
of powerful computer capacities, unsteady CFD computations have 
become the standard for the design phase of turbines. Fully 3D 
transient flow-field analysis has become feasible for detailed opti­
mization at modern design departments, even for multi-stage turbo­
machinery [80]. Figure 11 shows the example of the interaction 
between the combustor and a three-stage turbine for a 
10 MW-class gas turbine computed with a customized unsteady 
Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (URANS) solver based on 
Denton’s TBLOCK. With six combustor cans in total, a 60 deg 
sector of the turbine with adjusted blade and vane counts was 
modeled to get insight into the propagation of the hot streak 
leaving one of the combustors. Based on these results, the temper­
ature load on the stator vanes can be identified, and the cooling 
schemes of each stator vane could be independently adjusted to 
reduce overall coolant consumption. Furthermore, the optimum 
location of the turbine exhaust diffuser struts and temperature 
probes at the turbine exit plane that are used for power control of 
the turbine could be projected (Fig. 12).

3.2.3 Cooling Systems. Increasing the TIT in line with the 
pressure ratio increases the thermodynamic efficiency toward the 
optimum value of the ideal Brayton cycle, which is determined 
by the pressure ratio as discussed in Sec. 3.1. Temperatures have 
long exceeded those at which metallic alloys can operate over 
long periods of time. Therefore, hot gas path components need to 
be made of better materials, cooled or shielded from the hot gas. 
The coolant represents a thermodynamic loss and is only used to 
cool components that would otherwise overheat. Therefore, reduc­
ing the amount of coolant used by improving materials is essential.
Advances in turbine blade cooling enable higher operating tem­
peratures without compromising structural integrity. Modern 
cooling systems use internal channels and advanced coatings to 
efficiently cool the blades, extending their lifespan [67]. Currently, 
the amount of coolant used is between 20% and 33% for modern 
stationary gas turbines. Naturally, small industrial gas turbines 
and, above all, older models with significantly lower turbine inlet 
temperatures tend toward the lower end of this span. While there 
is a need to develop new cooling strategies with new engine 
designs, the requirements for legacy engines should not be under­
estimated either. As long as a new cooling design for such an 
engine does not necessitate an overhaul of the SAS, reducing the 
coolant in the blades and vanes can provide both thermodynamic 
and financial benefits.
The classical cooling schemes consist of impingement cooling 
[85,86] and pin-fin cooling [87,88] for the vanes, and rib-turbulated 
cooling [89–91] and pin-fin cooling for the blades. Both blades and 
vanes in the first stages normally feature film cooling [92,93], in 
addition to other cooling techniques [89]. This setup has not funda­
mentally changed over the last decades (see Ref. [94] for an over­
view of a typical cooling setup) and, consequently, holds limited 
potential for improvement, i.e., a reduction of component temper­
atures. Furthermore, the much smaller dimensions of industrial gas 
turbines make it more difficult to incorporate cooling designs from 
large engines into the blades and vanes at an acceptable cost and 
with an acceptable precision. Therefore, the design choices for 
small industrial gas turbines may differ from those for larger gas 
turbines. Aero-engines suffer less from this problem due to the sig­
nificantly higher number of engines built and the higher acceptable 
costs per engine.
Over the last few years, there has been some interest in using 
“phantom cooling,” which can be used equally well for large and 
small gas turbines. This means the effect of purge and leakage 
flows on the surface temperature of components is quantified, 
and the active cooling is reduced accordingly [95–99]. As can be 
seen in Fig. 15, Ref. [95], the purge air emitted from a slot 
between the combustion chamber and the nozzle guide vane, 
which is necessary for the assembly of the engine, contributes sig­
nificantly to the cooling of the endwalls. This effect is markedly 
different for old and new aerodynamic stator designs.
As Fig. 16 @ Ref. [100] shows, the designs of the slot or cooling 
holes have a significant impact on the cooling distribution as well 
as on the secondary flow. Although such modifications will cer­
tainly help to reduce coolant consumption in legacy engines, 
they will probably not be enough to significantly impact the 
design of new engines. For this reason, some novel concepts 
have garnered attention recently, all of which are by and large, 
enabled by AM [101].

and transition ducts, and has seen some limited use in other hot 
gas sections [102]. Some aspects of effusion cooling have gained

fuel flexibility: attaining lean premixed combustion across a range 
of H2 content from 0% to 100% is difficult, and NH3 and CH4O 
(which is liquid by nature) require entirely different burners. The 
resulting differences in exhaust gas compositions, as well as the 
vastly different turbine outlet temperature profiles, will have an 
undefined yet significant impact on the cooling design.
The impact of both artificial intelligence (AI) and machine-based 
learning on cooling design needs to be taken into account as well. 
Model-based learning was used for the adaptation of turbulence 
models for coolant–mainstream interaction, but the significant 
impact of the learning dataset precluded its use [107–110]. Other 
uses include the generation of load profiles for combustion 
chamber liners that can be equally well used for the evaluation 
of airfoils. Some advances were made using probabilistic 
methods in the design of SAS [111–113], and it remains to be 
seen how this could be extended to cooling designs. All of these 
advances do not only enable a more detailed assessment of the 
thermal load during steady-state operation (design point), but espe­
cially a better assessment of the impacts of part load operation and 
transients on the lifetime of the components.

to spatial constraints, which increase design complexity, and to 
uncertainties regarding future operational requirements and associ­

3.3 Enhanced Fuel Flexibility. Until very recently, the 
design principles of gas turbine combustors have been guided by 
the reduction of avoidable emissions inherent with the combustion 
of natural gas to meet legal requirements and to protect human 
health. All major gas turbine manufacturers have developed dry 
low-emission (DLE) technologies to cut down emissions of nitro­
gen oxide (NOX) and carbon monoxide without curtailing the 
power output and efficiency of their gas turbines. As they are not 
in the main focus of this contribution, the reader is referred to 
the overviews in Refs. [5,114].
Apart from these advancements, fuel flexibility has become a 
driving factor in combustor development [115]. In particular, 
industrial gas turbines have traditionally been expected to handle 
a wide range of fuels because of their installation as mechanical 
drives in upstream and midstream oil and gas applications with 
very changeable qualities of the natural gas. Liquefied petroleum 
gases need to be considered in certain industrial power generation 
plants to use waste gases of technical processes (e.g., blast furnace 
gas). Moreover, industrial gas turbines of utilities in remote areas 
require the capability to combust liquid fuels in certain island appli­
cations. Another example is dual-fuel flexibility to allow both the 
burning of gaseous and liquid fuels in the combustor.
In the future, this flexibility will be much more important. When 
it comes to the avoidance of greenhouse gases, a fuel shift from 
fossil to green fuels, i.e., sustainable fuels derived from renew­
ables, is the only promising way forward. Currently, (green) H2 
is the most prominent solution for power generation combined 
with inter-seasonal renewable energy storage of H2 in liquid or 
gaseous form and its derivatives, such as CH4O, NH3, methane 
(CH4), and synthetic fuels [116]. Biofuels are a complementary 
option and can currently be manufactured at a high, almost sulfur- 
free quality [117], but cannot cover the same (large) scale. Biofuels 
might be more suitable to be use in the mobility sector.
Gas engines and gas turbines can be converted from natural gas 
to H2 operation [20]. Blending H2 with natural gas and the conver­
sion of gas turbines have been investigated and already demon­
strated up to 100% H2 [118]. The competitiveness of actual 
H2-fueled gas turbines is driven by emissions restrictions when 
using different mixtures of H2, natural gas, and biomethane. The 
highest competitiveness of H2-fueled gas turbines (together with 
H2 storage) is found in electricity systems with high shares of 
wind power [119], although their overall economic feasibility 
remains a matter of some debate [120]. When 100% H2 is being 
used as fuel, the thermal efficiency of gas turbines is similar to 
that when using carbon-containing fuels.
Research is on the way to develop low NOx combustors for a 
blend of natural gas with H2 up to 100% by all major OEMs 
(e.g., Refs. [121–125]). Retrofitting of existing gas turbine plants 
without violating emission restrictions in NOx is ready for the 
market as long as the amount of H2 is less than 60% (by 
volume). For larger shares of H2, wet low-emission combustors 
with injection of steam are successful options that are mainly 
used in aeroderivative gas turbines. However, the adjustment of 
dry low NOx combustion chambers is at the foreground of the 
development. To avoid increased NOx emissions and flashback 
inherent with the high flame speed of H2-dominated fuel blends, 
fundamental changes in the combustion chamber need to be 
devised. The application of AM techniques allows for shaping 
complex multi-nozzle structures to encounter these problems and 
is in a mature development state by all major OEMs [126]. First 
full-scale installations have been demonstrated in certain publicly

The integration of NH3 into gas turbines not only supports the 
decarbonization of the energy sector but also enhances energy 
storage and transportation capabilities [129]. Because of the low 
energy content per volume of H2 as well as the problems storing 
and transporting it, NH3 is favored as an energy carrier. All neces­
sary technologies for the NH3 value chain are known, as it is 
already the largest internationally traded chemical commodity. 
Combustion (unlike the use in fuel cell vehicles) does not require 
absolute purification of the fuel.
Although H2 is in the focus of development in Europe, a signifi­
cant amount of research is being conducted on NH3 combustion in 
Japan [130]. The main drawbacks of NH3 combustion are its low 
energy content (poor combustibility) and the production of NOx 
as a byproduct. Furthermore, the combustion volume has to 
increase dramatically. The formation of NOx requires some form 
of SCR, but, naturally, the NH3 needed is readily available. 
These mixtures require approximately the same amount of 
coolant as pure hydrogen.
Applications involving 100% NH3 gas combustion have been 
explored [131,132], but challenges include difficulties in igniting 
NH3 gas and the need for a more easily ignitable fuel during 
startup. As was recognized very early, this can be mitigated 
by cracking the NH3 nitrogen and H2 and burning a mixture of 
70–90% NH3 and 10–30% H2 [133,134]. A demonstrator for an 
NH3/natural gas co-fired gas turbine was shown [135], revealing 
that NH3 combustion generates significant quantities of nitrogen 
oxides through both thermal mechanisms and from the fuel itself. 
These emissions, along with unburned NH3, need to be mitigated. 
Blending NH3 with other fuels can improve emissions performance 
[136].
Park et al. examined whether co-firing H2 and NH3 with natural 
gas in an industrial gas turbine could enhance the environmental 
performance of existing combined cycle power plants [137]. 
Nose et al. [138] described the research and future plans for an 
NH3 cracking gas turbine system, which burns H2 decomposed 
from NH3 using the exhaust heat of the gas turbine. They proposed 
two configurations: a co-firing system that burns mixtures of 
natural gas and cracked NH3 gas, and a mono-firing system that 
burns only cracked NH3 gas [138]. Nitrogen as ballast in the com­
bustion gas rather simplifies the combustor design [139].
A burner capable of burning both kerosene and NH3 was devel­
oped by Iki [140]. Initial operation with kerosene, followed by the 
gradual addition of NH3—as already stated—resulted in high NOx 
concentrations in the exhaust gases, which could be significantly 
reduced through selective catalytic reduction. Some other areas 
may be relevant, but have received much less attention. Compared 
to natural gas combustion, which involves various hydrocarbons, 
CH4–air gas turbines use pure CH4, resulting in cleaner and more 
efficient combustion [141]. For chemically recuperated gas tur­
bines (CRGT) based on CH4 steam reforming, the impact of 
various operating parameters on the efficiency of the CRGT was 
determined [142,143].
3.4 Digitalization and Industry 4.0. Digitalization is revolu­
tionizing the maintenance and operation of industrial gas turbines. 
The use of sensors, data analytics, and machine learning allows for 
real-time monitoring and analysis of operational data.
3.4.1 Predictive Maintenance. In the realm of gas turbines, 
predictive maintenance has emerged as a cornerstone strategy 
aimed at enhancing operational efficiency and minimizing down­
time. By harnessing advanced data analytics and machine learning 
techniques, it enables proactive identification of potential faults 
[144–146] and deterioration, thereby optimizing maintenance 
schedules and extending the lifespan of critical components. This 
approach not only improves reliability, but also reduces operational 
costs, making it indispensable in modern turbine management 
practices [52].
Machine learning algorithms are revolutionizing maintenance 
strategies by analyzing historical and real-time data to identify 
anomalies and recommend preventive actions, minimizing the 
risk of equipment failures [10]. Traditional maintenance 
approaches in the oil and gas industry often prove inefficient, as 
highlighted by Jambol et al. [147], who advocate for AI-driven pre­
dictive maintenance as a transformative solution. This approach 
leverages machine learning to anticipate maintenance needs, sig­
nificantly reducing downtime, cutting costs, and improving equip­
ment reliability. While the benefits of AI adoption are substantial, 
the necessary strategies and associated challenges also need to be 
examined.
To address the intricate problem of gas turbine fault diagnosis, a 
temporal convolutional network–autoencoder model has been 
developed. This innovative model combines the strengths of tem­
poral convolutional networks and multi-head attention mechanisms 
to accurately detect anomalies in vibration data, offering a high 
level of precision [148].
To further enhance gas turbine performance and reliability, 
advanced models have been introduced for the online evaluation 
of damage to components subjected to complex cyclic loads. 
These models employ sophisticated cycle counting techniques 
and incorporate variations in temperature and stress, enabling 
more accurate assessments of fatigue damage [149,150]. Addition­
ally, a probabilistic methodology has been applied to estimate the 
low-cycle fatigue life of a gas turbine compressor vane carrier 
under varying operating conditions. This approach integrates sen­
sitivity analysis and surrogate modeling to refine life predictions 
and adapt maintenance planning [151].
3.4.2 Digital Twins and Remote Operation. Real-time data 
enable precise control and optimization of turbine operation, 
leading to improved efficiency and reliability. Digital twins, 
which are virtual representations of physical turbines, allow oper­
ators to simulate various scenarios and adjust operation accord­
ingly [12,152].
Cruz-Manzo et al. developed a sensor fault diagnostic system 
specifically for industrial gas turbines. Integrated with a real-time 
digital twin, this system uses redundancy from a reference 
engine model to provide accurate estimates of engine variables, 
thereby enhancing turbine reliability and availability by proac­
tively addressing sensor failures [153].
Apostolidis et al. explored data-driven strategies such as 
condition-based and predictive maintenance in aviation, aiming 
to reduce maintenance costs and environmental impact by diagnos­
ing and forecasting aircraft system conditions. Using the general­
ized additive model, their research accurately predicts the 
evolution of exhaust gas temperature based on NASA’s synthetic 
datasets. This highlights the interpretability of the model and 
underscores the importance of reliable data for certifiable AI appli­
cations in gas turbine diagnostics [154].
Furthermore, advances in remote monitoring technology now 
enable centralized control of industrial gas turbines, minimizing 
the need for on-site personnel and facilitating rapid responses to 
operational issues [150,155]. For the effect of start-up and shut- 
down cycles on transient temperature loading of rotor and stator 
of a small industrial gas turbine, a transient solver for single-shaft 
and twin-shaft gas turbines was applied [156]. Compressor and air- 
cooled turbine characteristics were computed with a through-flow 
solver and coupled by a simplified combustion model. The 
model includes gas control schemes for the transient operation of 
the gas turbine and a calibrated transient tip clearance model. It 
allows the creation of a database for various geometry modifica­
tions of the components to trace back deviations from expected 
engine behavior during real-time operation.
The German national project TurboGruen [157] introduced 
groundbreaking dynamic reduced order models (ROMs) for real- 
time prediction and management of critical industrial components.

3.5 Integration of Renewable Energy. Industrial gas tur­
bines play a crucial role in integrating renewable energy into the 
power grid. They provide the necessary flexibility to balance the 
intermittent power production from renewable sources.
3.5.1 Combined Heat and Power. Gas turbines of the consid­
ered power range are commonly used in CHP plants to generate 
both electricity and heat. By integrating renewable energy 
sources such as biogas [158], these systems can further enhance 
efficiency while reducing their environmental impact [18,19]. 
The European Union (EU) has outlined its strategy for advancing 
gas turbine and CHP technologies, with a focus on improving per­
formance in the coming years [159]. Key goals of the early 2000s 
were achieving combined cycle efficiencies above 60%, industrial 
gas turbine system efficiencies of at least 50%, and small gas 
turbine efficiencies above 35%. Additionally, there was an empha­
sis on designing systems capable of using fuels with less than 25% 
of the heating value of natural gas. For CHP systems specifically, 
the primary objectives are to lower overall costs and develop 
biomass-fired systems with capacities greater than 40 kW [160].
Bespalov et al. have conducted research on assessing the life cycle 
of steam and gas turbine CHP plants, highlighting the potential 
for improving energy efficiency and minimizing environmental 
impacts [161]. Furthermore, variations in heat and power integration 
have been demonstrated between syngas-fueled and H2-fueled CHP 
systems [162]. CH4 steam reforming offers a method for recovering 
excess heat from steam in the form of syngas, although it reduces the 
electrical output of the CHP [163].
Bottoming organic Rankine cycles in CHP systems are particu­
larly effective in capturing additional energy from low-temperature 
waste heat sources that would otherwise go unused, thus enhancing 
the overall efficiency and sustainability of the energy conversion 
[164,165] employing organic fluids like hydrofluorocarbons, fluo­
rocarbons, and hydrocarbons as the working medium. Addition­
ally, studies have examined the use of a transcritical carbon 
dioxide cycle to recover waste heat from gas turbines, with the 
goal of simultaneously producing electricity and cooling by utiliz­
ing carbon dioxide as the primary working fluid [166]. The feasi­
bility of applying a pre-combustion carbon capture and storage 
(CCS) concept in CHP systems has also been investigated, offering 
another pathway toward reducing emissions and increasing system 
efficiency [167].
3.5.2 Hybrid Power Plants. Combining gas turbines with 
solar or wind power plants can enhance the stability and efficiency 
of the energy system. In hybrid power plants, gas turbines can ramp 
up or down quickly to offset the variability of renewable energy, 
ensuring a consistent power supply [168–170] (Fig. 18).

enhancing the overall efficiency of power systems through the inte­

hybrid system, which combines electricity generation from solid 
oxide fuel cells with the utilization of waste heat and hot exhaust 
gases from gas turbines. This integration not only achieves excep­

producing both electricity and heat. Beyond the thermodynamic 
performance, economic operation modes for the system have 
been extensively analyzed to optimize feasibility and cost- 
effectiveness [176–178]. However, great care must be taken

4 Global Perspectives and Market Trends
Having explored the technological innovations driving the evo­
lution of industrial gas turbines, the focus now shifts to their role 
within the broader global energy landscape. This section examines 
market trends and regional perspectives to provide a comprehen­
sive understanding of how these advances align with worldwide 
energy demands and policies.
4.1 Global Energy Market. The global energy market is 
undergoing significant changes. The transition to renewable 
energy and the increasing electrification of various sectors are 
influencing the demand for industrial gas turbines [22].
According to the International Energy Agency, global additions 
of renewable power capacity reached a record 507 GW in 2023, 
50% more than in 2022. Solar energy accounted for three-quarters 
and wind energy for one-quarter of this increase [21]. This devel­

In 2023, overall gas turbine MW capacity orders decreased by 
12.6%, reflecting the cyclical nature of the market. Despite this 
decline, the total number of units sold increased by 3%, with unit 
orders rising steadily from 389 units in 2019 to 453 units in 
2023. This suggests a halt, if not a reversal, in the downward 
trend of gas turbine sales observed from 2010 to 2019, with a 
shift to smaller units. Gas turbines are expected to have long-term 
potential in the renewable offset and peaking market. From 2019 to 
2023, global gas turbine sales fluctuated between 39,975 MW and 
44,101 MW [21].
Over the next 10 years, USD 137 billion is projected to be spent 
on new turbines and turbine packages, excluding auxiliary systems 
and related equipment. With over 39,000 industrial gas turbines 
currently in operation globally, the overhaul and repair aftermarket 
is projected to be worth more than USD 261 billion. By the end of 
2024, a 17% increase in unit orders in the Electric Power Utility 
sector and a 5% increase in unit orders in the O&G sector are antic­
ipated [21].
The global gas turbine market was estimated in 2023 to be 
valued between USD 10.19 billion and USD 20.79 billion. 
Growth forecasts for the market vary slightly, with the compound 
annual growth rate ranging between 3.6% and 4.0%. By 2030 or 
2032, the market is expected to grow to values between USD 
22.5 billion and USD 29.36 billion [180–183].
While global market forecasts for gas turbines are positive, 
regional variations in policy frameworks significantly affect 
demand. For instance, the European Union supports gas turbine 
upgrades and hydrogen-readiness through subsidies and regulatory 
incentives, whereas in many Asia–Pacific countries the focus 
remains on cost-effective coal-to-gas switching rather than 
large-scale hydrogen adoption. In North America, policies such 
as the Inflation Reduction Act promote low-carbon fuels, yet infra­
structure bottlenecks and uncertainty about carbon pricing slow 
deployment. These differences mean that the global growth projec­
tions may mask substantial regional disparities in investment 
timing and technology adoption.
The leading gas turbine OEMs for MWt orders in 2023 were 
Mitsubishi Power with 15,431 MW (35%), Siemens Energy with 
10,689 MW (24%), and General Electric with 7015 MW (16%) 
(Fig. 20).
As mentioned earlier, aeroderivative gas turbines are aero- 
engines that are modified for industrial use, with about 80% 
interchangeable components between the engines and their deriva­
tives. They offer quick start and shutdown capabilities and can 
cycle frequently without impacting maintenance schedules. From 
2017 to 2023, they held a 4% market share (10,548 MW) in the 
engine power unit (EPU) sector and a 42% market share 
(19,436 MW) in the O&G sector. Over the next 5 years, aeroderi­
vative turbine equipment sales are expected to reach USD 12.5 
billion, with an annual maintenance market valued at USD 3.3 
billion. These turbines are particularly in demand for peaking 
power and backup due to the increasing integration of renewables 
in the energy mix [21].
Heavy frame gas turbines are robust, industrially designed 
machines. They dominated the EPU sector with a 95% market 
share (217,014 MW) and held a 44% market share (20,106 MW) 
in the O&G sector from 2017 to 2023. Over the next 5 years, 
heavy frame turbine equipment sales are projected to reach a 
total of USD 48.6 billion, with an annual maintenance market of 
USD 19.6 billion.
Light industrial gas turbines combine features of both aeroder­
ivatives and heavy frame turbines. They captured 14% of the 
market share and 38% of units (600 units) in the O&G sector 
from 2017 to 2023. Projected sales for the next 5 years are 
USD 4.4 billion, with an annual maintenance market of USD 
652 million [21].
4.2 Global Perspectives on Industrial Gas Turbines. As the 
share of energy produced from renewable resources grows, thermal 
power plants must increasingly shift from providing base-load 
power to offering fluctuating back-up power in order to stabilize 
and regulate the grid. Since they were not originally designed for 
this role, these frequent fluctuations may lead not only to increased 
wear and tear, but more importantly, to reduced efficiency and, 
consequently, higher greenhouse gas emissions per unit of electric­
ity produced. To utilize existing transmission infrastructure, retro­
fitting existing plants and replacing coal-fired plants with newly 
built gas plants is considered an attractive option [184].
Significant load changes can be limited through load leveling, 
i.e., storing power during periods of low demand and delivering 
it during periods of high demand. Due to the increased integration 
of renewable power generation and its impact on the stability of the 
power grid, there is a growing need for long-term storage solutions. 
Battery technologies still face significant challenges, including 
dependence on rare earth elements and other raw materials 
sourced from countries with varying levels of political stability, 
questionable labor conditions, and a high carbon footprint. These 
factors could severely affect the overall CO2 life cycle balance 
when batteries are employed as a storage technology. Therefore, 
chemical storage, i.e., using electrical energy to produce energy 
rich and stable chemical compounds, is widely seen as a promising 
path. Among those conversion routes, production of green H2 via 
electrolysis of water is the most promising [185].

a commitment to align with the trend of driving the transition to 
renewable-gas power generation and to promote the development 
of combustion technologies for green gases in their gas turbines 
[186] (Fig. 21). In their statement of 2020 they committed to:

turbines is the availability of large amounts of H2 and prioritization 
of efforts to increase H2 production from renewable sources, partic­

With the aspired share of 85% renewable power by 2050, an abun­
dance of renewable power generated from on- and offshore wind 
parks will be available for storage and used to produce H2 at an indus­
trial scale. H2 transport lines need to be established beyond existing 
natural gas pipelines, and new compressor stations for H2 must be 
developed because of the thermodynamic properties and high 
Mach numbers of H2, which necessitate new designs [188]. While 
H2 and renewable energy will play an increasingly significant role 
in the future energy mix, natural gas turbines continue to be crucial 
components of power generation, particularly in regions with an 
established gas infrastructure and growing electricity demand.
In North America, the industrial gas turbine market is driven by 
the need for reliable and flexible power generation. This region is 
known for its mature energy infrastructure and a strong emphasis 
on reducing carbon emissions. In 2023, the US added 9132 MW 
of new gas turbine capacity. This includes 7376 MW in combined 
cycle plants, 1756 MW in simple cycle systems, and 142 MW in 
gas-fired reciprocating engines [21]. Due to rising power 
demand, there have been delays in the planned closures of coal 
plants in states such as Kansas, Nebraska, and Oklahoma [23], 
which offers bright opportunities for replacement with gas- 
powered plants.
Natural gas remains the dominant fuel for power generation in 
the US, providing 43% of the energy in 2023, according to the 
IEA. Following natural gas, nuclear energy accounts for 18%, 
coal for 16%, wind for 10%, and solar for 4%. The high usage of 
natural gas is attributed to its abundant and low-cost supply. Com­
bined cycle plants, which offer up to 64% efficiency, represent the 
largest segment for gas-fired plants, providing low-cost base and 
intermediate load power. Simple cycle gas turbines are the most 
flexible, catering to peaking and backup needs for renewable 
energy sources [21].
The electricity consumption in the Asia–Pacific region is 
expected to grow at a high rate, driven by industrialization, increas­
ing energy demand, and the rising need for clean technologies. As 
the region heavily relies on coal, government initiatives to reduce 
emissions are leading to a greater adoption of gas turbines, partic­
ularly in Japan, China, Australia, South Korea, and India. China’s 
14th Five-Year Plan supports this transition with favorable policies 
for new gas power plant projects [189].
In the European Union, policies and national regulations provide 
strong support for renewable energy and low-emission technolo­
gies. Grants, subsidies, and research funding are available for pro­
jects that contribute to energy transition goals. Several projects 
focusing on gas turbine technologies are on the way or planned, 
e.g., the EU-funded projects HYFLEXPOWER [127] and 
Flex4H2 [128], and for the 1.4 GW Magnum power plant (30% 
by volume co-firing) in the Netherlands [190].

Worldwide, around 3500 MW of H2-fired power plant capacity

focus on the use of H2 in combined-cycle or open-cycle gas tur­

Despite the growing number of hydrogen projects, the economic 
viability of hydrogen-fired power remains uncertain. The levelized 
cost of green hydrogen remains two to five times higher than 
natural gas on an energy-equivalent basis, even with carbon 
pricing [191]. Large-scale electrolyzer projects are capital-intensive, 
and their competitiveness depends on further cost reductions in 
renewable electricity and advances in electrolysis efficiency. 
Without sustained policy support, risk-sharing mechanisms, and 
infrastructure for H2 transport and storage, widespread hydrogen 
adoption in gas turbines may lag behind expectations, potentially 
leading to stranded assets or higher system costs.
Currently, H2 use for electricity generation amounts less than 
0.2% in the worldwide electricity generation mix, and H2 and 
NH3 combined may be also limited to 2% of global electricity gen­
eration by 2050 [192]. Whether H2 will become an important back­
bone of flexible power generation to bridge longer periods of dark 
doldrums will heavily depend on the availability and price of H2 
production utilizing excess electricity generated by renewable 
sources. Looking at the EU, even with ambitious ramp-up scenar­
ios, the anticipated need for residual electrical power can only be 
met to a moderate extend (2050: about 100 GW of 345 GW) due 
to high production cost and low round-trip efficiency of less than 
35% with gas turbine-driven plants [118]. The situation is similarly 
challenging in the US [183].
The growing energy demand of data centers creates a new market 
for industrial gas turbines. Their need for very high reliability (Tier 
IV, >99.995% availability) [193] and cleaner, longer-duration 
back-up power is driving a shift away from diesel generators. Gas 
turbines offer high power density, fast start capability, and the 
option to run on natural gas or hydrogen blends, enabling lower 
emissions and future decarbonization. This segment is expected to 
grow particularly in North America, Northern Europe, and Asia– 
Pacific, supported by partnerships between OEMs [194,195], utili­
ties, and data center operators [196–198].
5 Outlook and Technology Perspectives
Concluding the analysis of global perspectives and market 
trends, this section explores the future prospects and challenges 
for industrial gas turbines. It addresses emerging opportunities 
and key obstacles in adapting to evolving energy demands and 
advancing technologies.
More than a decade ago, the development of H2-fired gas tur­
bines was in focus in connection with CCS technologies. 
Oxy-fuel combustion was considered one of the most promising 
options [199]. With fading focus on CCS in power generation 
plants, the development of H2-fired gas turbines has slowed 
down in favor of increase of performance and operational flexibil­
ity as a response to a growing fleet of renewables. Further increase 
of volatile renewables as a countermeasure to greenhouse gas emis­
sions causes a growing demand for large-scale energy storage. 
Among these storage options, power-to-gas is best suited for long- 
term storage of large capacities. The direct combustion of H2, its 
carriers, and H2-enriched gases has garnered increasing attention, 
with gas turbine technologies evolving from their former role as 
bridging technologies to becoming a major driver of the transition 
to a renewable energy system.
To avoid that, new installations will end up as stranded assets in 
a future energy system dominated by renewables, and all OEMs are 
working on solutions to fully replace natural gas with H2 or its 
derivatives. While much of the development effort is naturally 
focused on DLE combustion systems, the fuel switch necessitates 
modifications across all core engine components.
As pointed out in the preceding section, small- to medium-sized 
gas turbines have already been developed for gas mixtures with up 
to 100% H2 for special applications. In some regions, the OEMs’ 
roadmaps are adjusted to incentives provided by strategies on a 
national level. One example is Japan, where a H2 strategy has 
been set up as early as 2017 covering all energy-related sectors, 
including automobility [200]. It states:
“Electricity can be generated through combustion, both as pure hydro­
gen and as a mixture with natural gas or coal. […] Power generation 
from hydrogen combustion is expected to be more cost effective than 
electricity generation by stationary fuel cells due to scalability. […] 
Conversely this means that power generation becomes less advanta­
geous in small-scale distributed power generators. To benefit from 
operation as a large-scale plant, it must also secure sufficient 
volumes of hydrogen fuel.”
In response to the national strategy, Japanese gas turbine manu­
facturers have developed combustion systems capable of handling 
increased H2 content. It has become evident that large volumes of 
H2 will need to be imported, and the world’s first liquefied H2 
carrier completed its demonstration voyage in 2022 [201]. The 
first commercially operated gas turbine with a DLN-combustion 
system capable for 100% H2 by KHI was installed in Kobe, 
Japan, (M1A-17D Series, ∼1 MWel), including a receiver station 
for blue H2 imported from Australia [202]. This concept is based 
on so-called Micro-Mix diffusion flames and complies with statu­
tory NOx emission regulations [203]. Mitsubishi Power Systems 
(MPS) announced that their combustion system of a large-scale 
heavy-duty gas turbine successfully passed a firing test using a 
mixture of LNG and 30% H2 (by volume) [204]. In 2021, Mitsu­
bishi Heavy Industries started the development to enable a 
40 MW-class gas turbine (H-25 Series) for the combustion of 
100% NH3-based fuel. Currently, feasibility studies are being con­
ducted in cooperation with Thailand’s major utility company [205]. 
GE has developed a new combustor, designated DLN 2.6e, for its 
9HA gas turbines, which is reportedly capable of operating on a 
natural gas blend containing approximately 50% H2 (by volume) 
[206]. For the GT26/36-series development activities are ongoing 
at Ansaldo aiming at even higher H2 fuel shares (HORIZON 
EUROPE-Project Flex4H2 [128]). Baker Hughes (BH) reported 
on the delivery of a NovaLT16 gas turbine for a green H2 power 
plant facility in South Australia claiming combustion of 100% 
H2 [206]. Siemens Energy is upgrading the combustion systems 
across its gas turbine fleet and has recently published details on 
the SGT-400 industrial gas turbine [206] as well as the 
HORIZON 2020 project HYFLEXPOWER [127].
In the future, gas turbine-powered CHP plants are expected to 
exhibit promising development and market potential. In the near 
to mid-term, they are likely to replace coal-fired power plants 
and boilers on a large scale to reduce greenhouse gas emissions 
associated with natural gas, thereby supporting the transitional 
phase toward zero-carbon power generation.
For alternative fuels with significantly different heating values, 
adjustments in the matching between turbine and compressor are 
required. To maintain fuel flexibility, the compressor’s operating 
range may need to be expanded, making an increased surge 
margin a key focus of current research. In this context, design fea­
tures from aero-engine compressors—such as casing treatments— 
may also be increasingly adopted in stationary industrial gas 
turbine compressors.
Apart from aerodynamic considerations in the expansion 
turbine, the exhaust gas composition changes and contains more 
H2O instead of CO2. As a consequence, the cooling flow demand 
will rise. The aero-thermal and mechanical layout of the entire 
turbine needs to be revised.
The industry will adapt to the demands of a highly volatile power 
generation market, requiring gas turbines to be flexible both in

6 Conclusion
The aim of this article is to provide a perspective on the future 
role of industrial gas turbines in global energy trends. The effi­
ciency, fuel flexibility, and stability of industrial gas turbines 
make them indispensable for power generation and industrial appli­
cations. Advances in materials, aerodynamics, and combustion 
technologies have significantly improved their performance, 
while digitalization and predictive maintenance enhance opera­
tional efficiency. The shift toward alternative fuels and integration 
with renewable energy sources further solidify their role in a sus­
tainable energy future.
In conclusion, industrial gas turbines will continue to be essen­
tial in the evolving energy landscape. Advancements in efficiency, 
sustainability, and digitalization will ensure their relevance in a 
decarbonized future. Their integration with renewable energy and 
improved fuel flexibility will define their role in providing reliable 
and adaptable power solutions.
