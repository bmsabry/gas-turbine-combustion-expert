Design procedures and a Developed Computer Code for 
Preliminary Single Annular Combustor Design 
B. S. Mohammad1 and S. M. Jeng2 
University of Cincinnati, Cincinnati, OH, 45220 
Gas Turbine Combustor (GTC) design is a challenging and time consuming process. 
Several methodologies for conventional combustor design have been developed. The 
objective of this paper is to provide detailed procedures for the preliminary design of a 
Single Annular Combustor (SAC) (also known as Fuel Rich Dome Combustor). The design 
algorithm employs empirical and semi-empirical models which include diffuser section 
design, air distribution computations, combustor sizing, fuel nozzle design, axial swirler 
design, heat transfer calculations (with/without cooling,  thermal barrier coating) and 
dilution holes design. The design process will provide the combustor geometry as well as an 
evaluation of the combustor performance (NOx and CO emissions). A computer code has 
been developed based on the design procedures. A SAC design has been implemented by the 
computer program in order to show the program capabilities. The program run time is in 
the order of magnitude of minutes. Our aim is to provide a tool to serve in educational 
purposes. Also, to provide a kit to help understand the important technical issues governing 
the GTC design. This paper shows the first version of the software. In future, the software 
will have more capabilities including the Lean Premixed Prevaporized (LPP) concept.

I. Introduction 
He design process of a Fuel Rich Dome Combustor (also referred to as Single Annular Combustor (SAC)) is a 
challenging task. The design procedures involve lots of compute-intensive calculations. The design process 
includes coupled non-linear equations, implicit equations, different charts and lots of mathematics and logic to 
synchronize the design process. 
 
Mellor1 and Lefebvre2 collected an extensive list of empirical and analytical tools. They discussed details about 
the assumptions behind different equations involved in the design process as well as their range of validity. 
However, they did not provide a method to automate their design process. There are several other published 
preliminary designs for aircraft Gas Turbine Combustor (GTC).3-5  
 
This paper presents a mechanism to automate the design procedures of a SAC in a detailed step-by-step manner. 
An integral part of our automation mechanism, is the development of software that automates the design process. 
The fundamental objective of this software is to minimize the time spent in the preliminary aircraft GTC design 
process. The software will also enable the designer to fine tune several critical parameters and check their impact on 
the combustor’s size and performance in a timely manner. 
In Section II, the detailed design procedures of the SAC are presented. In Section III, the performance evaluation 
of the SAC will be discussed, while in the Section 3, the automation code is used to design a SAC. The performance 
of that specific SAC is also analyzed in Section 3. The reader is encouraged to refer to the books by Mellor1 and 
Lefebvre2 for more details on the assumptions and derivations of the design equations.

D. Pre-Diffuser Design 
Typically the pre-diffuser begins with a constant area section to dissipate the wakes.  The constant area section is 
followed by a diffusing section to recover a good portion of the inlet dynamic head.  The diffuser schematic is 
shown in Fig. 3.1 Dump diffuser follows the diffusing section to further reduce the speed and to enable stable 
combustion. The dump diffuser is a diffuser with large area expansion and minimum axial length that will result in 
abrupt reduction of the flow velocity, but with a very slight pressure recovery. The geometrical variables of the pre-
diffuser are given in Fig. 4.

E. Fuel Nozzle design 
The fuel nozzle is responsible for delivering the required fuel flow rate at different flight conditions. The fuel 
provided should be atomized to a suitable diameter to enhance the fuel/air mixing. A Dual orifice fuel nozzle shall 
be designed. A typical dual orifice nozzle is shown in Fig. 5. 
The dual fuel nozzle is important because no single fuel nozzle will be able to deliver the engine requirements at 
different missions. The flow rate of the fuel is proportional to the square root of the differential pressure. Therefore, 
an increase in the flow rate by a factor of 40 requires an increase in the differential pressure by 1600 times.

F. Axial Swirlers Design 
The swirling flow is an essential feature of all modern GTC. A swirler imparts a swirl to the air before its 
admittance to the combustion dome. Consequently, a Central Recirculation Zone (CRZ) is set up. The CRZ is 
essential for three reasons. First, it is essential for anchoring the flame. The reduced flow velocities result in a flame 
that is stable and anchored close to the swirler exit. Second, the mixing between air and fuel is enhanced 
significantly. Third, it acts as a continuous source of ignition because a part of the combustion gases is mixed with 
the incoming air/fuel mixture and ignites it. Different swirler configurations are used in Gas Turbine Engines. A 
Typical co-axial swirler is shown in Fig. 6.12  
Two axial swirlers are to be designed. Each one has a hub diameter and a tip diameter. The primary swirler will 
provide the air necessary for the atomization process. It will have a hub diameter equals to the secondary nozzle 
swirler chamber diameter (assumed 3 times the outer diameter). The secondary swirler will provide the secondary 
swirling air. The hub diameter of the secondary swirler will be the tip diameter of the primary swirler (assuming 
zero thickness). Fig. 7 shows a schematic of the swirlers/fuel nozzle assembly. Now we summarize the design

G. Heat Transfer and Film Cooling Calculations 
The hot gases temperature in a GTC can be higher than 2000 C (3632 F). This will result in immediate melting 
of the liner. Thus, liner cooling is a must in all GTC. Frequently, the cooling is achieved using film cooling. The air 
film reduces the liner wall temperature and protects it from damage. Fig. 8 shows a schematic of a cooling strip. 
Our calculations start by assuming the liner is not cooled. Fig. 9 shows different heat transfer mechanisms 
involved in the gas turbine combustor.2 We notice that on the hot side convection takes place between the hot gases 
and the liner. Also, radiation exchange takes place between the hot gases and the liner. The net heat is then 
conducted through the liner to the other side. Heat transfer by convection takes place between the cold side of the 
liner and the air flowing in the passage. Finally, heat transfer by radiation takes place between the liner and the 
casing. All formulas for different quantities will be introduced shortly.

III. Performance Evaluation 
Since the main dimensions of the SAC have now been estimated, performance evaluation is the next step. The 
parameters involved in the evaluation process are: the fuel spray Souter Mean Diameter ( SMD ), droplet life time (
dt , time required for complete evaporation), combustor residence time (
rt ), and NOx  and CO  emissions. 
The SMD of the fuel spray is a good indication of the droplet evaporation characteristics. The droplet lifetime 
needs to be estimated and compared to the available residence time in the combustor. The life time must be less than 
the available residence time. Otherwise, fuel droplets would emerge from the combustor and cause low combustion

IV. Design of a SAC using the developed Code 
The developed code has a user friendly interface as shown in Fig. 12. There are nine modules and each one runs 
by pressing a button. The structure of all modules is similar. The user interface of each module is divided into two 
portions: the upper portion is for the user input while the lower is for the program output. Fig. 12 shows the startup 
or the main page of the program. The user has to go through all the design modules in order. When button one is 
pressed the engine parameters module is loaded and the screen changes as shown in Fig. 13. The Engine parameters 
we discussed previously are entered and then the user proceeds with the design process. Fig. 13 is self explanatory. 
All the design modules work in a similar fashion. Fig. 14 shows the aerodynamic diffuser design. Fig. 15 shows the 
air distribution through the combustor. Fig. 16 shows the combustor sizing process. Fuel nozzle design is presented 
in Fig. 17.  The axial swirlers design is shown in Fig. 18. Heat transfer calculations and temperatures estimation are 
shown in Fig. 19. Dilution holes design is shown in Fig. 20. Finally, the performance of the combustor is evaluated 
and shown in Fig. 21. The numerical values obtained from the developed code are practical and have good 
agreement with existing GTC.
