Study on the Impact of Fuel 
Staging on Flame Structure and 
Thermoacoustic Oscillation in a 
Diesel Multi-Nozzle Combustor
Modern gas turbine combustors usually adopt multinozzle lean premixed and pre- 
evaporated (LPP) combustion technology to reduce emissions. However, the thermoacous­
tic oscillation of this design poses a significant threat to the safe operation of gas turbines. 
This paper conducts experiments and numerical calculation studies on a diesel multinozzle 
LPP combustor under atmospheric pressure with inlet temperature near 380 C to 
investigate the influence of its fuel staging ratio (FSR) on the flame structure and 
thermoacoustic oscillation. In the experiment, the pressure fluctuation and the self- 
luminescence images of OH* were measured simultaneously. The flow field and flame 
structure were obtained by RANS calculation. The results show that for the diesel 
multinozzle LPP combustor, increasing the FSR can suppress the thermoacoustic oscillation 
of the combustor, and the oscillation amplitude is reduced by up to 60%. When the pilot stage 
is not ignited, the oscillation mode is double-period limit cycle oscillation, while after the 
pilot stage is ignited, the oscillation is single-period limit loop oscillation. The proper 
orthogonal decomposition mode shows that the main energy of the oscillation is 
concentrated in the global axial synchronous oscillation. The Rayleigh index and numerical 
calculation results indicate that the change of FSR alters the flame distribution in the 
combustor. The increase of FSR causes the flame to develop from the interference zone 
between the wall and the main stage to that between the main stage and the pilot stage. The 
change of flame structure is the main reason for the suppression of thermoacoustic 
oscillation. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4069916] 
Keywords: multi-nozzle, fuel staging, diesel combustion, thermoacoustic 
With the continuous increase in environmental protection 
standard, low emissions have become an important technical 
indicator for gas turbines. Diesel, which is a common, safe and 
economic fuel of gas turbines has high energy capacity so 
suppressing NOx emission during combustion of diesel became a 
new topic of advanced gas turbine research. One of the emission 
control methods widely used to date is multinozzle LPP (Lean 
Premixed Prevaporized) combustion. Fuel is staged in the 
combustor and independently controlled based on the load required 
so thermal load is evenly distributed among nozzles in order to lower 
the emission. As an example, DLN2.6þ system from GE realized a 
low NOx emission of 5 ppm@15%O2 [1]. Nonetheless, the 
thermoacoustic instability problem of multinozzle LPP combustion 
is more complicated than traditional single nozzle combustors [2,3] 
and poses great threat to the safe operation of gas turbines.
Lieuwen and Yang [4] pointed out that the triggering mechanism 
of oscillation combustion inside combustors is usually connected 
with multiple physical and chemical processes such as sound wave, 
fluctuation in flow, equivalence ratio or temperature of premixed 
gas, interaction between flame and turbulent flow or flame and sound 
wave, variance in flame front as well as fluctuation in heat release 
ratio. There are a lot of research on the response of single nozzle 
which provided useful insights into the cause of fluctuation in heat 
release rate including fluctuation in speed, swirling flow or 
equivalence ratio, interaction between flames and the stretching 
instability of flame [5–7]. Yu et al. [8] analyzed the thermoacoustic 
instability of laminar lean premixed diesel flame under self-ignition 
and conducted numerical simulation. The results showed that the 
overall heat release rate fluctuated with the flame front. The flame 
may amplify, attenuate, or filter acoustic energy depending on phase 
interactions between the velocity and pressure oscillations. Han 
et al. [9] studied the impact of air inlet temperature on 
thermoacoustic oscillation properties of LPP combustors and found 
that as temperature changes supercritical bifurcation occurred in the 
combustor and the amplitude of thermoacoustic oscillation changed 
abruptly when inlet temperature reaches 570 K. Biswas [10] 
researched the influence of variation in equivalence ratio on 
combustion instability mode and found that a decrease in 
equivalence ratio resulted in a drop in oscillation modes.
In the field of multinozzle combustor, KIM [11] looked into the 
effect of radical fuel staging on the mode of thermoacoustic 
instability, including varying fuel flowrate and different staging 
strategies. A method to identify axial and circular thermoacoustic 
coupled oscillation is developed base on experiments and CFD 
analysis [12]. Temme [13] conducted PIV on the oscillation

self-oscillation is strongly connected to the arrangement of swirlers. 
The influence of flame interactions on FTF/FDF is also studied [17]. 
The limit cycle amplitude when there is flame interaction present is 
tightly connected to the extent of coupling strength of two heat 
sources. The combined effect of flame interactions and flame-sound 
interactions lead to an increase in combustion instability.
As discussed above, the way multinozzle combustor differs from 
the single-nozzle combustor is that the interference between 
adjacent flames is one of the main reasons affecting thermoacoustic 
oscillations. Therefore, the flame structure of the multinozzle 
combustor is also an important subject of research. The research by 
Hardalupas et al. [18] has shown that the chemiluminescence 
intensity of OH* and CH* is proportional to the equivalence ratio, 
and both can be used to describe the intensity of heat release rate in 
flames. Chong et al. [19] studied the spectra of diesel and biodiesel 
flames and found that the chemiluminescence of OH* and CH* in 
the flames of such fuels both have distinct radiation peaks. 
Moreover, Chong et al. [20] used the chemiluminescence intensity 
of CH* to investigate the flame structure of biodiesel. Sung et al. 
[21], Wei et al. [22], and Pickett et al. [23] measured and analyzed 
the flame structure of diesel combustion using OH* chemilumi­
nescence. The images of OH* chemiluminescence intensity 
coincide with the images of soot incandescence, indicating that it 
is feasible to use OH* chemiluminescence to describe the flame 
structure.
There are many studies on the thermoacoustic oscillations of 
multinozzle combustors, but the fuel types are mostly limited to 
natural gas, hydrogen, and aviation kerosene. There are relatively 
few studies on diesel multinozzle LPP combustion. This research is 
based on a diesel 10-nozzle LPP combustor. Experimental and 
numerical simulation methods are used to study the impact of fuel 
staging on combustion stability. How changes in flame structure 
affect the thermoacoustic oscillations of diesel LPP combustion is 
also analyzed.
1 Research Methodology
1.1 Experiment System. The 10-nozzle diesel combustor test 
system consists of a combustor, air system, fuel system, cooling 
water system and purge air system as shown in Fig. 1.
The combustor is cylindrical with a length of 210 mm and an inner 
diameter of 165 mm, and the side walls have viewing windows for 
monitoring the combustion status.
The air system uses a 200 kW fan as the air source. The fan speed 
is controlled by a frequency converter to control the air flow. The air 
flow is measured by a vortex flowmeter. An electric heater preheats 
the air to a set temperature. The air then enters the combustor for 
combustion.
The fuel for the pilot stage and the main stage is controlled by two 
separate fuel systems. The fuel for the main stage is further divided 
into two branches. The first branch connects to six main combustion 
nozzles, while the second branch connects to three main combustion 
nozzles. The fuel pressure for all the main combustion nozzles is 
maintained the same.
The cooling water system consists of an exhaust section wall 
cooling system and a water spray cooling system. The wall cooling 
system delivers cooling water to the water-cooled jackets of the 
exhaust section through a water pump to cool the exhaust pipe. 
The water spray cooling system directly sprays cooling water into 
the exhaust pipe, where the evaporation of water droplets provides 
direct cooling of the high-temperature exhaust gas.
The purge air system is supplied by an air compressor. After each 
test session, compressed air is pumped into the fuel nozzles to blow 
out the residual fuel in the pipes and nozzles, preventing nozzle 
blockage caused by diesel coking. The ten-nozzle diesel combustor

1.2 Measurement System. The measurement system of the 
ten-nozzle diesel combustor is shown in Fig. 5. This system enables 
the measurement of pressure and flame image. The pressure 
fluctuations within the combustor are measured using a Kulite 
pressure transducer, model XTL-190M-120KPaA, with a sensitivity 
of 0.84 mV/kPa. The transducer is 293 mm away from the head of 
the combustor with water cooling. Since the cross-sectional area of 
the combustor is much larger than that of the burner outlet, the inlet 
of the combustor is approximately a hard boundary condition in 
terms of acoustics, located at the wave trough, with the maximum 
amplitude. However, if the transducer is too close to the high- 
temperature zone at the combustor, it may be damaged. Therefore, 
this measurement position is much more suitable. The signal from 
the pressure transducer is synchronously acquired by a data 
acquisition instrument. The voltage resolution of the collector 
instrument is 0.1 mV. According to the sensitivity of the pressure 
transducer (0.84 mV/kPa), the error is within 60.12 kPa.
An image intensifier is placed at the end of the test bench facing a 
viewing window and connected to a high-speed camera. A 
narrowband filter with a wavelength of 307 6 13 nm is installed in 
front of the lens to obtain the self-luminous images of OH* in the 
flame. The images captured by the camera are sent to a computer. 
The exposure time of the camera is 495 ls. The lens aperture is f2.8. 
The camera is triggered by a mechanical switch. Aside from the 
camera I/O the trigger signal is also sent to the data acquisition 
instrument for synchronous collection of the pressure data. The 
camera frame rate is 1000 fps, and the data acquisition instrument 
has a sampling rate of 10,000 Hz. By comparing the trigger time of 
the camera, the flame images and pressure signals can be 
synchronized within an error of 0.1 ms.

2 Results and Discussion
2.1 Effect of Fuel Staging on Pressure Fluctuation 
Characteristics. The pressure fluctuation signals of the combustor 
under different FSRs were analyzed using Fast Fourier Transform 
(FFT) with a sampling rate of 10,000 Hz. The analysis was 
conducted based on 30,000 data points, resulting in a frequency 
resolution of 0.33 Hz. The data overlap rate was 70%, and the results 
were averaged after repeating the analysis 15 times, yielding the 
pressure fluctuation spectrum shown in Fig. 6. Here, f represents 
frequency, and FSR denotes the fuel staging ratio.
When the FSR was 0.00, meaning the pilot stage fuel flow was 
zero, the combustor pressure fluctuation had two dominant 
frequencies: 248.0 Hz and 495.7 Hz. The 495.7 Hz frequency was 
a second harmonic of the 248.0 Hz frequency.
After the pilot stage was activated, the dominant oscillation 
frequency decreased by 11% to 220.0 Hz. The amplitude also 
dropped by 11%, and the harmonic virtually disappeared. This 
change was due to the alteration of the flame structure within the 
combustor caused by the introduction of the pilot-stage fuel, leading 
to a sudden shift in the dominant oscillation frequency.
As the FSR continues to increase, the amplitude of the combustor 
pressure fluctuation decreases, with the frequency ranging between 
217.0 Hz and 220.0 Hz. The maximum amplitude is 5743 Pa, while 
the minimum is 1981 Pa, indicating a reduction ratio of the 
fluctuation amplitude up to 65%. Moreover, when the FSR is 
increased to 0.21, a peak around 208 Hz appears, and its amplitude 
increases with the rise of the FSR.
The time-domain signals of combustor pressure fluctuation and 
their statistical histograms under different FSRs are shown in Fig. 7. 
When the FSR is 0.00, the statistical histogram of pressure 
fluctuations exhibits an asymmetric bimodal distribution. This is 
the result of the combined effect of the two dominant oscillation 
frequencies, which have similar phases. This causes the oscillation 
at 247.7 Hz, when superimposed with the harmonic at 495.3 Hz, to 
strengthen one peak and weaken the other within a single cycle, thus 
forming an asymmetric structure.
When the FSR is increased to 0.14 and 0.17, the statistical 
histograms of pressure fluctuations show a bimodal structure. At this 
point, the pressure fluctuations enter a stable periodic oscillation 
state. As the FSR further increases to 0.21 and 0.23, the statistical 
histograms of pressure fluctuations revert to a unimodal form and 
approach the characteristics of a Gaussian distribution. This 
indicates that the oscillations are evolving toward a stochastic 
oscillation mode.
Phase space reconstruction was performed on the pressure 
fluctuation signals under different FSRs, resulting in the phase 
space diagrams shown in Fig. 8. Here, td represents the time delay 
(the calculation method is described in Sec. 1.3). When the FSR is 
0.00, the phase diagram of pressure fluctuations shows a distinct 
double-period limit cycle structure, indicating that the combustor is 
undergoing limit cycle oscillations. This double-period structure is 
formed by the combined effect of the dominant frequency of the 
pressure fluctuations and its harmonics.
When the FSR is greater than 0.21, the limit cycle structure in the 
phase diagram becomes unstable, showing a tendency to converge 
toward a central attractor. The combustor pressure fluctuations 
decrease and tend toward a stochastic oscillation mode.
These results indicate that adjusting the FSR can suppress the 
thermoacoustic oscillations in the combustor.

Fluctuation. In addition to measuring the combustor pressure 
fluctuations, this paper also captured flame images under fuel 
staging conditions using a high-speed camera. By employing a filter, 
the OH* chemiluminescence distribution in the combustor was 
obtained whose intensity represents the intensity of the flame heat 
release rate.

shown in Fig. 9, with each image separated by a time interval of 
0.5 ms. When the FSR is 0.00, the flame heat release rate intensity

0.17, the flame still has periodicity, but the oscillation intensity is 
weakened. When the FSR is 0.23, the oscillation intensity is further 
reduced, making it difficult to directly identify periodicity from the 
images. A total of 1,000 flame images were used for POD to obtain 
the first three POD modes under different FSRs. Their mode

Based on the combined analysis of the experimental and 
numerical results, the significant changes in the chemical reaction 
zone are the main reasons for the suppression of thermoacoustic 
oscillations. In the absence of the pilot flame, the heat release rate is 
more concentrated, and the interactions between the main stages and 
between the main stages and the wall strengthen the instability of the 
flame. As the Fuel Staging Ratio (FSR) increases, the chemical 
reaction zone is divided into two parts: the wall region and the 
interference zone between the pilot and main stages. The local heat 
release rate intensity is weakened, and the oscillations are 
suppressed.

3 Conclusion
This paper studies the thermoacoustic oscillations in a multi­
nozzle diesel LPP combustor under fuel staging conditions. Through 
the experimental method of OH* chemiluminescence image and 
pressure fluctuation signals synchronized acquisition, the causes of 
thermoacoustic oscillations were studied using POD modal analysis 
and Rayleigh index analysis. Numerical calculations were also 
conducted to obtain the distribution characteristics of the OH radical 
in the combustor. The main conclusions are as follows:
(1) For the multi-nozzle diesel LPP combustor, whether the pilot 
stage is activated or not has a significant impact on the 
oscillation frequency. The frequency is reduced by 11% when 
the pilot stage is activated compared to when it is not. 
Adjusting the fuel FSR can suppress the pressure fluctuations 
in the combustor, with the fluctuation amplitude reduction 
reaching up to 60%.
(2) Under the condition without the pilot stage, the oscillation 
mode is an asymmetric double-peak oscillation. The phase 
space image indicates that the oscillation is a double-period 
limit cycle oscillation. After the pilot stage is activated 
(FSR0.17), the oscillation mode becomes a symmetric 
double-peak oscillation, and the phase space image shows 
that the oscillation is a single-period limit cycle oscillation. 
When FSR is greater than 0.21, the oscillation mode changes 
from double-peak to single-peak oscillation, and the phase 
diagram evolves from a single-period limit cycle toward 
chaos.
(3) The main energy of the oscillation is concentrated in the 
global axial synchronous oscillation. The Rayleigh index and 
numerical calculation results show that changes in FSR alter 
the flame distribution in the combustor. An increase in FSR 
causes the flame to shift from the wall and main stage 
interference zone to the main stage-pilot stage interference 
zone. This change in flame structure is the main reason for the 
suppression of thermoacoustic oscillations.
