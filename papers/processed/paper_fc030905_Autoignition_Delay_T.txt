Autoignition Delay Time
Measurements of Methane,
Ethane, and Propane Pure Fuels
and Methane-Based Fuel Blends
Autoignition delay experiments in air have been performed in an atmospheric ’¼éow reac-
tor using typical natural gas components, namely, methane, ethane, and propane. Autoi-
gnition delay measurements were also made for binary fuel mixtures of methane/ethane
and methane/propane, and ternary mixtures of methane/ethane/propane. The effect of
CO2 addition to the methane-based fuel blends on autoignition delay times was also
investigated. Equivalence ratios for the experiments ranged between 0.5 and 1.25, and
temperatures ranged from 930 K to 1140 K. Consistent with past studies, increasing
equivalence ratio and increasing inlet temperatures over these ranges decreased autoi-
gnition delay times. Furthermore, addition of 5ŌĆō10% ethane or propane decreased au-
toignition delay time of the binary methane-based fuel by 30ŌĆō50%. Further addition of
either ethane or propane showed less signi’¼ücant reduction of autoignition delays. Addi-
tion of 5ŌĆō10% CO2 slightly decreased the autoignition delay times of methane fuel mix-
tures. Arrhenius correlations were used to derive activation energies for the ignition of
the pure fuels and their mixtures. Results show a reduction in activation energies at the
higher temperatures studied, which suggests a change in ignition chemistry at very high
temperatures. Measurements show relatively good agreement with predictions from a
detailed kinetics mechanism, speci’¼ücally developed to model ignition chemistry of C1-C3
alkanes. DOI: 10.1115/1.4000590

Introduction
Natural gas is a common fuel used in gas turbines for power
generation 1. It is becoming increasingly advantageous for in-
dustrial gas turbines to be capable of operating on a variety of fuel
blends while maintaining performance criteria such as low emis-
sions and ’¼éame stability 2,3. The fuels of interest are lique’¼üed
natural gases from all over the world, coal-derived syngas, bio-
mass, land’¼üll gas, and process gas. These fuels have a broad range
of compositions, which were shown to have signi’¼ücant effects on
combustor performance, affecting ef’¼üciency, stability, and emis-
sions 2. Natural gas composition varies globally and, to a lesser
extent, within the United States. U.S. natural gas composition can
contain methane concentrations as low as 74.5% by volume and
ethane and propane concentrations as high as 13.3% and 23.7%
by volume, respectively, with the balance made up of higher-
order hydrocarbons, carbon dioxide, oxygen, nitrogen, and hydro-
gen sul’¼üde 4ŌĆō6.
Even a few percent of higher-order hydrocarbon was shown to
signi’¼ücantly promote the ignition of methane-based fuels 5. This
is of serious concern in premixed systems such as industrial dry,
low emission DLE gas turbines, which often use aerodynamic
methods to stabilize the ’¼éame in a particular zone. Variations in
autoignition phenomena, based on fuel composition, could result
in ’¼éashback and ’¼éame stabilization in unintended locations, such
as the premixer, causing performance problems and hardware
damage 7. Researchers recognized the need to quantify the pro-
motional effects of higher-order hydrocarbons on natural gas ig-
nition and combustion processes 8. Autoignition delay measure-
ments for natural gas components and mixtures provide valuable
insight into the effects of additives on methane ignition, as well as
validation data for chemical kinetics mechanisms for predicting
ignition of methane-based fuels.
Recent studies showed that natural gas ignition chemistry can
be suitably represented by methane/ethane /propane mixtures
9,10. The current study was established to provide reliable au-
toignition delay time measurements of methane/ethane/propane
pure fuels and mixtures at relevant gas turbine inlet temperatures.
The measurements extend the current database of available igni-
tion data for natural gas fuels and provide much needed validation
for the development of kinetics models in this parameter space.
Pure methane oxidation has been extensively studied experi-
mentally for a number of years. A summary of the ignition delay
time measurements made up to 1969, mostly focusing on tempera-
tures above 1400 K and pressures near atmospheric, is provided
by Spadaccini and Colket 7. In recent years, the parameter space
for methane autoignition was well investigated for pressures up to
300 atm and temperatures ranging from 849 K to 1600 K
5,11ŌĆō17. Pure ethane and propane oxidation has been less exten-
sively studied. An excellent summary of experimental ethane au-
toignition delay time measurements is provided by de Vries et al.
18. Measurements exist for temperatures ranging from 1218 K
to 1860 K and pressures up to 3 atm 18,19 for ethane oxidation,
and temperatures ranging from 900 K to 2211 K and pressures up
to 30 atm for propane oxidation 14,20ŌĆō23. Figure 1 summarizes
the autoignition delay times for methane, ethane, and propane
pure fuels reported in the literature, showing the range of condi-
tions investigated by various researchers.
de Vries et al. 16 provided a summary of experimental studies
to date, which have investigated the effects of higher-order hydro-
carbons on methane ignition behavior. Goy et al. 12 studied the
effect of signi’¼ücant 15% by volume additions of ethane and
propane on methane autoignition delay for temperatures ranging
from 952 K to 1428 K and pressures up to 20 atm. Signi’¼ücant
changes in activation energy for the fuel blends were observed,

The dif’¼üculties of making autoignition delay time measure-
ments in ’¼éow reactor apparatus include achieving ŌĆ£instantaneousŌĆØ
mixing of fuel and air, and a zero temperature gradient at the
entrance to the test section to maintain ideal plug-’¼éow conditions.
In the current experiments, a swirler, followed by an expanding
duct, was used to rapidly mix the fuel and air upstream of the ’¼éow
reactor 27. However, the mixing of the fuel and air still has a
’¼ünite mixing time-scale. It was demonstrated that chemical and
physical mixing effects upstream of the ’¼éow reactor can be ac-
counted for by modeling the premixing section with a chemical
reactor network of ideal reactors 29. Therefore, in the present
work, experiments were performed with ethylene to characterize
the physical and chemical mixing effects in the premixing section
on ignition delay time measurements.
A chemical reactor model CRM, consisting of a large number
of perfectly stirred reactors with in’¼ünitely small volumes in a
series, was developed, based on the geometry and ’¼éow conditions
in the transfer lines and premixing section to account for the mix-
ing effects on the ignition delay time measurements. The mixing
time upstream of the test section of the ’¼éow reactor was therefore
numerically computed as the sum of the residence times of the
series of perfectly stirred reactors representing the premixing sec-
tion. The mixing time was then subtracted from the experimental
measurements to obtain autoignition delay time. The computed
mixing time-scale in the premixing zone ranged between 125 ms
and 205 ms for various temperatures and ’¼éow rates studied.
The accuracy of several available chemical kinetics mecha-
nisms in predicting existing ethylene combustion data was inves-
tigated in order to identify the most appropriate mechanism for
use in the CRM. Because ethylene is a key intermediate in the
oxidation of higher-order hydrocarbons, as well as an important
fuel in high speed propulsion devices 19,30, combustion of this
fuel was widely studied using a variety of techniques over a broad
range of conditions. Ethylene autoignition delay times and decom-
position pro’¼üles were reported in the literature 20,23,30ŌĆō34. The
conditions of each experiment were modeled using existing
chemical kinetics mechanisms 35ŌĆō39, most of which were de-
veloped speci’¼ücally to model ethylene combustion.
Figure 5 shows the typical performance of the available chemi-
cal kinetics mechanisms in predicting ethylene ignition. This ’¼üg-
ure shows the accuracy of the kinetics mechanisms in predicting
autoignition delay times of ethylene reported by Brown and Tho-
mas 20 at temperatures between 1102 K and 1771 K and pres-
sures between 1.4 atm and 3.4 atm. The kinetics mechanism de-
veloped by Varatharajan and Williams 37, focusing on ethylene
combustion, consistently predicted the experimental results of
other researchers most accurately.
The mechanism developed by Varatharajan and Williams 37
was therefore used in the CRM to model the ’¼éow reactor appara-
tus in the current study. Mixing times calculated from the CRM
were subtracted from the experimental data to obtain corrected
autoignition delay times of ethylene. Figure 6 shows that these
corrected autoignition delay times have good agreement with pre-
dictions made by the ethylene mechanism of Varatharajan and
Williams 37. Figure 6 also shows low-pressure i.e., 1ŌĆō5 atm
ethylene/air ignition delay time data from the literature 20,23,33
for comparison. Most of these experiments from the literature

autoignition delay times shorter than in the current study.
The resulting mixing times obtained in the ethylene experi-

this study. While errors may be introduced in assuming the mixing

to the calculation of mixing times are bulk ’¼éow rates, temperature,

The ethane and propane measurements shown in Figs. 8 and 9
suggest a decrease in Ea at T1000 K. This trend is most promi-
nent for ethane at the leaner equivalence ratios. Other researchers
have noted this trend at lower temperatures near 800 K for el-

tive temperature coef’¼ücient for hydrocarbon ignition 16. The
predictions of autoignition delay time by the kinetics mechanism
of Petersen et al. 1 do not capture this reduction in effective Ea
but rather predict an effectively constant Ea over the entire range
of temperatures studied, as indicated in Figs. 8 and 9. Further, the
mechanism underpredicts the experimental measurements of au-
toignition delay time for both ethane and propane.
The autoignition delay times measured for pure ethane and pro-
pane show a slight decrease when increasing the equivalence ratio
from 0.5 to 1.0. The predictions of Petersen et al. 1 suggest that
this trend is followed only below a certain temperature, and that as
temperature increases well above 1000 K, increasing equivalence
ratio will increase autoignition delay time. This predicted trend at
high temperatures was not observed in the experiments.
This shifting trend with temperature regime was experimentally
veri’¼üed by Healy et al. 25, who studied methane/propane fuel
mixtures. The authors have observed that fuel-lean mixtures react
faster than fuel-rich mixtures at high temperatures, and fuel-rich
mixtures react faster than fuel-lean mixtures at lower tempera-
tures. The authors attribute this phenomenon to the fact that at
lower temperatures, chain-branching is dependant on fuel concen-
tration. Therefore fuel-rich mixtures will exhibit faster kinetics,
resulting in quicker autoignition. At higher temperatures, the H
+O2ŌåÆO+OH reaction becomes the rate-limiting chain-branching
reaction, and thus, reactivity is more strongly dependent on O2
than on CxHy. Therefore, mixtures with lower  and therefore
higher O2 will have shorter autoignition delay time.
The temperature at which the shift in controlling kinetics occurs
has been shown to be highly dependent on pressure. Healy et al.
25 found very good agreement of their data, which was collected
at pressures ranging from 10 atm to 30 atm, with predictions made
using the kinetics mechanism of Petersen et al. 1. The authors
found that as pressure increases, the critical temperature for the
shift in controlling kinetics increases. The atmospheric pressure
measurements made in the current study, however, do not agree
well with predictions made by this kinetics mechanism. The ki-
netics mechanism of Petersen et al. 1 predicts the critical tem-
perature for a shift in the controlling kinetics at 1112 K for ethane
and 1025 K for propane at atmospheric pressure. For all tempera-
ture conditions experimentally studied for methane, ethane, and
propane autoignition, it was found that increasing  reduced au-
toignition delay time, indicating that the experiments were per-
formed at temperatures below the critical temperature associated
with the kinetics regime shift at atmospheric pressure. The mea-
surements made in the current study can therefore provide critical
validation data for further development of the kinetics mecha-
nisms at atmospheric pressure.

lower temperatures because autoignition was not achievable in the
experimental reactor for the residence times of this study. For the
higher test temperatures, reductions in methane autoignition delay
time of up to 50% were observed in the mixtures composed of up
to 10% ethane or propane by volume. On the other hand, promo-
tional effects of ethane and propane on methane autoignition
greatly diminish above 20% additions.
Figure 13 also shows the signi’¼ücant increase in autoignition
delay time of pure propane with the addition of methane. This
trend was also seen in the addition of methane to pure ethane.
Increases in autoignition delay times of ethane and propane of up
to 50% were observed with the addition of methane, making the
resulting concentration of the methane blend 25% methane/75%
ethane or propane.
Differences in autoignition delay times were observed if ethane
or propane was an additive; ethane addition tended to reduce au-
toignition delay times of methane blends more than propane. The
majority of tests indicate that ethane addition typically reduced
methane autoignition delay time about 10% more than that of
propane addition. While some results show this difference as high
as 40%, few results show similar reductions with either ethane or
propane, and even some results show larger reductions with pro-
pane than ethane.
Very few researchers have derived global rate parameters for
the ignition of the fuel blends studied. Only Petersen et al. 5
derived global activation energies for the oxidation of the fuel
blends. In their study, an 80% methane/20% propane mixture was
found to have an effective activation energy of 41.9 kcal/mol be-
tween 1189 K and 1615 K at 12.2 atm. This agrees with Ea
=41.8 kcal/mol, derived from the data collected in the current
study. Petersen et al. 5 also found an effective activation energy
of 42.1 kcal/mol for 90% methane/10% ethane mixture oxidation,
which agrees with Ea=42.2 kcal/mol, derived from the data col-
lected in the current study. The derived Ea for the methane fuel
blends was found to be lower than Ea for pure methane, but higher
than Ea for ethane or propane pure fuel additive.
The kinetics mechanism of Petersen et al. 1 predicted a very
similar autoignition delay time for the methane/ethane and
methane/propane blends. The mechanism overpredicted the autoi-
gnition delay times of methane, but underpredicted the autoigni-
tion delay times of pure ethane and propane. However, the mecha-
nism performed very well in predicting autoignition delay times
of methane blends containing low concentrations of ethane and
propane. The mechanism of Petersen et al. 1 predicts a reduction
in autoignition delay time with a reduction in equivalence ratio for
the conditions of the binary mixtures studied. This trend was again
evident in the predictions made above a critical temperature for
pure ethane and propane autoignition.
The kinetics mechanism predicts a shift in the controlling ki-
netics of methane/ethane fuel blend oxidation near 930 K. This
prediction was not veri’¼üed by the experiments, as fuel-rich
methane/ethane mixtures were found to have shorter autoignition
delay times than fuel-lean mixtures for the entire temperature
range investigated. A similar trend was predicted for methane/
propane mixtures. Figure 12 indicates that, for the conditions
studied, the mechanism predicts a reduction in methane/propane
mixture autoignition delay time with decreasing equivalence ratio.
It was found in the current study that the critical temperature for a
shift in the controlling kinetics, indicated by the effect of equiva-
lence ratio on ignition delay time, was lower than the predictions
by this mechanism. The data collected in the current study will
provide a basis for future kinetics model validation in the atmo-
spheric pressure regime.

The effect of CO2 addition on the ignition of natural gas type
fuel blends was investigated. One binary methane/ethane fuel
mixture was selected to serve as the basis for these tests: 75%
methane/25% ethane. This mixture was then diluted with CO2,
such that mixtures containing 5% and 10% CO2 by volume were
created. Autoignition delay times were measured for both stoichi-
ometric mixtures and mixtures having an equivalence ratio of 0.5.
The autoignition delay times measured for the mixtures containing
CO2 are compared with the measurements made for the original
75% methane/25% ethane mixtures in Fig. 15.
The resulting autoignition delay times for the mixtures contain-
ing CO2 do not signi’¼ücantly deviate from the measurements of
autoignition delay times for the mixture containing no CO2. Only
one data series offered insight into a possible effect of CO2 on
autoignition delay time. For the mixtures at an equivalence ratio
of 0.5, it was observed that at the highest test temperature 1137
K, adding 5% CO2 to the mixture increased the autoignition de-
lay time by only 2%, but increasing the concentration to 10% CO2
increased the autoignition delay time an additional 46%. This ob-
servation can be explained in part, due to the third-body collision
ef’¼üciencies of CO2 being an order of magnitude greater than those
of N2 36. Therefore, the presence of CO2 promotes recombina-
tion reactions, and hence, reduces the fuel reactivity. Due to the
relatively small amount of data collected in this investigation,
however, further experiments are needed to quantify the effect of
CO2 on fuel mixture ignition delay time.

Conclusions
Autoignition delay time experiments were conducted in an at-
mospheric ’¼éow reactor for methane, ethane, and propane pure
fuels;
binary
methane/ethane
and
methane/propane;
ternary
methane/ethane/propane; and methane/ethane/CO2 fuel mixtures.
The measurements provide a comprehensive database, which can
be used to improve the accuracy of chemical kinetics mechanisms
for predicting ignition of typical natural gas fuel blends. Most
experimental autoignition delay time data reported in the literature
for the fuels studied herein has been collected at elevated pres-
sures and temperatures using shock tubes and rapid compression
machines. The measurements made at low to intermediate tem-
perature and atmospheric pressure ’¼üll some regions in the param-
eter space of the published autoignition delay time of natural gas
fuels where previous data was sparse. Important trends of reduc-
tions in autoignition delay times of the fuels and fuel blends with
increasing temperature and equivalence ratio were observed. Fur-

Further, the trends observed in this study indicate that addition of

these pure fuels and mixtures of these fuels were derived. For the

methane/propane fuels of 41.8 kcal/mol. Differences in autoigni-
tion delay times were observed for ethane versus propane addi-
tives; ethane addition tended to reduce autoignition delay times of
methane blends about 10% further than propane.
An important ’¼ünding, because of its application to practical
natural gas fuels, was that the most dramatic decrease 30ŌĆō50%
in autoignition delay time of binary, methane-based fuels occurs
with the addition of 5ŌĆō10% of ethane or propane. Further addition
of ethane or propane continued to reduce autoignition delay time,
but not as dramatically. This trend was found for all binary mix-
tures of methane/ethane and methane/propane.
This study indicates that current kinetics predictive tools seem
to capture the promotional effects of ethane and propane indi-
vidual additives on methane autoignition, but not the effect of
combinations of ethane and propane on methane autoignition de-
lay time adequately. Further, the data collected indicate that for
the temperature range studied 930ŌĆō1140 K, autoignition delay
times of methane, ethane, and propane pure fuels and mixtures
decrease with increasing equivalence ratio at atmospheric pres-
sure. This is not captured by the current kinetics mechanisms,
which predict a shift in the controlling oxidation kinetics at atmo-
spheric pressure in the temperature range studied. Additional
mechanism development, inclusive of the data collected herein, is
needed to better model oxidation of natural gas fuel blends. Inter-
esting trends of increased autoignition delay time of methane-
based fuel blends were seen with CO2 addition; however, because
such little data was collected in this area, further study is required
to draw signi’¼ücant conclusions about the effect of a fuelŌĆÖs CO2
concentration on its autoignition delay time.
