30 Years of Dry-Low-NOx
Micromix Combustor Research
for Hydrogen-Rich Fuels—An
Overview of Past and Present
Activities
This paper presents an overview of the past and present of low-emission combustor
research with hydrogen-rich fuels at Aachen University of Applied Sciences (AcUAS). In
1990, AcUAS started developing the dry-low-NOx Micromix combustion technology.
Micromix reduces NOx emissions using jet-in-crossﬂow mixing of multiple miniaturized
fuel jets and combustor air with an inherent safety against ﬂashback. At ﬁrst, pure hydro-
gen as fuel was investigated with lab-scale applications. Later, Micromix combustor pro-
totypes were developed for the use in an industrial gas turbine Honeywell/Garrett GTCP-
36-300, proving low NOx characteristics during real gas turbine operation, accompanied
by the successful deﬁnition of safety laws and control system modiﬁcations. Further, the
Micromix was optimized for the use in annular and can-combustors as well as for fuel-
ﬂexibility with hydrogen-methane-mixtures and hydrogen-rich syngas qualities by means
of extensive experimental and numerical simulations. In 2020, the latest Micromix appli-
cation is demonstrated in a commercial 2 MW-class gas turbine can-combustor with full-
scale engine operation. This paper discusses the advances in Micromix research over the
last three decades. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4049764]
1
Introduction
The negative effects on the earth’s climate make the reduction
of the potent greenhouse gases carbon dioxide (CO2) and nitric
oxides (NOx) an imperative of combustion research. Hydrogen-
based gas turbine systems are in the focus of the energy-
producing industry, due to their potential to eliminate CO2 emis-
sions as combustion product, if the fuel is produced from renew-
able and sustainable energy sources. Due to the differences in the
physical properties of hydrogen-rich fuel mixtures compared to
conventional gas turbine fuels, well-established gas turbine com-
bustion systems cannot be directly applied for dry-low-NOx
hydrogen combustion.
In response to the industrial demand for gas turbine operation
with hydrogen-rich fuels, AcUAS initiated the dry-low-NOx Micro-
mix (MMX) combustion research in 1990. A central design feature
of this nonpremixed combustion system and enabler for low NOx
emissions is the jet-in-crossﬂow mixing of multiple miniaturized
fuel jets with combustor air. The miniaturization guarantees signiﬁ-
cant NOx reduction due to the very short residence time of the reac-
tants in the small ﬂamelets. The risk of ﬂashback is avoided due to
the absence of ﬂammable fuel-air mixtures before injection.
Starting with 100% hydrogen fuel, ﬁrst lab-scale testing at
atmospheric conditions initiated an extensive development and
optimization process. Soon after, Micromix prototype combustors
were applied in an APU Honeywell/Garrett GTCP-36-300 and
proved the postulated low NOx characteristics under realistic gas
turbine operating conditions. Besides, the necessary safety laws
and control system modiﬁcations for conventional gas turbines
were investigated and successfully tested.
During several follow-up projects targeting hydrogen combus-
tion in the aviation- and power-producing industry, the combus-
tion principle was further optimized for can burner and annular
combustion chambers. The outcome of the different experimental
optimization studies and accompanying combustion and ﬂow sim-
ulations are summarized in this paper.
The combustion principle ﬁnds its latest application in a com-
mercial 2 MW-class gas turbine combustor with full-scale engine
demonstration in 2020. Also, the successful efforts undertaken
toward fuel ﬂexibility are presented expanding the fuel range of
Micromix combustion. Low-pressure testing with variable hydro-
gen methane mixtures and full-scale gas turbine operation with
hydrogen-rich syngas showed satisfactory operating behavior at
low NOx emissions.
2
The Early History of Micromix
The kickoff of two research projects, the Euro-Quebec-Hydro-
Hydrogen-Pilot-Project (EQHHPP) [1] and the project CRYO-
PLANE [2], initiated the hydrogen gas turbine research activities
at AcUAS that resulted in the long lasting Micromix research,
development, and optimization activities in the following decades.
With special regard to engine control and hydrogen fuel metering,
two gas turbines, a Deutz KHD T216 (Cologne, Germany) and a
Honeywell/Garrett GTCP 36-300 (Phoenix, AZ), were success-
fully modiﬁed to operate in their original kerosene conﬁguration
and with gaseous hydrogen applying minor changes of the fuel
injection systems. Tests with both gas turbines not only proved
the feasibility of hydrogen operation but also showed the necessity
of combustor modiﬁcations for hydrogen operation. The test runs
with hydrogen-produced more NOx emissions than the kerosene
operation (Fig. 1). Hotspots around the conventional injector noz-
zles formed in hydrogen operation, and overly rich mixtures of air
and hydrogen during part load operation were detected. Based on

2.1
Micromix First Generation Combustion. Based on the
operational parameters of the APU GTCP 36-300 and KHD T216,
the ﬁrst generation hydrogen testburners are developed, which
joined several small air-injection-tubes in a single nozzle injecting
hydrogen perpendicular (Fig. 2). The construction and manufac-
turing of these nozzles proved difﬁcile and hotspots occurred at
some hydrogen injection points during testing.
In a further optimization, the straight hydrogen-injector-holes
were replaced by injectors with tilted bores. The resulting spin in
the hydrogen injection jet should improve the air-hydrogen-
mixing. Hotspots also occurred and the further increased complex-
ity of the manufacturing process was disadvantageous for a practi-
cal application.
2.2
Micromix Second Generation Combustion. A second
generation hydrogen combustor approach concentrated on inverse
diffusion ﬂame combustion. Hydrogen was introduced into a sin-
tered metal structure to create a hydrogen atmosphere in which air
was injected (Fig. 3). A two-step rich-lean-combustion strategy
ensured a stable ﬂame. Test results showed an inhomogeneous
distribution of hydrogen within the sintered metal structure, and
therefore the hydrogen layer was very irregular. Regardless, the
sintered metal combustor showed already signiﬁcantly reduced
NOx emissions under gas turbine operation conditions of the APU
GTCP 36-300 (Fig. 1). But the mechanical integrity of the sin-
tered metal ruled out a continuous operation of this combustor.
Furthermore, the complex manufacturing and assembly proved
costly and inefﬁcient for a practical application.
2.3
Micromix Third and Fourth Generation Combustion.
Besides an intermediate test approach with miniaturized 0.3-mm-
diameter H2 injector nozzles with an injection angle of 45 deg
declined to the air jet, which showed an excessive heat at the heat-
shields on the burner front surfaces [5], a third generation test-
burner was developed, injecting hydrogen perpendicular into the
air jet. In this approach, each hydrogen injector was paired with
one single air gate. The combustor summarized six air-gate-/
hydrogen-injector-systems in one single tube. These tubes were
arranged to a burner segment, and several burner segments were
combined to a combustor module (Fig. 4). Based on this conﬁgu-
ration, a full-scale gas turbine combustor was created to ﬁt into
the KHD T216 can-combustion-chamber.

In parallel, a more simpliﬁed design was developed to ﬁt the
GTCP 36-300 gas turbine annular combustion chamber module

injectors on six concentrically arranged rings equally distributed
over the combustion chamber cross section. A separate fuel sup-

operation and a feasible fuel-metering for hydrogen with minor
modiﬁcation impact to existing gas turbine fuel metering architec-
ture, was developed [1–3,5,6].
3
Fuel Metering and Gas Turbine Control
Besides the optimization of low emission Micromix combustor
designs, the development and implementation of a safe and reli-
able engine control philosophy always was an accompanying task
[2,4,5,7,8]. The ﬁrst approach was to achieve a stable startup and
operation of the gas turbine Deutz KHD T216 [6,9] and the GTCP
36-300 with hydrogen using the ﬁfth generation prototype as dem-
onstrator [5,7,9,10]. In later years, when high-hydrogen syngas
and hydrogen–methane-mixtures in fuel-ﬂexible Micromix appli-
cations became an important research topic, safe control strategies
always remained an essential research topic [8].
3.1
Hydrogen Control Strategy. A kerosene fueled gas tur-
bine for low NOx operation with gaseous hydrogen as fuel
demands (besides the modiﬁcation of the applied combustor tech-
nology) an adaptation of the control and metering strategy of the
gas turbine. The chosen control strategy for hydrogen operation in
the GTCP 36-300 is to achieve similar operational behavior as in
kerosene operation using the same thermal heat ﬂowrate for each
point of operation regardless of the used fuel, deﬁning particular
fuel-steps to archive a gentle acceleration [4–7,9,10].
The control strategy must also consider the change of some
thermodynamic characteristics in a hydrogen fueled gas turbine
[8]. Since kerosene is a liquid fuel, the conversion to gaseous
hydrogen requires the adaptation of the control software as well as
hardware modiﬁcations. A separate fast and precise hydraulic
metering unit for hydrogen (HMU) was developed due to the chal-
lenging combustion characteristics of hydrogen, Fig. 6.
The GTCP 36-300 is a constant-speed-operated gas turbine.
During normal operation in the environment control setting mode,
the rotational speed is to be maintained at 99% rotational speed
regardless of the chosen load condition; for the main engine start
(MES) mode, the high-power operation mode of the GTCP 36-
300, the rotational speed is increased to 101%. The hydraulic
valve (manufacturer MOOG Inc., East Aurora, NY), is controlled
by the versatile engine control box (VECB). Details on the control
parameter optimization and the safety laws for the application of
hydrogen are given in Ref. [9].
The minimal required fuel mass ﬂow is calculated by a simpli-
ﬁed thermodynamic model including gas turbine–speciﬁc control
modes, e.g., load compressor power by inlet guide vanes move-
ment [10]. Figure 7 shows the calculated energy amount and the
measured kerosene energy amount against the speed of the gas
turbine together with the applied hydrogen energy amount. Espe-
cially for hydrogen operation, “fuel spiking” like in kerosene
operation for the startup is not necessary.
The setting of the control parameters allows a secure operation
of the hydrogen fueled gas turbine. By using deﬁned fuel steps in
the startup-controller during acceleration, a gentle and secure
startup of the GTCP 36-300 up to 95% rotational speed is
achieved. Subsequently, the on-speed governor operational gas
turbine controller takes over and stabilizes the gas turbine at 99%
idle speed (Fig. 7).
Figure 8 shows the capability of the on-speed governor control-
ler for the most challenging load change from idle-operation to
the maximum power operation point MES and back to idle, prov-
ing successfully safe and stable operation of the GTCP 36-300
with hydrogen with the Micromix combustor installed [7].

4
The Micromix Combustion Principle
Based on the fourth-generation-MMX-design, the cross inﬂuences
of essential combustion and aerodynamic ﬂow parameters within the
combustion structure were systematically investigated in detail with
power density variations and initial ﬂame size scaling [11].
In the following years of research, a deep understanding of
Micromix design was developed as baseline to expand Micromix
applications into a multitude of industrial applications with opti-
mized performance and emission characteristics maintaining a
low total pressure loss.
At ﬁrst, Micromix research kept its focus on pure hydrogen
combustion in the GTCP 36-300 gas turbine with an optimized
design for industrial manufacturing and easy gas turbine integra-
tion together with viable fuel metering and engine control modiﬁ-
cations [7,9–11]. The Micromix combustion principle uses
multiple miniaturized injection points for hydrogen that are mixed
into a crossﬂow air jet. To identify geometric parameters inﬂuenc-
ing Micromix hydrogen combustion, a multitude of experimental
test-burners and numerical studies were investigated.
Figure 11(a) shows a typical cross-cut of a MMX burner illus-
trating the jet-in-crossﬂow-characteristics (JICF) of two fuel
injectors and the corresponding air-guiding-panel (AGP) with the
related air-gates. The air enters the Micromix burner structure,
passes the AGP-gate, and mixes with the fuel jet that is injected
perpendicular into the air jet. The free air-jet that mixes with the
fuel is separated from the inner recirculation zone by the inner
vortex shear layer, deﬁning a critical injection depth ycrit,
Fig. 11(b). As long as the actual injection depth y of the fuel-jet
into the air-jet is below ycrit, the fuel-air-mixture can discharge
freely into the combustion zone, the residence time of the mixture
in the ﬂame region is short, and, in consequence, the NOx emis-
sions are low. Once the fuel-jet penetrates the shear layer and
enters the inner recirculation vortex, a fuel-air-mixture within the
vortex would be formed. This mixture would react and circulate
in the vortex increasing temperature and residence time of the
mixture, leading to higher NOx emissions. Therefore, the JICF
needs to be designed to meet these requirements. For dual-fuel or
ﬂexible-fuel operation, the assumption is always to run the gas
turbine at constant thermal power regardless of the fuel. The
change in Wobbe Index needs to be considered in the design of
the air gate height to avoid the above-mentioned phenomena
resulting in increased NOx emissions. Therefore, the JICF is
designed for the most critical operating point and fuel mixture
ensuring safe and low-emission operation. For ﬂame-anchoring,
ﬂame-stabilization, and ﬂame-positioning, inner and outer recircu-
lation vortices are used, created by a bluff-body design [12]. The
size of these recirculation vortices in front of the air guiding panels
and the burner segments can be inﬂuenced by the bluff-body
dimensions of AGP- and burner-segment. The ﬂame anchors at the
burner edge and is aerodynamically stabilized between the inner
recirculation zone behind the air guiding panels and the outer recir-
culation zone in front of the burner segments, Fig. 11(a). Inner and
outer recirculation areas must be designed to anchor the ﬂame in
the desired radial position. In addition, to keep the Micromix ﬂame-
lets centered and stabilized in circumferential position, the distance
between two adjacent injectors must be optimized.
To maintain low NOx ﬂame characteristics, the blockage ratios
of the burner segment and the AGP need to be optimized for a
low-emission-interaction of inner and outer recirculation vortices,
Fig. 12. Also, the maximum injection depth yn,j and the injector
diameter di, as well as the distance between two injectors s as a
function of the power density have been investigated to create a
considerable knowledge and database of feasible design parameter
combinations. Some cross-inﬂuences of critical design parameters
are described in Ref. [13].

Variable Micromix Power Density for Can and

For a deep understanding of the low NOx Micromix parameters,
an interactive research and optimization approach was chosen that
considers experimental and numerical test-burner studies, full-
scale combustor gas turbine integration and operation, as well as
design and manufacturing (Fig. 15).
In 2011, a long-term cooperation started between Kawasaki
Heavy Industries, Ltd. Japan (KHI) and AcUAS to investigate
Micromix for the application in a heavy-duty gas turbine. KHI
focuses on research and development projects for a future hydro-
gen society. The complete cycle from the production of gaseous
hydrogen, its reﬁnement and liquefaction for transportation and
storage, and the utilization of hydrogen in gas turbines for elec-
tricity supply were the scope of work [16]. One key technology in
the research is a stable and low NOx hydrogen combustion
method. KHI, AcUAS, and B&B-AGEMA GmbH investigated

The integration into the can-casing required an adaptation of
the Micromix combustor power density [13]. Several ring-
arrangements were investigated including Micromix combustors
with one ring up to ﬁve rings. To vary the overall power density,
the power per fuel injector needs to be adapted. To keep the pro-
ven Micromix low NOx characteristics, a variation of the power
per injector imposes a variation of the injector nozzle diameter;
by increasing the ﬂame size, in consequence, the ring and AGP
design are also increased in size, while the number of required
injector nozzles decreases based on the design dependencies in
Fig. 12. This deﬁnes the number of required burner rings
(Fig. 17). In addition, the circumferential distance “s” between the
fuel injectors must be optimized for increased ﬂame sizes
[13,17,19].
Experimental testing and numerical studies created a database
for optimized design and scaling parameter dimensions. A multi-
tude of parameter combinations for integration, low NOx, and
combustion performance has been regarded. The investigations
have been done by a stepwise increase of injector nozzle
diameters to increase the thermal power per injector, to optimize
the power density for gas turbine can-casing integration. This
reduces also the manufacturing complexity by reduction of
required fuel distribution rings and number of injector nozzles
[19,22]. An experimental and numerical thermal validation of a
burner ring and AGP section were performed in Ref. [19], assur-
ing the mechanical integrity of the burner.
The results of experimental atmospheric testing and numerical
calculations conﬁrmed the successful application of variable
power injectors maintaining the low NOx Micromix characteris-
tics under part load, design point, and off-design conditions.
Figure 18 shows the NOx emissions of three atmospheric test-
burner conﬁgurations, from 0.45- and 0.55-mm-injector-nozzles
up to a maximum diameter of 1.00 mm [24,25]. Comparing the
measured and calculated results of the three tested conﬁgurations
with similar power density, the low NOx Micromix characteristics
are maintained, regardless of the applied thermal power per injec-
tor. An advantage of the enlarged injector nozzles is a reduction
in manufacturing complexity and costs, and a better robustness
against fuel contamination in industrial environments. For large
injector diameters, experimental testing of a can type burner in
Ref. [23] showed thermoacoustic instabilities, which can be
reduced and even avoided choosing a smaller injector diameter.
The work also highlighted that power densities up to 14 MW/
(m2 bar) are possible. Finally, the work resulted in a power density
optimized 3- and 4-ring Micromix combustors for the Kawasaki
M1A gas turbine of around 7.0 MW/(m2 bar) [22], Fig. 17.

fuel injectors, also the design for fuel ﬂexibility with high-
hydrogen fuel for the Micromix principle became possible.
6
Micromix Dual-Fuel High-Hydrogen Syngas
Application
Starting with the scope of an increased power density with
dual-fuel-feasibility using hydrogen-rich syngas, the work focused
on the GTCP 36-300 gas turbine and continued the combustor
research with the 1.5-ring-Micromix combustor.
Gas turbines for power generation are operated with fuels of a
wide range of qualities and compositions. In power plants with an
integrated gasiﬁcation combined cycle, utilized syngas qualities
are generally a mixture of hydrogen and carbon monoxide, but
their compositions differ depending on the origin of the feedstock.
In the framework of Micromix syngas combustion, a syngas mix-
ture consisting of 90%-vol. H2 and 10%-vol. CO (SG 90/10) was
deﬁned. This composition was chosen, since its heating value and
SAR are in the range of methane [29].
Various atmospheric test burner conﬁgurations and a multitude
of parametric studies with different power densities were
investigated experimentally and numerically to identify key
design parameters for a dual-fuel syngas/hydrogen operation
[20,29,30]. A dual-fuel Micromix prototype combustor with a
power density of around 8 MW/(m2 bar) was ﬁnally realized for
the GTCP 36-300 and successfully integrated into the annular
combustion chamber (Fig. 20).
Testing this sixth generation prototype in the GTCP 36-30 gas
turbine under real operational conditions has proven the feasibility
of a stable Micromix low NOx gas turbine operation, both with
hydrogen and syngas SG 90/10 simultaneously. The dual-fuel,
low emission operation has been realized without hardware adap-
tations of the gas turbine control infrastructure; only minor adjust-
ments in the fuel settings of the control software for a safe engine
startup and load operation are necessary.

Micromix Hydrogen-Methane Fuel-Flexibility
Power production based on conventional renewable energy
sources like solar or wind energy is strongly weather dependent.
Thus, the expansion of renewables requires energy storage tech-
nologies for the compensation of the ﬂuctuating and unpredictable
energy output of these sources.
An example is the power-to-gas application, using excess
renewable energy for electrolyzing hydrogen from water or pro-
ducing methane from hydrogen and carbon dioxide by methana-
tion. The synthesized gases are combustible and can be used for
reconversion into electrical energy by gas turbines [31,32].
For the transition from the current natural gas infrastructure to
a reliable, sustainable, ecological, and economic hydrogen-based
energy supply, bridge technologies are needed, e.g., by blending
increasing amounts of hydrogen into the natural gas grid [33].
With an increase of hydrogen in the fuel mixture, the emission
of CO2 is reduced (Fig. 22). For a reduction of the CO2 emissions
by 50%, the gas turbine fuel must contain approx. 76.5%; only a
hydrogen-rich fuel combustion is an effective way of reducing
CO2 emissions.
Therefore, ﬂexible fuel operation of the Micromix combustion
system with variable hydrogen-methane mixtures up to 68.5%
methane content was the next research focus.
The challenges in the ﬂexible fuel adaption of the Micromix
combustion system are the combustion properties of the applied
fuel mixtures that change signiﬁcantly over the investigated fuel
range. This variability in combination with the interdependency of
geometric and operational parameters required a multitude of
parametric investigations to examine the inﬂuences on combus-
tion stability and exhaust gas emissions.
For experimental validation of the Micromix combustion char-
acteristics under ﬂexible fuel operation, the exhaust gas composi-
tion
at
variable
operating
conditions
is
measured
in
an
atmospheric test rig. In order to accommodate ﬂexible fuel
mixtures of hydrogen and methane, a gas mixing facility was inte-
grated into the test stand environment that continuously controls
the component mass ﬂows of methane and hydrogen and mixes
both streams in a static mixer [34].
For interpretation of the experimental results, combustion and
ﬂow simulations are used, which also aid at understanding the
underlying physical mechanisms. For a reliable numerical predic-
tion of the combustion characteristics, the accompanying combus-
tion and ﬂow simulations were successively enhanced throughout
recent years.
Starting with simple eddy-break-up combustion models that
applied a single chemical reaction for hydrogen combustion [29],
higher ﬁdelity combustion models were used [35]. In Ref. [30],
the feasibility of complex chemistry combustion models applying
extended reaction schemes has been tested for hydrogen and syn-
gas operation. Since the numerical effort rises with the number of
transported species, approaches for reducing the computational
effort for large reaction schemes have been tested. Here, the
Flamelet generated manifold model proved to greatly reduce the
computational effort while maintaining most of the accuracy of a
complex chemistry calculation.
Evolving from pure hydrogen and syngas combustion, the
numerical approach had to be enhanced to accommodate ﬂexible
fuel mixtures of hydrogen and methane. For identiﬁcation of suita-
ble numerical combustion models capable of predicting the Micro-
mix combustion process fueled with hydrogen/methane mixtures
accurately, a numerical study based on the bluff-body stabilized
ﬂame HM1 by the University of Sydney was conducted [36].
The study identiﬁed the 22 species DRM22 reaction mechanism
[37] as best compromise between modeling accuracy and calcula-
tion time.
In the latest numerical approach applied in Micromix combus-
tor research, this reaction mechanism is used to account for ﬂexi-
ble fuel operation with hydrogen methane mixtures. The reactive
ﬂow regime is solved by a three-dimensional, steady, pressure-
based RANS solver using the realizable k–e-turbulence model
with all yþ wall treatment [34].
To overcome certain limitations of the RANS approach con-
cerning the prediction of turbulence effects in the jet-in-crossﬂow
mixing regime on NOx formation, also ﬁrst large eddy simulations
have been successfully applied [38].
The numerical combustor model incorporates a ﬂuid region for
the combustion and ﬂow simulation and solid regions that account
for conjugate heat transfer through the combustor walls. For the
spatial discretization, adaptive mesh reﬁnement, based on several
solution properties, is applied [35].
Micromix fuel ﬂex prototype combustors were successfully
operated over a wide fuel mixture range. Initial testing under
atmospheric pressure conditions conﬁrmed satisfactory opera-
tional behavior with high combustion efﬁciency, low NOx emis-
sions, and typical Micromix ﬂame characteristics
even at
increased methane levels [34,39].
Figure 23 shows the visual ﬂame appearance of Micromix
ﬂames with four different fuel mixtures at constant thermal power
output. Despite the differing combustion characteristics between
the fuels, the images show clearly the separated miniaturized
ﬂamelets as required for Micromix combustion.
The methane content in the fuel correlates to the visual axial
expansion of the ﬂame. Hydrogen ﬂames are small and become
larger as the methane content in the fuel is increased. The change
of the ﬂame shape and size is a result of the reactivity of the
applied fuel. With increasing amount of highly reacting hydrogen
in the fuel mixture, heat release rates are greatly increased
[34,39]. This results in elevated peak temperatures that facilitate
NOx formation by the thermal NO pathway. This is underlined by
the resulting NOx emission measured in the exhaust gas jet, dis-
played in Fig. 24. For the numerical prediction, a thermal NO
model was used.

ation with a single combustor geometry, the results show low NOx

The applied staging sequence of the conceptual M1A three-

with methane as a startup fuel. At 30% rotational speed, hydrogen
is added in the middle ring up to 100% rotational speed. The
hydrogen ﬂames show stable combustion for all operating points
between 20% and 80% rotational speed (Fig. 26(b)). At low load

the NOx target values of 35 ppm (at 16% O2), which poses half of
the air pollution control act requirement of Japan, against a low
total pressure loss ratio (TPLR). Reducing the pressure loss of the
air jet required intensive aerodynamic optimization for stabilizing
the Micromix ﬂames, and therefore assuring low NOx emissions
in all operating points, Fig. 27. Several high-pressure combustion
tests were conducted, which conﬁrmed NOx emissions below
35 ppm (16% O2) during all part- and full-load conditions
(Fig. 27) at M1A operating conditions. These tests conﬁrmed sta-

rotational speed conditions, and ﬂame stability for fuel ﬂuctuation
at 100% load conditions including sudden increase/decrease tests

ing points and within acceptable limits. Also, endurance full-load

9
Conclusion
The Micromix combustion system is investigated and opti-
mized for over 30 years and features a low NOx, fuel-ﬂexible
combustion with inherent safety against ﬂashback. While the ini-
tial research work focused on pure gaseous hydrogen in aero gas
turbines, the scope of work gradually shifted to an expanded range
of applications in the power generation industry and for fuel ﬂexi-
bility toward high-hydrogen syngas like in integrated gasiﬁcation
combined cycle-power-plants and methane-hydrogen-mixtures.
In the ﬁeld of design optimization and gas turbine implementa-
tion, Micromix combustors can be integrated into annular com-
bustion chambers as well as in can-casing types; the feasible
integration power density ranges from 5 up to 14 MW/(m2bar). In
all ranges of operation, the proven low NOx characteristics can
reliably be maintained. This opens the opportunity of a wide range
of Micromix applications even in gas turbines with several can-
combustors installed. The work also highlights that the limits of
the Micromix principle are not reached yet.
The extensive knowledge and database of Micromix design
laws for stable low NOx combustion ensures a deep understanding
of the physical mechanisms of Micromix combustion processes,
aerodynamic ﬂame stabilization, and the validation of numerical
approaches for future optimizations and applications. The demon-
stration plant in Kobe, Japan, will offer an insight in durability
and gas turbine effects on the Micromix combustion principle.
