Toward the Development of an 
NH3-RRQL System—Part II: 
Effects of the Primary Combustion 
Zone Length and Secondary Stage 
Number of Holes on Stability 
and Emissions
Staged combustion systems like the rich–relaxation–quench–lean (RRQL) offer the potential 
for low nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions while burning ammonia (NH3). This process 
involves rich premixed NH3–air combustion in a primary zone, allowing time for NOx 
relaxation, followed by a lean secondary combustion zone via air injection. However, 
improper design of the secondary stage can lead to high NOx and nitrous oxide (N2O) 
emissions, offsetting the climate benefits of carbon-free fuels. This study investigates the 
effects of primary zone length and secondary stage geometry on the stability and emissions of 
a lab-scale RRQL system. Experiments were conducted at atmospheric pressure using a 
modular axial swirl burner (swirl number ¼ 1.1, 16 vanes), with primary equivalence ratios 
(/primary) of 1.13, 1.15, and 1.18. Two quartz lengths (76 and 178 mm) were tested using a 
five holes (2.03 mm) secondary injection design. Strong flame interaction and elevated NOx 
were observed for the 76 mm liner. A longer chamber allowed better NOx relaxation and NH3 
cracking. Additional tests with 5-, 10-, and 16-holes configurations showed that fewer holes, 
implying higher momentum flux ratios, yielded lower NOx–N2O emissions, especially at 
/primary ¼ 1.13. These effects diminished as /primary decreased from 1.18 down to the 
optimum 1.13. Diffusion-like combustion was seen for 0.90  /global  1.10, leading to 
inefficient combustion marked by excess O2 values compared with equilibrium. Optimal 
performance was achieved with /primary ¼ 1.13 and 0.70  /global  0.90, with estimated 
burner outlet temperatures between 1720 and 1970 K. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4069538] 
Introduction
Hydrogen (H2) as fuel for electrical power generation is an 
important vector toward a zero-carbon economy, if solutions are 
found to alleviate its main limitations, including low volumetric 
density, high risk of explosion, and storage and transport [1]. 
Instead, ammonia (NH3), a H2 carrier, has attracted a lot of attention 
in recent years owing to its mature and well-established infra­
structures for production, storage, and distribution [2,3]. Many 
countries, such as Japan, South Korea, Australia, and U.S., to name a 
few, are now funding research programs to promote NH3 for energy. 
The major challenges of burning NH3 are its very low reactivity, 
which can be overcome by blending with methane (CH4) or H2 to 
stabilize the flame [4–6], and the very high NOx emission [7–9]. For 
example, researchers found that the NOx emission level is 
unacceptably high when burning NH3 at the conditions under which 
most gas turbines operate [10–13]. Moreover, NH3 is toxic and 
corrosive, raising additional difficulties when operating combustion 
devices with this fuel. However, blending NH3 with other fuels 
could be challenging from an infrastructural point of view, and very 
few studies are available about pure NH3–air combustion for gas 
turbines.
Rich NH3–air and NH3–H2/CH4–air premixed flames with 
equivalence ratios around 1.10 to 1.30 are known to yield marginal 
NOx emissions without needing to resort to selective catalytic

reduction [13,14]. However, these conditions lead to unburned NH3, 
H2, and/or CH4, so a second combustion stage is needed to oxidize 
all the fuel. This strategy is typically referred to as rich–quench–lean 
[15–17] or two-stage combustion [18,19], which relies in a NH3 
flame with rich primary equivalence ratio (/primary) to produce low 
NOx, and then a secondary air is injected to burn out the remaining 
fuel in a secondary lean combustion zone, producing a lean global 
equivalence ratio (/global).
Tu et al. [20] experimentally and numerically studied 
NH3–CH4–air premixed swirling flames with air-staging showing 
that using 30% of the air budget for the secondary stage, which 
produced a /primary ¼ 1.21, was the optimum staged-air ratio for low 
NO emissions and high combustion efficiency. However, they 
demonstrated that NO emissions could reach high values either by 
injecting secondary air too close to the primary flame or by setting 
too high staged-air ratios. Finally, high jet-to-crossflow momentum 
flux ratio (J) could produce air jets impinging on each other, creating 
an upstream recirculation which was found to negatively affect the 
NO emissions. A˚ nestad et al. [21] investigated the flame structure 
and emissions performance in a two-stage lab-scale generic 
combustor design operated with NH3–H2 blends. They found 
minimum NOx emissions at /primary  1.15, which occurred when 
excess NH3 in the main stage was largely converted through 
pyrolysis to H2, which was then burnt off with additional air in the 
second stage. However, in both cases, using constant fuel and air 
budgets and then splitting the air into primary and secondary streams 
changes the /primary for each splitting ratio, making it difficult to 
isolate the effects of diverse parameters to compare between cases, 
and conclude about the sole impact of the secondary stage on 
exhaust emissions and flame stability.
Avila Jimenez et al. [22] investigated the effects of secondary air 
injection on the emissions and stability of two-stage NH3–CH4–air 
swirl flames using a lab-scale microgas turbine burner. They 
demonstrated that having more secondary air holes for the same air 
addition could reduce NO emissions. In addition, they demonstrated 
for the first time that there is a secondary air addition threshold 
(which depends on the secondary air injection geometry) above 
which primary flame instabilities are triggered, which increased the 
NO emissions prior to blowout. Recently, Kim et al. [23] explored 
how secondary air injection impacts the flame structure and NO 
emission characteristics in turbulent premixed CH4–NH3–air flames 
within a two-stage swirl model combustor. They discovered that 
minimal NO emissions, along with low CO emissions, occur when 
there is weak interaction between the two combustion zones and 
when the secondary air jet has a moderate velocity. Nonetheless, 
there has been limited investigation into the effects of other 
parameters on flame stability beyond just the secondary air flow rate.
Takagi et al. [24] performed staged combustion experiments by 
adding NH3 to a hydrocarbon (C3H8) and nonhydrocarbon 
(CO þ H2) fuel blend. They demonstrated that the conversion of 
NH3 (only product for NH3–nonhydrocarbon blend) into NO in the 
lean secondary combustion stage is much lower than that for HCN 
(additional product from NH3–hydrocarbon blend). Therefore, 
mixing hydrocarbons to stabilize rich NH3 flames could not be a 
good strategy due to the possible HCN emissions from the rich 
primary stage that can potentially increase the NO formation in the 
secondary stage.
Cole et al. [25] recently introduced the rich, relaxation, quench, 
lean (RRQL) strategy for NH3 combustion. The experimental setup 
designed in Ref. [25] was upgraded with a redesigned modular 
swirler, for which the procedure is discussed in Part I of this study. 
This paper (Part II) presents an experimental study of the impact of 
the combustion chamber length and the secondary air injection 
number of holes on combustion stability and exhaust emissions. 
First, two quartz liners with 76 mm and 178 mm length were 
implemented for the primary combustion zone while injecting 
secondary air through a secondary stage geometry with five holes of 
2.03 mm diameter. Then, three different secondary stage geometries 
with 5-, 10-, and 16-holes were tested with the 178 mm long quartz 
were studied.
Materials and Methods
Burner and Conditions. Experiments were conducted with a 
laboratory scale modular burner, shown in Fig. 1. The burner 
consists of a modular swirler, and the swirler center body houses a 
hydrogen torch and spark ignitor which is used only to ignite the 
premixed NH3–air. This burner simulates the rich, relaxation, and 
lean secondary stage combustion sections of an RRQL NH3 
combustor, and the reader can refer to Ref. [25] for further details 
about the burner head end. The burner has been designed also to

Two experimental campaigns were conducted. First, the com­
bustion chamber length impact on emissions and flame stability was 
studied. Two quartz lengths of 76 mm and 178 mm were 
implemented for the primary combustion zone while injecting 
secondary air through a secondary stage geometry of five holes with 
2.03-mm diameter, as shown in Fig. 1. The /global was decreased to 
around 0.50 or flame blowout. Time-averaged broadband flame 
images of the primary combustion zone were recorded with a DSLR 
camera to characterize the interaction between the secondary and 
primary flames. The camera was set with an exposure time of 1/8 s, 
an aperture of f/8, and an ISO number of 800. White balance was 
kept constant at 4000 K (white, fluorescent light) throughout the 
experimental campaigns.
Second, the exhaust emissions for different number of holes in the 
secondary stage were investigated. Three different secondary stage 
geometries with 5-, 10-, and 16-holes were tested with the 178 mm 
long quartz. Time-averaged broadband flame images of the 
secondary combustion zone were recorded with the same DSLR 
camera from a bottom view (see Fig. 1, right-hand side) to 
characterize the secondary combustion zone. The camera settings 
were changed to an exposure time of 2 s, an aperture of f/8, and an 
ISO number of 1600.
Equivalence ratios and the nozzle velocity are calculated in real- 
time using the mass flow ratios for the fuel and air circuits. The mass 
flow rates are metered by pressure drop through critical orifices and 
controlled using manual and electronic pressure regulators to 
control upstream pressure. An upstream pressure transducer and 
thermocouple in combination with a downstream pressure trans­
ducer allow the operator to ensure sonic flow for effective metering. 
An additional thermocouple is located upstream of the swirler to 
track the reactant temperature. Constant nozzle velocity of 
U0 ¼ 0.90 m/s was utilized and defined using the reactant density, 
effective nozzle area, and reactant mass flow. The current air and 
fuel flow control setup leads to accuracy for the /global better than 
1%.
Table 1 summarizes the experimental procedure followed. There 
is a blockage plate on the top of the top quartz (see Fig. 1) to prevent 
backflow or interactions with surrounding air. The long quartz is 
meant to allow NOx emissions to relax toward equilibrium with 
residence times around 100 ms, depending on the nozzle velocity 
and expansion ratio. As shown in Ref. [26], these values should 
enable the capture of the first order of magnitude NO relaxation at 
1 bar and nearly the full approach to equilibrium at 10 bar.
Exhaust Emissions. Exhaust emissions data are collected using a 
California Analytical Instruments 700 LX Series NDIR/O2 Ana­
lyzer for O2 and 700 FTIR multigas analyzer with NOx, NH3, and 
N2O measurement capabilities. An area-weighted, water-cooled 
rake probe was installed 76 mm downstream from the secondary 
stage holes plane, and the reader is referred to Ref. [25] for additional 
information about the sampling probe design. The probe is complete 
with thermocouples on the water supply, water return, and sample 
lines to ensure that the sample is properly quenched to freeze NOx 
reaction chemistry and to avoid water condensation in the sampling 
line. Additionally, the sample temperature is closely monitored, and 
the emissions sample line between the probe and analyzer is heated 
to 150 C. Exhaust emissions were normalized by the standard of 
15% dry O2 [27]. All the raw (non-normalized by the standard of 
15% dry O2) emissions data are plotted as Supplemental Materials
on the ASME Digital Collection. The exhaust emission gas 
analyzers are calibrated before every run using span gas with a 
known concentration for O2, CO, CO2, NO, NO2, N2O, and NH3. In 
addition, exhaust emissions concentration is contrasted using a 
standard well-known flame every time before starting an experi­
mental campaign. By this process, it is confirmed that the accuracy 
of the reported data is within the accuracy of the gas analyzers 
provided by the company (California Analytical Instruments, 
Orange, CA). Unfortunately, the combustion efficiency could not 
be calculated due to lack of H2 capability in the current gas analyzers 
setup. As an alternative for this metric, the equilibrium O2 was 
computed and compared with the measured values. In this sense, 
higher combustion efficiency is rated by the proximity of measured 
O2 to equilibrium O2, i.e., higher measured O2 than equilibrium 
indicates lower combustion efficiency.
Results and Discussion
Long Versus Short Quartz. Figure 2 shows the concentration of 
NOx (top panel), N2O (middle panel), and NH3 (bottom panel) in the 
exhaust as a function of /global for the two combustion liners’ length 
tested (76 mm and 178 mm). Solid and dashed lines show the results 
for long and short liners, respectively. Lower operability range was 
obtained with the short liner compared to the longer one. The lowest 
/global achieved with the short liner before primary flame blowout 
was /global ¼ 0.7660.02, regardless of the /primary tested, although 
exhaust emissions were recorded only until /global ¼ 0.80 (or higher) 
due to primary flame instabilities. These flame instabilities occurred 
due to strong interaction between the secondary stage with the 
primary flame (see Fig. 3). In contrast, stable primary flame was 
observed until /global ¼ 0.50 with the long liner (/global < 0.50 could 
not be studied due to air supply limitations). This means that longer 
combustion liner is desirable for RRQL NH3 combustion system 
since the lean stability limit is highly increased. It is also concluded 
that implementing a short liner, which is closer to a retrofitted 
burner, not only carries flame instabilities for lean /global but also 
higher NOx emissions.

the rich flames (no secondary air addition) regardless of the 
combustion liner length. However, higher NOx concentration was 
measured with the short liner compared to the long one at the same 
/global when secondary air was supplied. One explanation, as 
described later by analyzing primary flame images, is that strong 
interactions between the secondary and primary combustion zones 
may produce local lean pockets, conditions that are well-known to

the secondary combustion zone and the primary rich flame, and as

flame is produced with the short liner when adding secondary air, 
explaining again the higher measured NOx concentration. Although 
slightly higher N2O and NH3 emissions are produced with the long 
liner, it is observed that N2O emissions are sub-10 ppm and NH3 are 
negligible for /primary ¼ 1.13 and 1.15 for /global < 1.0. Therefore, 
the long liner is better not only for lower NOx but higher flame 
stability. Finally, it is important to note that /primary ¼ 1.13 yields 
the lowest exhaust emissions with the long liner for all the lean 
/global tested.
Figure 3 presents the flame images of the primary flame for the 
short (top row) and long (bottom row) liners as a function of the 
/global for a /primary ¼ 1.18. This /primary was selected since it allows 
for easier secondary flame observation by the eye, but the same trend 
is followed by flames with /primary ¼ 1.13 and 1.15. It is observed 
that the secondary combustion zone, denoted by the curved white 
line, grows and travels upstream by decreasing /global, regardless of 
the combustion liner length. However, due to the reduced distance 
between both stages, the secondary combustion zone interacts with 
the primary flame while using the short liner. This interaction 
triggers pulsating flame instabilities that eventually lead to flame 
blowout. This behavior was reported recently by Avila Jimenez et al. 
[22] for a different combustor architecture. These interactions also 
could produce slightly lean pockets that might explain the higher 
NOx [20,23] and lower NH3 emissions measured (see Fig. 2), and 
produce higher local temperatures, explaining also the lower N2O 
emissions. In contrast, the secondary combustion zone does not 
interact with the primary flame for the long liner as observed in the 
bottom row for a wider /global range, until 0.70. Interactions between 
both combustion zones were only noticeable at far lean conditions 
/global < 0.70. Although flame instabilities were not observed, these 
interactions could still create local lean pockets, explaining the 
increase in NOx at the leaner /global (see Fig. 2, top panel). 
Therefore, the next section (Number of Holes) is presented only for 
the 178 mm long quartz.
Number of Holes. Figure 4 shows the concentration of NOx (top 
panel), N2O (middle panel), and NH3 (bottom panel) in the exhaust 
as a function of /global for the three geometries and /primary tested. 
The different colors are for different /primary, and the solid, dashed, 
and dotted lines are for 5-, 10-, and 16-holes in the secondary stage, 
respectively. Once again, very low NOx emissions (sub-12 ppm) 
were measured for the rich flames (no secondary air addition) 
regardless of secondary geometry. However, NOx emissions 
increase by decreasing /global for all geometries. Even though there 
are no flame instabilities or strong combustion zones interactions, it 
is observed that higher NOx in the secondary combustion zone is 
produced as a function of the /primary. This is explained by the much 
higher unburned NH3 (see Fig. 4, bottom panel) available for the 
secondary stage combustion zone as /primary increases. It is 
important to note that the improvement in NOx emissions by 
implementing a longer combustor liner for the primary flame could 
be canceled by poor control of the /primary and poor design of the 
secondary combustion zone.
For most cases, a monotonic trend is observed for NOx and N2O 
(Fig. 4, middle panel). It was found that /primary ¼ 1.13 produced the 
lowest NOx emissions for over the lean cases. In addition, the 
secondary stage geometry plays a role. Lower number of holes 
yields better NO–N2O emissions. However, these differences in NO 
and N2O emissions between geometries are more noticeable by 
increasing /primary. This means that there is a lower impact of the 
secondary stage geometry on emissions when /primary is tuned.
Figure 4 (bottom panel) shows that the unburned NH3 is rapidly 
consumed in the secondary combustion zone for /primary ¼ 1.13 and 
1.15, conditions that yield zero NH3 emissions. However, unburned 
NH3 is still measurable for /primary ¼ 1.18, explaining the higher NO 
and N2O emissions for this condition. It is acknowledged that high 
heat losses through the walls can be responsible for part of the NH3 
emissions and lowering the heat losses by implementing a metal 
liner and using high preheat temperatures could bring NH3 even 
lower and enhance the H2 production for the secondary stage. It is 
concluded that /primary ¼ 1.13 is the optimum primary equivalence 
ratio, and 0.70  /global  0.90 seems to be an optimum operability 
range for the RRQL system using the current burner. This /global 
range produces a theoretical burner outlet gas temperature of around 
1720 K  /global  1970 K. It is acknowledged that gas turbines, 
especially the ones fitted with dry low NOx combustors, run at lower 
/global to control turbine inlet temperature (TIT). However, TIT 
could be modified in practice by placing a dilution stage downstream 
the secondary lean combustion stage, which is out of the scope of the 
present study. Nevertheless, the authors acknowledge that with NH3 
as fuel, the lower /global studied are relevant to J-class gas turbines.

increasing J, produces a relocation of the secondary combustion

The comparison of flame images and exhaust emissions indicates 
that partially premixed-like combustion, with a /global slightly 
leaner than the transition value from diffusion-like (avoiding the 
interaction between both combustion zones), is an optimal 
secondary combustion process. It is also concluded based on the 
current results that high J that allows for a fast transition from 
diffusion-like to partially premixed-like combustion regime is key 
for the design of the secondary air injection geometry for an efficient 
RRQL system.

Conclusions
An experimental study was carried out to understand the impact of 
the primary combustion zone length and secondary stage number of 
holes on stability and emissions of rich premixed NH3–air flames 
under the RRQL system. The main conclusions are as follows:
 Long combustion chamber for the primary flame is better for 
lower NOx and higher primary flame stability. This reduces the 
primary and secondary flames interaction which produces first 
higher NOx emissions and then primary flame blowout if /global 
is reduced from a certain threshold (approximately 
/global ¼ 0.76 for a 76 mm combustor liner).
 For the tested burner, an optimum primary equivalence ratio 
was found to be /primary ¼ 1.13. This value not only allowed for 
a stable primary flame but also produced simultaneous low 
NOx and NH3 emissions.
 The secondary stage geometry plays a role on overall exhaust 
emissions. It was found that lower number of holes yields better 
NOx–N2O emissions. However, these differences between 
geometries observed for the exhaust emissions are more 
noticeable when increasing /primary, probably due to the higher 
unburned NH3 available for the secondary combustion zone.
 Diffusion-like combustion was found at low J values (/global 
0.85, depending on the number of holes) producing higher O2 
concentration in the flue gases than expected from equilibrium 
calculations. This implies that such a combustion regime is not 
desirable due to lower combustion efficiency. Instead, 
premixed-like combustion was observed for higher secondary 
air additions, reaching an optimum operating range of 0.70 
/global  0.90, which allows for a theoretical turbine inlet 
temperature range of TIT ¼ 1720–1970 K.
The key takeaways are that a longer combustion chamber will not 
only increase the residence time for NOx to reach equilibrium and 
crack unburned NH3 but also increase the primary flame stability, 
increasing the operability range. In addition, we found that it is 
crucial to tune the rich primary flame equivalence ratio and then 
inject enough secondary air to avoid inverse-diffusion flames 
forming and instead reach a premixed-like secondary combustion 
zone for lower NOx emissions.
