Effect of Exhaust Gas 
Recirculation on Combustion 
Efficiency in a Swirl Stabilized 
Flame With Blends of Natural Gas 
and Hydrogen
Reducing the carbon impact of power-generation and industrial gas turbines can be 
achieved through blending of low-carbon fuels as well as carbon capture. In gas turbines, 
carbon capture is facilitated by the use of exhaust gas recirculation (EGR), where exhaust 
gases are mixed with inlet gases to increase carbon dioxide concentrations in the exhaust, 
which in turn increases the efficiency of carbon capture technologies. However, increasing 
the diluent fraction of the reactants can decrease flame static stability and lead to flame 
elongation, potentially affecting combustion efficiency and emissions. In this study, we 
measure emissions in a single-nozzle model combustor at a range of EGR levels and 
compositions for blends of natural gas (NG) and hydrogen (H2) to understand the impact of 
EGR on combustion efficiency. Different blends of diluents that mimic the effects of EGR are 
tested with oxygen mole fractions from 21% (no diluent injection) down to 15%. Blends of 
NG and H2 are tested with up to 40% H2 by volume. Flame imaging is used to better 
understand the connection between EGR, fuel composition, flame stabilization, and 
combustion efficiency. As EGR level increases, the flame becomes longer and more diffuse. 
H2 blending aids flame stabilization and combustion efficiency. The experimental results are 
complemented by detailed chemical kinetic modeling to identify the changes in reaction 
pathways that are driven by high levels of diluents and various fuel compositions. We 
conclude with a discussion of the impact of low combustion efficiency on both cycle efficiency 
as well as the performance of downstream carbon capture systems.

Keywords: exhaust gas recirculation, diluents, hydrogen combustion, emissions 
1 Introduction
In recent years, there has been a push to reduce the impact caused 
by greenhouse gas emissions, specifically carbon dioxide (CO2). In 
the United States, over a third of CO2 emissions are emitted by the 
power generation sector, with approximately 30% of electricity 
generation coming from the combustion of natural gas (NG) [1,2]. 
Within this sector, gas turbine engines are one of the key 
technologies that rely on combustion of NG. To reduce the carbon 
impact of gas turbines, a transition toward net-zero emissions has 
been receiving increasing attention. To this end, various technol­
ogies and scenarios are being developed. For example, the use of 
low-carbon intensity fuels, such as hydrogen (H2), can result in 
lower CO2 emissions. Several demonstrations of hydrogen blending 
into natural gas in power-generation gas turbines have shown that 
many machines can operate normally with up to 40% hydrogen 
[3–5]. Alternatively, the deployment of carbon capture and storage 
(CCS) technologies can reduce the CO2 emissions of gas turbines 
running on carbon-bearing fuels such as NG. However, these 
solutions can pose issues in terms of engine operability, specifically 
for the combustion process.
The use of these decarbonization strategies in modern gas turbine 
engines designed for natural gas operation can have detrimental 
impacts on combustor operability and cycle performance. For 
example, the use of H2 fuel can modify the thermoacoustic 
instability characteristics of a combustor, increase thermal loading, 
and can potentially lead to flashback issues [6–8]. On the other hand, 
the use of CCS can impact on the overall efficiency and performance 
of a plant. To enable CCS as an economically feasible solution, 
exhaust gas recirculation (EGR) is used. When using EGR, 
combustion products are recirculated to the engine inlet, which 
results in an increase in the CO2 concentration at the outlet of the 
engine. This increase in CO2 concentration improves the 
efficiency of CCS technologies [9–12]. However, the increased 
fraction of diluents in the oxidizer stream can affect the combustion 
process.

The use of EGR can reduce the overall reactivity of the reactants 
by either affecting the chemical kinetic rates or by changing the 
thermophysical properties of the mixture in the combustor, with 
different EGR composition affecting the combustion process in 
different ways [13–15]. The effects of EGR can result in decreased 
flame speed, poor flameholding, and changes in dynamic and static 
stability, which can result in flame blowout depending on the 
concentration and composition of EGR used [15–21].
The use of both alternative fuels and EGR has been studied for 
certain operating conditions and injector configurations. Research 
has shown that the use of H2 and EGR can by themselves, drastically 
change the combustion process and their combined effect can have 
additional effects on the combustion behavior of a system [14,21]. 
For example, Galeotti et al. [21] considered the improvements of a 
CO2-diluted NG flame with a nonpremixed H2 pilot and how it 
affects emissions. They showed an improvement in flame stability 
and CO emissions over baseline NG conditions, demonstrating the 
potential for decarbonizing gas turbines through combined EGR and 
H2. Cycle analysis studies have demonstrated the possibility of 
benefiting from both H2 and EGR solutions without sacrificing 
engine performance [10]. Additionally, some researchers have 
looked at the economic performance and challenges of H2 and EGR 
combined for improving the performance of CCS technologies and 
have shown desirable performance compared to baseline cases. 
These benefits can be particularly important for load-following gas 
turbines where carbon-based fuels can be used while still 
maintaining low emissions [9,10]. As such, gas turbine decarbon­
ization with EGR and H2 is a solution that merits further evaluation.
Our previous work showed the effects of various levels and 
compositions of EGR on a neutrally piloted, swirl-stabilized model 
combustor operating on NG [15]. Under the conditions tested, the 
flame becomes elongated and dynamically stable at intermediate 
oxygen (O2) levels. However, at the lower O2 conditions, the flame 
exhibits what seems to be a nonthermoacoustic instability most 
likely driven by extinction and reignition behavior. This work was 
extended to H2-enriched natural gas flames, where the same 
conditions were tested with up to 40% H2 by volume [20]. The 
addition of H2 noticeably improved the static and dynamic stability 
of the lower O2 conditions, demonstrating the benefits of combining 
both EGR and H2 as viable methods for decarbonizing gas turbine 
engines. Our prior work, however, did not evaluate the emission 
performance for these test conditions. As the EGR level increases, 
particularly for low levels of H2, the flame is quite long. We expect 
that the combustion efficiency at these conditions may be subpar 
compared to the baseline cases, which would result in an increase of 
carbon monoxide (CO) and unburned hydrocarbon (UHC) emis­
sions. The effects on emissions from the combination of H2 and EGR 
are different for different species: CO and UHC tend to increase with 
EGR and decrease with H2, while the trend for nitric oxide (NO) is a 
reduction with EGR and an increase with H2 [18,19,21]. Under­
standing how the H2 and EGR interactions can affect combustor 
emissions is a crucial step toward understanding the viability of 
these techniques for reducing CO2 emissions.
The goal of this work is to quantify the impact of dilution level and 
composition as well as H2 level on combustion efficiency for a 
constant flame temperature test matrix. In this study, combustion 
efficiency is obtained by measuring emissions for stable flame 
conditions, eliminating the impact of thermoacoustic instabilities on 
the flame and its emissions. Combustion efficiency is defined as the 
ratio of the CO2 level measured in the combustor to the equilibrium 
CO2 level calculated using chemical kinetic modeling. Results show 
that the combustion efficiency is lowest for the high dilution cases. 
CO emissions are also highest at the lowest oxygen condition tested. 
The addition of H2 in the fuel results in a moderate decrease in the 
CO emissions at each condition tested. Comparison of flame images 
between cases shows that the time-averaged flame shape has a 
relationship with the emission outputs, where highly diffuse flames 
have low efficiency and high CO emissions. Companion chemical 
kinetic analysis shows agreement with the trends in CO2 and 
combustion efficiency, providing insight into the changes in 
chemical pathways that occur with increasing diluents in the 
oxidizer and H2 in the fuel. To interpret experimental results, 
perfectly stirred reactor (PSR) calculations are performed to 
elucidate the experimentally determined combustion efficiency 
and its impact of unburned hydrocarbon emissions. Reaction 
pathway analysis is used to identify the ways in which the diluents 
affect the combustion efficiency and resultant pollutant emissions.
2 Experimental Overview
2.1 Experimental Facility. This work is done in an experi­
mental rig equipped with a dual-fuel injector compatible with liquid 
and gaseous fuel; our previous work with the same test matrix used a 
different, gaseous-only fuel injector [15,20]. The experimental rig, 
shown in Fig. 1, is a variable-length combustor that consists of a 
single-nozzle model combustor with a dual-fuel injector installed in 
a settling chamber. In this study, only gaseous fuels are used (NG 
and H2). The injector consists of a main circuit with swirler vanes 
and a central pilot circuit inside a centerbody. The rig is operated at 
atmospheric pressure and the oxidizer mixture (air, CO2, and N2) is 
preheated to 250 C using an 88 kW electrical heater. The oxidizer 
mixture is delivered to the settling chamber through a choke plate 
where it then branches into the main and pilot circuits. Fuel is 
delivered directly to the injector, with the main and pilot circuits 
independently controlled. The fuel is mixed with the air in the 
injector, resulting in a technically premixed flame. The gases (fuel 
and oxidizer mixture) are metered using thermal mass flow meters. 
Approximately 5% of the gases are delivered to the pilot circuit with 
the remaining fraction delivered through the main annular passage. 
In this study, the combustor is operated with neutral piloting, where 
both the main flame and the pilot flame have the same equivalence 
ratio (/). The injector configuration is representative of industrial 
hardware used in operational gas turbine engines.
Downstream of the injector is an optically accessible quartz 
combustor with a length of 34 cm. Further downstream is a metallic, 
variable-length exhaust section where a water-cooled piston can be 
moved between combustor lengths (Lcomb) of 68.5 cm and 132 cm. 
The combustor length is the distance from the injector outlet to the 
piston. Moving the piston changes the acoustic modes of the 
combustor, allowing for a survey of acoustic behavior at each 
operating condition. In the current study, the combustor is initially 
tested at various combustor lengths for all operating conditions and 
only one combustor length that has a thermoacoustically stable 
flame for all operating conditions is used.
A representative stability map for the combustor is shown in 
Fig. 2; this map was taken at a variety of combustor lengths at the 
baseline condition (no diluents) for 0% H2 in NG and 40% H2 in NG. 
Figure 2 also shows the frequency in the spectrum with the highest 
amplitude. At most combustor lengths, this frequency is in the 
200–500 Hz range, which is typical for a longitudinal mode of this 
rig. The rig exhibits instability at the longest combustor lengths, 
where instability is defined as a pressure fluctuation amplitude above 
1% of atmospheric pressure. This mapping was completed for all the 
operating conditions in this study, and a similar instability map was 
seen. For this study, the emission sampling process is conducted for 
a combustor length of approximately 81 cm to avoid thermoacoustic 
instability at any of the operating conditions.
2.2 Diagnostics and Data Processing. The rig is instrumented 
with K-type thermocouples to monitor the gas stream temperature 
ahead of the injector and to maintain the integrity of various 
components downstream of the flame. Additionally, dynamic 
pressure measurements are taken at a port on the dump plane of 
the experiment with a water-cooled dynamic pressure transducer 
(PCB 113B28); this signal is used to determine the thermoacoustic 
stability of the combustor. Additional pressure transducers are 
located in the fuel lines to monitor fluctuations in the fuel. Pressure 
signals are acquired at 8921 Hz for 8 s and evaluated in the frequency 
domain using a fast Fourier transform to identify peaks in the signal.

A Photron Fastcam SA4 camera (Tokyo, Japan) was used with a 
LAVISION intensified relay optics (IRO) intensifier to evaluate the 
time-averaged flame shape by capturing CH* chemiluminescence 
images. The camera is filtered with a 430610 nm bandpass filter. 
For all test conditions, the flame is imaged at 2000 frames per second 
over 1 s. For the stable flames evaluated in this study, the acquired 
image set is sufficient to capture the time-averaged flame shape. The 
intensifier gain settings are such that the flame is clearly observable 
without saturating the sensor. The same intensifier gain settings are 
used for all conditions. The images are time-averaged and an Abel 
inversion process is used to visualize the center slice of the 
axisymmetric flame. First, median and averaging filters are applied 
to the raw images to reduce spatial noise. The flame is considered 
nominally axisymmetric and so the time-average flame shape is 
processed using an Abel inversion algorithm by applying a 
Hankel–Fourier operator [22]. To account for the total heat release 
rate associated with each pixel location, the images are radially 
weighted to produce images representative of CH* emission 
integrated over the azimuthal coordinate.
The emission sampling is done with a Horiba MEXA-584L 
equipped with oxygen (O2), CO2, total hydrocarbon (HC), and 
carbon monoxide (CO) sensors. The analyzer has a repeatability 
of 0.01% volume or 1.7% of the reading for CO and 3.3 ppm or 
1.7% of the reading for HC [23]. The device was calibrated using 
prescribed gas mixtures at the start of each test day and verified 
at the end to measure the drift in calibration. Additionally, the 
sampling line is cleaned at the end of each test day and HC hang- 
up tests are conducted on the line before and after testing. A 
water-cooled, copper-tipped stainless-steel sampling probe is 
attached to a port downstream of the quartz liner in the postflame 
region to obtain samples for all test conditions. The probe design 
has been used in previous studies and has been adequate for 
measuring emissions in the postflame region [24]. The probe is 
located 42 cm downstream of the dump plane but 39 cm ahead of 
the cooled piston, so no water from the piston cooling is expected 
to be captured by the sampling system. The probe is water-cooled 
and the gases are delivered to a filter and a water dropout section 
prior to reaching the analyzer. The cooling water used for the 
probe is kept at approximately 70 C. The analyzer and sampling 
line filters are replaced before every test. The sample has an 
approximate residence time of 1.4 s inside the probe line before 
reaching the analyzer, with approximately 0.02 s of residence 
time in the combustor prior to entering the sampling line. To 
ensure that the emission measurements are representative of each 
test condition, the samples are taken only after the flame and the 
rig temperatures are stable and the analyzer readings have 
stopped fluctuating. Repeatability tests were conducted for each 
condition on different days. In addition to the analyzer display 
values, the measurements are acquired as analog signals directly 
from the analyzer and sampled over a 16 s window. The acquired 
signals are time averaged to obtain a representative value for the 
condition of interest. The time-averaged values are used to verify 
any discrepancies with the analyzer display values during testing. 
During testing, the sampling line is inspected to make sure that 
water is collected in the water dropout portion of the sample line 
and not in the analyzer inlet or the remainder of the sampling 
line.
Before testing in the rig, the analyzer was baselined using a 
McKenna burner [25] operated with methane and air at room 
temperature and pressure. The device was tested with a sweep of 
equivalence ratios from 0.70 to 1.00 as the conditions of interest are 
lean, premixed flames. The results for the emission sampling, 
specifically for CO2, are shown in Fig. 3 as a function of equivalence 
ratio. The analyzer ideally removes all the water, resulting in a dry 
basis measurement. The measured data align well with equilibrium 
values for a dry and wet basis calculated using ANSYS CHEMKIN PRO 
[26] using USC MECH II [27]. The largest difference between 
measurements occurred for an equivalence ratio of 0.80 and is less 
than 3% difference between values, showing good repeatability for 
the measurements. To account for any variability that the analyzer 
can introduce, repeat tests will be conducted for all test conditions on 
different days.
2.3 Chemical Kinetic Modeling. Chemical kinetic modeling 
with CHEMKIN PRO with the USC MECH II [27] was used to calculate the 
initial baselining data for the McKenna burner test of the emission 
sampling device. The equilibrium solver was used to calculate the 
products for all methane/air conditions tested. Additionally, the test 
matrix was also designed using the equilibrium solver in CHEMKIN 
with USC MECH II. The solver was used to calculate the adiabatic flame 
temperature (Tad) and equilibrium product composition at the inlet 
conditions (523 K and 1 atm) for each mixture. The adiabatic flame 
temperature is held constant at roughly 1834 K for the whole test 
matrix; the equivalence ratio is modified for each condition to 
maintain that temperature.
While the equilibrium calculation only uses a chemical 
thermodynamic database, flame behaviors are highly dependent 
on the fidelity of a chemical kinetic model utilized, which is used to 
understand the impact of fuel and diluent composition on emissions. 
To assess the prediction fidelities of chemical kinetic models, four 
other chemical kinetic models were used: GRI 3.0 [28], REAL FUEL 3

Finally, the experiments were performed with natural gas, 
whereas the key combustion properties are derived from using 
pure methane as a surrogate for natural gas. The chemical 
composition of natural gas used in experiments was obtained from 
the local gas company near Penn State. It is composed of 96.793% 
CH4, 3.05% C2H6, 0.139% nC3H8, 0.007% iC4H10, 0.011% nC4H10, 
thus, can be categorized as a dry natural gas. We computed three key 
combustion properties: laminar flame speed, extinction stretch rate, 
and maximum flame speed at the extinction limit. To access the use 
of methane as a surrogate for dry natural gas, the Pearson 
coefficients between methane and dry natural gas for these 
combustion properties are compared and found to be greater than 
0.99, confirming the adequacy of methane as a surrogate for dry 
natural gas.
2.4 Test Matrix. The test matrix for this study focuses on a 
constant adiabatic flame temperature condition. This test matrix 
mimics the operational scenario of a gas turbine engine where 
turbine inlet temperature is maintained as constant. In this case, the 
adiabatic flame temperature is 1834 K, which is the Tad from a 
baseline case used in several previous studies [15,20,33–35]. For 
each diluent and fuel composition, the equivalence ratio is adjusted 
to maintain the Tad within 11 K of the baseline condition. Table 1
shows the test matrix. For each diluent mixture, the hydrogen 
content in the fuel varies from 0% to 40% by volume while the O2 
fraction varies from 21% to 15%. The H2 enrichment of the fuel 
results in a Wobbe Index range of approximately 48–43 MJ/m3, with 
a decrease in the index as the H2 fraction increases to 40% by 
volume. The test matrix consists of a Baseline condition with air as 
the oxidizer along with a set of conditions focused on decreasing the 
O2 fraction in the oxidizer stream by adding higher quantities of N2 
(conditions beginning with “N”) and a set of CO2 (conditions 
beginning with “C”). The naming convention for each test includes 
the diluent condition, the O2 content of the oxidizer, and the 
hydrogen content in the fuel (e.g., N17_H40 is a high-N2 test 
condition with 17% O2 in the oxidizer and 40% H2 in the fuel).
3 Results and Discussion
3.1 Time-Averaged Flame Shape. Figure 6 shows the time- 
averaged, Abel-inverted flame shape as a function of both diluent (in 
columns) and hydrogen level in the fuel (in rows). The top-half of 
each image is provided at each condition and a sketch of the injector 
and combustor liner is indicated in the top-left image. The flame has 
two parts: a main flame, which is stabilized in the inner shear layer of 
the swirling jet and expands outward until it impinges on the wall, as 
a typical V-flame, and a central pilot jet flame, which is visible along 
the centerline in all cases.
The flame has a similar shape for the conditions with an inlet O2 
level above 17%. In all these cases, the main flame is clearly 
stabilized on the centerbody. The main impact of the decreasing 
oxygen content is the length of the flame, which increases as the 
oxygen level decreases. Note that there is not a significant difference 
between the C17 and N17 cases, meaning that the diluent 
composition does not have a significant impact on the flame 
structure at this level of O2. At the 15% O2 conditions, the flame is 
weakly anchored on the centerbody and becomes noticeably

3.2 Emissions Measurements. The combustor emissions, 
particularly CO, CO2, O2, and UHC, were measured at each 
condition. Figure 7 shows the CO2 and CO emissions and Fig. 8
shows the O2 and UHC emissions as a function of inlet oxygen level 
condition for the three levels of H2 in the fuel. In the case of CO2, 
trends match those predicted from equilibrium calculations, 
presented in Table 1. For a given O2 level, the high-CO2 cases 
have proportionally higher CO2 cases than the high-N2 cases, as was 
intended in the test matrix. Additionally, the amount of CO2 at each 
condition decreases with increasing H2 in the fuel. These CO2 results 
are used in the calculation of combustion efficiency, discussed next.
The CO levels are negligibly small at each of the conditions 
except for the 15% oxygen conditions. For both C15 and N15, the 
CO levels are highly elevated compared to the rest of the test matrix. 
Further, increasing the amount of hydrogen in the fuel decreases the 
CO fraction, likely a result of both low production of CO and better 
conversion of CO to CO2 through routes involving OH. Finally, the 
unburned hydrocarbon levels are relatively similar across the test 
matrix. The reason for this lack of trend in UHC is likely twofold. 
First, the level of unburned hydrocarbons in a lean NG/hydrogen 
flame is relatively low to start with and likely challenge the fidelity 
of the instrument at these levels. Further, we expect that some of the 
heavier UHCs may drop out of the line with the water as it is 
condensed before the emissions measurement, which would result in 
a lower level of measured UHC in all cases.
Figure 9 shows the calculated combustion efficiency based on 
measured CO2 versus equilibrium CO2, accounting for the drying 
process ahead of the emissions measurement. The combustion 
efficiency is above 0.95 for most of the cases, with a variation 
between test days of around 0.05 for most conditions, with the 
exception of the 15% O2 conditions. The variation presented for the 
15% O2 conditions is independent of the H2 level and has only been 
measured in the C15 condition (high CO2 condition). Our previous 
work in a similar experiment [15] encountered transient behavior for 
the C15 condition that was not encountered for the other conditions.

The coefficient of spatial uniformity is calculated from the 
time-averaged, Abel-deconvoluted flame shapes to see if there is 
any relation between the flame shape and the emissions that can 
be applied for all the diluents and H2 conditions of interest. The 
relations obtained for the combustion efficiency and measured 
CO2 as a function of the coefficient of spatial uniformity are 
shown in Fig. 9(b). The results for the CO2 show a clear decrease 
in emitted CO2 with increasing coefficient magnitude. The larger 
coefficient values indicate that the flame is more compact and 
localized. The compact flame shape tends to happen for conditions 
with higher flame speeds, which are those with either low levels 
of diluent injection or sufficiently high H2 in the fuel, which 
translates into a reduced CO2 fraction at the outlet. The highly 
diluted conditions with low levels of H2 in the fuel tend to have 
diffuse and elongated flames that are well distributed within the 
combustor, leading to a smaller coefficient magnitude. However, 
the relation in the coefficient of spatial uniformity for the C15 
condition follows the behavior of the combustion efficiency and 
shows some variation for said test case. As previously discussed, 
this variation could be due to some transient behavior or low 
amplitude fluctuations in the flame along with the fact the 
elongated flame might cause greater variation in the emissions at 
the probe inlet.
3.3 Chemical Kinetic Modeling. Chemical modeling is used 
to help interpret experimental results as the diluent and fuel 
compositions change. The use of a chemical reactor network 
approach is common for interpreting chemical kinetic behaviors in a 
gas turbine combustor, which requires a substantial optimization 
process to appropriately estimate the emissions formation compa­
rable to the experimental results [36,37]. While the chemical reactor 
network will be explored through optimization in the future, here we 
use a single perfectly stirred reactor (PSR) to assess the role of 
chemical kinetics on the experimental results from a high level.

condition (Baseline_H40), exhibiting the combustion efficiency 
defined as the ratio of CO2 to the CO2 in equilibrium as a function of 
flow residence time in the PSR. The combustion efficiency 
decreases with decreasing flow residence time in PSR. The main 
UHC at the exit of the PSR are CH4 and CH2O and exhibit a rapid 
increase in their mole fractions as the extinction limit is approached. 
The solid vertical line indicates the flow residence time between the 
flame and the emissions probe in the experiments, estimated using 
volumetric flowrate and combustor volume (0.02 s). To evaluate 
the impact of flame properties on combustion efficiency, the flow 
residence time in the PSR that matches experimentally determined 
combustion efficiency is calculated and indicated in the dashed line.

To identify the potential unique contribution of CO2 in the 
diluents over N2 (C15 versus N15 cases), a series of flux analyses 
were performed, primarily focusing on key radicals (e.g., OH, H) 
and CO oxidation. The CO consumption pathway is dominated by a 
single reaction, CO þ OH ¼ CO2 þ H (>99% in total consumption 
flux), regardless of flow residence time, as expected, suggesting the 
importance of OH for CO oxidation and thus combustion efficiency. 
At the flow residence time of 0.02 s, two major reactions play 
dominant roles in OH production, H þ O2 ¼ O þ OH and 
H2O þ O ¼ OH þ OH, both chain branching reactions responsible 
for >80% of total OH production, regardless of the H2, CO2, and N2 
content in the initial mixtures. Figure 13 also shows that the overall 
OH consumption pathways are not much affected by the initial 
diluent compositions, except suggesting a strong impact by 
increasing H2 content. While the variations in CO2 and N2 contents 
at low H2 conditions do not affect overall OH consumption 
pathways, the increase in H2 content augments the OH consumption 
channel through H2 þ OH ¼ H þ H2O, producing the H atom, which 
is then recycled to produce OH by the reaction of H þ O2 ¼ O þ OH 
(a major OH production pathway), which has a very strong 
temperature dependence.
On the other hand, CH4 and CH2O oxidation contribute 
considerable OH consumption flux. The increase of CO2 content 
results in slightly lower reactor temperature compared to N2 content, 
thus resulting in the increase of PSR flow residence time at the 
extinction limit, which can be attributed to higher specific heat value 
for CO2 over N2. The slightly reduced temperature in the C15 case 
results in slightly slower production of OH by temperature sensitive 
reaction over the N15 case, thus relatively lower combustion 
efficiency.
Although not shown in the figure, the OH mole fraction rapidly 
decreases near the extinction limit in the PSR. Major consumption 
channels for CH4 and CH2O oxidation are by the reaction with OH, 
thus the rapid decrease of OH mole fraction near extinction limit 
results in the rapid increases of CH4 and CH2O in exhaust gas, as 
shown in Fig. 10. Regardless of the initial mixture composition, the 
modeling results suggest that CH4 and CH2O are the major species in 
UHC (>80% of total UHC mole fraction). At 0.02 s flow residence 
time, the average mole fraction of CH4 in dry exhaust gas is found to 
be 21 ppm with 2.7 ppm standard deviation, and that of CH2O to be 
4 ppm with 0.46 ppm standard deviation for all tested conditions. 
When the flow residence time is matched with the experimentally 
determined combustion efficiency, the average mole fractions of 
CH4 and CH2O in dry exhaust gas, except for the C15 cases (0, 20, 
40% H2) are found to be 17 ppm and 3.4 ppm, respectively. In the 
C15 cases (0, 20, 40% H2), where combustion efficiencies are 
significantly lower at 0.88, the modeling results suggest average 
values of 143 ppm CH4 and 21 ppm CH2O as UHC in dry exhaust 
gas. While CH4 in UHC is well known as methane slip in natural gas 
combustion, the CH2O in UHC needs further attention, particularly 
considering its notorious health impact.

numerical result suggests an increase in combustion efficiency with 
pressure, which can be attributed to augmenting overall reaction 
rates due to the increase of the number density of molecules at high 
pressure, thus increasing temperature. On the other hand, the 
increase of pressure results in the substantial reduction of radical 
pool concentrations (OH, O and H), which leads to the reduction of

4 Conclusion
This study considers the effect of simulated EGR and hydrogen 
enrichment on the emissions performance of a natural gas swirl- 
stabilized flame. The test matrix includes various levels and 
compositions of EGR, using CO2 and N2 mixed with air, and 
hydrogen blends up to 40% by volume in natural gas. The 
combustion efficiency is noticeably affected by the highly diluted 
conditions, with CH* chemiluminescence imaging showing the 
change in flame shape as the reactant stream is modified. The 
addition of high levels of diluents results in an elongated, diffuse, 
and evenly distributed flame that exhibits variability in its 
combustion efficiency with different diluent compositions. Addi­
tionally, the emission measurements show an increase in CO for the 
highly diluted conditions. Companion chemical kinetic analysis 
identified critical reaction pathways that drive differences in the 
combustion efficiency, particularly the formation and consumption 
of the OH radical, which is highly dependent on the level of H2 in the 
fuel. The use of EGR with fuel-flexible combustors can pose 
challenges in terms of both emissions and flame behavior that must 
be further addressed.
