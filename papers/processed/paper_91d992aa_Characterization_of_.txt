Characterization of a Superheated
Fuel Jet in a Crossﬂow
An experiment was conducted to characterize a superheated fuel jet (Jet-A) injected into
an unheated crossﬂow of air. The liquid phase of the fuel jet was characterized with high
speed imaging and phase Doppler interferometry. The transition from a shear-atomized
to a ﬂash-atomized spray at a fuel temperature of 513 K 465°F was observed at an
ambient pressure of 1 atm, which is consistent with the bubble and dew point curves
predicted for JP-8. The explosive breakup that was seen in the ﬂash-atomized spray
produced submicron droplets with a high radial momentum. This unique behavior makes
superheated fuels an attractive design feature for fuel preparation devices that can em-
ploy ﬂash boiling to enhance fuel atomization and mixing in a compact volume.

1
Introduction
New advances in fuel heat-sink technology that deoxygenate
and enhance the thermal stability of JP-8 will lead to increases in
aeroengine efﬁciency and thrust/weight ratio. These advances
have already demonstrated fuel stability up to fuel temperatures of
589 K 600°F 1,2. Research plans exist 3 to extend this limit
to 700–755 K 800–900°F, where pyrolytic coke deposition be-
gins to take place. This increase in JP-8 thermal stability will
allow for active fuel cooling of the engine and airframe, increas-
ing the enthalpy of the fuel delivered to the combustor and thus
improving the thermal efﬁciency of the propulsion system.
While the system-level beneﬁts of this technology are apparent
based on simple analyses, it is unclear how heated fuel phase
changes will affect the fuel delivery and fuel injection processes.
Some effects are well understood. For example, fuel boiling in the
fuel system during engine transients can cause an interruption of
the fuel ﬂow to the combustor and can lead to combustor blow-out
4. The effects of increased fuel temperature on the fuel atomi-
zation and fuel/air mixing processes are poorly understood,
though, and are the focus of this paper.
In order to examine the effects of fuel heating on fuel injection,
we have chosen the liquid jet in gaseous crossﬂow as a canonical
problem that simulates many of the physical processes that are
important in most practical injection devices. This problem has
been widely studied for cold fuel 5,6 and provides an excellent
base line. For the purpose of practicality, the current work is lim-
ited to 589 K 600°F fuel injected into crossﬂowing air at ambi-
ent pressure and temperature. These limits enable the investigation
of the phenomenology of superheated fuel injection with conven-
tional diagnostics.
2
Background
A ﬂuid is superheated when its vapor to liquid mass ratio is
below its equilibrium value. The superheated state can be induced,
for example, if the ﬂuid is rapidly injected from a compressed
subcooled state to a pressure below its bubble point. Aerosol cans
employ this concept to ﬂash-atomize ﬂuids that boil at tempera-
tures below room temperature. Because of its potential for rapid
atomization and mixing, superheated fuel injection appears ideal
for combustion applications that can take advantage of the ﬂash
boiling process.
Superheated fuel injection has been studied in the automotive
industry 7,8 because ﬂash boiling can occur in the spark ignition
engine manifold if the manifold pressure drops below the bubble
point of the fuel. Fuel heating can also occur in the injector as-
sembly because of the heat buildup in the engine head.
With regard to aviation fuels, ﬂash-vaporizing fuel injection
systems trace back to the early Whittle engines 9, where the fuel
was heated in tubes immersed in the combustor. More modern
systems have avoided ﬂash vaporization and the associated fuel
heating due to coking concerns.
Recent developments 1–3 in fuel heat-sink technology have
provided the opportunity to re-examine ﬂash vaporization as a
viable fuel injection tool in aeropropulsion. Studies have been
performed for the application of ﬂash vaporization of JP-8 to
pulse detonation engines by researchers at AFRL 10. Lee et al.
11 produced a model for JP-8 that simulates the thermodynamic/
transport properties of the multicomponent fuel and incorporates a
nonequilibrium vaporization model for superheated JP-8. One of
the results from this study is a set of predicted bubble and dew
point thermodynamic equilibrium curves for JP-8.
As shown in Fig. 1, the bubble and dew point curves for JP-8
are distinct but converge at the critical temperature and pressure
beyond which the ﬂuid may exist in a supercritical state. Above
the bubble point curve, JP-8 exists in a subcooled, compressed
liquid state. Below the dew point curve, JP-8 should exist as a
vapor in equilibrium. The region bounded by the bubble and dew
point curves is a transition zone in which the ﬂuid is a mix of both
liquid and vapor states. To ﬂash-atomize fuel in a gas turbine
combustor, the fuel pressure in the injector must lie above the
bubble point curve and inject into a combustor chamber pressure
that falls below the bubble point curve. The objectives of this
study were to determine the feasibility of using superheat to in-
duce ﬂash atomization of a multicomponent aviation fuel and to
assess the impact of ﬂash atomization on the dispersion of a liquid
jet in crossﬂow.

3.3
Operating Conditions. Tests were conducted at atmo-
spheric pressure with a crossﬂow air velocity of approximately
120 m/s. The air temperature 110–130°F, 316–328 K was
higher than the ambient air temperature because of heat generated
from the blower. A jet-to-crossﬂow momentum-ﬂux ratio J of 25
was targeted. The volumetric ﬂow rate of Jet-A was set at 0.254
l/min 0.067 gpm. The fuel temperature was the primary condi-
tion that was varied in the tests. For the range of fuel ﬂows and
fuel temperatures that were tested, the fuel pressure in the injector
was high enough to prevent vapor lock, which the bubble and
vapor curves from Ref. 11 conﬁrmed.

air as well as the J and Weg for selected high speed video cases.
The J and Weg presented in Table 2 account for the decrease in
density, viscosity, and surface tension as the fuel temperature
increases.
The PDI measurements were acquired in separate tests. Table 3
lists the operating conditions for these measurements. Case PDI1
targeted an unheated fuel condition. Because PDI data could not

Every effort was made to conduct the high speed video and PDI
tests at the same fuel temperature for the baseline unheated fuel
condition. The fuel temperatures ranged from a low of 296 K

97°F for the PDI test case PDI1. This difference in fuel tem-
perature corresponded to a 4% change in surface tension and a
1.2% change in fuel density. The change in these fuel properties
did not signiﬁcantly affect the two dimensionless numbers that
determine jet penetration and atomization—the momentum-ﬂux
ratio and the aerodynamic Weber number.
4
Discussion of Results
4.1
Spray Atomization Regimes. Each high speed video clip
was viewed in its entirety to assess the structure of the liquid jet at
the point of injection. Three atomization regimes were identiﬁed
and are represented by selected single-frame images in Fig. 5. The

with a diameter that was approximately equal to the oriﬁce diam-

in previous studies 5,6. The impact of the crossﬂow caused the
column to bend and ﬂatten in the cross section. The impact also
ampliﬁed the surface waves on the windward side of the column.
The surface wave instabilities grew until a primary column
breakup occurred. Fine droplets along the column length up to the
primary breakup point were also stripped from the edges of the
ﬂattened column. Column stripping, which was expected given
the high aerodynamic Weber numbers for these conditions see
Table 2, resulted in a dense plume of ﬁne droplets immediately
downstream of the column. With respect to an increase in fuel

The second row of images in Fig. 5 corresponds to a transition
regime at 513 K 464°F in which the fuel jet oscillated between
two modes: shear-instability induced atomization and ﬂash atomi-
zation. At this fuel temperature and at 1 atm, the fuel jet should
have been in a superheated state, as predicted by the bubble and
dew point curves in Fig. 1. The images representing the transition
regime at 513 K 464°F correspond to a sequence of three im-
ages taken 0.4 ms six frames apart in time. The ﬁrst image
shows an intact jet core that was similar to that of the shear-
atomized jet. The second image shows the onset of ﬂash atomiza-
tion. The jet injected from the oriﬁce rapidly expanded to form a
jet structure that appeared to be three times the original jet diam-
eter. A cusp in the upper spray trajectory denoted by an arrow
marked the transition from the shear-atomized jet to the ﬂash-
atomized jet. Subsequent frames show the cusp being convected
downstream until it disappeared by the third image shown in the
row. The penetration of the liquid phase of the ﬂash-atomized jet
in the third image was lower than that of the shear-atomized jet in
the ﬁrst image.
The third row in Fig. 5 shows images of sprays that are fully
ﬂash atomized. The images shown here were acquired at different
camera settings and image resolutions see Table 1 in order to
capture the ﬁne spray mist formed by ﬂash boiling. At fuel tem-
peratures of 540 K 513°F and 550 K 530°F, the fuel spray
expanded as a conical spray that penetrated radially outward upon
injection and that penetrated upstream before being turned by the
crossﬂow. The spray appeared to consist of a dense cloud of ﬁne
mist that quickly vaporized downstream of the injection location.
The fuel injection inlet and exit pressures 1 atm for all six
cases from Fig. 5 and additional HSV cases are presented on a
pressure-temperature plot that also shows the bubble and dew
point curves for JP-8 from Ref. 11 see Fig. 6. Each case is
represented by a near-vertical line that corresponded to the
isenthalpic pressure drop across the oriﬁce from an initial fuel
supply pressure to the ambient environment. Because no vapor
lock was observed, the fuel injection process basically involved a
rapid drop in pressure that was sufﬁciently fast to prevent vapor-
ization within the injector. Thus, the fuel injection through the
oriﬁce is depicted as having a nearly constant temperature
throughout the process.
Each video clip was classiﬁed as being in either the shear-
atomized, transition, or ﬂash-atomization regimes. Flash boiling,
which occurred in both the transition and ﬂash-atomization re-
gimes, was observed at fuel temperatures and pressures below the
dew point curve predicted for JP-8 by Lee et al. 11.
The onset of ﬂash atomization was speciﬁc to the design of the
injector. In tests with a plain oriﬁce injector, Brown and York 12
showed that the geometry of the oriﬁce affected when the onset of
ﬂash atomization in a superheated liquid occurred—i.e., at the
injector exit or at some distance from the exit. For the liquid jet in
crossﬂow, this delay meant that the superheated fuel jet could
initially undergo shear atomization before the ﬂash-atomization
mode began to dominate.
4.2
Spray Penetration. A time-averaged image was derived
for each video segment in order to compare the penetration of the
shear- and ﬂash-atomized sprays. The spray penetration corre-
sponded to the maximum spray height as a function of the down-
stream distance from the injection point. Figure 7 shows the spray
penetration extracted from the images from the shear-atomized
cases 1–3 and ﬂash-atomized cases 5 and 6 regimes. The
curves for the shear-atomized cases veriﬁed that the fuel jet at
room temperature case 1 penetrated farther than the heated jets
cases 2 and 3.
While the ﬂash-atomized jets at temperatures of 540 K 513°F
and 550 K 530°F cases 5 and 6 produced nearly identical
penetration curves, these curves were signiﬁcantly different from
the curves for the shear-atomized cases despite having similar
momentum-ﬂux ratios. In particular, the ﬂash-atomized sprays had
an initial conic shape and a lower penetration height. Flash atomi-
zation resulted from the rapid expansion of vapor bubbles in the
fuel, which shattered the fuel upon injection and imparted enough
radial momentum to cause the spray to penetrate upstream into the
crossﬂow. The conical spray consequently changed the interaction
between the jet with the crossﬂow. This, along with the vaporiza-
tion of the ﬁne spray generated by the ﬂashing jet, resulted in the
low penetration of the liquid phase.

Figure 8 compares the results for case 1 shear-instability at-
omization and case 6 ﬂash atomization with the upper spray
trajectories predicted by liquid jet-in-crossﬂow correlations from
Becker and Hassa 6 and Wu et al. 13. The Becker and Hassa
correlation was derived from shadowgraphs acquired from an un-
heated kerosene jet in crossﬂow at varying ambient pressures and
is valid for the ranges of J and Weg in this experiment and for x/d
between 2 and 22. The correlation from Wu et al. 13 was derived
from PDI measurements of a water jet injected into a crossﬂow
under atmospheric temperature and pressure. The Wu et al. corre-
lation performed better than the Becker and Hassa correlation in
predicting the spray trajectory at 296 K 74°F case 1. The
discrepancy in the predicted trajectories can be caused by various
factors that included different ﬂow boundary conditions e.g., ori-
ﬁce inlet geometries and possibly crossﬂow velocity proﬁles and
different measurement techniques that were applied in the two
studies to acquire data for the correlations. Both correlations from
Refs. 6,13 did predict a slightly lower spray penetration for the
high fuel temperature condition case 6: 550 K Tfuel=530°F
because the momentum-ﬂux ratio was lower J=20. However,
both correlations did not sufﬁciently predict the large difference in
penetration observed between cases 1 and 6.
The discrepancy between the predicted trajectories and the
measured liquid penetration of the ﬂashing spray case 6 sug-
gested that the liquid jet-in-crossﬂow correlations from Refs.
6,13, which are a function of the momentum-ﬂux ratio and the
axial distance, needed to account for other physical phenomena
associated with the ﬂash-atomized jet. A correlation from Stenzler
et al. 14, which accounted for column bending effects by includ-
ing a liquid viscosity term, was applied to the same case 1 and
case 6 conditions. However, the Stenzler et al. correlation—
obtained by testing various liquids at room temperature—
predicted that the penetration would increase for higher fuel tem-
peratures, which was contrary to what was observed.

4.3
Atomization Quality. PDI results were acquired at a
downstream x/d of 50 at fuel temperatures of 309 K 97°F and
505 K 450°F shear-atomized liquid jet just before the transition
regime. PDI measurements were also attempted for a ﬂash-
atomized spray at 547 K 525°F at axial distances x/d of 25
and 50, but the PDI instrument was not capable of acquiring reli-
able measurements in the dense spray plume of submicron drop-
lets. This ﬁnding conﬁrmed that the ﬂash-atomized spray pro-
duced a ﬁne mist of droplets within an x/d of 25.
The PDI data for the shear-atomized cases can still reveal in-
formation regarding the effect of fuel temperature on spray
breakup. Figure 9 presents the droplet Sauter mean diameter
SMD, mean droplet axial velocity, and liquid volume ﬂux pro-
ﬁles for the 309 K 97°F and 505 K 450°F cases. The effect of
fuel temperature was clearly seen in the SMD and volume ﬂux
proﬁles. A higher fuel temperature reduced the overall magnitude
of the SMD proﬁle. Atomization was the primary mechanism that
produced the smaller SMD values in the 505 K fuel case. velocity,
the increase in fuel temperature from 309 K 97°F to 505 K
450°F lowered the fuel density and the surface tension such that
the aerodynamic Weber number was 2.5 times higher in the 505 K
450°F fuel jet. We would thus expect to see smaller droplet
sizes in the 505 K 450°F case.

The liquid volume ﬂux proﬁles showed that the 505 K 450°F
case produced a spray with a relatively low liquid volume ﬂux and
with a peak location that was 3.81 mm 0.15 in. higher than that
for the 309 K 97°F case. The composition of the spray in terms
of drop size can be obtained by extracting the PDI data rates for
selected drop size classes see Fig. 10. The 309 K 97°F case
was comprised of a higher occurrence of large drop sizes e.g., the
51–60 m class compared to the 505 K 450°F case because
of a lower degree of atomization coupled with low vaporization
rates. While the average droplet velocities for the two conditions
were similar, the higher population of larger drops produced the
higher volume ﬂux proﬁle for the 309 K 97°F condition see
Fig. 9.
5
Conclusions
The effect of superheating a liquid fuel jet was investigated in
order to determine its atomization and dispersion in an unheated
crossﬂow at 1 atm. An experiment was designed and built to heat
Jet-A to temperatures up to 589 K 600°F. The fuel jet transi-
tioned from a subcooled, compressed liquid state to a ﬂash-
atomized spray upon injection as temperatures approached 513 K
464°F. The ﬂash atomization of Jet-A at this temperature oc-
curred in the region below the dew point curve for JP-8 that was
predicted by Lee et al. 11.
In terms of the spray characteristics, increasing the fuel tem-
perature in the subcooled state increased the formation of smaller
droplets via shear atomization. The tendency of hotter liquid jets
to produce smaller droplets was expected because the changes in
fuel properties resulted in a higher aerodynamic Weber number.
With a sufﬁcient increase in temperature, ﬂash-atomization oc-
curred. Vapor bubbles formed in the fuel jet rapidly expanded
upon injection into a lower-pressure environment. This rapid ex-
pansion improved atomization and caused the fuel to penetrate
radially outward from the oriﬁce as a conical spray, thus altering
the jet’s interaction with the crossﬂow signiﬁcantly. The primary
beneﬁt of ﬂash atomization was the generation of ﬁne droplets
that vaporized rapidly. However, further study is needed to deter-
mine how the ﬂash-atomized fuel spray impacts fuel-air mixing
relative to the shear-atomized fuel spray.
Deoxygenation technologies and fuel formulations with higher
thermal stability will enable fuel to attain a superheated state pro-
vided that there is enough heat available for absorption. The fuel
injector
will
need
to
be
designed—perhaps
as
a
staged
injector—to accommodate both subcooled and ﬂash-atomized
sprays across the entire ﬂight cycle. Correlations that describe the
penetration and mixing of the liquid and gaseous phases of the
ﬂash-atomized spray are needed to help with designing such a fuel
injector. Various liquid jet-in-crossﬂow correlations did not pre-
dict the signiﬁcant decrease in liquid penetration that occurred
when the fuel was heated to a superheated state that induced ﬂash
atomization. Additional work with different diagnostic techniques
should be performed to characterize the vapor fuel distribution, to
quantify the resulting fuel-air mixture, and to derive correlations
suitable for injector design.
