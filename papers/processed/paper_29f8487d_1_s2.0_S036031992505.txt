diffusion combustor: A multi-objective optimization via orthogonal design

, Dongqi Jiang , Tao Zhang , Jianrong Wu

A B S T R A C T
Micro-mixed diffusion combustion technology represents a viable technical pathway for the engineering appli­
cation of hydrogen as a clean energy source. While this technology has been applied in gas turbines, it still faces 
challenges such as poor variable-load adaptability, narrow stable combustion range, and high NO emissions. In 
this study, a vortex generator (VG) was innovatively introduced as a key control component into the structure of 
a typical micro-mixed diffusion combustor. Orthogonal design methods and high-precision numerical simulation 
techniques were comprehensively employed to systematically analyze the influence of VG geometry parameters 
on the combustion performance of a hydrogen micro-mixed diffusion combustor. Through range analysis, the 
weight differences of each parameter on core performance indicators were quantitatively revealed. The results 
show that VG significantly enhances combustion performance through multiple flow field regulation mecha­
nisms: by increasing the penetration depth of hydrogen jets and inducing local micro-vortices, the mixing effi­
ciency of hydrogen and air is strengthened; by controlling the redistribution of primary air to reduce the 
mainstream air velocity, uniform distribution of the temperature field in the combustor is achieved; the tem­
perature peak and the residence time of nitrogen in high-temperature zones are significantly reduced, thereby 
inhibiting NO formation. Within the scope of this study, compared with the original model, the optimized scheme 
achieves a 9.7 % increase in average outlet temperature, a 90 % reduction in the overall temperature distribution 
factor OTDF, and a 5.9 % reduction in NO mass fraction.
1. Introduction
Low-carbon and low-pollution emission technologies represent one 
of the key development priorities for future aero-engines, aerospace 
vehicles, 
and 
gas 
turbines 
[1]. 
Lightweight, 
efficient, 
and 
high-calorific-value fuels have long been urgently sought in aerospace 
applications. Due to its low emissions and high calorific value, hydrogen 
has consistently been regarded as the preferred fuel for near-space 
propulsion systems [2]. However, hydrogen’s low density poses signif­
icant challenges for sufficient mixing with the mainstream air in 
high-velocity air jets. Additionally, the conventional combustor head 
structures struggle to achieve adequate hydrogen-air mixing, often 
leading to localized hydrogen concentration hotspots that generate 
high-temperature regions and substantial nitrogen oxides [3].
Structurally, replacing traditional swirl mixing channels with 
arrayed micro-mixing channels represents a viable technical approach. 
Micro-mixed combustion technologies broadly encompass two cate­
gories: micro-mixed premixed combustion and micro-mixed diffusion 
combustion. Compared to premixed combustion, diffusion combustion 
offers the advantage of effectively preventing flashback during 
hydrogen combustion, significantly enhancing combustion stability [4]. 
The micro-mixed diffusion combustion technology pioneered by Aachen 
University of Applied Sciences in Germany serves as a leading example. 
With over 30 years of research experience since the 1990s, the sixth 
generation of micro-mixed diffusion combustors has been developed. 
Through the design and application of multiple micro air channels 
combined with fine hydrogen micro-nozzles, this technology has effec­
tively reduced NOx formation during pure hydrogen combustion [5–8].
While the developed micro-mixed diffusion combustion scheme has 
successfully controlled the residence time of nitrogen in high- 
temperature regions to reduce NO formation However, precise control 
over the jet penetration depth of hydrogen still remains necessary. 
Additionally, due to the small size of the air channels, the flame is prone 
to blowout when the flow conditions increase. In previous studies, to 
address these challenges, a bluff body is placed at the outlet of the air 
channel to disturb the mainstream air and achieve rapid mixing of

3. Numerical details
3.1. Numerical model
In the Sydney Bluff-Body Burner [11], where both jet and swirling 
flows coexist, the standard k-ε turbulence model has been validated to 
accurately predict the flow field. Based on the comparison results of this 
model, this paper revises the model parameter Cε1 to 1.6 [12].
Flamelet Generated Manifold (FGM) with diffusion flamelets is 
employed for modelling hydrogen combustion and turbulence- 
chemistry interaction. The FGM model uses the flow pattern in one- 
dimensional laminar flames to replace multi-dimensional flames. Its 
basic idea is the same as that of the Steady Laminar Flamelet Method 
(SLFM), which both map complex multi-dimensional chemical reaction 
information onto one or more characteristic variables. This approach not 
only considers detailed chemical reaction mechanisms but also reduces 
the number of transport equations to be solved, greatly improving 
computational efficiency. In this paper, the GRI3.0 [13] detailed reac­
tion mechanism is used to calculate one-dimensional freely propagating 
laminar premixed flames, and turbulent chemo-thermodynamic tables 
are obtained by assuming a Beta distribution integral.
3.2. Verification of computational method
To investigate the reliability of the numerical model, the experi­
mental setup and data of the Sydney bluff-body flame HM1e were used 
for validation [15]. Due to the low density of hydrogen, its diffusion is 
restricted, which easily leads to the formation of local high-temperature 
zones. In such combustion scenarios, the thermal mechanism is regarded 
as the dominant mechanism for NO formation [14]. In the process of 
mixed combustion of hydrocarbon fuels, the formation of NO is calcu­
lated with the addition of the prompt mechanism. As shown in Fig. 2, a 
recirculation zone and a jet diffusion region coexist downstream of a 
circular bluff-body nozzle with a diameter D = 50 mm. For more details 
about the experimental setup and data, refer to Refs. [16–18].
Fig. 3 presents a comparison between the numerical results and 
experimental data of the radial distribution of mass fractions for O2, 
H2O, CO, and NO at different axial positions. It can be seen that the 
predicted values of temperature, mass fractions of the main reactant O2, 
main product H2O, and intermediate product CO obtained using the 
above reaction mechanism and numerical model are in good agreement 
with the experimental results.
3.3. Mesh independence test
The computational domain was meshed using unstructured grids 
with grid refinement applied to the H2 inlet and VG, as shown in Fig. 4. 
Four grid generation schemes were employed for the mesh indepen­
dence test. As illustrated in Fig. 5, the temperature along the central axis 
of the combustor were selected to compare these four schemes. The 
results show that when the grid size in the primary combustion zone is 
0.05 mm, the total number of grids is 8 million, meeting the re­
quirements for computational stability and accuracy.

3.4. Comparison of reaction mechanisms
Although the simulation results were compared with the experi­
mental results in Section 3.1, it should be noted that the fuel used in the 
Sydney bluff-body flame contains a portion of methane (CH4). In 
contrast, the fuel studied in this paper is pure hydrogen, which does not 
involve the prompt NOx reaction mechanism. Therefore, two pure 
hydrogen reaction mechanisms Polimi and C3 [19,20] were compared 
with the Gri3.0 reaction mechanism, and the NO reaction mechanism 
with NNH as an intermediate was also considered [21]. The comparison 
results of temperature and NO mass fraction are shown in Fig. 6. The 
calculated temperatures of the three reaction mechanisms are basically 
consistent, but the NO mass fraction calculated by the C3 reaction 
mechanism is higher than that by the other two mechanisms, while the 
calculation results of the Gri3.0 and Polimi reaction mechanisms are 
similar. Subsequent calculations are carried out using the Gri3.0 reac­
tion mechanism.

4.2. Range analysis
The range analysis method was used to analyze the numerical data of 
9 sets of orthogonal tests. Tables 4–6 present the analysis results of three 
evaluation indicators: average outlet temperature, OTDF, and average 
outlet NO mass fraction. In the tables: Kij represents the average value of 
test results at level j (j = 1,2,3) for indicator i (i = 1,2,3; denoting T, 
OTDF, and Y(NO), respectively); Ri represents the range value of indi­
cator i. The range value reflects the sensitivity of each factor to the test 
indicator—the larger the range value, the greater the influence.
4.2.1. Average outlet temperature
Range order: A > C > B, indicating that the VG length has the 
greatest influence on the average outlet temperature, followed by the VG 
position, and the VG width has the smallest effect. Higher average outlet 
temperature signifies higher combustion efficiency. The optimal 
parameter level is A3B1C1.
4.2.2. OTDF
Range order: A > C > B, indicating that OTDF is most sensitive to the 
VG length, followed by the VG position, with the VG width having the 
least impact. A smaller OTDF indicates a more uniform temperature 
field. The optimal parameter level is A3B2C2.
4.2.3. NO emissions
Range order: A > B > C, indicating that NO formation is most 
influenced by the VG position, followed by the VG length, while the VG 
width has a negligible effect. Lower Y(NO) is preferable. The optimal 
parameter level for NO emissions is A3B2C1.
Fig. 7 shows the effect curve of the orthogonal test design for each 
indicator, reflecting the influence law of structural parameter factors on 
combustion performance indicators. As shown in Fig. 7 (a), the average 
outlet temperature increases with the increase of VG length, and de­
creases with the increase of VG width and the distance from the H2 
nozzle. As shown in Fig. 7 (b), the OTDF is negatively correlated with the 
VG length; the OTDF first decreases and then increases with the increase 
of VG width and the distance from the H2 nozzle. As shown in Fig. 7 (c), 
NO emissions reduction with the increase of VG length, especially in the 
stage where A changes from 0.3 mm to 0.6 mm, The outlet NO emissions 
first decrease and then increase with the increase of VG length, When the 
VG is close to the H2 nozzle, NO emissions reduction.
4.3. Combustion performance optimization
In summary, the optimal parameter combinations for the three in­
dicators are A3B1C1, A3B2C2, and A2B2C1, respectively. Considering the 
weight of each indicator, the comprehensive optimal parameter com­
bination is determined to be A3B2C1. Specifically, when the VG length is 
set to the maximum value of 0.9 mm, the width is 0.4 mm, and the 
distance from the H2 nozzle is minimized to 0.25 mm, the combustion 
performance reaches its optimum. As shown in Table 7, the results 
before and after optimization indicate that the outlet combustion tem­
perature is increased by 9.7 %, the OTDF is reduced by 90 %, and the NO 
emissions are decreased by 5.9 %.

5. Conclusions
This study systematically investigated the influence of VG structural 
parameters (length, width, and distance from the H2 nozzle) on the 
combustion performance of a hydrogen micro-mixed diffusion 
combustor using orthogonal design and numerical simulation methods. 
Within the scope of this study, the range analysis results showed that the 
VG length was the most critical factor affecting the average outlet 
temperature, OTDF and NO emissions.
Through multi-index comprehensive optimization, the optimal 
parameter combination was determined as a VG length of 0.9 mm, width 
of 0.4 mm, and distance from the nozzle of 0.25 mm. This scheme 
increased the combustion temperature by 9.7 %, reduced the OTDF by 
90.0 %, and significantly decreased NO emissions by 5.9 %. The opti­
mized parameters improved combustion efficiency, temperature uni­
formity, and pollutant control performance.
Flow field analysis revealed that VG enhanced mixing capabilities by 
increasing hydrogen penetration depth, inducing miniature recircula­
tion zones, and generating entrainment vortices at the jet tip. This 
effectively reduced the temperature peak, homogenized the temperature 
field distribution, and shortened the residence time of NO precursor 
species in high-temperature regions. These mechanisms provide insights 
for the design of low-pollution and high-efficiency hydrogen burners.
