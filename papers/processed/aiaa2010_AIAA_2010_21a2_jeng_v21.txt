Gas Turbine Single Annular Combustor Sector:  
Combustion Dynamics  
J. Cai1, F. Ichihasi2, B. S. Mohammad2, S. B. Tambe2, Yi-Huan Kao 2  and S. M. Jeng3 
University of Cincinnati, Cincinnati, Ohio, 45220, USA 
 
This paper investigates the combustion dynamics of a single cup gas turbine combustor 
sector, a swirl-stabilized burner with dilution and cooling air holes. The burner was fueled 
by natural gas and was operated at one atmospheric pressure with various air inlet 
temperatures, air pressure drops, and air–fuel ratios. Acoustic emissions as functions of 
operating conditions were measured. Chemiluminescence imaging with a high-speed CCD 
camera and simultaneous dynamic pressure measurements has been used to characterize the 
combustor’s behavior. Imaging of the combustion energy release rate has provided an 
insight on the flame structure and its interaction with the dilution air jets. The combustion 
dynamics were correlated well with the heat release rate in the primary zone or in the 
dilution air jet region. Two low-frequency combustion dynamics modes (fuel-rich combustor 
domes with frequency around 420Hz, and fuel-lean combustor domes with frequency 
around 280 Hz) were identified by a dynamic microphone, dynamic pressure transducers, 
and a high-speed CCD camera. A two-dimensional distribution of the heat release rate 
dynamics (amplitude and phase angle) reveals different combustion dynamics driving 
mechanisms for fuel-lean and fuel rich modes. The acoustic emissions for the fuel-lean 
combustion mode correlated well with the energy release rate dynamics of the swirling jet 
inside the combustor dome. The acoustic emissions for the fuel-rich mode were associated 
with the unsteady chemical energy release rate from interactions between the dilution air 
jets and the fuel-rich mixture convected from the combustor dome.

I. Introduction 
he concept of premixed combustion (LPP and RQL) has been widely applied in the industry, particularly in gas  
turbine industry, as an effective means to achieve high combustion efficiency and meet the stringent 
environmental standards of NOx emissions. This type of combustion exhibits a wide range of combustion dynamics, 
induced by coupling between unsteady heat release and acoustic oscillations. This is often manifested as high 
magnitude acoustic noise, known as thermo-acoustic instabilities, which were first investigated by Rayleigh [1]. The

occurrence of combustion dynamics in gas turbine combustors presents a great challenge for engineers. Not only are 
combustion dynamics detrimental to the operation of the engine and combustor, but the difficulty in predicting and 
remedying dynamics problems can lead to significant financial loss, and delays in engine development. Therefore, 
thermo-acoustic instabilities must be considered in the early stages of the combustion system design phase. 
  
A variety of complex physical processes may be involved in the combustion system stability. Extensive 
experimental and theoretical works have been conducted to identify the fundamental mechanisms and to devise 
analytical models. Various mechanisms have been founded to initiate thermo-acoustic instabilities. For instance, air–
fuel ratio oscillations, induced by oscillating pressure, found at the combustor inlet (Crocco et al [2,3]),  may couple 
with the heat release oscillations and drive the instability. Interactions of the acoustic velocity oscillations with the 
flame cause periodic flame front variation, which induces a periodic heat addition process (Candel [4]). Variations 
of turbulence intensity (Schuermans et al [5]), atomization, vaporization, and mixing [2, 3] could also result in a 
periodic supply of fuel to the flame and an oscillatory equivalence ratio that produce heat release oscillations. 
Vortex shedding activity (Paschereit et al [6-9]) may distort the flame front and cause its surface area to oscillate, 
thus producing an oscillatory heat-release process that also drives the acoustic field. In gas turbine combustion, the 
feed-coupling mechanism leading to air–fuel ratio oscillations ( Lieuwen et al[10]) and hydrodynamic instabilities 
have been identified as the most likely drivers for combustion noise. Although these examples illustrate the various 
mechanisms capable of driving combustion noise, the amount of energy fed back into the initial perturbation is 
modulated by the resonant acoustics of the combustion systems. Promoting the growth of the natural frequency 
modes of the combustion system, is one of the few characteristics common to all thermoacoustic instabilities 
(Lieuwen et al [11]).  
 
While the global mechanisms of thermo-acoustic instability have been extensively studied, identifying the initial 
mechanism remains a difficult task. In all reality, several physically distinct mechanisms can drive combustion noise 
for the same combustor, depending on combustor’s operating conditions. The composition and temperature of the 
mixture entering the combustion chamber, the swirl intensity, and the pressure, among others, are functions of 
operating conditions, and can determine the flame temperature, the flame front location, and the flame shape, which 
all have a direct impact on the flame’s susceptibility to external perturbations.  Although this topic has been widely 
studied by scientists and engineers, the added geometric complexities and injector configurations of a practical 
combustor make their dynamic behavior currently unpredictable. Current industry design techniques are largely 
empirical and not clearly defined with respect to combustion instabilities.  
To improve our understanding of stability properties in such complex systems encountered in many industrial 
applications, the flame structure, of an atmospheric swirl-stabilized burner that included dilution and cooling air 
holes and was fed with natural gas fuel, was systematically investigated for various inlet temperatures, pressure 
drops, and air–fuel ratios. Chemiluminescence imaging with a high-speed CCD camera and simultaneous dynamic 
pressure measurements has been used to characterize the combustor’s dynamic behavior. By changing the test 
conditions like the equivalence ratio, inlet temperature and pressure drop at the swirler, a complete map of the 
combustion pressure dynamics and acoustic emissions were established. By analyzing the high-speed camera 
images, the mechanisms of combustion dynamics have been identified.  
 
 
II. Experiment setup and measurement methodology 
  
 The experimental measurements were conducted in an atmospheric combustion test rig that simulates a Single 
Annular Combustor  (SAC) Sector. Figure 1 schematically shows the test facility employed in the present work. The 
test rig consists of an air inline heater, flow conditioning chamber, and the combustor. The test rig was oriented 
horizontally with the combustion air preheated by a 72 kW inline electrical heater to the desired temperature. 
Compressed air was delivered to the combustor by a compressor with the maximum mass flow rate of 1 kg/s during 
the test. A Micro-motion CMF 300 flow meter upstream of the heater measures the air flow rate. The flow 
conditioning chamber is constructed from 6 inches of schedule 40 stainless steel piping, with an inner diameter of 
154.1 mm (6.065 inch) and a total length of 610mm (24 inch). Two PCB 112A05 type dynamic pressure transducers 
were mounted on the wall to measure the dynamics pressures in the flow conditioning chamber. A pipe expander, 
152.4 mm (6 inch) inlet diameter and 203.2mm (8 inch) outlet diameter, was used to connect the air settling 
chamber to  the combustor flange. A type K thermocouple and a static pressure transducer were used to measure the 
burner inlet air temperature and pressure.

conditions were acquired by a computer.

Figure 2 is a generic sketch of the Single Annular Combustor Sector used in this investigation. The swirler 
cup features two radial inlet swirlers that have counter-rotating directions, and a dual-fuel (liquid or gas) nozzle is 
placed at the center of the swirler cup. The fuel from the nozzle impinges onto the venturi wall of the inner swirler. 
At the tip of the venturi, fuel was mixed with the counter-rotating swirling air from the inner and outer swirlers. The 
air-fuel mixture then was injected into the burner. Upstream of the swirler cup, a long diffuser was used to recover 
the pressure head and to reduce the air speed. A set of dilution holes were located downstream of the burner. The 
dilution jets were used to enhance the air-fuel mixing and reduce the equivalence ratio. Further downstream, another 
set of dilution holes were used to further reduce the gas temperature and to trim the outlet temperature profile. 
Besides the dilution holes, many cooling air holes were distributed in the burner to reduce the burner liner wall 
temperature. For a typical combustor design, about one third of the total air flow rate enters through the dome (swirl 
cup and dome cooling), and about one third of the total air goes through the dilution holes and the rest of air is used 
for combustor liner cooling.  At one side of the burner, a   quartz window was mounted for flow visualization. At the 
other side of the burner, a stainless steel plate, having the same size as the quartz window, was installed with four 
PCB dynamic pressure transducers, as shown in Fig. 3. The first transducer was used to measure the dynamic 
pressure inside the dome area. The second pressure transducer was used to measure the dynamic pressure near the 
first dilution air jet.  The third transducer was located between the two sets of dilution holes and the fourth 
transducer was located around the second set of dilution holes. 
 
 
 
 
In addition to the flow meters, pressure transducers, and thermocouples that were used to monitor the tests, 
an AKG D112 dynamic cardioids microphone was positioned 304.8 mm (1 foot) away from the side of the burner to 
record the combustion acoustic emissions. A regular speed video camera and a PHANTOM V7.3 high-speed CCD 
camera were used to record the burning modes and flame dynamics. A type B thermocouple was used to monitor the 
center gas temperature at the combustor exit. 
The experimental approach consisted of investigating the burning modes, acoustics emissions and 
combustor pressure dynamics as a function of equivalence ratio Ф, swirler pressure drop ratio ∆P/P, and preheated 
inlet air temperature Tinlet. A typical measurement was initiated by setting ∆P =2% of atmosphere pressure, and 
setting Tinlet and Ф to the previously measured fuel lean blowout limit. The equivalence ratio was then continuously 
increased up to the stoichiometric equivalence ratio or fuel rich blow out by steps of Ф = 0.05. The inlet air preheat 
temperature was then increased by steps of ∆T = 200F and the procedure was repeated for the entire range of air–
fuel ratios within the lean and rich limits. The tests were repeated at ∆P =4% and 6% of atmosphere pressure as 
well. All data were acquired with 10s test duration for each operating condition. At the same time, a regular video 
camera continuously monitored and recorded the combustion modes. The high speed CCD camera was used to 
record flame dynamics at some selected test conditions. The frame rate of the high speed camera was 6000 frames 
per second, which is sufficient to cover the frequency range of the combustion dynamics.

III. Results and discussion 
 
 
The manifestation of combustion dynamics of the investigated combustor sector differs in many aspects .The 
flame structure, frequency spectra, and the relationship between combustion dynamics modes and flame structures 
are primarily dependent on the air-fuel ratio. In this section, we will first present data on flame and acoustic 
spectrum at different test conditions, and then discuss the relationship between the flame structures and the 
combustion dynamics modes. 
A. Combustion dynamics modes, flow field and flame structures.

The knowledge of the flow field inside the combustor 
should be helpful in understanding the flame structure and its 
related acoustic mode. The combustor studied in this paper 
has a complicated flow field distribution that can cause the 
significant flame structure changes during various operating 
conditions. Figure 9 shows a mean velocity distribution inside 
the combustor, measured by an LDV (Bassam et al [12]). Near 
the swirler exit, a strong central recirculation zone was located 
in dome zone with high speed swirling flow attached to the 
dome wall. The swirling flow and the recirculation zone in the 
dome region play an important role in anchoring the flame. 
The unstable flow structures such as the vortex shedding and 
precessing vortex core (PVC) inside the swirling flow, 
however, could also cause combustion instability.  Downstream 
of the dome zone, the primary dilution air jets are injected in to 
the combustor with high velocity. A low velocity wake region 
exists slightly downstream of the primary jet, and could be a 
possible location for flame anchoring. The unstable jet flow and 
the possible vortex shedding in the wake region may also 
induce unstable combustion.  Because the flow field shows 
significantly different images at different regions inside the 
combustor, the flame structure will be entirely different, 
depending on where the flame is anchored.  
To investigate the connections existing between acoustic 
modes and flame structures, the high speed camera pictures are 
essential to exhibit the combustion dynamics associated with 
the acoustic modes. The pictures obtained from high-speed 
video can be used to represent the locations of the flame 
structures corresponding to the different acoustic modes. Figures 
10a and b show two instantaneous flame images associated with 
the first and second acoustic modes. For lean combustion 
conditions (first acoustic mode dominates), shown in Fig. 10 (a), 
combustion occurred mainly within the dome region. The flame 
was anchored at the swirler exit and most of the combustion was 
completed before the location of first set of dilution holes. No 
interaction happened between flame and dilution air. This 
combustion instability mode is strongly coupled with the 
swirling flow and central recirculation zone in the dome region. 
For the case with higher equivalence ratio (Fig. 10 b), the 
reactant mixture was so fuel rich that almost no combustion 
occurred in the dome region. The flame was anchored 
downstream of the dilution holes. At this location, the flame 
front interacts with the dilution air jet and the vortex structure in 
the wake region. The second acoustic mode could be attributed 
to such strong interaction between the flame front and the 
aerodynamics.

swirling jet and recirculation zone. Figure 11b shows the phase angle distribution inside the burner. The phase angle 
difference between top and bottom high acoustic energy spots is about 180 degrees (3.14 radian). The 180 phase 
angle difference of these two opposite locations indicates a circumferential energy release rate related to this

C. The effect of the inlet air temperature to the combustion dynamics 
Figure 13 shows the effect of inlet air temperature to the combustion dynamics. The tests were performed 
at a swirler pressure drop of 4% of atmospheric pressure. Figure 13 presents the acoustic modes observed for air 
inlet temperatures of 200 F, 400 F and 600 F. Both first and second acoustic modes were observed for all three inlet 
air temperature cases. With increase of the inlet air temperature, both acoustic modes were observed to shift to 
higher frequencies. This kind of shift is predictable since the increase of the air temperature raises the flame 
temperature which results in an increase in the premixed burning speed, convective speed and acoustic velocity.

D. The effect of the pressure drop to the combustion dynamics 
Figure 14 shows the effect of the pressure drop across the 
swirler cup to the combustion dynamics. Three pressure drops of 
2%, 4% and 6% of atmospheric pressure were chosen. The inlet 
temperature was 600F for all three pressure drops. Both acoustic 
modes were observed for all three test conditions. But the effects of 
pressure drops on the two acoustic modes are different. Figure 14(a) 
shows the effect of pressure drop on the first acoustic mode 
(Ф=0.32 for all three pressure drops). With an increase in the 
pressure drop, the first mode acoustics moves to higher frequency. 
The acoustic frequency is observed to be proportional to the 
convective air speed. This again indicates the first acoustic mode is 
tightly connected to the characteristics of the swirling flow. Unlike 
the first acoustic mode, figure 14(b) shows that the effects of the 
pressure drop on the second acoustic mode (Ф=0.66 for all three 
pressure drops) are minor for 4% and 6% pressure drop. The 
physical processes that govern this behavior are still under 
investigation.

IV. Conclusion 
 
The acoustic emissions and flame dynamics of an atmospheric swirl-stabilized burner fed with natural gas 
has been investigated experimentally. This study revealed the existence combustion dynamics from both fuel-lean 
and fuel rich domes.  The fuel-lean dome dynamics was associated with a flame structure which is anchored at the 
swirler exit and the energy release was completed within the dome. As equivalence ratio was increased, the flame 
front will propagate downstream. The fuel-rich dome combustion dynamics occurred when the primary flame front 
was anchored downstream of the primary dilution jets. The inlet air temperatures and air pressure drops do not 
change the combustion dynamics modes but they do affect the acoustic emission frequencies. The physical 
processes govern the acoustic frequency changes are still under investigation. 
 
 
High speed video has been shown to be a powerful tool to analyze combustion instability. The images from 
the high speed video reveal the flame structures associated with a particular acoustic mode. Applying FFT analysis 
on the images reveals interactions among the energy release rate, the flame motion and the acoustic modes. The FFT 
analysis of the fuel-lean mode dynamics shows that a circular oscillation mechanism related to this mode, which is 
strongly coupled with the unsteady swirling flow. While the fuel-rich mode dynamics is related to an interaction of 
the unstable dilution air jet with the unburned fuel-rich dome flows.
