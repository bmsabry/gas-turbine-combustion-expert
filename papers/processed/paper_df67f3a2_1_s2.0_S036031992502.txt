A B S T R A C T
Addressing climate change requires the advancement of carbon-free fuels as a main strategy for reducing global 
greenhouse gas emissions. Among carbon-free fuels, ammonia is gaining attention for its carbon-free combustion 
and potential as a hydrogen carrier. However, its practical utilization in combustion systems is hindered by low 
reactivity, narrow flammability limits, slow flame speed, and elevated NOx emissions. This review presents a 
comprehensive analysis of ammonia combustion characteristics and recent developments aimed at overcoming 
these limitations. Enhancement strategies such as blending with hydrogen or methane, oxygen-enriched com­
bustion, partial ammonia cracking, plasma-assisted ignition, and oxidizer modification are critically examined. 
Blending ammonia with 30–60 % hydrogen or 20–50 % methane significantly improves flame speed and sta­
bility, while oxygen enrichment up to 28 % O2 can triple the laminar burning velocity. Partial ammonia cracking 
enhances reactivity, and plasma-assisted combustion extends lean blowout limits and reduces NOx emissions. 
Rich-lean staged combustion has proven effective for NOx control in gas turbine systems, with emissions reduced 
to below 100 ppm in optimized configurations. The combustion behavior of ammonia, both as pure fuel and in 
blends, is analyzed concerning flame characteristics and emission profiles across varying conditions. Despite 
these advancements, challenges remain in achieving stable, efficient, and low-emission combustion under 
operational limitations. This review provides a comprehensive reference for ongoing research and supports the 
continued advancement of ammonia as a promising carbon-free fuel within future clean energy systems.

1. Introduction
Energy is a fundamental necessity for sustaining human life. His­
torically, the conventional method for energy generation has relied on 
the combustion of fossil fuels, including coal, oil, and natural gas, due to 
their high energy density and widespread availability. The growth in 
global population and industrialization has led to a significant increase 
in energy demand, which in turn has escalated greenhouse gas emissions 
(GHG). These emissions result from various combustion processes used 
in electricity generation, transportation, and industrial operations. They 
contribute to a range of environmental issues and pollution concerns. 
Among these emissions, carbon dioxide (CO2) represents a significant 
threat to the ecosystem. It is the most abundant greenhouse gas pro­
duced by human activities and is responsible for approximately 30 % of 
the global warming effect. The continued release of CO2 into the at­
mosphere intensifies the greenhouse effect, leading to a rise in global 
temperatures [1,2]. Since combustion is a major contributor to GHG 
emissions, transitioning from traditional fossil fuels to renewable energy 
sources and biofuels is essential for reducing carbon emissions [3–5]. 
Renewable energy sources such as wind, solar, and tidal power offer 
clean alternatives, but their intermittent nature presents challenges for 
maintaining a continuous energy supply in combustion-based systems 
[6].

fuels like ammonia remains crucial for ensuring energy stability and 
reliability in future low-emission power systems. Moreover, the global 
transition away from conventional industrial burners presents an addi­
tional challenge. As a result, the development of alternative fuels to 
replace traditional fossil fuels is of critical importance for achieving 
sustainable energy goals.
Over the past decades, there has been a significant increase in 
research efforts and global interest in hydrogen (H2) energy, which is 
widely recognized as a key enabler of future sustainable energy systems. 
Hydrogen combustion produces no CO2 emissions, making it a clean 
energy source with minimal environmental impact [9,10]. However, the 
very reactive nature of H2 is coupled with unusually high hazard levels 
in its combustion and storage, limiting its use as fuel on a large scale [11,
12]. The most common techniques employed for H2 production and 
storage include fuel reforming, compression, liquefaction, hydrides, and 
adsorption. However, ammonia (NH3) is increasingly recognized as a 
suitable hydrogen carrier and a promising option for significantly 
reducing CO2 emissions in the energy sector. Its growing popularity is 
largely attributed to its high hydrogen content, which makes ammonia 
an effective medium for hydrogen storage and transportation [13]. 
Ammonia possesses several favorable attributes for use as a carbon-free 
fuel, including ready availability, established production infrastructure, 
ease of storage and transportation, and compatibility with existing fuel 
handling materials. However, compared to conventional hydrocarbon 
fuels, it has notable drawbacks such as lower calorific value, limited 
flammability, and reduced reactivity, which must be addressed for 
practical combustion applications. Ammonia is considered relatively 
safe to handle due to its long-standing industrial use, supported by 
well-established protocols for storage, transportation, and safety man­
agement [14,15]. As a fuel, it offers several practical advantages over 
hydrogen for many applications, particularly in terms of ease of storage 
and distribution. Ammonia can be liquefied, compared to liquid H2, has 
a higher energy density than liquid and compressed hydrogen, and is a 
more economical cost per unit stored energy [16]. Liquid NH3 features a 
significantly high volumetric energy density of 3.5 kWh/L, exceeding 
that of both liquid H2 at 2.9 kWh/L and compressed H2 at 2.1 kWh/L, as 
illustrated in Fig. 1 [17]. This characteristic enables liquid NH3 to 
necessitate a reduced storage volume compared to H2, facilitating a 
vehicle’s ability to achieve an adequate driving range.
For instance, over 182 days, the cost to store NH3 is $0.54/kg of H2 
equivalent, while storing pure H2 during the same time frame costs 
$14.95/kg [18]. Additionally, NH3 is easier to liquefy, store, and 
transport, as it can be contained in standard liquefied gas cylinders, 
whereas H2 necessitates specialized materials for its storage. The 
Haber-Bosch process, widely used in industrial usage today, synthesizes 
NH3, with nitrogen sourced from the air. However, fossil fuels still 
provide a source of hydrogen, leading to CO2 emissions during the 
production process [19,20]. The ammonia lifecycle for electricity gen­
eration is illustrated in Fig. 2. As a carrier of hydrogen, ammonia is 
produced from either conventional fuel, such as methane reforming, or 
alternative energy sources, wind, and biomass, in the form of green 
ammonia. Among these alternatives, ammonia production from 
renewable energy represents a sustainable and environmentally friendly 
pathway for the future energy economy. It enables a zero CO2 footprint 
throughout its lifecycle, positioning ammonia as a strong candidate for 
clean energy applications [21,22]. Furthermore, ammonia offers the 
advantage of easy storage and transportation using existing infrastruc­
ture. Ammonia can subsequently function as a crucial energy source for 
both combustion systems and fuel cells. In combustion applications, it 
can be directly burned or co-fired with other fuels to generate thermal 
energy, offering a low-carbon alternative to conventional fossil fuels. 
Meanwhile, in fuel cells, ammonia undergoes catalytic decomposition to 
release hydrogen, which is then converted into electricity through an 
electrochemical process.
These diverse applications underscore ammonia’s versatility as a 
sustainable energy carrier, contributing to the advancement of cleaner 
and more efficient energy technologies.
This review presents a comprehensive analysis of NH3 as a carbon- 
free fuel, emphasizing its combustion characteristics, associated chal­
lenges, and recent advancements aimed at enhancing its performance. It 
examines fundamental properties such as laminar burning velocity 
(LBV), flame stability, and emission behavior, both for pure ammonia 
and when blended with hydrogen or methane. Key enhancement stra­
tegies, including oxygen-enriched combustion, partial cracking, plasma- 
assisted ignition, and oxidizer modification, are critically assessed. The 
review also synthesizes findings from both experimental and numerical 
studies that investigate flame behavior, emissions, and reaction kinetics 
across a range of conditions. Applications of ammonia in combustion 
technologies such as gas turbines, internal combustion engines, marine

2. Characteristics of pure ammonia combustion
Ammonia offers the benefit of being free from carbon emissions and 
does not contribute directly to GHG effects. Ammonia can be produced 
by different methods, mainly by utilizing fossil fuels such as coal, oil, 
and natural gas. Additionally, ammonia can be synthesized through a 
completely carbon-free process by utilizing renewable energy sources 
such as solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, and ocean energy, primarily 
through electrolysis-powered hydrogen production [13,14]. Further­
more, NH3 can be synthesized using water, biomass, organic waste, and 
air as primary raw materials, with hydrogen extracted from these 
sources and combined with nitrogen through chemical processes [24,
25]. The complete combustion of NH3 fuel theoretically yields only N2 
and H2O, without producing CO2, allowing for safe release into the 
environment. Within this context, NH3 emerges as a promising option, 
providing a flexible and sustainable approach to energy storage, trans­
port, and use. Although ammonia holds promise as a clean fuel, its direct 
use in combustion systems presents several challenges. Ammonia ex­
hibits a high latent heat of vaporization and self-ignition temperature, as 
well as a slow flame speed. Furthermore, its nitrogen content contributes 
to elevated NOx emissions during combustion, requiring emission con­
trol strategies [18]. Therefore, further research is required to investigate 
the combustion characteristics and emission behavior of NH3 to address 
these limitations before it can be effectively used as an industrial fuel. 
The physical and chemical properties of NH3 in comparison with various 
fuels are summarized in Table 1.
Recent studies highlight a significant need to blend ammonia with 
other fuels that possess better combustion properties, such as H2 and 
CH4, to enhance the weak combustion performance of pure NH3 fuel 
[28,29]. Research frequently investigates blending NH3 with N2 and H2 
to simulate the combustion of partial cracking of NH3. It is important to 
highlight that NH3 begins to crack at approximately 200 ◦C. Using a 
catalyst, it can achieve a cracking efficiency of about 99 % at 425 ◦C, 
while this efficiency can be attained at 500 ◦C without a catalyst [30]. 
Numerous studies in the literature have been conducted to study the 
characteristics of NH3 as a standalone fuel or in combination with other 
reactive fuels such as H2 and CH4. These studies examine different 
factors, including flame propagation speed, flame stability, flammability 
limitations, and pollutant emissions. These investigations aim to utilize 
NH3 as an energy source in industrial combustion applications [31]. 
Zhang et al. [32] performed an experimental investigation to evaluate 
the effects of mixing CH4 and H2 with NH3 on NOx emissions. Their 
findings indicated that burning NH3/CH4/air mixtures can lower NOx 
and CO emissions, whereas NH3/H2/air mixtures can enhance com­
bustion intensity without raising NOx emissions. Kurata et al. [33] 
investigated the combustion of NH3/air in a 50 kW gas turbine 
combustor. They observed that the emissions of NO and unburnt NH3 
were affected by the combustor inlet temperature. Using NH3/CH4/air 
mixture revealed that increasing the NH3 ratio raised NO emissions but 
lowered the NO conversion rate. Frig and Gentili [34] investigated the 
effects of the NH3/H2 mixture in spark ignition (SI) engines, where the 
H2 was generated onboard via catalytic reforming. Their findings 
demonstrated that the addition of H2 significantly improved combustion 
stability by reducing cycle-to-cycle variations and instabilities in cylin­
der pressure. Moreover, the incorporation of H2 resulted in a higher LBV, 
thereby improving the overall efficiency of the combustion process. The 
change in the oxidizer’s concentration or type might also enhance the 
combustion of pure NH3, and catalytic combustion may be a potential 
method of reducing NOx emissions [35,36].
2.1. Laminar burning velocity (LBV) of ammonia fuel
Laminar burning velocity (LBV) plays a crucial role in analyzing 
combustion characteristics and evaluating combustor performance. This 
parameter is also crucial for validating reaction mechanisms, developing 
mathematical models, and providing essential data for combustion 
simulations. Accordingly, the study of premixed LBV is of significant 
importance [37,38]. The use of NH3/air as a fuel in combustion appli­
cations is constrained by its lower LBV and limited flame stability, as 
highlighted in reference [39]. Comprehensive experimental and nu­
merical studies have been conducted on the flame speed of NH3/air fuel. 
Various investigations by Roney et al. [40], Pfahl et al. [41], Takizwa 
et al. [42], Hayakawa et al. [43], Jabbour et al. [44], Wang et al. [45], 
Lhullier et al. [46], and Ji et al. [47] have measured the LBV of NH3/air 
with different equivalence ratio (ϕ), as shown in Fig. 5 [48]. In 1978, 
Zakeznov et al. [49] investigated the LBV of NH3/air flame. Notably, 
Hayakawa et al. [43] and Lhullier et al. [46] conducted their measure­
ments using a constant volume cylindrical combustion (CVCC) vessel, 
which enables precise control of pressure and ignition conditions. In

2.3. Emission characteristics of NH3 flame
During fuel combustion, the nitrogen (N2) present in both the fuel 
and the air serves as the primary source of NOx emissions. Nitrogen- 
containing organic compounds in carbon-based fuels undergo thermal 
decomposition, yielding intermediate species like NHi, HCN, and CN, 
which subsequently oxidize to generate fuel NOx [13,59]. Fuel NOx 
formation typically occurs within a moderate temperature range of 
600–900 ◦C. However, temperature dependence is complex and not 
solely governed by fuel decomposition temperatures; it also depends on 
reaction pathways involving transient species and local flame condi­
tions. This complexity is particularly evident in ammonia combustion, 
where the oxidation of intermediate species such as NH2 and NH leads to 
NOx formation, influenced by both temperature and flame structure 
[60]. Ammonia combustion is associated with high NOx emissions due 
to its nitrogen content. Fuel NOx forms from the oxidation of fuel-bound 
nitrogen, while thermal NOx arises at high temperatures via reactions 
between atmospheric nitrogen and oxygen [59]. These combined 
pathways result in higher NOx emissions than carbon-based fuels. 
Mitigation strategies such as flame temperature control, steam dilution, 
and fuel blending are essential for cleaner ammonia combustion [61,
62].

lindrical bushing and a cyclone with a low swirl number (Sg = 0.736), 
which had a length of 200 mm. The experimental results for a mixture 
inlet speed of 3.14 m/s are presented in Fig. 11. Under lean fuel con­
ditions, the emissions of NH3 and H2 are minimal, whereas the NO 
concentration
reaches approximately 2000 ppm. The quantity of NO reduces

negligible. Conversely, the concentration of unburned NH3 rises quickly

ϕ = 1.1. Therefore, the lowest concentrations of NH3 and NO occur at

using the reaction mechanism proposed by Matheu and Petrsen [63]. 
The analysis indicates that NO is the predominant contributor to NOx

emissions drop significantly, while unburned NH3 begins to emerge in 
the richer combustion zone. The occurrence of unburned NH3 suggests 
incomplete combustion at a higher ϕ.

slightly rich combustion for efficient ammonia utilization and emissions

According to different research, inserting a porous medium in the 
combustion chamber at higher inlet velocities can decrease NO gener­
ation and emissions by reducing the flame temperatures and improving 
heat transfer to reduce the localized high-temperature regions. Addi­
tionally, the porous medium enhances the flame stability within the 
combustor and promotes more uniform combustion [66]. However, NO 
and N2O emissions are impacted differently depending on the heat 
conductivity and porosity of porous materials. This technique cannot be 
applied in practical industrial combustion systems. The use of porous 
media in boilers and gas turbine combustion chambers presents several 
challenges, including thermal stress, material degradation, and 
increased pressure drop. There has been research into additional stra­
tegies to reduce NOx pollutants. Arima et al. [67] examined the impacts 
of introducing water to NH3/air burning under controlled parameters 
with negligible oxygen dilution. For each range of equivalency ratios in 
the non-premixed scenario, a decline in NOx concentration was seen as 
the steam content increased. The greatest impact was observed at ϕ of 
0.7, where increasing the steam addition led to a notable reduction in 
NOx concentration. NOx emissions decreased by 21 %, 29 %, and 36 % 
compared to the pure NH3 case for water contents of 25 %, 50 %, and 75 
% of the fuel mixture, respectively. The steam can decrease NO emis­
sions by suppressing the NH2 + O ↔ NO + H2 reaction via the H2O + O 
↔ OH + OH reaction. In the stoichiometric condition, lowering NOx 
emissions to less than 100 ppm is possible. Furthermore, when the 
reactant is mixed with water, the greatest significant decrease in NOx 
emissions occurs at lean mixture conditions. The amino radical NH2 is a 
crucial intermediate in combustion systems involving N2 compounds.
In oxygen-rich environments, NH2 can interact with O2 to form NO, 
which then contributes to higher NOx emissions. Conversely, in fuel-rich 
conditions, NH2 can effectively reduce NOx emissions by reacting with 
NO to produce N2 and H2O through the reaction NH2 + NO → N2 + H2O. 
The presence of NH2 can increase or decrease NOx formation, depending 
on the combustion conditions and the availability of O2 and other 
reactive species. It is primarily formed through reactions involving NH3 
and O/H radical groups [68–70], with the primary reaction being 
NH3+OH → NH2+H2O [71]. Under lean burn conditions, OH radicals 
are more available; therefore, the reaction NH3 + OH → NH2 + H2O 
becomes more significant [46]. In addition, there are almost no emis­
sions of unburned NH3 in this scenario, which is quite significant for 
actual industrial combustion.

3.2. Partial ammonia cracking
Due to its substantial hydrogen content, ammonia can act as an 
effective storage medium for small hydrogen molecules. Under appro­
priate conditions, the decomposition of NH3 can yield H2 and N2. The 
hydrogen produced features a low ignition energy and burns at a rapid 
rate. However, partially decomposing NH3 can enhance its combustion 
properties by generating H2 [76,77]. Therefore, one possible method to 
enhance NH3 combustion is to pre-crack the NH3 and its byproducts 
before introducing them into the combustion chamber [78].
Bowen et al. [30] study the effects of pressure on the LBV of partially 
decomposed ammonia. The LBV decreases with an increasing initial 
pressure at ϕ of 1.0. As the NH3 dissociated, the LBV rose significantly 
from 8 cm/s to 22.8 cm/s, highlighting the effectiveness of partial NH3 
cracking in enhancing combustion Fig. 18. Under stoichiometric con­
ditions, an NH3 fuel mixture with a 60 % dissociation degree can achieve 
an LBV even higher than that of stoichiometric CH4/air. However, the 
concentration of NO initially rises with the percentage of NH3 dissoci­
ation, peaking at around 70 % dissociation with a maximum concen­
tration of approximately 12,000 ppm.
Hussein et al. [79] investigated NH3/H2 combustion in a swirl 
combustor to decrease NOx emissions. Different NH3/H2 mixtures were 
introduced after the main reaction zone to identify the optimal blend 
that minimizes NOx concentration. The results showed that a 60 %–40 % 
NH3–H2 blend achieved the lowest unburned NH3 and highest flame 
temperature, but with high NO emissions. Introducing a small volume 
(4 %) of NH3/H2 mixture downstream significantly decreased NO 
emissions, attributed to improved circulation and extended residence 
time. The investigation indicated that the injection strategy effectively 
reduces NOx emissions, providing a promising approach for cleaner NH3 
combustion in gas turbines. In another study, Lesmana et al. [80] studied 
the LBV of partially dissociated NH3/air at ambient conditions, utilizing 
both experimental and numerical methods. They adjusted the NH3 
dissociation levels from 0 to 50 % and utilized various reaction mech­
anisms in Chemkin modeling to predict the LBV. The results showed that 
higher NH3 dissociation led to larger initial flame kernels and a more 
luminous flame. The presence of H2 boosted the presence of crucial 
radicals like CH, OH, H, O, and NH2, notably increasing the LBV. 
Hydrogen is highly reactive, producing heat and free radicals that 
accelerate the combustion reactions, leading to faster dissociation and 
breakdown of NH3 and other fuel components, resulting in higher flame 
speeds and more efficient combustion. Partial ammonia cracking en­
hances NH3 fuel combustion by producing a hydrogen-enriched mixture, 
improving flame speed and stability while reducing NOx emissions. This 
approach holds significant potential for sustainable energy technologies.
3.3. Plasma-assisted combustion
Plasma, recognized as the fourth state of matter, is fundamentally a 
gas in an ionized state that plays a crucial role within the universe. 
Interestingly, plasma constitutes nearly 99 % of the cosmos. In this 
energized condition, it comprises a dynamic assortment of reactive 
particles, including free radicals, charged ions, electrons, atomic species, 
neutral molecules, and their high-energy variations, which drive 
numerous natural and technological processes. This state presents an 
exclusive capability for managing combustion and emissions owing to 
its distinctive capacity to generate heat, dynamic species, and alter 
transfer mechanisms. When energetic ions or electrons interact with O2 
molecules, they produce O radicals. These radicals can be utilized to 
modify and enhance the fuel oxidation mechanism in combustion sys­
tem applications [81,82].
Over the past two decades, plasma-assisted combustion (PAC) tech­
nology has been regarded as a highly effective method for enhancing 
combustion processes in propulsion systems, internal combustion en­
gines (ICEs), fuel conversion, and environmental management [83–86]. 
This method utilizes both equilibrium plasmas, such as spark plugs and 
plasma torches, as well as non-equilibrium plasmas, including micro­
wave discharge, streamer, surface, and nanosecond repetitively pulsed 
discharges, to improve combustion efficiency and reduce emissions [82,
87–90]. These innovative plasma technologies have shown great po­
tential in various scientific and engineering applications, offering new 
avenues for enhancing combustion processes and environmental sus­
tainability. Due to its narrow flammable range, NH3 faces significant 
limitations in actual combustion processes. Leveraging plasma to 
enhance NH3 combustion could potentially yield more favorable out­
comes in this regard.

4. Combustion characteristics of NH3/H2 and NH3/CH4 mixtures
Ammonia blends are preferred to combat the inherent limitations of 
pure NH3 combustion, such as low LBV and temperature, unstable 
combustion with poor kinetics, and high NOx emissions. The conse­
quences of incorporating hydrogen and methane into ammonia-based 
combustion will be discussed in the ensuing section.
4.1. Characteristics of NH3/H2 flame
Hydrogen is a highly desirable clean fuel due to its many advantages. 
It burns without releasing any dangerous pollutants or GHG other than 
water. It is a common choice due to its superior calorific value, which 
surpasses that of all fossil fuels, chemical fuels, and biofuels. This 
remarkable energy density makes it highly efficient for various com­
bustion and energy generation applications. However, the high tem­
perature at which H2 burns causes a substantial amount of thermal NOx 
to be produced [96]. Blending NH3 with H2 presents a strategic method 
for managing combustion characteristics. The low reactivity of NH3 
helps to increase the combustion intensity of H2, effectively lowering 
thermal NOx emissions. However, this blending also introduces addi­
tional N2, which may result in increased fuel NOx emissions. Never­
theless, the inclusion of H2 in the mixture boosts the LBV, thereby 
improving overall combustion efficiency [81]. In pure NH3 combustion, 
stability typically occurs around 1200 K. However, adding H2 enhances 
the presence of important radicals like H, O, and OH within the flame 
regions, enabling NH3 combustion to initiate at a lower temperature of 
approximately 950 K. The ϕ of NH3/H2/air mixture influences the start 
and end temperatures of NH3 oxidation [97]. NH3/H2/air flames exhibit 
lower blow-off and leaner combustion limits than NH3/CH4/air flames. 
However, NH3/H2 flames have a narrower lean combustion limit 
compared to NH3/CH4 flames. For instance, it is shown that the lean 
blowout limit for NH3/H2 mixtures occurs at an ϕ of 0.6, whereas 
NH3/CH4 mixtures can stabilize the flame at lower equivalence ratios, 
down to approximately 0.4 [98]. This narrower limit in NH3/H2 blends 
can pose challenges for achieving stable combustion in lean-burn ap­
plications. The range of stability expands with the rise in the H2 mole 
fraction (XH2) in NH3/H2/air mixtures. Nevertheless, it is crucial to 
consider that the high diffusivity of H2 can lead to flashback, which may 
hinder the combustion stability of the NH3/H2/air mixture, particularly 
at high H2 concentrations [99].

sharply increases to ϕ of 0.79 when XNH3 reaches 1.0 [103]. Across the 
full range of ϕ from 0.17 to 1.4, a higher NH3 concentration in the fuel 
mixture results in decreased reactivity and slower flame speed, which 
contributes to blowout [102]. From XNH3 equal to 0.4, ϕ at flashback 
increases with a higher concentration of NH3 in the fuel. However, for 
XNH3 greater than 0.5, this ratio rises much more sharply compared to 
when XNH3 is less than 0.5, climbing from 0.47 at XNH3 of 0.5–0.9 at XNH3 
of 0.7.
In the same combustor, Khateeb et al. [104] investigated stability 
limits at elevated pressures with bulk jet velocity and Sg = 1. The lean 
stable range of ϕ is from 0.2 to 1.1which extremely limited for different 
pressures (about Δϕ ≈0.05) when the XNH3 is between 0 and 0.50 [104]. 
As illustrated in Fig. 30, in the NH3/H2 mixture, higher pressure en­
hances flashback and delays lean blowout limits, but only when XNH3 is 
above 0.75. Rising pressure slows the transition from flashback to rich 
blowout as the proportion of NH3 in the fuel blend increases. When a 
sufficient amount of NH3 is incorporated into the blend, substituting 
NH3 for H2 increases the stability range. The widest range of stable ϕ, 
approximately Δϕ ≈0.9, is observed at an XNH3 of 0.85 and a pressure of 
1.0 bar, which is changed from ϕ of 0.2 at XNH3 of 0.01 to ϕ of 1.1 at XNH3 
of 0.85.

4.2.1. Laminar burning velocity of NH3/CH4 mixture
Continuing the discussion on NH3/H2 blends, this subsection exam­

flames demonstrates a nearly linear increase with the ratio of CH4, 
whereas the addition of H2 to NH3/air flames results in a more pro­

reactivity of H2 compared to CH4. This improvement is observed across 
both lean and rich conditions, mainly due to methane’s greater reac­

mixture approaches theoretical combustion conditions, but subse­

[113,114].

4.2.2. Stability limits of NH3/CH4 flame
The effects of adding NH3 on flame macrostructure and stability 
limits are explored in this section. T. F. Guiberti et al. [116] examined 
the flame structure and stability of the NH3/CH4 fuel mixture in a swirl 
burner. As illustrated in Fig. 37 the time-averaged flame images of the 
premixed NH3/CH4 combustion with various XNH3 at Re = 5000 and 
swirl number of 1 (Sg). The incorporation of NH3 appears to have little 
effect on the overall flame structure. However, an increase in the in­
tensity of the orange-yellow color can be observed as XNH3 increases. 
This color is primarily attributed to the NH2, often known as the nitro­
gen luminescence [117]. Temperature levels significantly influence 
flame radiation wavelengths. Higher temperatures enhance radiation 
intensity in the visible and near-infrared regions by exciting molecular 
species like NH2, while lower temperatures reduce the intensity of the 
wavelengths. Increasing XNH3 can lower flame temperatures due to 
lower reactivity of NH3 compared to CH4, thereby affecting the flame’s 
spectral characteristics.
Fig. 38 depicts the flammability and stability limits of the mixture 
with a different XNH3 from 0 to 1. The grey area indicates the flammable 
limit, which decreases as XNH3 increases in the mixture. Substituting CH4 
with NH3 in the fuel mixture reduces the rich flammability limit’s 
equivalence ratio from 1.69 to 1.46. Meanwhile, the ϕ value of the lean 
flammability limit rises from ϕ of 0.48 for CH4/air to ϕ of 0.63 for NH3/ 
air [118].
The blue region illustrates the range of flame stability, with lean 
blowout denoted by red triangles, flashback represented by black circles, 
and rich blowout indicated by blue squares. These markers delineate the 
operational limits of stable combustion under varying conditions. The ϕ 
value at lean blowout increases with the addition of NH3, rising from 0.5 
for pure CH4 to 0.79 for pure NH3. As NH3 is added, the ϕ at rich blowout 
decreases, from 1.40 at XNH3 of 0.50–1.24 at XNH3 of 1. The shift from 
flashback to rich blowout above 50 % NH3 is due to ammonia’s lower 
reactivity, resulting in a broader stability range compared to CH4/air 
[116].
Khateeb et al. [104] demonstrated the stability limits for 
NH3/CH4/air mixture in a swirl combustor at elevated pressures, as 
shown in Fig. 39. It is indicated that as the XNH3 increases, the value ϕ for 
both the lean blowout and flashback also increases. This is due to the 
reduced reactivity and slower flame speed caused by higher NH3 content 
at a fixed ϕ. The stable range of ϕ remains consistent and narrow, about 
Δϕ ≈0.1, for XNH3 below 0.6. When XNH3 exceeds 0.6, the flame velocity 
needed to induce a flashback cannot be achieved. The effect of pressure 
variation on stability limits with varying XNH3 is almost similar across 
different pressure values. However, the ϕ values for both lean blowout 
and flashback decline as pressure increases across different values of 
XNH3. Additionally, the XNH3 value at which the transition from flash­
back to rich blowout occurs rises as the pressure rises from 0.60 at 1 bar 
to 0.75 at 5 bar.
Another study by Giovanni et al. [119] investigated the stability 
limits of NH3/CH4 blends and observed that, under fuel-lean conditions, 
the extinction ϕ remains fairly steady at around 0.4, up to 75 % NH3 in

fuel-lean extinction behavior is shaped by the interplay between

ϕ. In fuel-rich conditions, the extinction ϕ stays consistent from 0 % NH3

a nearly linear manner as the NH3 percentage increases, reaching 1.4 for 
pure NH3, as observed in Fig. 40. The observed extinction behavior is 
associated with the kinetic stabilization of the reactive structure. The

4.2.3. Emissions characteristics of NH3/CH4 flame
When NH3 and CH4 are mixed and combusted, it results in the 
emission of both CO2 and NOx, making the emission characteristics of 
the combustion significant. Researchers have conducted emission 
studies of NH3/CH4/air with various conditions, and they indicated that 
adding CH4 to NH3/air in a swirl burner increases CO2 emissions, but at 
low XCH4, NOx emissions are not much impacted. But, when the XCH4 is 
increased to 30 %, both NOx and CO emissions increase [53]. However, 
the relationship between CH4 addition and NOx emissions depends on 
the fuel’s combustion pattern.
Xiao et al. [113] carried out a numerical study on the NH3/CH4 fuel 
mixture to study NO, CO2, and CO emissions across a broad range of 
XCH4 and ϕ. Fig. 41 illustrates the variation in NO emissions in flue gases 
across different NH3 fractions and ϕ. It is indicated that the NO emission 
peak value changes considerably depending on ϕ. The peak value of NO 
occurs at around 20 % and 60 % of NH3 fraction at ϕ of 1.4 and 1, 
respectively. This variation highlights the critical impact of ϕ on the 
production of NOx emissions, as demonstrated in Fig. 41. It is indicated 
that at high NH3 regions, NOx emissions and adiabatic flame tempera­
ture show a correlated trend. When NH3 exceeds 60 % in stoichiometric 
conditions, both temperature and NOx decrease with NH3 increase. 
Conversely, NOx rises as more CH4 is replaced by NH3 below 60 % NH3. 
These observations align with findings previously reported by others 
[33,116].
The impact of NH3 mole fraction and ϕ on CO2 and CO emissions is 
illustrated in Fig. 42 [113]. As the NH3 mole fraction rises, CO2 de­
creases because of the reduced carbon availability in the fuel blends. 
Stoichiometric conditions exhibit higher CO2 outputs compared to lean 
and rich conditions, attributed to improved combustion efficiency. The 
mixtures with over 60 % NH3 generate half the CO2 compared to pure 
CH4. The concentration of CO emissions decreases as the NH3 mole 
fraction increases. These decreases are primarily due to the reduction in 
CH4 concentration within the NH3/CH4 blend.
Zheng et al. [120] carried out a comprehensive study to investigate 
swirl-stabilized features of ammonia-methane-air mixture combustion 
emissions from a swirl-stabilized burner. The fuel mixture, which has a 
high CH4 content of 90 %, leads to a substantial rise in the concentration 
of NOx emissions. Whenever XCH4 exceeds 0.5, there is a reduction in 
NOx emissions. However, if XCH4 is decreased beyond this point, NOx 
emissions start to decline, as illustrated in Fig. 43. NH3/CH4/air mix­
tures display notable NOx emissions in fuel-lean conditions, in contrast 
to traditional fuels. This implies that utilizing the lean fuel combustion 
technique for this fuel blend cannot achieve low NOx emissions. For 
NH3/CH4/air combustion, maximum NOx content is located around ϕ =
1, and the concentration of NO is decreased in rich combustion condi­
tions. When the fuel is lean, the NOx emissions peak as XCH4 gets close to 
0.5. Therefore, it is recommended to avoid blending CH4 and NH3 in 
identical molar ratios during actual combustion. NOx emissions are not 
affected by the initial fuel temperature, making their effect almost 
negligible [121]. NOx emissions are higher in lean and theoretical fuel 
conditions compared to rich fuel conditions. The deNOx process at high 
ϕ is aided by unburned NH3, but it also results in increased emissions of 
unburned NH3. Therefore, in NH3 combustion, it is important to 
consider not only low NOx emissions but also NH3 emissions. Pure NH3 
as fuel has a negligible NH3 concentration in fuel-lean combustion 
exhaust. When the ϕ is more than 1.1, NH3 emissions significantly rise 
and can reach 4000 ppm at ϕ equal to 1.2 for pure NH3 combustion. 
However, it is conceivable to simultaneously control NOx emissions and 
unburned emissions of NH3 at appropriate levels at ϕ of 1.1 [53].
Analyses of the generation rates of various NOx reaction mechanisms 
are conducted to identify the primary NOx generation mechanism for 
NH3/CH4/air mixture combustion. This approach helps to pinpoint the 
dominant processes contributing to NOx emissions under these specific 
conditions. Table 6 illustrates the four primary pathways involved in the 
generation of NOx in flames containing NH3. In the stoichiometric case, 
the formation of NOx emissions is primarily driven by the thermal 
pathway, whereas the HNO pathway contributes minimally.
Conversely, under lean mixture conditions, the HNO pathway serves 
as the dominant mechanism for NOx formation, playing a crucial role in 
the overall emission characteristics. Under rich mixture conditions, the 
formation of NOx emissions primarily originates from the NHi pathway.

improving detailed chemical kinetics mechanisms that accurately 
simulate the NH3 combustion, taking into account variables such as 
equivalence ratio, temperature, pressure, and burning flame velocity. 
Additionally, there’s significant interest in applying these mechanisms 
to dual fuel mixtures, such as NH3/H2 and NH3/CH4 mixtures, to capture 
differences in combustion characteristics. This part of the overview 
highlights the development and performance of these mechanisms in 
different combustion cases.
Miller et al. [125] were the pioneers in publishing a comprehensive 
kinetic mechanism for the oxidation of NH3, providing fundamental 
insights into its reaction pathways and combustion dynamics. Their 
pioneering work introduced an intricate kinetic model for the oxidation 
of NH3, involving 22 distinct species and 98 elementary reactions. This 
mechanism emphasized the significance of NHi radicals (where i = 0, 1, 
2) in the formation of NO and N2, highlighting their crucial involvement 
in the combustion mechanism. The fundamental reaction pathways 
involving these radicals are depicted in Fig. 44, which represents the 
intricate interactions that culminate in the generation of combustion 
byproducts. Different experimental data were used to validate this 
model, such as flame stability and propagation of NH3/O2 and 
NH3/H2/O2 flame configurations. These validations demonstrated a 
high degree of agreement with experimental results across a spectrum of 
conditions, ranging from lean to slightly rich conditions. Nonetheless, 
the model encountered difficulties in accurate forecasts for rich condi­
tion cases, highlighting the need for further improvement and investi­
gation in these areas. Expanding upon the initial research, Miller et al. 
[126] broadened their chemical kinetic model to include the oxidation 
reactions between C1 to C2 fuels and NH3, incorporating an extensive 
mechanism of 234 reaction pathways. They explored various processes, 
such as the thermal formation of NO, N2 conversion in fuel, and Thermal 
De-NOx mechanisms, in addition to the formation and removal of NO2 
and N2O. Despite these significant advancements, the exact mechanisms 
through which these reactions contribute to N2O formation remain 
ambiguous, underscoring the necessity for additional improvement and 
investigation.
Building on this foundation, Lindstedt et al. [127] introduced a 
comprehensive chemical kinetic mechanism for NH3 oxidation encom­
passing 95 intermediate reactions and 21 species, to investigate the 
impact of NH3 on mitigating NOx emissions. The authors focused on the 
breakdown of NH3 and the generation pathway of NO and N2O from 
various radicals generated during NH3/O2 or H2/O2 combustion. They 
identified that NH3 breakdown occurs through the interaction with 3 
different radicals in different flame conditions. In rich NH3/O2 flames, 
the H radical initiates an interaction with the NH3 molecule. In stoi­
chiometric flames, the OH radical breaks down the NH3 molecule (NH3 
+ OH → NH2 + H2). In lean flames, the O radical interacts with NH3 
(NH3 + O → NH2 + OH).
Additionally, the study examined NO generation to facilitate the 
potential development of future mechanisms aimed at alleviating NO 
emissions. It is concluded that HNO radicals are responsible for about 
70 % of NO production, while N contributes between 10 % and 15 % of 
the total NO generation, due to the slow production rate of nitrogen 
atoms. The formation of N2O is attributed to the reaction between NH 
radicals and NO (NH + NO → N2O + H). The NH radicals can also 
produce N2 and OH (NH + NO → N2 + OH.
Konnov [128] significantly advanced the improvement of chemical 
kinetic models of NH3 combustion. He initiated the process by refining 
the Miller model, integrating sub-mechanisms for the N2H3 and N2H4 
generation via the polymerization reactions of NHi radicals. This 
enhancement significantly impacted the calculated rise-time and peak 
concentrations of NH and NH2 radicals, underscoring the critical role of 
these reactions in accurately modeling NH2 combustion dynamics. In 
another study Konnov [129] refined the H2/O2 chemical kinetic mech­
anism by revising almost all 30 kinetic rates by the most recent chemical 
information available. Additionally, Konnov [130] developed the NCN 
pathway for intermediate NO formation, enabling precise forecasts of 
NO production in both lean and rich mixture combustion of methane, 
ethylene, ethane, and propane. Additionally, the study demonstrated 
alignment with existing results of NCN radicals for low-pressure com­
bustion, highlighting the resilience and precision of his kinetic models.
Duynslagher et al. [131] developed an enhanced chemical kinetic 
model for NH3 oxidation, which is valid for predicting the structure of 
the flame for NH3 and H2 under low-pressure conditions. The study 
aimed to improve the understanding of NOx generation during NH3 
combustion, which is crucial for its application as a renewable fuel in SI 
engines. The improved mechanism was based on the Konnov model 
[128], with modifications to better predict N2O and NH2 concentrations. 
The generation and consumption pathways involving these radicals of 
the modified Konnov mechanism are illustrated in Fig. 45 (a) and (b). 
The researchers fine-tuned the rate constants of key reactions involving 
these species, using theoretical calculations and experimental data to 
achieve better agreement with experimental flame structures. The 
improved mechanism was then reduced to 80 elementary reactions and 
19 chemical species for incorporation into CFD simulations. The 
research highlighted the importance of accurately modeling NOx for­
mation pathways to guarantee the environmental sustainability of NH3 
as a fuel source.
Shrestha et al. [54] developed a detailed chemical kinetic mecha­
nism for NH3/O2 combustion, with a specific focus on NOx formation 
pathways and potential mitigation strategies. The kinetic model is 
validated against various experimental setups, including propagating 
and combustor-stabilized flames, shock tubes, jet-stirred burners, and 
plug-flow combustors. This study emphasizes the importance of NOx 
chemistry, showing how doping with NO2 can influence H2/O2. The 
model accurately predicts LBV, ignition delay time (IDT), and species 
concentration, demonstrating its robustness and applicability in com­
bustion simulations. Stagni et al. [57] developed a comprehensive 
chemical kinetic mechanism for NH3, incorporating 31 species and 203 
reactions, to enhance the pyrolysis and oxidation modeling of NH3 in 
scenarios characterized by low temperatures and high dilution. The

Song et al. [133] carried out experiments on NH3 oxidation at 
elevated pressures of 30 and 100 atm, within a temperature range from 
450 to 925 K. They indicated that NH3 oxidation was more reactive 
under oxidizing conditions, with complete NH3 consumption at 875 K. 
The kinetic model highlighted the significant role of the H2NO inter­

involving NH2 and HO2 radicals as shown in Fig. 46. In another study by 
Wang et al. [134] carried out an experimental investigation on the LBV

model’s reliability and precision in predicting these parameters. Sensi­

Mendiara and Glarborg [135] conducted a study on NH3 oxidation in 
the context of CH4/O2 combustion, with a particular emphasis on the 
impact of elevated CO2 levels. Their findings indicated that elevated 
levels of CO2 augmented the formation of NO. They developed a kinetic 
model containing 97 species and 779 reactions. The model identified 
major reaction pathways, showing that high CO2 levels facilitated NO 
formation by increasing the OH/H ratio and enhancing HNCO genera­
tion through the reaction of NH2 + CO. Nevertheless, reactions forming 
HNO and NH were inhibited due to reduced O and H radical concen­
trations, resulting in increased N2 formation instead of NO as illustrated 
in Fig. 47.
Tian et al. [136] carried out an experimental study coupled with 
chemical kinetic modeling of NH3/CH4/O2/Ar premixed combustion at 
low pressure. The research examined the impact of varying the NH3/CH4 
mole ratio on flame structure. The proposed kinetic model, comprising 
84 species and 703 reactions, successfully predicted the concentration 
patterns of primary species and intermediate compounds. Jiang et al. 
[137] updated the San Diego chemical kinetic nitrogen model [138] to 
better predict of combustion behaviors of NH3 and NH3/H2 mixtures. 
The revised mechanism encompasses 60 elementary reactions involving 
19 reactive species, improving predictions for conditions of elevated 
temperature and pressure pertinent to industrial utilization. The revised 
model was validated using experimental data, showing better agreement 
for ignition delay times, LBV, and NOx formation compared to the 
original mechanism. Okafor et al. [114] investigated the LBV of 
CH4/NH3/air premixed flames experimentally and numerically. They 
found that the LBV decreased with increasing NH3 concentration. The 
mechanism proposed by Tian et al. [136] underestimated the LBV due to 
the dominance of specific HCO conversion reactions. In contrast, GRI 
Mech 3.0 [139] provided closer LBV predictions but lacked key reaction 
pathways relevant to NH3 combustion. A new detailed reaction mech­
anism combining GRI Mech 3.0 and the Tian et al. mechanism was 
developed, accurately modeling both LBV and NO emissions.
Arunthanayothin et al. [140] explored the oxidation of CH4/NH3 in 
both JSR and FR, within the temperature range from 600 to 2000 K at a 
pressure of 1 atm. They found that NH3 addition promoted CH4 reac­
tivity below 1200 K, primarily due to the role of NOx in improving 
reactivity. At temperatures exceeding 1200 K, the reactivity was pre­
dominantly driven by hydrogen abstractions occurring in both fuels. The 
study employed a kinetic model to analyze the findings, highlighting the 
significant impact of NOx as an intermediate in NH3 oxidation, which 
anticipates CH4 reactivity, especially under lean conditions. In another

Recent studies have extensively examined the LBV of NH3, both as a 
standalone fuel and in hydrogen-enriched blends. These investigations 
have aimed to identify the key chemical pathways that influence flame 
propagation under atmospheric conditions, frequently employing 
sensitivity analyses and kinetic modeling to do so. Alnasif et al. [143] 
conducted a detailed evaluation of NH3/H2 blends (70/30 vol%) by 
combining experimental LBV measurements with a comparative anal­
ysis of 36 detailed reaction mechanisms, as summarized in Table 7. This 
wide-ranging comparison provided insights into model accuracy across 
different ϕ, highlighting significant disparities in predictive capability 
under both lean and rich combustion regimes. Their findings revealed 
that the mechanism developed by Duynslaegher et al. [131] showed the 
highest accuracy under lean conditions, with an average percentage 
error (APE) between 2.0 % and 6.0 %. In contrast, the mechanism by 
Nakamura et al. [144] provided more reliable predictions for rich 
combustion scenarios. These results underline the critical importance of 
selecting context-specific kinetic models for accurate simulation of 
NH3/H2 combustion.
The Duynslaegher mechanism notably outperformed the widely used 
Konnov mechanism [128], which was found to overpredict NH2 mole 
fractions and underpredict N2O formation. This improvement is largely 
attributed to the inclusion of intermediate N2Hx species, which play a 
key role in nitrogen chemistry and NOx pathways. Furthermore, Alna­
sif’s sensitivity analysis under lean conditions identified two dominant 
reactions affecting LBV: NH2+ NH2⇌ N2H2 + H2, which enhances the 
LBV, and NH2+ H ⇌ NH + H2, which has a suppressing effect. These 
reactions govern radical formation and energy release, and their accu­
rate representation is essential for kinetic models used in 
ammonia-based fuel design.
It is important to recognize that each kinetic mechanism is designed 
and validated for particular conditions, specifically aligned with the 
goals of individual studies. Nevertheless, certain models demonstrate 
superior performance in predicting specific combustion characteristics 
when compared to others. Consequently, additional investigation is 
necessary to create a comprehensive chemical kinetic model that can 
accurately predict combustion characteristics under a variety of mixing 
parameters. To offer a thorough overview of the progress in this area, 
Table 8 compiles the different chemical kinetic mechanisms for NH3 and 
its blends. This summary provides a clear and detailed understanding of 
the various mechanisms and their applicability under different 
conditions.
In summary, optimizing the combustion characteristics of NH3 has 
been a focal point of extensive research efforts. Through the investiga­
tion of experimental data and the refining of the rate constants, re­
searchers endeavor to improve the precision of kinetic mechanisms, 
thereby enhancing predictions related to flame velocity, NOx genera­
tion, and IDT. to create an all-encompassing mechanism capable of 
precisely predicting combustion characteristics over a wide range of 
mixing conditions. This ongoing research is vital for advancing our 
understanding and optimizing combustion processes in various fuel 
mixtures.

6. Utilization of ammonia in different energy system 
applications
Extensive research has confirmed that ammonia holds great promise 
as an alternative, sustainable substitute for traditional fossil fuels. 
Various studies have assessed the NH3 viability in SI engines, CI engines, 
micro combustors, and GT engines. NH3 offers several advantages over 
conventional fuels due to its physical characteristics. These benefits 
include higher energy density, easier storage and transportation, and 
reduced GHG. Additionally, NH3 can be generated from renewable re­
sources, making it a more sustainable option for the future [192]. The 
utilization of NH3 as an energy source spans multiple sectors and can be 
broadly classified into five main categories, as shown in Fig. 49. 
• Power Generation: Ammonia is utilized as an energy source in 
various combustion applications, including gas turbines (GT) for 
power generation, steam generators, and cogeneration systems. The 
energy produced can serve diverse purposes such as industrial pro­
cessing and heating. This approach supports a broad spectrum of 
energy demands while contributing to the reduction of carbon 
emissions.
• Land transportation: Ammonia serves as a sustainable alternative 
fuel for IC engines. Commonly used in vehicles such as automobiles, 
trucks, and buses, these engines can be adapted to run on NH3, 
providing a renewable and environmentally friendly energy source. 
This transition presents a promising strategy for reducing emissions 
and limiting air pollution from the transportation sector.

6.1. Ammonia in gas turbines
The use of NH3 as an alternative fuel for GT remains largely in the 
research and development phase, although there are some existing ap­
plications. Recently, using NH3 in combination with other fuels for gas 
turbines has garnered significant interest among scientists and engi­
neers. This approach offers several advantages, including enhanced 
combustion efficiency and reduced NOx emissions [193]. Okafor et al. 
[122] carried out both experimental and theoretical investigations to 
analyze the NOx and other pollutants in micro GT burners using 
NH3/CH4 mixture. The micro swirl burner, as shown in Fig. 50, has a 
swirl number of 0.88, equipped with a 12-hole fuel injector designed 
with a 45◦injection angle. NOx emissions exceeded 5000 ppm with ϕ 
ranging from 0.8 to 1 and decreased with higher ϕ. Comparing 
NH3/CH4/air flames to NH3/air flames, it was observed that the NOx 
emissions were found to approximately double for the NH3/CH4/air 
mixture. The NO emission in NH3 flames was found to be highly 
dependent on the O, H, and OH radicals. N2O production primarily 
occurred through reactions of NO with NH and HO2. At ϕ below 1.2, the 
low concentrations of HCN and unburned NH3 fuel in the primary 
combustion zone limited significant NO reduction. In richer flames, a 
higher concentration of HCN and unburned NH3 fuel from the primary 
combustion zone promoted the formation of NO rather than its con­
sumption in the secondary combustion zone. This is attributed to the 
high concentrations of O2, CO, and H2, which facilitate NO production.

condition primary burning zone resulted in low NO production. How­

HCN, and unburned hydrocarbons. The oxidation of CO and H2 within 
the secondary burning zone produced elevated levels of O/H radicals, 
which promoted the transformation of HCN and NH3 predominantly 
into NO. A single-stage combustion process for NH3/CH4 produced

in the two-stage combustion process.
Numerous studies are actively investigating different NH3/H2 
mixture compositions to evaluate combustion properties, including LBV, 
combustion temperature, and calorific value. The findings indicate that 
incorporating H2 or CH4 substantially influences the combustion prop­
erties of NH3, potentially enhancing its performance and reducing 
associated issues such as low flame speed, poor flammability limits, and 
high NOx emissions associated with pure NH3 combustion [59,195]. 
Ammonia co-firing has been demonstrated in pilot-scale gas turbines. 
The main challenge lies in achieving flame stability and reducing NOx 
emissions. Further work is needed to optimize combustion staging and 
emission control strategies.
6.2. Ammonia in internal combustion engines
Ammonia emerges as a promising clean energy source for next- 
generation engines, with research suggesting that its utilization as a 
fuel requires minimal modifications to existing engine structures [196,
197]. This adaptability enhances its feasibility for widespread adoption 
in sustainable energy systems. However, engines running on pure 
ammonia fuel encounter several issues. To address these challenges, 
Zamfirescu and Dincer [198] investigated various performance metrics, 
including system stability, operational range, fuel tank efficacy, and cost 
of operation. Their study assessed the feasibility of employing NH3 as a 
fuel for engines by analyzing these metrics and proposed potential so­
lutions to challenges such as low energy density, storage complexity, 
and combustion inefficiencies associated with the use of pure NH3 in 
engine applications.
Several studies have demonstrated that blending NH3 with diesel or 
biodiesel can result in decreased fuel consumption and lower carbon 
emissions in engines, while still achieving a high-power output. This 
approach enhances combustion efficiency and contributes to more sus­
tainable energy utilization. Ebrahim et al. [199] developed a 1-D 
simulation model to investigate the use of NH3 and biodiesel as fuels. 
They analyzed the impact of different NH3 flow rates while keeping the 
biodiesel injection rate constant, focusing on engine performance and 
NOx emissions characteristics. Their findings indicated that NH3 could 
substitute up to 69.4 % of biodiesel in the fuel mixture. An increase in 
the NH3 flow rate led to a decline in brake thermal efficiency because 
ammonia has a lower energy density compared to traditional fuels like 
gasoline or diesel. This means more fuel is needed to produce the same 
amount of power, reducing efficiency. However, it also results in lower 
emissions of CO2, CO, and HC due to the reduced carbon content in 
ammonia, thus producing fewer carbon-based emissions.
Researchers are dedicated to enhancing the efficiency and perfor­
mance stability of mixed fuel combustion by optimizing blending ratios, 
refining combustion chamber designs, and advancing combustion con­
trol strategies. These efforts aim to maximize fuel efficiency while 
minimizing emissions and operational challenges. Reiter and Kong 
[200] performed a comprehensive analysis of the performance charac­
teristics of dual-fuel engines operating on diesel and NH3. Their study 
focused on evaluating combustion efficiency, emission characteristics, 
and overall engine performance when using a blend of these two fuels.

6.3. Ammonia in marine engines
As a carbon-free renewable fuel, NH3 attracts considerable interest in 
the shipping industry because of its significant potential to contribute to 
global decarbonization efforts. Dual-fuel technology is expected to play 
a pivotal role in advancing combustion applications for marine engines, 
offering a more efficient and environmentally sustainable propulsion 
solution. Nevertheless, there has been a lack of previous research 
regarding the utilization of NH3 in the maritime sector. In 2021, MAN 
Energy Solutions [202] evaluated the feasibility of utilizing NH3 as a 
marine fuel and announced its commitment to developing the first 
NH3-powered marine engine, with plans for its release by 2025.
Wang et al. [203] conducted a feasibility study on using a hydrogen 
jet flame to ignite NH3 fuel in a marine engine. The proposed system 
used a dual-fuel natural gas engine modified to run on NH3, with H2 
injected into pre-combustion chambers to create a jet flame that ignites 
the NH3. The results showed that the hydrogen jet flame effectively 
promoted early combustion of NH3, achieving full-load power output 
with only 1.68 % H2 by energy ratio. The study found that the optimal 
ignition timing could reduce NOx emissions. The system demonstrated 
good potential for emissions reduction and improved engine perfor­
mance, with the hydrogen jet flame providing the necessary thermal and 
reactive atmosphere for efficient NH3 combustion.
In another study, Xu et al. [204] examined the performance and 
emission characteristics of a dual-fuel marine engine utilizing NH3 and 
diesel. The results indicated that substituting natural gas with NH3 
resulted in reduced combustion efficiency and elevated emissions of 
NOx and N2O. The study found that NO emissions were mainly attrib­
uted to fuel NO generated from the oxidation of NH3 and thermal NO 
produced in the diesel flame region. The optimized operation and in­
jection strategy achieved a 45 % reduction in CO emissions, a 60 % 
decrease in total GHG emissions, and an 89 % reduction in CO2 emis­
sions when compared to the liquefied natural gas/diesel engine. The 
study concluded that NH3 has potential as a marine fuel, but further 
optimization is needed, such as adjusting the injection timing, strategy, 
and air-fuel ratio. Additionally, optimally modify the combustion 
chamber design to enhance the mixing and combustion characteristics. 
These modifications aim to improve efficiency and reduce emissions by 
utilizing the advantages of NH3 as a marine fuel.
Ichikawa et al. [205] conducted an experimental study on the com­
bustion process of NH3 stratified spray in large two-stroke marine en­
gines. The study addressed the challenges of NH3 combustion, such as 
difficult ignition and slow combustion, by proposing a three-layer 
stratified fuel injection system. The study concluded that the NH3 
stratified injection system could be effectively applied to large 
two-stroke marine engines, with the initial burnt gas lump and the third 
layer’s rapid combustion playing crucial roles in the combustion pro­
cess. Ammonia blends have been applied in marine engines, showing 
operational feasibility. The primary challenge is controlling NOx emis­
sions and addressing low flame speeds. Future developments should 
focus on engine design and emissions after-treatment.
6.4. Ammonia in aircraft engines
Ammonia is increasingly being acknowledged as a feasible alterna­
tive fuel for air transportation, driven by the growing imperative to 
mitigate GHG emissions within the aviation industry. Its potential as a 
sustainable aviation fuel aligns with global decarbonization efforts. 
Combustion of ammonia presents notable benefits, such as the potential 
for zero carbon dioxide emissions. However, its high ignition energy and 
low flammability pose challenges that require modifications to existing 
aircraft engines. These modifications often involve blending NH3 with 
more reactive fuels like H2 to enhance performance. Lu et al. [206] 
improved a thermodynamic model to assess the performance and 
emission characteristics of NH3-fueled scramjet engines. Their study 
tackled challenges such as low combustion rates and elevated 
self-ignition temperatures by comparing NH3 with conventional fuels 
like H2, jet-A, and methanol. The study found that NH3 scramjets ach­
ieved 54.5 % higher specific thrust at Mach 9 compared to hydrocar­
bons, although their specific impulse was only 64.3 % of that of 
hydrocarbons. The research also indicated that blending ammonia with 
Jet-A or hydrogen and adding boron could enhance engine performance, 
with boron significantly improving volume-specific impulse. Boron 
addition increases energy density and promotes complete combustion, 
which improves thermal efficiency. This leads to a higher 
thrust-to-weight ratio and specific impulse.
In another investigation, Otto et al. [207] conducted a critical 
assessment of NH3 as an aircraft fuel, emphasizing its potential as a 
hydrogen carrier for the aviation sector. The study highlighted several 
advantages of NH3 over H2, including its higher volumetric energy 
density, well-established supply infrastructure, and non-coking proper­
ties, making it a more practical and efficient alternative fuel for various 
applications. These attributes make NH3 particularly suitable for 
compressor intercooling and various thermal management applications. 
Despite its higher toxicity, ammonia’s lifecycle emissions are signifi­
cantly lower than conventional jet-A fuel, making it a viable option for 
reducing aviation’s carbon footprint. The study proposed using 
ammonia for selective catalytic reduction to mitigate NOx emissions and 
demonstrated that integrating NH3 into current aircraft systems could be 
achieved with minimal modifications. While ammonia offers consider­
able potential as a sustainable aviation fuel due to its favorable storage 
properties and low lifecycle emissions, overcoming challenges related to 
flammability, combustion efficiency, and NOx control remains essential 
for its practical integration into future aircraft propulsion systems.
6.5. Ammonia in fuel cells
Ammonia offers significant potential for integration into fuel cell 
technologies. Fuel cells, which effectively transform chemical energy 
into electrical energy via electrochemical reactions, can employ NH3 
either as a direct fuel or as a source of hydrogen. Key fuel cell types that 
can leverage ammonia include solid oxide fuel cells (SOFCs), proton 
exchange membrane fuel cells (PEMFCs), and alkaline fuel cells (AFCs). 
This section explores ammonia’s role in these systems and discusses the 
challenges associated with its adoption. Ammonia fuel cells are classi­
fied as either direct or indirect, based on the location of ammonia 
decomposition. In indirect fuel cells, ammonia is first thermally 
decomposed into hydrogen outside the cell, and this hydrogen is sub­
sequently used as fuel. In contrast, direct ammonia fuel cells utilize

improve overall efficiency [209].
SOFCs operate at elevated temperatures ranging from 600 ◦C to

operating temperatures allow SOFCs to tolerate impurities and effi­

Meng et al. [211] demonstrated that a direct ammonia-fed SOFC 
utilizing an oxygen-ion-conducting Ce0.8Sm0.2O1.9 electrolyte exhibits 
high conductivity at operating temperatures ranging from 550 to 650 ◦C. 
In such direct NH3 fuel cells, the power density is only marginally lower 
compared to hydrogen fuel cells [211]. SOFCs are categorized into two 
types based on their electrolytes: oxygen anion-conducting SOFCs 
(O–SOFCs) and proton-conducting SOFCs (H–SOFCs) as illustrated in 
Fig. 53. In both types, ammonia undergoes a two-step oxidation process, 
where it is first cracked into hydrogen and nitrogen, and the hydrogen is 
then electrochemically oxidized. In O–SOFCs, oxygen introduced at the 
cathode is reduced to oxygen anions, which travel across the electrolyte 
to react with hydrogen at the anode, resulting in the formation of water. 
In H–SOFCs, protons serve as the charge carriers, with hydrogen 
oxidized to protons at the anode, which then react with oxygen at the 
cathode to form water [212]. While nitrogen remains inert, NOx for­
mation at the anode is a potential challenge, though it can be mitigated 
with appropriate catalysts. Water vapor, unreacted gases, and nitrogen 
exit the cell through their respective outlets depending on the configu­
ration [213].
AFCs have been widely utilized in different applications like space 
exploration, transportation, and energy storage systems. The first 
ammonia-based AFC was developed in 1960 by Cairens et al. [214], used 
a potassium hydroxide (KOH) electrolyte operating between 50 ◦C and 
200 ◦C. Progress in molten hydroxide electrolytes has since enabled 
higher performance. Hejze et al. [215] achieved a power density of 40 
mW/cm2 at 450 ◦C using a NaOH/KOH electrolyte, while Yang et al. 
[216] attained a peak power density of 16 mW/cm2 at 220 ◦C utilizing 
an NH3-fueled molten hydroxide NaOH/KOH fuel cell, demonstrating its 
potential for efficient energy conversion in high-temperature fuel cell 
applications. Although ammonia fuel cells employing alkaline electro­
lytes present promising advantages, they are accompanied by several 
challenges. Researchers have evaluated ammonia as a potential fuel for 
alkaline membrane fuel cells, employing a nickel anode and MnO2 
cathode setup [217]. These systems operate similarly to traditional 
alkaline fuel cells, with hydroxide ions transferring through the elec­
trolyte. While ammonia fuel cells utilizing alkaline membrane electro­
lytes present promising advantages, they are accompanied by several 
challenges. A major challenge is selecting efficient anode and cathode 
catalysts. Ammonia crossover through the membrane lowers voltage 
and efficiency, while its oxidation at the cathode produces toxic NOx 
emissions [218]. Although progress is being made in commercializing 
ammonia fuel cells, several challenges, such as improving power density 
and stability at low temperatures, must be addressed before they can be 
widely adopted. These fuel cells offer a clean and efficient energy source, 
supporting the shift to a sustainable, low-carbon future.
7. Conclusions and future directions
The growing global demand for decarbonization requires the 
development of low-carbon or carbon-free sustainable energy systems 
across all industrial sectors. Hydrogen (H2), as a carbon-free fuel, has 
attracted considerable attention. However, its practical use is hindered 
by storage and transportation challenges. As an alternative, ammonia 
(NH3), which contains 17.6 % hydrogen by weight and is also carbon- 
free, has gained momentum as a hydrogen carrier and a potential 
combustion fuel. Ammonia offers several favorable attributes that sup­
port its potential as an alternative fuel. These include well-established 
production infrastructure, compatibility with existing storage and 
transport systems, and global availability. Collectively, these charac­
teristics have driven intensified research efforts toward integrating 
ammonia into different energy systems, such as gas turbines and internal 
combustion engines. Nevertheless, ammonia’s low reactivity and high 
nitrogen content lead to flame instability and elevated NOx emissions, 
presenting key challenges to its large-scale use. This review critically 
assessed the combustion characteristics of ammonia and its blends and 
explored enhancement strategies and practical challenges in ammonia- 
based combustion systems. The main conclusions can be outlined as 
follows. 
1. Ammonia combustion offers a promising alternative to traditional 
fossil fuels, producing energy without CO2 emissions. This makes it a 
crucial component in the global effort to mitigate climate change and 
reduce greenhouse gas emissions.
2. Ammonia has narrower flammability limits and a substantially lower 
LBV than methane, making flame stabilization in both laminar and 
turbulent regimes more difficult without additives. Its thick flame 
front modifies flame turbulence interactions, and the high N2 content 
leads to elevated NOx emissions, particularly under lean conditions, 
with peak NO levels near ϕ ≈0.9. As a result, lean premixed stra­
tegies effective for methane are less suitable for NH3 combustion due 
to its distinct combustion kinetics and emission behavior.

3. In swirl-stabilized combustion, NO emissions peak around ϕ ≈0.9, 
reaching ~1000 ppm under lean conditions. As the equivalence ratio 
increases beyond this point, NO levels decrease sharply, stabilizing 
near 10 ppm for ϕ > 1.1. The optimal trade-off between low NO and 
unburned NH3 emissions occurs at ϕ ≈1.05. NOx formation is pri­
marily governed by the fuel NOx pathway, with HNO acting as a key 
intermediate under lean conditions. In rich mixtures, reaction 
pathways shift toward NH3 dissociation and promote N2 formation 
via the NNH mechanism, effectively suppressing NO production.
4. Several strategies have been proposed to improve ammonia’s com­
bustion performance. Blending with H2 or CH4 significantly en­
hances flame speed and stability. However, these additives also 
elevate NOx emissions, with experimental studies showing a peak 
NOx concentration at XCH4 = 0.5 in gas turbine model combustors. In 
ammonia flames, NO is primarily generated via the fuel NOx 
mechanism, driven by the oxidation of NHᵢ intermediates. The 
presence of hydrogen or methane increases the concentration of 
reactive radicals (H, O, OH), which intensifies the HNO pathway and 
leads to higher NO formation.
5. A key method to improve flame stability in ammonia combustion is 
oxygen enrichment, which significantly raises LBV. Additionally, 
partial cracking of ammonia to generate hydrogen has proven 
effective in enhancing reactivity. Emerging approaches such as 
plasma-assisted combustion and the use of reactive oxidizers like 
H2O2 offer further potential for improving combustion performance 
under difficult operating conditions.
6. For future prospects, it is important to advance our understanding of 
the flame stabilization mechanisms and NOx formation processes 
associated with ammonia and its blends. Firstly, fundamental studies 
are needed to clarify how ammonia’s specific combustion charac­
teristics influence flame dynamics under various operating condi­
tions. Secondly, high-speed, high-resolution diagnostic tools such as 
NO and NH measurement techniques should be developed to capture 
critical species and reaction zones. In addition, simplified and ac­
curate chemical kinetic models are required to support reliable CFD 
simulations under realistic combustion conditions. Finally, research 
should focus on advanced combustion strategies, including high- 
pressure operation, staged combustion, NH3/H2 blends, and 
plasma-assisted combustion, to enhance flame stability and reduce 
emissions.
