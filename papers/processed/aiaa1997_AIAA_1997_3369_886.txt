Gas Turbine Combustion Design, Technology and Research
Current Status and Future Direction
By
Hukam C. Mongia*
Combustion Center of Excellence, GE Aircraft Engines, Cincinnati, Ohio
Significant advances have been achieved in the turbopropulsion gas turbine combustion products in regard to operating
conditions; combustion system length and volume; performance, operability, gaseous and smoke emissions. Our basic
understanding of the critical combustion processes has improved considerably due to the advances made in the
measurement technology, numerical simulation and resulting increase in model accuracy. This has in turn benefited the
combustion design process which now uses routinely both the approximate and CCD-based methods unlike the old "cut-
and-try" design practice. The recently increased collaboration between industry and the combustion research community
needs to be further strengthened in order to quickly transition the fundamental know-how to better design tools, improve
predictability, and reduce cycle time between "idea-and-product" introduction.
Introduction
Gas turbine combustion design has come a long way in regard to
heat release rates, operating pressures (exceeding 40 arm) and
temperatures (3000 F in commercial technology engines and
near-stoichiometric in high-performance military engine
demonstrators), durability, and combustor length. Single annular
combustors ( SAC) are being pushed to the lowest possible levels
of NOx consistent with the other propulsion combustion system
requirements.
The dual annular combustors (DAC) have been certified for low
emissions turbopropulsion engines that produce an even lower
levels of NOx emissions during the landing-takeoff cycle (LTO)
as well as for typical flight missions.
The triple annular combustion system for dry ultralow NOx (with
potential exhaust NOx levels down to single digits) has been
successfully introduced as an alternate to the wet low-emissions
control technology for industrial application of aeroderivative
gas turbines with nominal pressure ratios of 30 or higher.
Empirical/analytical combustor design approach has become
industry's standard for advance technology, new product
introduction and current product support activities.
Noninrrusive measurement techniques have made significant
progress that has helped create an extensive benchmark quality
database for assessing the quality of current turbulent
combustion models and providing direction for further
improvements.

Description of Modern Combustors
Figure 1 gives a cross-section of the CF6 gas turbine engine
combustion system that is used on a number of wide-body
commercial aviation airplanes. This engine is produced in
several models with the sea-level standard-day static take-off
(SLSTO) thrust rating between approximately 175 and 300
kN. The corresponding range of the engine SLSTO pressure

requirements including performance, reliable operation over
the entire flight envelope and structural durability in addition
to minimizing the undesirable pollutants (NOx, CO,

Emissions of Turbopropulsion Combustors
All large commercial propulsion engines are required to
measure the exhaust emissions over the landing takeoff
(LTO) cycle, as specified in Annex 6, Vol. II of the ICAO
Chicago Convention. The measured emissions at idle,
approach, climb-out and take-off conditions are generally
presented as characteristic values (g/kN) for the LTO cycle.
The LTO NOx values for several old and newly-certified
propulsion engines are shown as a function of the sea-level
static takeoff (SLTO) overall engine pressure ratio (OPR) in
Fig. 5. The old NOx standard of 40+2OPR (g/kN calculated
over the LTO cycle) has recently been replaced by
32+1.6OPR (effective December 31, 1995). Here OPR
denotes the rated sea-level static, standard-day take-off
pressure ratio. The slopes of 2.0 (for the old standard) and 1.6
(for the new standard) are allowed in recognition of the higher
combustor inlet temperatures and the attendant flame
temperatures for the high-pressure ratio energy-efficient
turbofan engines. As discussed later, the measured slope
values can be significantly different from 1.6. It is a common
practice to compare the LTO NOx at a nominal pressure ratio
of 30. Therefore, the maximum LTO NOx limits established
by the old and new standards at 30 pressure ratio are 100 and
80 g/kN-LTO cycle, respectively. The characteristic NOx for
the CF6 (Fig. 1), CFM SAC (Fig. 2) and DAC (Fig. 3)
interpolated for 30 pressure ratio are 50.7, 67.0 and 42.5 g/kN
-LTO cycle, respectively.
It is a common practice to rank the competing emissions
technology options by comparing the NOx levels, and the
trends of the NOx/CO and NOx/HC curves. For example, Fig.
6 compares the characteristic values of NOx and CO for all
the engines presented in Fig. 5. Other than the old technology
engines, all state-of-the-art commercial propulsion engines
have CO and HC emissions less than the ICAO standards,
namely 118.0 and 19.6 g/kN-LTO cycle, respectively.
Fig. 7 presents the LTO NOx emissions of the CF34 single-
annular combustor (SAC), CFM SAC, the CFM dual-annular
combustor (DAC) and the GE90 DAC as compared to the
1996 ICAO standard, and the goals of a recently initiated
Advanced Subsonic Transport (AST) combustion technology
programs for application in the regional and wide-body
airplanes. The slopes of the curves for the CFM SAC, CFM
DAC and the GE90 DAC are 3.3 (R2 = 1.0), 2.3 (R2 = 0.95)
and 2.9 (R2 = 0.97), respectively, as compared to 1.6 allowed
in the 1996 ICAO standard. Since the fuel-air mixing
technologies of both the CFM and GE90 DACs are
comparable, the LTO NOx of both of these combustor can be
best-fitted with a single linear curve with a slope of 3.1 and
R2 = 0.99.
The low-emissions technology evolution can be best
illustrated by comparing NOx (for example, as in Fig. 7), the
NOx/HC and NOx/CO characteristics, as shown in Figures 8
and 9. The CF6 family of engines (with the rated take-off
pressure ratio and thrust ranges of approximately 25-33 and
175-300 kN) have three different levels of emissions
technologies (identified as the CF6-6/-50, CF6-80C and CF6-
80LEC) developed during the last 20 years. The most recently
certified third-generation low-emission technology (the CF6-
80LEC) has significantly lower HC and CO emissions (2.7
and 29.3 g/kN, respectively) in addition to the low
characteristic NOx level of 50.7 g/kN for a nominal pressure
ratio of 30. Through continuous improvements in the CF6
single annular combustor design, approximately 20%
reduction in NOx has been achieved in addition to more than
90% and 50% reduction in unburned hydrocarbons and CO
emissions, respectively.
In order to reduce emissions beyond what has been achieved
to-date by the single annular combustors, a number of
advanced concepts have been developed starting with the
NASA sponsored Experimental Clean Combustor, ECC
program [3,4]. The CFM56-5B engine model certified
recently with a low-NOx dual-annular combustor emits 35%
lower NOx over the LTO cycle compared to the single annular
combustor in a similar engine model. In addition, the DAC's
altitude cruise NOx emission index is approximately 50%
lower than that of SAC. The low-emissions technology
developed under the ECC and subsequent Energy Efficient
Engine, E3, programs was applicable to large turbopropulsion
engines of nominal pressure ratio of 30. Its application to the
GE90 of nominal pressure ratio of 40 has been quite
successful in that the NOx versus pressure ratio, HC/NO;, and
CO/NOx characteristics are identical for both the CFM56 and
the GE90, as shown in Figures 7, 10 and 11, respectively.

annular DLE [2], and axially-staged [4 and 5] ) pose a number
of design challenges that are being addressed as part of new
product introduction activities. The DAC engines' entry into
revenue service has been considered successful by its launch

Emissions of Aero-derivative Industrial Engines
The water/steam injection (wet) control technology is
generally used for meeting the local stringent emissions
standards for industrial gas turbine applications. In order to
meet 25 PPM NOx emissions (referenced to 15% O2 content
in the exhaust) requirements for a high-pressure aero-
derivative gas turbine engine ( nominal pressure ratio of 30
and typical 225 PPM NOx with NG and no-control), one is
required to achieve approximately 90% reduction hi NOx
within acceptable levels of CO emissions. Many installations
allow 42 PPM NOx for Diesel fuel (DF-2) fired gas turbine
installation as compared to the uncontrolled NOx (for a
nominal 30 pressure ratio engine) of 425 PPM, as shown
typically in Fig. 13.
The dry-low emissions (DLE) control technology is preferred
over the wet control technology because of several reasons
including life-cycle cost and water conservation. The DLE
combustion system described by Davis and Washam [6] is
being used in several heavy-duty combustion turbines. Many
DLE combustion systems [7-11] have since been developed
and installed in customer sites including the highest pressure
ratio LM6000 DLE combustion system described by Leonard
and Stegmaier [9] and Joshi et al [2]. This combustion system
(as shown in Fig. 4) uses a multi-stage combustion concept
with enhanced lean-blowout, blow-out avoidance control logic
and acoustic energy absorption/modulation devices to achieve
the ultra-low emissions, performance, and durability
objectives.
A typical comparison between the uncontrolled NOx and DLE
with NG is shown in Fig. 13. As compared to 225 PPM NOx
at the 100% rated power with an uncontrolled diffusion flame
combustor, the DLE produces less than 25 PPM and the
attendant 90+% NOx reduction. The corresponding NOx-CO
emissions characteristics are compared hi Fig. 14. The premix
DLE combustion system with a five-stage combustion staging
logic involving the three partially-separated combustion
domes meet all the design requirements including significantly
reduced NOx and CO emissions for the power demands
above 50% of the rated output.
Approximate Design Calculation Methods
In order to meet the challenging design requirements of new
environmentally acceptable energy efficient gas turbines, one
needs to develop the following two levels of design tools.
(1) A simplified computationally cheap analysis methods that
utilizes the state-of-the-art empirical, semi-empirical and
semi-analytical understanding of combustion processes that
impact emissions, performance, operability and life
characteristics. The validated correlations are used for the
preliminary design, trade studies, detail design, data reduction
and analysis (DRA) of existing and future design concepts.
(2) Anchored computational combustion dynamics (CCD)
modeling approach based on refined empirical turbulence
model constants and boundary conditions targeted for the
design system applications in current and growth engine
combustors. This topic will be covered later in this paper.
A number of semiempirical expressions have been proposed
over the years for correlating NOx, CO and unburaed
hydrocarbon emissions of gas turbines. A "single reactor"
modeling approach has been used by Lefebvre [12] to
correlate emissions data from the several military engine
combustors, but required design specific ("arbitrary")
constants. In an alternate approach, Mellor and his associates
[13-14] have used the characteristic-times concept to correlate
emissions. Rizk and Mongia [15] extended Lefebvre's
approach by including the effects of spray evaporation and
mixedness on emissions. Rizk and Mongia [16] proposed
semianalytical expressions for NOx, CO and HC based on a
network of seven reactors and a detailed propane-air
combustion mechanism. With this approach they showed that
different expressions are needed for the NOx formation rates
hi the primary zone when cp is below or above 1.08. Moreover,
a different expression was proposed for the intermediate
zone.
Lefebvre's correlations [12] have been developed without
making explicit use of the airflow distribution around the
combustors. Rizk and Mongia [16] have utilized the calculated
airflow distribution in their formulation; but their approach
has been verified for detailed rig emissions database on only
one experimental combustor hardware.
In order to develop the first-generation DRA tools for
emissions, Danis, Pritchard and Mongia [17] used the
calculated airflow distribution and extended Lefebvre's
approach [12] for several modern certified commercial

Another example of DRA is the semi-analytical formulation
and validation of an airblast nozzle model by Rizk and
Mongia [18]. The authors used semi-analytical expressions

shows typical comparison between the measured and

a significant improvement over the state-of-the-art empirical
modeling techniques reviewed by Lefebvre [19].
Qualitative Help from CCD in Design Process
The conventional design approach makes extensive use of
database, empirical and semi-empirical design correlations,
simplified calculation methods (mostly quasi-ID), correlative
multi-dimensional heat transfer and stress analysis, coupled
with an extensive use of the single-cup, sector and annular test
rigs, and development engines to design and develop the
combustors. This is followed by well-defined engine
certification process. The limitations of this mostly empirical
design process has been well-known. The first published
report wherein extensive use of multi-dimensional
computational dynamics (CCD) was made to augment the
conventional empirical approach is by Mongia et al [20]. This
empirical/analytical design methodology has been used
extensively during the last 20 years [21-22] in the design and
demonstration of the 15 advanced technology combustors,
four demonstrators, three certified product engines, and one
small turbine augmentor.
In addition, CCD has helped in improving our basic
understanding and providing guidance for addressing design,
development and product-sustaining issues in several engines
including the TFE731, TPE331, GT601, 501-K34, 570, T56-
A427, T406/GMA2100, GMA 3007, CFM56-5B DAC1 and
DAC2, CF6-80C2, CF6-80LEC and GE90DAC1 and DAC2.
Even though the empirical/analytical design methodology is
now used widely in the gas turbine industry [20-27],
significant advances need to made in the modeling of the
critical combustion processes as explained in the following
paragraphs.
Limitations of CCD
A number of articles have compared model predictions with
data for the flows relevant to gas turbine combustors [21,28-
48]. From these investigations, one can conclude that the
current models are not accurate enough to give the level of
accuracy the combustion designers would like to have as
given in Table I. Even though a number of model
improvements have been made during the last 15 years, the
models used in gas turbine combustion design system
generally use the standard k-e model, a two-step kinetic
scheme with a variant of the modified eddy break-up model
[20] and/or fast chemistry with specified PDF, stochastic
spray model etc. There are several critical processes that need
to be modeled for gas turbine combustion. We have selected
the following three examples to quantify a typical level of
disagreement for the models currently used in the design
system:
1) Flow field inside and downstream of a "production"
airblast nozzle.
2) Modeling of the CFM56 swirl cup shown in Fig. 2
3) Dilution jet mixing
The ability to predict fuel injector flowfield and atomization
quality is very critical since the fuel nozzle directly influences
a number of aerothermal performance parameters, emissions
levels, wall temperature levels and gradients. Nikjooy et al
[45] conducted a systematic experimental and analytical study
for nozzle-swirler interaction. Their work showed that the
Reynolds stress model of the 80's is only slightly better than
the k-s model when the calculations start with the profiles
measured downstream of the airblast injector. However for
design application, where one has to start calculation from
within the airblast passages, the agreement between
measurements and predictions is very poor [49].
Consequently, the comprehensive fuel injector modeling
activities conducted over the last ten years have not provided
any reliable design tool for optimizing the airblast
nozzle/dome interaction. Fig. 19 is a typical illustration for the
lack of agreement between the measured and predicted radial
profiles of SMD from a comprehensive airblast injector model

The dilution jet mixing process is considered critical for
achieving the required burner exit temperature quality
including radial profile and pattern factor. Holdeman [50] has
given an extensive review of the available experimental
database, and simplified correlations in dilution jet nixing, in
addition to somewhat limited review on the multi-dimensional
computations. Although the k-s model-based 3-D calculations
agree qualitatively with data, one needs to make significant
improvement in numerics, turbulence modeling and resolve
boundary condition related issues in order to achieve
acceptable results.
Perhaps the best illustration for the lack of a good jet mixing
model can be made by referring to the experimental and
analytical investigation reported by McGuirk and Spencer
[48]. They used two concentric tubes of 154 and 94 mm
diameters, respectively, with a single row of six dilution
orifices located 90 mm downstream from a reference station.
They made LV measurements for a constant jet momentum
ratio of 25. 3D elliptic flow calculations were made for both
the annulus and tube flowfields by using cylindrical polar and
curvilinear coordinate systems. With a coarse grid network of
48 x 40 x 16 (axial x radial x circumferential direction,
respectively), approximately 75 nodes simulated the half
dilution orifice. With a finer mesh of 74 x 110 x 20 nodes, the
half dilution orifice was simulated by 200 nodes. The half
circular orifice was simulated by stair-step (castellated)
approximation and exactly by the cylindrical polar and
curvilinear coordinate systems, respectively. The predicted
effect of the four simulations (fine and coarse grids,
castellated and exact simulation of the dilution orifice) on the
jet mixing characteristics were significantly different, as
shown in Fig. 21 for the axial velocity profiles along the tube
centerline in-line with the dilution orifice. The best and worst
agreements between the data and predictions were obtained
with the most approximate (coarse castellated) and exact (fine
fitted) simulations, respectively.
Mongia [51] has presented several other examples of both the
reacting and non-reacting flows relevant to gas turbine
combustors that clearly show that the turbulent combustion
models and the numerical simulations used in the state-of-the-
art combustion system design codes are good enough only for
providing qualitative guidance during design. In order to get
more reliable quantitative guidance as summarized in Table I,
industry needs to adopt and refine the most recent turbulent
combustion modeling and simulation techniques in the design
process, as discussed later in this paper.
Quantitative Help from CCD in Design Process
Due to the limitations of the state-of-the-art turbulent
combustion models as pointed out by many including
references [20-22, 39, 45, 49-51], a number of modeling
techniques have been proposed [1, 52-61] to achieve the
quantitatively accurate predictions of performance and
emissions parameters to the level of accuracy required by the
combustion designers. All of these approaches have been
useful for correlating and analyzing quantitatively existing
combustor designs. With the cumulative experience with these
approaches, it is now possible to use the state-of-the-art
turbulence combustion model through the anchoring process
as described in Reference 1, to design a new combustor with
more confidence than the empirical/analytical design
approaches similar to that of Reference 20.
As described by Danis, Burrus and Mongia [1], the anchored
CCD was first calibrated with one of the five modern
turbopropulsion combustors. Fig. 22 shows a typical
comparison between predicted and measured average radial
profiles of temperature at the burner exit plane. The anchored
models were then run for the other four combustors. The
predicted results correlated well with measured NOx, CO, HC,
LBO and exit temperature quality data, demonstrating a
broader applicability of the anchored method. The models
were also used for designing a new combustion concept. The
pre-test predictions agreed well with the sector rig data from a
development hardware, as shown in Fig. 23 for NOx (from

Advanced Diagnostics for Design
The first extensive data collection from practical combustors
for model calibration used intrusive techniques [20-21] for
detailed measurements inside a number of rig combustor
configurations as well as several "production" combustors.
Only a few nonintrusive measurements even at the combustor
exit plane [62-63] are available for conducting model
calibration. However, a number of advance diagnostic
techniques have been used recently for studying several
combustion subcomponents and the critical processes. For
example, the detailed measurements on model combustors
[64-69] have been used extensively for model calibration.
Similarly, one can learn a lot from the measurements for the
flow over a backward facing step [70], swirling flow in a can
research combustor [71], the vortex break-down of a strongly
swirling confined flow including the effect of density ratio
[72-73], the impact of shroud on the nozzle flow field [74],
and finally the effect of the initial profiles on the swirling jet
expansion including vortex breakdown [75].
As summarized by Bachalo [76], the advanced diagnostic
techniques have been used extensively to study injection,
dispersion and combustion of liquid spray. Sun et al [77
investigated the atomization and vaporization of plain jet
atomizer in a venturi tube, a candidate approach for
application in ultra-low NOx combustors. Dodge and Scwalb
[78] conducted detailed experimental investigation of a
research swirl atomizer and showed that starting with
measured information at an axial station sufficiently
downstream from the nozzle exit, one can get fairly good
agreement between predictions and measurements. A similar
observation was made by Bulzan et al [79] with another air-
assist airblast nozzle design. The effect of ambient pressure on
the atomization and spray distribution has been investigated
in References 80 and 81 for the research swirl atomizer and a
practical fuel injector, respectively. The investigation on
another practical liquid fuel injector with propane as reported
by Hedman et al [82], and Schmidt and Hedman [83] has
clearly shown the complexity of the process of flame
stabilization, mixing and heat release. The fundamental
studies reported by Nakabe, Mizutani and Hirao [84] on the
burning characteristics of premixed sprays and gas-liquid
coburning mixtures; Trichet, Lavergne and Biscos [85] on
amplitude and temporal response of droplets in turbulent flow
field; Chakravarty, Yang and Richards [86] on the droplet-
vortex interactions in a turbulent round jet; and Li, Libby and
Williams [87] on spray structure in two-phase counterflowing
streams with and without a flame have provided valuable
information to help improve the fuel-air mixing in practical
devices. Finally, the flame stabilization and lean blowout
processes, as measured by Sturgess et al [88], Sturgess and
Shouse [89], and Switzer et al [90], clearly show the
inadequacy of current mechanistic models.
As indicated in the above paragraph and by Bachalo [76],
even though the phase Doppler interferometer (PDI) has been
used extensively for investigating complex two-phase flows,
detailed measurements on production hardware is less
prevalent. A series of studies have been conducted on
hardware developed by GE Aircraft Engines at UCI and
AFWL as summarized in References [91-97]. The time
averaged picture of the flow structure from the measurements
or CFD calculations [47] is somewhat misleading in that the
flow is actually highly dynamic in the tune resolved picture as
illustrated in Wang et al [93, 94]. The hardware scaling
studies [95] revealed that, while the gas phase mean and
fluctuating velocities scaled well, the droplet behavior did not
since the atomization mechanism present in the simplex
atomizers utilized did not produce drop sizes which scaled
with basic nozzle descriptors (e.g., flow number). Examples
of other studies include limited parametric studies of
geometry, e.g., spray angle, flare expansion angle [92]. In
these examples, it was found that spray angle has little effect
on the droplet distribution or gas phase structure for the case
studied whereas the flare exit had a significant effect on both
gas and droplet behavior. An increase in large droplets in the
center of the spray with the wide flare seemed to correlate
with increased CO levels along the center of the combustor in
that case.

In addition, consideration of reacting flows produced with the
swirl cup have been reported [97], as well as measurements
for cases at proper inlet temperatures and pressures [96]. In

reacting and non-reacting flows. A critical difference was that

The above studies, while a reasonable start in providing data
for CCD challenges have some significant shortcomings.
First, the complex interaction between the two counter-
swirling air streams and the liquid filming process is not
properly characterized due to the lack of optical access. As a
result, key physics associated with the mixing process are not
documented. Second, the information obtained is time
averaged. Little is understood about the complex dynamic
coupling between the phenomena occurring. Third, the
parametric variations have been too limited to provide useful
correlations or necessary insight into the effect of geometry or
operating conditions. Finally, the studies have largely
documented the flow structure without regard for performance
issues. Whether or not the spray angle affects the
aerodynamics may not be relevant in the absence of
performance data (e.g., emissions, temperature patterns, etc.).
Recognizing the complexity associated with production
fuel/air mixing devices, simpler test configurations that
capture the essential processes in controlled manner need to be
investigated. A good example, from industry's perspective is
the study reported in References 98 and 99. An extensive
velocity and temperature data base, including triple velocity
correlations, has been compiled for double-concentric
turbulent ah-jets and hydrogen jet diffusion flames, with or
without swirl. Realizing this and for other practical reasons,
GE Aircraft Engines GEAE) in partnership with four
universities (the University of California campuses at
Berkeley and Irvine, the University of Illinois at Urbana, and
the Penn State) has started a joint experimental investigation
for gas turbine combustor relevant simple flows, and the two
generic classes of fuel/air mixing devices (namely axial and
radial swirlers) sponsored under the AFOSR Focused
Research Initiative (FRI). The main objectives of this four-
year effort is:
A. Measure the spatial and temporal variations of flowfield
variables including the three velocity components,
mixture fraction, major species (CO2 and unburned fuel,
as minimum), intermediate combustion products (CO, as
minimum), temperature, and NOX.
B. Formulate and verify semi-analytical mechanistic models
(SAM) for:
a) Recirculation zone characteristics (mass being
recirculated, location, size, equivalence ratio and
combustion efficiency, etc.).
b) Lean blowout and ignition.
c) Combustion efficiency, CO, UHC, NOX and soot.
d) Radial profiles of major variables (axial velocity, swirl
velocity, temperature, combustion efficiency, UHC, CO,
NOX.
e) Combustion dynamics input required for approximate
methods and multi-dimensional non-linear unsteady flow
calculations.
A parallel effort for the advanced model development is
described in the following section.
Laurendeau and his associates [100-103] have applied LIF
over the past five years to better understand NO formation in
high-pressure gaseous flames. They have shown that point
measurements of NO can be made up to at least 15 atm by
employing narrow-band detection of NO fluorescence; and the
feasibility in premixed flames down to 1 PPM of NO. This
approach is being explored for application to lean premixed,
partially premixed and diffusion flames using gaseous fuels up
to 30 atm. GEAE is working with Laurendeau to extend the
LIF approach for studying a lean-direct liquid fuel injection
device.
The design challenges for maintaining exhaust smoke
emissions well-below the threshold of visibility are becoming
more demanding for the next-generation high-pressure low-
emissions combustors. From the list of papers reviewed by
Glassman [104] and Santoro, the combustion designer can
appreciate the complexity of soot formation/oxidation
processes, especially in regard to soot particle precursor
chemistry, soot particle inception, surface growth and
oxidation. Whereas these fundamental studies have improved
the designers' insight, high-pressure investigations need to be
taken starting from simpler flows and moving on ultimately to
tackling the production devices in a manner somewhat similar
to what UCI and AFWL have accomplished for studying the
fuel-air mixing in GEAE's swirl cup[91-97].
Advanced CCD for Design
The failure of the second-order closure models of the 80's for
predicting complex non-reacting flows [21, 39,45,47,49, 51]
has stalled the development of more accurate gas turbine
combustor models for performance, emissions, liner wall
temperatures and dynamics. Consequently, the combustion
designer has to rely on hybrid modeling [52-61] and/or
anchored approach [1] for making quantitative comparison
with data. Recent turbulence modeling activities [106-116]
appear to show promise enough for GEAE to investigate its
adaptation in the design system.
The two most recent and extensive review by Faeth [117] and
Sirignano [118] summarize recent progress made in the
modeling of practical sprays, in addition to the topical review
by Bachalo [76]. Of particular interest to us are the recent

studies reported in References [79, 119-121]. These
improvements in regard to droplet dynamics, heat-up and
evaporation, and the enhancements due to the studies
conducted by Raju [122] on the Monte Carlo PDF simulation
of reacting sprays are in the process of inclusion into GEAE's
combustion design system.
The challenging job of finding a reduced kinetic scheme that
does not have too many species and yet does a reasonable job
for simultaneously predicting NOx, CO and unburned
hydrocarbons from idle to maximum power is no where close
to completion [123-127]
The baseline flamelet approach provides as an extension of the
fast chemistry to account for finite rate chemistry. However,
the traditional flamelet approach still relies on the assumption
that 1) the time scales for chemical kinetics are much shorter
than both the time scales of convection and diffusion; 2) the
flame thickness is smaller than the Kolmogorov length scale.
Combustion generated pollutants, such as soot and NOX, are
formed near the reaction zone. Since the formation rates of
pollutant species are relatively slow compared to combustion,
the concentration of pollutant species depend strongly on the
residence time. The flamelet assumptions made for
combustion species is generally not applicable to pollutant
species; consequently, the pollutant concentrations can not be
obtained directly from the flame library [128].
One of the controversial issue in the flamelet approach is the
choice of the parameter to describe the nonequilibrium nature
of combustion [129]. It has been well known in laminar
flames that NOX formation is strongly influenced by the
chemical kinetics. Two possibilities have been proposed in
the past to quantify the nonequilibrium state of chemical
kinetics. The traditional one is associated with the
characteristic time of turbulent mixing and the alternative one
is correlated to the fluid dynamics strain imposing on the
flame. Although these two parameters are related to each
other, their dependence on the turbulent flow field can be
significantly different. Consequently, the predicted emissions
will be highly sensitive to the choice of the nonequilibrium
parameter. Some preliminary studies been conducted for
turbulent hydrogen jet flames suggesting that the characteristic
mixing time is more appropriate than the strain rate for
predicting NOX emissions [128].
Another issue in the flamelet model is related to the detailed
molecular transport used in generating a flamelet library. The
flamelet library consists of laminar flames that are computed
using detailed molecular transport, and hence they contain full
effects of preferential diffusion among heat and species.
Recent experimental data on turbulent jet diffusion flames
indicate that the effect of preferential diffusion depends on the
jet exit velocity and on the axial location in turbulent jet
flames [130-131] The flamelet approach tends to over-predict
the preferential diffusion effects which can lead to inaccurate
NO and CO predictions [132]. Issues like these need to be
resolved before one can be reasonably sure for applying the
laminar flamelet modeling with reduced kinetics to gas turbine
combustors.
The Monte Carlo scalar and/or joint velocity/scalar PDF
modeling approaches address many of the second-order
closure modeling issues as summarized in References [133-
135]. Consequently, the scalar and joint velocity/scalar PDF
approaches have been applied [136] to a bluff body stabilized
burner [137] and the CFM56 single annular gas turbine
combustor, and compared with a specified PDF approach. All
the three approaches gave similar results indicating that more
work is needed to sort out the various issues, not the least of
which is the computational time with the Monte Carlo
simulation, before one can start using the latter in the design
system. Considering the importance of computer and/or turn-
around times in the design cycle, it is recommended to have a
back-to-back comparison between the simulation techniques
used by Pope [134] and Kollmann [138] adopted by Chen
[128] and Raju [122].
The random vortex [139-143] and large-eddy simulations with
linear eddy subgrid modeling [144-148] address many of the
issues facing the laminar flamelet and Monte Carlo modeling
approaches. Even though these advanced simulations
currently may not be ready for solving the entire combustor
flow field, its application for selected highly dynamic special-
purpose problems is considered timely by GEAE.
The appropriate modeling of the dense part of a spray is
crucial in determining the control parameters in the region
most prone to soot and CO formation and oxidation in next-
generation high-pressure ratio engines. Additionally, the
evolution of the spray in the region most prone to NOx
production depends upon the characteristics of the dense spray
region. Many of these issues are being addressed by
extending the dilute-spray Lagrangian formulations [118].
Results from the Eulerian-based models and numerical studies
of clusters of drops [149-152] have predicted that it is
precisely in the dense spray region that most of the control
parameters can be found. These clusters of drops models
account for drop interactions in terms of flow and drop
dynamics, and limitations on heat and mass transfer. The
current challenge is finding an approach to incorporate these
cluster models as submodels into a spray code. The
requirements are that the approach should both describe the
crucial drop interaction in a turbulent flow and be economic to
use for design purposes. The Eulerian-Eulerian approach must
compromise on the time history of the drops but has the
potential of a state-of-the-art description of interaction of
groups of drops with turbulence. Monte-Carlo approaches can
follow test drops and could, in principle, describe both drop
interaction and interaction of drops with turbulence, although
at great computational cost; their difficulty is in the "testing"
of the number of drops to be followed in order to achieve a
meaningful solution given the fact that this testing must be

conducted, for example, every time changes in injectors,
atomizers or fuels occur.
Summary
The gas turbine combustion design community has been
making qualitative use of the recent fundamental
understanding of aerothermal chemistry aspects of
combustion, in addition to the computational combustion
dynamics in its design process. Whereas this has been very
useful in the past, mechanistically more accurate models are
needed for designing the next-generation energy-efficient gas
turbines with ultra-low emissions. The combustion research
community and industry need to work closely and utilize
advanced diagnostics and dynamically accurate turbulent
combustion modeling approaches for the next-generation
combustion design system.
