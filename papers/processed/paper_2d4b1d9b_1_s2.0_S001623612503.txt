A B S T R A C T
This study explores the impact of oxygen enrichment level (23, 25, and 29 % O2 in air) on plasma-assisted 
combustion of low-calorific gases (20–40 % CH4 in CO2) at various fuel-to-air ratios. Plasma parameters were 
fixed (120 kHz, 4.66 kV). Results indicate that oxygen enrichment combined with plasma-assisted combustion 
affects the process differently. High oxygen enrichment level enables stable combustion of very low-calorific 
mixtures without plasma, which was impossible otherwise. In addition, using the SPECAIR software, the influ­
ence of plasma discharges on electronic, rotational, vibrational, and translational temperatures was determined. 
Since plasma alone could not fully combust mixtures of 20 vol% CH4 in CO2, oxygen enrichment was applied for 
co-enhancement via a temperature-raising mechanism. For mixtures with 25–35 % CH4 in CO2, CO concentra­
tions in flue gases dropped by 86–90 % due to higher combustion temperatures, while NOx emissions rose by 
~93 % and ~87 %. Linking infrared emissions with CO and NOx emissions could potentially provide a better 
understanding of the optimal regimes of the low-calorific gas combustion process.
1. Introduction
Through the operational time of the landfill, the methane content in 
the emitted gas trends to decrease [1]. As landfill gas degrades to 35–40 
% methane, it is mainly co-combusted in high-temperature flares [2,3], 
for emission control, until the methane content in the gas mixture would 
not decrease to 20–25 % [4], after which those gases could not be easily 
utilised. Currently, low-calorific gases from landfills have limited pro­
cessing and utilization options for value-added products [5]. Even 
though low-calorific gases are a versatile alternative to fossil fuels with 
various practical applications [5]. However, their use in heat and power 
generation faces significant challenges. Mainly, CO2 dilution lowers the 
flame temperature and narrows the stability range [6,7]. High vari­
ability in hydrocarbon content and the presence of ballast gases, like 
CO2, severely affect fuel properties and combustion characteristics. Key 
parameters such as heating value [7,8], flame and self-ignition tem­
peratures [8,9], ignition delay [10], laminar burning velocity [11], 
flammability limits [12], combustion stability [13–15] and even com­
bustion kinetics [16,17] are altered compared to conventional fuels. 
Furthermore, changes in blend composition can cause ignition 
difficulties [18], acoustic-based combustion instabilities [19], high 
emissions, and reduced efficiency [20]. These issues highlight the need 
for effective solutions to optimize low-calorific gas combustion.
Several approaches have been proposed to improve the combustion 
temperature, stability, and heating value of low-calorific gases. These 
include optimizing fuel nozzle design, applying swirling techniques 
[21], and adjusting fuel and air velocities [22]. Chemically, combustion 
enhancement often involves adding small amounts of highly reactive 
fuels like hydrogen or strong oxidizers such as oxygen. Studies show that 
even minor H2 additions can markedly enhance flame stability [23,24], 
increase flame temperature, and improve heating value [26,27]. How­
ever, this also tends to raise NOx emissions, particularly under low to 
moderate strain rates [25]. Another promising method is MILD (Mod­
erate and Intensive Low Oxygen Dilution) combustion, aimed at 
reducing fuel consumption and addressing flame instability. Research on 
low-calorific gas flames in MILD conditions highlights that supple­
menting O2 and H2 and preheating the air effectively increases heat 
release and promotes NO formation [26,27].
The aforementioned methods for improving low-calorific gas com­
bustion are effective, but have practical and environmental drawbacks. 
H2 enrichment, for instance, can significantly reduce power output, pose

coupled with real and perceived safety concerns. Hydrogen is also more 
costly per unit of energy compared to common fossil fuels and is less 
widely available [28]. In some operating and design scenarios, H2 
enrichment offers no significant benefits and can be detrimental [28]. 
Regarding MILD technology, combustion systems often cannot operate 
in flameless mode until the combustor reaches the autoignition tem­

unstable, increasing the risk of incidents [29].
Oxygen enrichment is a promising method to improve low-calorific 
gas combustion by using an oxidant with a higher O2 content than air 
[30]. It raises combustion temperatures, enhances thermal efficiency, 
improves ignition, stabilizes flames, and increases heat release while 
reducing exhaust gas volume and equipment costs [30]. This process 
also lowers fuel consumption per production unit [32], enabling more 
efficient use of low-calorific gases. Studies on premixed and diffusion 
flames confirm their potential for advancing energy efficiency and sus­
tainability. For example, Wang et al. [31] proposed enriching low- 
calorific gas flames with propane and oxygen, reporting significant 
flame structure changes and increased free radical production. Mean­
while, Li et al. [32] employed GriMech 3.0 to analyze low-calorific gas 
diffusion flames under varying oxygen enrichment levels, showing that 
O2 enrichment boosts net heat release and promotes free radical for­
mation. Yilmaz et al. [33] investigated premixed low-calorific gas flames 
in a pilot-scale burner, finding that flame stability improves up to 24 % 
O2 but deteriorates beyond 31 % O2, with peak NOx emissions at 28 % 
O2 [36]. According to [34], this could be explained by the NO formation 
pathways shift with methane content. In blends containing ≤12 % 
methane, the NNH mechanism dominates, while at higher methane 
levels, the prompt (Fenimore) pathway becomes more significant. 
Increased oxygen enrichment further drives thermal NOx formation via 
the Zeldovich mechanism due to elevated flame temperatures and pro­
motes the N2O route (though with less impact than the thermal 
pathway), while reducing prompt and NNH contributions irrespective of 
methane concentration. Improving combustion of low-calorific gases 
without increasing methane content is appealing due to the high costs 
associated with upgrading such gases [33,34,38]. Another technology 
that can complement oxygen enrichment for low-calorific gas combus­
tion is plasma-assisted combustion (PAC). Non-thermal plasma (NTP) 
has a wide range of applications, from medical uses like intensifying 
healing rates for various injuries [35] to material surface treatments, 
providing anti-bacterial and self-cleaning properties to polyester/cellu­
lose fabrics [36], CO2 conversion [37], and environmental control of 
harmful substances [38]. In the past decade, PAC has gained attention as 
a method for enhancing combustion and stability by manipulating gases 
with an electric field. This field energy, through thermal and kinetic 
mechanisms [39], breaks down larger molecules into smaller species 
that require less energy for oxidation and utilization [40]. Plasma 
assistance can improve combustion parameters by increasing calorific 
value, shortening induction zone length, and lowering ignition tem­
peratures [41,42]. Non-equilibrium plasma types like the gliding arc, 
used in this study, exhibit high electron temperatures (1–10 eV), making 
them more kinetically active. They rapidly produce active radicals and 
excited species via electron impact dissociation, excitation, and energy 
relaxation [43,44]. Many electron impact processes strongly depend on 
electron energy, and radicals are major influencers in combustion [45]. 
The species generated by gliding arc plasma include NO*, N2*, and OH*. 
Research has demonstrated differences in their spatial distribution [46]. 
CH* and OH* radicals, formed from hydrocarbon decomposition, serve 
as chemiluminescence signals to track flame shape, position, and tem­
perature changes under plasma influence, useful for analyzing com­

Combining 
plasma-assisted 
combustion with oxygen-enriched oxidizer could significantly impact 
the combustion process. Oxygen, as a strong oxidant, forms radicals with 
even higher oxidative abilities. For example, the O2 (a1Δg) radical is 
particularly important due to its long radiation lifetime (up to 3500 s) 
[49], enabling it to exit the pre-combustion zone and travel to the flame 
core. In contrast, O2 (b1Σ+g) has a shorter lifetime (up to 13 s) [50] but 
can still reach the combustion zone due to the treatment zone’s prox­
imity. Experiments [51] showed O2 (a1Δg) at a mole fraction of 0.01 
reduced induction zone length by a factor of 2 and doubled the flame- 
covered area. According to [52], a fuel–air mixture with 2–5 % O2 
(a1Δg) could reduce self-ignition distance and lower ignition tempera­
tures. Smirnov et al. [51] reported that O2 (a1Δg) or O2 (b1Σ+g) at 0.01 
mol fraction shortened the ignition zone by up to 50 %.
For low-calorific gases with high CO2 and low CH4 content, plasma 
assistance improves stability and efficiency, but still does not fully uti­
lize the fuel potential due to low combustion temperatures, insufficient 
for complete CO oxidation from plasma reforming and decomposition. 
In that case, combining oxygen enrichment with plasma assistance 
emerges as a key solution for cleaner and more efficient combustion of 
low-calorific gases.
The literature review highlights the importance of research on 
combustion instability and emission behaviour of low-calorific gas 
mixtures. Oxygen enrichment reduces combustion instability and in­
creases fuel utilisation rates, but limited attention has been given to 
combining plasma assistance and oxygen enrichment. The reaction 
mechanism of such coupling was not extensively studied. Knowledge 
about non-thermal plasma’s influence on mixture dissociation is limited 
and often not discussed. Plasma and oxygen enrichment effects (ther­
mal, kinetic, breakdown) can enhance CO formation from plasma- 
processed CO2, dissociation of hydrocarbons, improve combustion sta­
bility, and increase combustion temperatures, leading to reduced CO 
emissions and especially extend the flammability of low calorific gases. 
For this purpose, this study examines the flame characteristics and 
combustion products of low-calorific value mixtures (20, 25, 30, 35, and 
40 vol% of CH4 in CO2) under non-thermal plasma assistance at different 
oxygen enrichment levels. The plasma operated at a fixed frequency of 
120 kHz and a voltage of 4.66 kV. Simultaneously, the oxygen enrich­
ment level was changed from 21 to 29 vol% of O2 in N2 and tested at 
three fuel-to-air ratios at lean combustion conditions (φ = 0.83, 0.71, 
0.63). The flame chemiluminescence and emission spectra in UV and IR 
wavelength ranges were obtained. In addition, a correlation between 
infrared emission intensities and CO/NOx emissions in flue gases was 
found, and it could allow holding plasma assistance and oxygen 
enrichment levels in optimal regimes for emission and temperature 
control.

2. Materials and methods
2.1. Experimental stand and burner
The experimental stand includes: a plasma-assisted low swirl burner, 
a high voltage pulse generator, mass flow controllers, the UV–VIS and 
NIR spectrometers, the flame chemiluminescence capturing and flow gas 
analysis systems, which are schematically shown in Fig. 1.
The plasma-assisted low-swirl burner contains a cylindrical outer 
body, made from stainless steel type 321, which serves as the ground 
electrode. It has an external diameter of Ø = 28, the part length is 44 
mm, the diameter of the inner hole is Ø = 22 mm (see Fig. 2). The inner 
electrode, shaped in the form of a cut cone serves as an inner electrode, 
connected to a high-voltage line. Cone body diameter (in widest part) is 
Ø = 20 mm, height – 38 mm and the cut cone top surface diameter 
Ø=10 mm. High voltage electrode as well is made from steel type 321.
2.2. Experimental procedure
To achieve optimal gas mixing rates, a system with a mixing chamber 
was utilized. After the mixing process, gases were directed to two 
radially positioned inlet nozzles, each with an internal diameter of 6 
mm, ensuring a stabilized velocity field for uniform gas flow. After 
reaching the swirler, the flow traversed the burner’s internal cavity, 
converging at the narrowest gap where the gliding arc was initially 
formed. The gas flow elongated the arc along the electrodes, extending 
until extinguished or short-cut by a shorter current path. This process 
covered the exhaust channel’s cross-section with plasma arcs, gener­
ating a rotating gliding arc plasma. The experimental rig is enclosed 
with plexiglass-made shields to ensure safety while combustion experi­
ments. In the top part of the rig, the ventilation port is mounted, which is 
connected to the central suction system for flue gases evacuation from 
the inner volume of the experimental rig. The burner is covered with a 
quartz tube transparent for UV and IR wavelengths (transmittance > 90 
%) with a diameter of 130 mm and a height of 730 mm. Required gases 
for combustion (air, oxygen, methane and carbon dioxide) were sup­
plied from the cylinders, and the flow controlled using mass flow con­
trollers (Brooks SLA 5800). The parameters of the gases and combustion 
conditions are presented in Table 1. The thermal power of the burner 
was kept constant (~1.32 kW) for every investigation point with various 
fuel-to-air ratios (φ) and mixtures. Gas compositions were selected to 
simulate low-calorific value gases with 20–40 vol % CH4 in CO2, which 
combustion is instable, hardly combustible or non-combustible without 
plasma assistance or oxygen enrichment decreasing methane content in 
CO2. In the current work were used five mixtures of CH4/CO2, with the 
CH4 content of 20, 25, 30, 35 and 40 vol% in CO2. For easier identifi­
cation of different mixtures, abbreviation BGXX/YY with indexes were 
introduced: XX represent CH4 content in the mixture and YY – CO2 
content of the mixture, e.g. BG20/80 would represent the mixture con­
taining 20 vol% of the CH4 and 80 vol% of the CO2 in the mixture. The 
experiments were performed with and without plasma assistance and 
using different composition oxidizer, starting from normal air (O2/N2 – 
21vol %/79 vol%) to oxygen enriched air (O2 – 23–29 vol % in N2). For 
oxygen-enriched air condition, the oxygen concentration was increased 
from 21 vol % to 23 vol %, 25 vol % and to 29 vol % of O2 in N2 (see 
Table 1).
During the plasma-assisted combustion, gliding arc discharges were 
formed using a DC high voltage generator, made by Redline Technolo­
gies (G2000). The voltage applied to the burner was set constant at 4.66 
kV and on a permanent driving frequency of 120 kHz, with power 
consumed from the socket by the generator being 135–145 W.

2.4. Flue gas analysis
A flue gas sampling probe was positioned within the chamber, 60 cm 
away from the burner exit to perform sampling. This probe was secured 
within a metal cap that covered the quartz glass along the central axis. 
The sampling probe was connected to a Testo 350XL analyser, which 
was used to measure NOx and CO emissions in the flue gas. Each sam­
pling test lasted 120 s to allow for parameter stabilization.
2.5. Plasma analysis
For the plasma analysis was used SPECAIR 3.0 software [55] which 
allowed to compute and fit the obtained spectra from the UV–VIS data to 
the calculated one. The SPECAIR code [56] provides the ability to model 
up to 37 molecular transitions, including NO*, N2*, N2+*, O2*, CN*, 
OH*, NH*, C2*, and CO*, as well as atomic lines of N, O, and C. The 
model provides accurate simulations of the absolute spectral emission 
and absorption intensities of air from 80 nm to 5.5 µm. SPECAIR has 
been widely used for temperature calculations in numerous studies, 
showing good correlation with emission intensities graphs obtained via 
OES for various plasma types and experimental parameters: dielectric 
barrier [57], nanosecond pulse [58,59], gliding arc [60] and micro­
second pulse discharges [61].
3. Results and discussion

chemiluminescence

to the flame structure and its distribution and intensity is changing with 
the gas composition and combustion conditions. A similar change was 
observed in the spatial distributions of OH* and CH* emission in­
tensities, although the distribution of C2* emission intensities takes 
place at the base of the flame, and its vertical spatial distribution cor­
responds to the flame shape, and it is not affected by plasma discharges.

3.2. Analysis of emission spectra from flames
The flame chemiluminescence revealed the influence of CO2 in 
mixtures and flammability range extension when coupling plasma 
assistance and oxygen enrichment, and emission intensities of radicals 
increase. In order to understand deeper the effect from plasma assistance 
coupling with oxygen enrichment, the UV spectra were obtained and, in 
addition, analysed using Specair software. The emission intensity graphs 
in the UV range, from 200 to 1000 nm, with and without plasma assis­
tance under different levels of oxygen enrichment for φ = 0.83, are

The most relevant and influencing on the combustion process is OH* 
radical [66,67], which was detected at 309 nm. It showed the biggest 
reaction on plasma assistance, while oxygen enrichment was rising the 
emission intensity levels of all radicals by some amount. Comparing 
emission intensity values for OH* radical with and without plasma, for 
all enrichment stages and all studied mixtures (from BG20/80 to BG40/60), 
it could be seen that OH* emission intensity increases by 16.21–2.47 
times for 21 % of oxygen in the nitrogen; by 7.41–1.42 for 23 %, by 
3.28–2.18 for 25 % and 2.8–1.6 times for 29 % of oxygen in the air, 
respectively. Emission intensities of other radicals raised not that 
rapidly, differing by 1.85–2.95 times for CH* and 2.01–2.44 times for 
C2*. That, probably, can be explained by the difference in dissociation 
energy of CO2 and CH4. Plasma-assisted processes affect OH* radical 
formation through fuel cracking mechanisms, particularly when 
methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2) are subjected to plasma 
treatment. For efficient CO2 conversion into carbon monoxide (CO) at 
lower energy levels, a plasma-chemical reaction pathway involving 
multiple vibrationally excited states of CO2* can be utilized. These 
states, with minimal energy gaps, undergo collisions that lead to disso­
ciation at approximately 5.5 eV [68], the energy needed to break a C–O 
bond in CO2, facilitating vibrational ladder climbing [69]. Furthermore, 
favourable conditions enable atomic oxygen, produced in the process, to 
react with CO2, generating an additional CO molecule at 0.3 eV, 
reducing the total energy required to around 2.9 eV [70]. In contrast, 
methane conversion under gliding arc plasma requires less energy, 
about 1.6 eV [71]. Plasma influences OH* radical formation by 
decomposing CH4 and CO2 into reactive species such as CH*, H2*, C2*, 
and OH* radicals. Hydroxyl radicals react with hydrogen, ozone, and 
oxygen radicals to form H2O, where the latter radicals exhibit superior 
oxidative capabilities compared to molecular oxygen. It also should be 
noted, that the graph plateau trends to decrease with increasing CO2 
content in the mixture (see Fig. 4). The same phenomena were observed 
by the Ghabi et al. [72]. Most probably, graph plateau trends to decrease 
with an increasing CO2 content in the mixture due to the combustion 
temperature decrease, while more CO2 being introduced in the com­
bustion process, which causes a decrease in the emission intensities rates 
of the radicals. [76–80].
For a deeper analysis of the obtained UV spectra from flames was 
used SPECAIR software to determine electronic, vibrational and rota­
tional temperature changes at different combustion conditions. In gen­
eral, plasma is in equilibrium or near-equilibrium state, and a single 
temperature can describe all internal energy states, including vibra­
tional, rotational, and electronic modes. This unified temperature can be 
identified using the absolute intensity of any atomic or molecular 
characteristic or through Boltzmann plots representing vibrational or 
rotational population distributions. Conversely, in nonequilibrium 
plasmas, the gas temperature is more accurately determined by ana­
lysing the intensity distribution of rotational lines. Depending on the 
plasma excitation level, transitions of O2*, N2*, N+2*, and NO* (in dry 
air) and OH* (in humid air) are useful [56]. Below temperatures of 
approximately 5000 K, the spectral bands of NO*, OH*, and O2* are 
predominant. At higher temperatures, the spectrum includes the second 
positive system of N2* (C–B), the first negative system of N+2 (B–X), and 
atomic lines of O and N. [56]. or low-temperature humid air plasmas, 
the emission spectrum of the OH* A–X transition around 300 nm is 
particularly effective for measuring the rotational temperature. At 
elevated temperatures or when an electric field is present, the OH* 
transition overlaps with strong emission intensities from the N2* C–B 
system (second positive system), a method extensively used by various 
researchers [57–61]. The hydroxyl (OH*) molecular band (A2Σ+, υ = 0 
→ X2 Π, υ’=0, 306–314 nm) appears in the emission spectrum due to the 
dissociation of water molecules within the plasma. The rotational tem­
perature of the OH radical is generally similar to the gas temperature 
[73]. This temperature is measured using the OH* (A2Σ+) band at 
306.357 nm [74–76]. Synthetic OH* spectra are generated by summing 
the intensity of each line within the 306 to 314 nm range. Similar to 
studies on atmospheric-pressure discharges [77], it is presumed that the 
rotational temperature (Trot) of OH* (A2Σ+) can approximate the gas 
temperature (Tg), given that the species’ typical lifetime (approximately 
10–100 ns) far exceeds the rotational-to-translational energy transfer 
time (approximately 0.1–1 ns) at atmospheric pressure [78]. Due to the 
OH* A-X transition providing the best fit in this study, it was chosen for 
temperature determination. A spectral range from 280 to 330 nm was 
selected for further processing to avoid interference from other plasma 
species. The OH* A–X transition is among the most intense systems 
emitted by low-temperature air plasmas containing even a small fraction 
(~1 %) of H2* or H2O*. The rotational temperature can be derived by 
fitting the entire band, or more straightforwardly, from the relative in­
tensities of two groups of rotational lines corresponding to the R and P 
branches of the OH* A–X (0, 0) vibrational band [56]. These branches 
form distinct peaks at about 307 nm and 309 nm, respectively, are 
shown in Fig. 5.
The curve on the Fig. 5 was simulated with SPECAIR software with 
the experimentally obtained one, for various oxygen enrichment re­
gimes under constant fuel-to-air ratio (φ = 0.83) and one mixture (BG30/ 
70). The spectroscopic model implemented in SPECAIR for the OH* A–X 
transition is detailed in [79]. According to the authors, the response of 
typical detection systems remains nearly constant over the narrow 
spectral range of interest [56,79,80]. Using that, were calculated various 
temperature profiles (electronic, rotational, vibrational and trans­
lational) for the regimes with and without plasma assistance for BG25/75, 
BG30/70 and BG35/65, respectively. They could be seen on the Fig. 6.
It should be noted, that the change in rotational, vibrational and

3.6. Correlation of flue gas emissions and infrared emission intensities
Spontaneous transitions between distinct molecular energy states 
allow infrared-active gases to absorb or emit radiation. Each molecular 
structure could emit or absorb these photons at particular wavelengths. 
The identity of a molecular species can be established by analyzing the 
intensity of the intense characteristic wavelengths. The distribution of 
molecules across energy levels is temperature dependent, i.e., the 
Boltzmann distribution. Therefore, both the gas temperature and the 
frequency of molecules determine the absorption or emission intensity 
[88].
Absorption in matter–radiation interaction is a fundamental prop­
erty of the interaction of radiation with each molecule at intrinsic energy 
levels specific to the molecule. Molecules absorb only certain fre­
quencies (or wavelengths) of near-infrared radiation if they match the 
energy difference between vibrational levels. [89]. Fourier-transformed 
infrared spectroscopy is a known method for the flue gas analysis and 
the provision of quantity and quality results in terms of CO, CO2, NO and 
NOx monitoring [90–95]. While the method is proven to work [96], it 
requires specific equipment and provides results that requires post- 
processing for being properly understood. Due to the partial over­
lapping of the emission spectra for CO2, H2O and the absorption spectra 
for CO and NO [96,97] at their respective parts of a spectrum, potential 
emission spectroscopy could serve as a quality (but not quantity) 
method of undesirable emission analysis. Based on the ability of the

4. Conclusions
The combustion enhancement of various low calorific gas mixtures 
(40–20 vol% of CH4 in CO2) by plasma assistance and oxygen enrich­
ment was investigated in the low swirl burner in lean-fuel conditions. 
Flame characteristics and performance under plasma assistance and 
oxygen enrichment were determined using the flame emission spec­
troscopy method in UV–VIS and NIR diapason, and using the flue gas 
analyser for post-combustion products, namely CO and NOx, analysis, 
respectively. As a result, the following conclusions were made: 
(1) Increasing the combustion temperature by enriching the air with 
oxygen (23–29 %) led to almost complete combustion of carbon 
monoxide in the combustion chamber, even for low-calorific 
gases. For a 35 % methane blend, CO in flue gases dropped by 
~90 % (from 831-2325 ppm to 48–184 ppm) with 25 % O2 
enrichment and plasma support. A 25 % methane blend demon­
strated a similar trend, requiring 29 % O2 enrichment to cut CO 
emissions by 86–91.8 % (from 2243 to 3104 to 216–420 ppm).
(2) The increase of the combustion temperature leaded to elevated 
thermal nitrogen oxides emissions (via Zeldovich mechanism). 
For 35 and 25 % methane-containing blends, NOx emissions grew 
by 91–94 % (from 20 to 25 to 303–386 ppm) and by 82–91 % 
(from 37 to 70 to 390–437 ppm), respectively, with NO and NO2 
formed in nearly equal amounts. For the lowest calorific value 
blend (20 % of methane), the increase of the excess air led to 
decreased NOx emissions, probably due to the inability of the 
process to maintain sufficient temperature, ensuring heating of 
the entire mass of the ballast nitrogen.
(3) Analysis of the modelled OH* transitions with and without 
plasma assistance with the SPECAIR program suggests that the 
primary effect of the plasma on the combustion was likely kinetic 
rather than thermal. Rotational temperature (indicator of the gas 
temperature at the plasma discharge zone) showed minimal 
difference with and without plasma. In contrast, electron tem­
peratures rise significantly with plasma activation.
(4) Plasma-assisted combustion with oxygen enrichment had the 
greatest effect on the generation of OH* and the C2* radical. The 
emission intensity of the radicals was more than 10 times higher 
than conventional combustion, for lower calorific value gases. As 
the methane content in the mixture was increasing, the difference 
between the emission intensity of OH* with and without plasma 
decreased, while remaining more than fivefold. Only for the 
mixture with the highest calorific value (40 % methane), it was 
1.42. C2* radical followed the same trend, but was lower in ab­
solute values. On average, the emission intensity increased by ~2 
times, comparing combustion modes with and without plasma 
support.
(5) Plasma combined with oxygen enrichment improved both com­
bustion quality and flammability limits. The blend with 20 % of 
methane was not able to sustainably combust conventionally, 
with the flame extinguishing within seconds after plasma deac­
tivation. Air enrichment to 29 % of oxygen enabled stable unas­
sisted combustion for this mixture for all tested access air 
coefficients. Plasma support under higher enrichment levels 
enhanced combustion stability – the became more turbulent and 
elongated, with a clearly defined active combustion core. Plasma 
produces a stable, well-formed V-shaped flame that was not 
tending to blow-off.
(6) A clear correlation was established between flue gas emissions, 
infrared emission intensities and combustion temperature, and 
which curve was fitted into the experimentally obtained points, 
from which the equations were generated. The resulting R2 values 
were 0.974 for CO, 0.8957 for NOx, and 0.9421 for NO. A sigmoid 
function was similarly describing the temperature dependence on 
infrared emission intensities, with R2 values of 0.91, 0.90, and 
0.88 for φ = 0.82, 0.71, and 0.63, respectively. These correla­
tions, combined with insights of the plasma behaviour (difference 
in the characterizing plasma temperatures) suggest the potential 
usage of this method for achieving plasma assisted combustion 
with minimized emissions of gases having Global Warming Po­
tential. Furthermore, monitoring near-infrared emission in­
tensities2 could provide control over combustion temperatures 
and optimize the usage of oxygen for enrichment, preventing 
burner overheating.
