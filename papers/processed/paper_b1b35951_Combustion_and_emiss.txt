Abstract
The practical challenge in research in the ®eld of gas turbine combustion mainly centres around a
clean emission, a low liner wall temperature, a uniform exit temperature distribution for turbomachinery
applications along with a fuel economy of the combustion process. An attempt to meet up the challenge
has been made in the present paper in developing a computational model based on stochastic separated
¯ow analysis of a typical diusion controlled spray combustion of liquid fuel in a gas turbine combustor
to study the in¯uences of combustor pressure and inlet swirl on the combustion and emission
characteristics within the combustor. A standard k±E model with wall function treatment for near wall
region has been adopted for the solution of conservation equations in gas phase. The initial spray
parameters are speci®ed by a suitable PDF size distribution and a given spray cone angle. A radiation
model for the gas phase, based on ®rst order moment method, has been adopted in consideration of the
gas phase as a grey absorbing±emitting medium. Formation of thermal NOx, as a post combustion
reaction process, is determined from Zeldovich mechanism.
It has been recognized that an increase in swirl number reduces the NOx emission level and improves
the pattern factor at all combustor pressures. However, though at lower pressure an increase in swirl
number reduces combustion eciency, the trend is exactly the reverse at higher pressure. With increase
in pressure, pattern factor improves while the combustion eciency falls and the level of NOx emission
increases. # 1999 Elsevier Science Ltd. All rights reserved.

1. Introduction
Extensive research works in the area of gas turbine combustion have gone on over the
last two decades in order to improve the design of both stationary gas turbines for power
generation and aero-propulsion gas turbines. The principal objectives of these research
works are to improve the combustion process in order to achieve higher combustion
eciency with a uniform exit temperature and allowable liner wall temperature distribution
and to reduce the combustion-generated polluting gases. Problems like acid rain and
stratospheric ozone layer depletion increase the concern of today's world about the ill-

hand, focus on the in¯uence of some important operating parameters like inlet swirl, air±
fuel ratio, inlet pressure and temperature on ¯ow and combustion characteristics in the
combustor using both gaseous and liquid fuel. It has been recognized from the previous
works that pollutant emission levels from the combustor are particularly sensitive to
operating parameters like inlet air swirl and combustor pressure [6]. Most of the works

2. Theoretical formulation
Evaporation and combustion of a continuously injected liquid fuel spray in a stream of air is
solved in a can-type gas turbine combustor with divergent entry following a particle-source-in-
cell (PSIC) modelling technique. The size and shape of the combustor (Fig. 1) is considered in
conformity to the available literature. The air supply to the combustor is split among the
swirler at entry and through two radial jets in the form of secondary and dilution air. The
following assumptions are considered in the study:
(i) The problem is considered to be axi-symmetric. Hence the radial jets are taken to be
uniform over the periphery.
(ii) Body forces and buoyancy forces are neglected.
(iii) Virtual mass force and Basset force on the liquid particles are not considered due to high
density ratio between the two phases.
(iv) There is no nucleation, collision, break-up, coagulation and micro-explosion of droplets.
(v) Droplets do not take part in radiative energy exchange which is solved as a purely gas
phase phenomena.
(vi) The fuel is considered to be n-hexane.

3. Method of solution
3.1. Numerical scheme
The numerical code consisted of two dierent modulesÐone for gas phase computation and
the other for calculation of the droplet phase.
The gas phase conservation equations were solved with appropriate boundary conditions
following explicit ®nite dierence technique. The conservation equations were ®rst non-
dimensionalised against reference quantities and then discretized following a staggered grid
arrangement. The reference length was taken to be the combustor diameter (D), the reference
velocity to be the axial velocity through the swirler (Uzin), the reference temperature to be the
ambient temperature (Ta=300 K) and the reference time to be (D/Uzin). The diusion and
convective terms were discretized by central and hybrid dierencing schemes respectively. The
coupling between pressure and velocity was accomplished using a MAC-based SOLA
algorithm [17] . Energy and species conservation equations were solved in a coupled form with
the continuity and momentum equations to account for the variation in density with
temperature and concentration. The solution procedure in this module continued till a steady
state convergence was attained.
The ordinary dierential equations of the droplet phase were solved by a fourth order
Runge±Kutta method to generate droplet information required for the gas phase source terms.
The solution in this module continued until all the representative droplets of the dierent
classes extinguished in the process of evaporation.
The two modules were invoked alternately to solve the respective equations until a ®nal
convergence was arrived at. This was considered to be achieved when the result of any module
did not alter the result of the other module.
3.2. Numerical mesh
A numerical mesh of 85  30 grid nodes was used after several experiments, which
showed that further re®nement in either direction did not change the result (maximum
dierence in velocity and other scalar functions in the carrier phase) by more than 2%. The
grid spacings in axial and radial directions were changed smoothly to minimize the
deterioration of the formal accuracy of the ®nite dierence scheme due to variable grid
spacing and in such a way that a higher concentration of nodes occurs near the swirler and
the radial holes.
3.3. Operating parameters and boundary conditions
The total air ¯ow to the combustor was split among the swirler and the two radial
jets according to a ratio of 5:7:8 following Cameron et al. [10]. An air±fuel ratio of 60 was
used. Swirl number was calculated assuming a solid body type rotation generated by the
helicoidal vanes in the swirler. The air entering the combustor was considered to be at

weighing eects depend on the range of operating parameters, mainly the inlet pressure and
mean drop diameter. At lower pressure the adverse eect of reduced droplet penetration due to
an increase in inlet swirl of primary air plays the dominant role in reducing the combustion
eciency. However, when the pressure in the combustion chamber is high, the droplet
penetration is as such low. Hence, the favourable eect of enhanced droplet vapourization and
mixing of fuel vapour in air on the burning rate due to an increase in inlet air swirl plays the
prominent role in increasing the combustion eciency.

5. Conclusion
A numerical model of liquid fuel spray combustion in a gas turbine combustor has been
developed to recognize the in¯uence of inlet swirl number and combustion pressure on
important combustion and emission characteristics as follows:
. In¯uence of inlet swirl number on combustion eciency shows opposite trends at low and
high combustion pressure. An increase in swirl number reduces the combustion eciency at
a combustion pressure of 100 kPa, while it increases the combustion eciency when the
combustor pressure is 600 kPa. However, with an increase in inlet swirl number, pattern
factor remains almost unaected while NO formation is reduced at all pressures.
. An increase in combustor pressure, for a ®xed inlet temperature, results in (i) a reduction in
combustion eciency, (ii) further non-uniformity in the exit temperature distribution, and
(iii) an increase in the formation of NO in the combustor. The variation of mean NO at exit
with pressure is found to have an exponent of around 1.5.
