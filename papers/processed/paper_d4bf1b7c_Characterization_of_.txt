Introduction
The term ŌĆ£combustion instabilityŌĆØ generally refers to the sus-
tained high amplitude pressure ’¼éuctuations in a combustion cham-
ber, resulting from the coupling between the system acoustics and
the unsteady heat release. It is by nature a self-excited oscillation,
involving complicated physical phenomena such as unsteady
combustion, acoustic ’¼éuctuations, heat transfer, and the vorticity
’¼üeld. A large number of studies using experimental, theoretical,
and numerical simulation approaches have been performed to in-
vestigate instability mechanisms, controlling parameters, and ac-
tive or passive control methodologies to suppress the instability
intensity level to acceptable ranges. Ultimately, to accurately pre-
dict instability characteristics at the development stage, a com-
plete understanding of the nature of combustion instabilities will
be required and sophisticated theoretical models must be devel-
oped 1,2.
The development of a theoretical model capable of predicting
the formation of combustion-driven pressure oscillations is con-
tingent on the physical understanding of the response of a ’¼éame to
periodic disturbances. Heat release perturbations occur for several
reasons, such as inlet velocity ’¼éuctuations, equivalence ratio ’¼éuc-
tuations, and perturbations of thermodynamic properties. For tur-
bulent, totally premixed ’¼éames, the direct in’¼éuence of equiva-
lence ratio, pressure, temperature, and density variations on the
heat release rate perturbations is negligible 3. For this type of
’¼éame, the inlet velocity ’¼éuctuation is dominant, and it governs the
’¼éame dynamics. To quantitatively describe the response of a ’¼éame
to upstream perturbations, the ’¼éame transfer function FTF is
introduced, de’¼üned as the normalized ratio of heat release and

Experimental Methods
All measurements are performed in a lean-premixed, variable-
length, gas turbine combustor facility, shown in Fig. 1. This facil-
ity consists of an air inlet section, a siren, a mixing section, an
optically-accessible quartz combustor section, a steel combustor
section, and an exhaust section. The air can be heated to a maxi-
mum temperature of 400┬░C by a 30 kW electric heater. Two high-
temperature globe valves are used to control the bypass ’¼éow rate,
i.e., the inlet velocity ’¼éuctuation amplitudes. A siren-type modu-
lation device is used to provide acoustic modulations. The siren is
driven by a variable-speed dc motor, which permits changing of
the forcing frequency 100ŌĆō400 Hz. The mixing section is
0.333 m long and has an annular cross section that is de’¼üned by a
19.1 mm o.d. centerbody and a 38.1 mm i.d. mixing tube. The
centerbody is centered in the mixing tube, and it is positioned
such that its downstream end is ’¼éush with the combustor dump
plane. A 30 deg ’¼éat-vane axial swirler is mounted in the mixing
tube at 76.2 mm upstream of the combustor dump plane. Fuel
natural gas+H2 is injected and mixed with air at upstream of the
choked inlet in order to eliminate equivalence ratio ’¼éuctuations.
High-frequency response, water-cooled, piezoelectric pressure

tions in the mixing denoted by PT1ŌĆōPT4 in Fig. 1 and the com-
bustor PTc and PT5 sections. The pressure signals are condi-
tioned by ampli’¼üers, digitized by an analog-to-digital converter,
and stored in a microcomputer memory for processing at a sam-

PT4 and 50.8 mm PT3 upstream of the combustor dump plane
