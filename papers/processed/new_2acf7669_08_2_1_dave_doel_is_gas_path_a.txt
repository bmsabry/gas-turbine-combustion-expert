Is Gas Path Analysis Enough?
Some musings on the status of gas turbine engine 
diagnostics and where we should be headed.
Abstract
Abstract
If one googles the phrase “gas turbine engine diagnostics” or a similar phrase, 
many, if not most of the hits describe efforts to use gas path analysis or some 
variant to diagnose engine problems using gas path sensors.  The citations start 
in the early seventies and continue unabated in 2008.  Is this the right emphasis?  
Using Google (actually Google Scholar) to determine fault frequency is not so 
easy (most hits describe solutions rather than problems).  This presentation 
describes a limited review of the types of problems being referred to the OEM 
and suggests that more such surveys are needed to ensure that the right 
technologies are developed.

Gas Turbine Engine Diagnostics is Tough, Because
y
Gas turbine engines are extremely reliable
◦
 
In-service events such as in-flight shutdowns, unscheduled engine removals and aborted 
takeoffs receive individual attention to identify and correct root cause
◦
 
Once a fault mode is understood, it will be designed out if it can
x
As a retrofit if possible
x
Else, as a change to the next upgrade
y
Engine sensors are not as reliable as the engines
◦
 
Engine designers add them to engines reluctantly
x
The designer prefers to eliminate fault modes rather than diagnose them 
x
A sensor cannot be allowed to break and damage the engine (it could be the source of a fault)
x
Also, sensors are expensive and generally may deliver bad news (e.g. false alarms)
◦
 
Most sensors are single element probes positioned where they can
 
be accommodated
◦
 
If they do not prove their merit, they are candidates for cost reduction
y
When problems occur in the field, the emphasis is getting the engine back on line 
or replacing it
◦
 
Often, just replace a suspected part and go on
◦
 
Limited interest in diagnostic troubleshooting (Sherlock Holmes is not a mechanic)

Fault Models
y Many simpler types of faults have already been modeled in 3D 
tools
◦
 
Clearance, chord erosion, leading edge shape, surface finish, leaks, . . .
◦
 
We just need to take advantage of these models
y Damage caused by fatigue failures, FOD, DOD, etc. are “random”
and far more difficult to model
y Prediction of sensor responses may also be difficult
y Ignoring these harder problems doesn’t solve anything!
◦
 
If we can’t develop ways to perfectly localize a fault to the appropriate 
component, we can at least quantify the uncertainty in our solutions
y I’m convinced these uncertainties dwarf any possible benefits of

◦
 
And a multi-point algorithm, in particular, must be based on an

y

◦
 
To replace a square matrix inversion algorithm
◦
 
Target: overhaul acceptance runs
x

◦
 
Originally included hand-tailored baselines and standard deviations
x
Derived from prior acceptance test data
x
Labor intensive (to put it mildly)
y
An on-wing version using cruise data was created in the late-1970s
◦
 
As an ingredient in GE’s GEM engine monitoring package
◦
 
Baselines were developed from cruise data
x
Even more labor intensive

y
Meanwhile, GE sought to reduce the development cost (and address the question of how to 
create baselines for a newly developed engine model)
◦
 
Evolved to using production baselines and cell correlations for overhaul test cells

Which shortchanged gas path temperatures and pressures (not addressed by the cell correlations)
y
Recent programs are generally less effective as a result
