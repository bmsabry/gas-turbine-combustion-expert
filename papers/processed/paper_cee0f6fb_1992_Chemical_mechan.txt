Crucial reactions for NOt and CO were identified, expressions for their kinetics were im- 
proved using theoretical predictions, and the improved mechanism was exploited by probing 
a data set from high-pressure turbulent combustion with a two-reactor model. Reaction-path 
analysis and linear sensitivity analysis were combined to identify the reactions that were most 
important in determining NOx and CO concentrations in lean (q~ = 0.6), premixed, laminar 
methane/air flames ranging in pressure from 1 to 40 atm. These reactions were then analyzed 
using Quantum-RRK techniques and literature data. Revised rate constants and additional 
reactions were proposed. Analysis revealed that thermal or Zeldovich formation of NO from 
N2 + O apparently proceeds via the electronically excited O(~D) state, not by direct reaction 
with ground-state O(3P). 
The chemical mechanism was used to model NOx measurements taken from the burned- 
gas zone of lean, premixed, turbulent, 5 to 10 atm methane and ethane flames stabilized on 
a perforated plate. The flow field of the turbulent flames was modeled as a perfectly stirred 
reactor (PSR) and a plug flow reactor (PFR) in series so that the complex chemistry could 
be included. Agreement between the model and the data was good, indicating that the PSR/ 
PFR approximation is valid for predicting NOr from lean, premixed flames. The nitrous oxide 
(N20) pathway was shown to be the dominant route to NOx formation at lower temperatures 
(more fuel-lean), with thermal NOx becoming more important at higher temperatures (closer 
to stoichiometric). The contribution of prompt NOx to the total NOx formed was found to be 
relatively small under fuel-lean conditions, although it becomes the dominant route to NOx 
under fuel-rich conditions. 
Introduction 
The gas-turbine industry is developing lean, pre- 
mixed combustors that can burn liquid fuels and 
natural gas while achieving emissions of less than 
25 ppmv NOx (= NO + NO2) and CO (dry, 15% 
02 basis). Gas-fired turbines typically operate with 
preheated air at temperatures up to 620 K (650 ~ F), 
pressures up to 10 atm, and combustor exit tem- 
peratures in excess of 1640 K (2500 ~ F). Aircraft en- 
gines operate at even higher pressures and inlet 
temperatures. 
For these reasons, chemical mechanisms of NOx 
and CO formation and destruction are of great in- 
terest. Mechanisms have been studied extensively, 
both theoretically and experimentally, 1 but little of 
this effort has focused on well-defined systems at 
temperatures and high pressures representative of 
gas-turbine conditions. An important uncertainty is 
that pressure dependence of reaction kinetics can 
be different than in low-pressure flames. 
Glarborg et al. 2 and Miller and Bowman 3 devel- 
oped a detailed mechanism of CHa and NOr chem- 
istry, comparing their detailed mechanism to pre- 
mixed low-pressure flames and atmospheric-pressure 
well-stirred reactors. Drake, Blint, and co-workers 4'5 
analyzed pressure dependence of selected reactions 
and computed species profiles in stoichiometric, 
premixed, laminar flames at pressures up to 20 atm. 
Along with Malte and Pratt, 6 Miller and Bowman, 3 
and Correa, 7 they found an important role for NzO 
chemistry in making NO. 
This paper focuses on the chemical mechanisms 
of NOx and CO formation and destruction in lean, 
premixed, high-pressure flames, conditions similar 
to the those found in gas turbines. Using laminar, 
premixed flames and the Miller and Bowman 
mechanism, 3 reactions critical to predictions of NOx 
and CO were identified. These reactions were re- 
analyzed using recent literature data and Quantum- 
RRK analyses, and rate constants, reactions, and

Discussion 
There must be a recirculation zone to stabilize 
these turbulent flames, but it will not be perfectly 
mixed. Given the state-of-the-art in turbulence/ 
chemistry modeling, the choice is either (1) includ- 
ing the details of the flow field and simplifying the 
chemistry or (2) simplifying the flow-field model and 
including all of the detailed chemistry. The latter 
was chosen here. We believe that the volume of 
the model PSR is related to the volume of the ac- 
tual turbulent flame zone, which in turn depends 
on inlet temperature and pressure. Volume of this 
flame zone decreases as the pressure and temper- 
ature increase, both experimentally and in the 
model. 
The model predicts that three major pathways 
contribute to NOx formation: thermal (Zeldovich) 
NOx, nitrous oxide NOx, and prompt NOx 2~ (initi- 
ated by attacks on N-2 by CH or CH-2). Predictions 
for the 10-atm methane/air flame with 615 K inlet 
temperature (Fig. 1) are shown in Fig. 3. At low 
equivalence ratio (low temperature), the nitrous ox- 
ide mechanism is found to be the dominant path- 
way of NO~ formation. As ~b and temperature in- 
crease, the relative importance of the thermal NOx 
pathway increases. 
Prompt NOx is a small contributor to total NO~ 
under these fuel-lean conditions. However, addi- 
tional calculations in PSR/PFR geometries show that 
prompt NOx becomes the dominant route to NOx 
under fuel-rich conditions. Prompt NO~ would be 
more important in lean combustion of fuels like CzH4 
and C2Hz that yield higher concentrations of CH 
and CH.e. 
The PSR/PFR model predicts that almost all of 
the NO~ formation occurs in the PSR for the fuel- 
lean cases. The reason for the decrease in NOx for- 
mation in the PFR zone is that CH, CH2, and O- 
atom concentrations drop rapidly once the flow 
leaves the PSR. At higher temperatures, NOx for- 
mation can continue throughout the PFR, predom- 
inantly through the thermal NO~ mechanism. Mea- 
surements of the NO~ concentration as function of 
distance in the perforated-plate combustor support 
this conclusion. At low equivalence ratio, there was

Conclusions 
Chemical mechanisms of NOx and CO formation 
and destruction have been analyzed under lean, 
premixed conditions representative of conditions in 
a gas turbine. New rate constants, reactions, and 
species were identified. 
The Zeldovich mechanism a~parently proceeds 
via the electronically excited O(D) state, not by di- 
rect reaction with ground-state O(3p). Given the 
abundance of N2 during combustion with air, re- 
actions which produce O(1D) will almost certainly 
lead to NO formation. Inclusion of O(ID) chemistry 
in addition to O(3P) reactions may be necessary for 
accurate NOx predictions. 
Premixed gas turbine combustors attempting to 
achieve low NO~. emissions will operate under fuel- 
lean conditions near the blowout limit. Under these 
conditions, the nitrous oxide pathway will be the 
dominant route to NOx formation, and almost all of 
the NOx will form in the flame zone. Thermal NO~ 
becomes increasingly important at higher temper- 
atures. Prompt NOx is relatively unimportant for 
methane and ethane under lean conditions, but it 
becomes the dominant route to NO~ under fuel-rich
