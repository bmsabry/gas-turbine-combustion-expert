GE Aircraft Engines ( GEAE) has introduced several new aeropropulsion gas turbine engines with improved
combustion systems since 1994 including the CFM56 dual annular combustor ( DAC), the CF6-80 low-emissions
single annular combustor ( LEG), the GE90 DACII, in addition to the dry low emissions (OLE) for the LM1600,
LM2500 and the LM6000. GEAE has also made significant progress in the development and application of the
semi-analytical mechanistic (SAM) models and anchored CCD during the combustion design and development
process as described in this paper. GEAE is working with NASA in the development and validation of advanced
turbulence and radiation models as part of the National Combustor Code (NCC). These activities have benefited the
GEAE combustion design process which now uses routinely both the SAM and CCD-based methods unlike the most
conventional "cut-and-try" design practice.
I Introduction
GE Aircraft Engines (GEAE) has recently introduced several new
turbo-propulsions combustion products including the Low
Emissions Combustor (LEG) for the CF6-80C, unstaged (rich-
cup) Dual Annular Combustor (DAC) for the GE90 by further
improving the LEG 
Single Annular Combustion (SAC)
technology, and staged lean DAC for the CFM56-5B and -7B.
Over the last several years, lower emissions have become one of
the key characteristics of gas turbine engines for power generation
and other industrial needs. A 'lean premixed' approach has been
used in the development of the Dry Low Emissions (DLE)
technology used in the LM1600 (aero-derivative engines used for
Land and Marines applications), the LM2500 and the LM6000
engines. The LM6000 and the LM2500 combustors employ a
triple dome design with staging of fuel and air flows to achieve
lean-premixed operation from light-off to full power. Because of
the higher bleed capabilities of the LM1600 engine, we are able
to meet all the design requirements of this engine model with a
two-dome DLE combustion system. The DLE engine produces low
level of NOx/ CO/UHC emissions over a wide load setting. NOx
emissions goals of 25 PPM have been successfully met at site
rating conditions for the entire family of LM DLE products.
Summary of these low emissions combustors is given in this
paper.
Empirical/analytical combustor design approach has become
GEAE's standard process for advance technology, new product
introduction and current product support activities. Significant

The unburned and/or partially oxidized hydrocarbons emissions is
strongly influenced by fuel nozzle spray quality (droplet size and
velocity distribution, spatial and temporal concentration); fuel/air
preparation; primary 
zone characteristics (ignition, flame
stabilization and propagation; mean residence time, equivalence
ratio and completion of combustion processes; the spatial and
temporal variations in fuel/air ratio, temperature and velocity);
post-flame and CO oxidation characteristics of intermediate
combustion zone; interaction of the cooler film air (surrounding
the dome and liner walls) with the core combustion zone. The
combustion development experience during the last 20 years has
shown us that there is a considerable level of tradeoffs required to
meet low NOx/CO/HC/smoke emissions 
without adversely
impacting other design requirements including durability,
operability, combustor exit temperature quality, and pressure
drop. In order to achieve the most optimum low emissions
combustor, it has been a common practice to plot NOx versus HC
emissions indices or preferably LTO NOx versus LTO HC.

Several semi-analytical approaches have evolved over the last
25 years for correlating and predicting gaseous emissions.
Mellor10 gives an outstanding review of the early work in this
area. In a more recent work, Rizk and Mongia12 have used a
reactor net-work(RNS) of perfectly stirred reactors (PSR), for

combustors. One of the biggest disadvantages of RNS
approach is that the modeled reactor volumes for the
combustion zones of interest (e.g., primary, secondary and
dilution zones) are significantly smaller than the corresponding
physical volumes due primarily to the fact that these models
essentially capture only the kinetics effects under the most
ideal conditions.
Pratt13, recognizing the deficiencies of the RNS modeling,
proposed a gas turbine combustion model that took into
account several critical processes and its refinements
continued especially for jet-stirred reactors (e.g., Tonouchi and
Pratt14). Tonouchi, Held and Mongia15 have extended this
approach to low-emissions rich-lean combustors. This SAM
model takes into consideration micro-macro mixing, inlet non-
uniformities and uses a more recently developed skeletal
kinetic scheme for heptane by Held, Marchese and Dryer16.
Held, Mongia and Tonouchi17 have recently extended SAM to
essentially premix flames of dry low emissions combustors of
Joshi et al.6.
A typical target for the accuracy goals for the empirical
correlations has been close to that of the engine and/or rig data
scatter, albeit the range of applicability of the correlation has
been quite restrictive; this might even be reduced to the data
interpolation. On the other hand, most of the published works
on the semi-empirical and/or semi-analytical correlation have
been limited to feasibility demonstration. Consequently, the
accuracy and reliability of these correlations have not been
good enough for providing significant advantage over the use
of the most popular empirical correlations used for refining
design modifications during combustion development process.
Therefore, the designers have relied mostly on the use of
empirical correlations for design, and on the semi-
empirical/analytical correlations for getting the qualitative
guidance.
The prime objective of the recent activities of Mongia and his
coworkers (e.g., References 8, 15 and 17) is to improve the
accuracy of SAM consistent with the target values listed in
Table 2 so that SAM can be used effectively during the
conceptual, preliminary and detail combustion system design
phases, in addition to conducting a real-time test Data
Reduction and Analysis (DRA), and reliable and timely
response to field problems consistent with customers'
expectations.

VI Status of Semi-Analytical Mechanistic Models
A summary of the semi-analytical mechanistic modeling
activities of References 15 and 17 is given in the following
paragraphs.
Tonouchi et al.15 model a rich-dome gas turbine combustor by
two reactors in series; the first is a partially-stirred reactor
(PaSR) simulating the primary zone, and the second is a
"partially-stirred" plug flow reactor (PFR) representing the
dilution zone. The Gas Particle Monte Carlo (GPMC)
simulation was initially based upon the micro/macro-mixing
reactor model of Tonouchi and Pratt14, and Tonouch37. Fluid
flow and combustion are treated as the interaction of gaseous
fluid particles which are "born" as they enter the combustor,
"age" as they reside in the combustor, and "die" as they exit.
The GPMC model accounts for many of the major processes in
a gas turbine combustor, such as imperfect premixedness of
the fuel and air inlet stream, finite-rate macro-mixing (the
gross convective recirculation which brings the thermally hot
fluid particles into proximity with the unignited cold fluid
particles), and finite-rate micro-mixing (the molecular
diffusion among particles that allows the hot particles to ignite
the cold particles through the diffusion of heat and mass). The
model also simulates other features in the combustor such as
finite-rate mixing of dilution air jets and cooling film air.
The GPMC model uses a two-reactor approach to account for
the fundamentally different flowfields within the combustor.
The "primary zone reactor" is used to model the highly
recirculating flow in the primary zone. The secondary reactor
models the approximately plug flow of the gases as they exit
the primary zone and pass through the intermediate and
secondary zones. The potential of the GPMC model was
demonstrated by calculating emissions from the three certified
propulsion engine combustors.
Figure 10 presents the calculated and measured CO and NOX
emissions from one of the three combustors, identified as
Combustor 1. The modeled results are the particle ensemble
mean properties exiting the dilution flow reactor. The data in

VIII Future Direction
Having established Generation 1 SAM and anchored CCD
methodology, we have recently initiated several activities to
bench mark and/or improve:
a) CFD capability of the design code
b) Baseline turbulent combustion models based on eddy-
breakup (EDC) and scalar transport PDF
c) Turbulence models relevant to gas turbine combustion
d) Radiation model
e) Spray model
f) 
Laminar flamelet modeling with reduced and skeletal
mechanisms
g) Large eddy simulation (LES).
The following paragraphs give brief description of our
activities in each of these eight areas.
An Advanced Combustion Code (ACC) is currently being
developed to further advance the use of Computational
Combustion Dynamics (CCD) at GE. ACC will have complex
geometry representation capability via unstructured multiblock
grids, and will comprise of a number of advanced
combustion/spray/turbulence models that are being developed
in a modularized fashion. The ACC solver is based on a fully
implicit pressure correction algorithm with colocated grids. An
overview of an early version of ACC is given by Tolpadi et
al.41. This code will allow a more accurate representation of
staged 
combustion systems including 
DLE as shown in
Figures 18 and 19.
Since the first use of CFD in combustion design 18'19, we have
felt strongly for the need to improving turbulence models for
gas turbine combustion. In spite of concerted effort by several
turbulence modeling groups over the last 25 years, our
predictive accuracy for turbulent combustion field has not
improved. Even after having expended a considerable effort on
carefully measuring and modeling isolated subcomponent
flowfields relevant to gas turbine combustors, we have not
achieved any significant improvement in the turbulence
modeling accuracy, e.g., see References 42 and 43. Even for
the reacting flows where the turbulence model is expected to
do a reasonably good job, we have not been to achieve
accuracy target levels (Table 2) desired by the designers. This
point is illustrated by the following test case.

IX. Summary
Several low-emission technology combustors, including the
CFM56-5/7B dual annular combustors, the CF6-80C2 low-
emissions single annular combustor, and the GE90 unstaged
dual 
annular 
combustor (for 
turbopropulsion 
engine
applications), the dry-low emissions staged multi-dome
annular combustors for aero-derivative industrial engines (the
LM1600, LM2500 and LM6000) have recently been
introduced by GEAE. These products have significantly
reduced the gaseous emissions as summarized in this paper. In
addition, significant progress has been made in the
development of semi-analytical mechanistic models and the
CCD-based anchoring technique to help combustion designer
with their tasks of designing and servicing the products to meet
customers' needs.
