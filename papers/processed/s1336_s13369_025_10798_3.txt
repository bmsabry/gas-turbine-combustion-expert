Impact of Combustion Chamber Innovations on Jet Engine
Development: A Comprehensive Review

Abstract
The paper reviews the signiﬁcant inﬂuence of combustion chamber technologies on jet engine design, with a focus on
innovations such as annular combustors, rich-burn, quick-quench, lean-burn (RQL) combustors, and pulse and rotating
detonation combustors (PDCs and RDCs). It provides an overview of their principles, beneﬁts, and operational challenges.
The study also highlights advanced cooling techniques, including effusion and ﬁlm cooling, as well as thermal barrier coatings
that minimize thermal and mechanical stress, thereby enhancing durability and reliability. Innovations in fuel injection
systems are examined for their precision and ability to maintain combustion stability at high altitudes. A comparison of these
combustion chamber technologies, including annular, RQL, and detonation-based combustors, reveals how they enhance jet
engine performance, reduce emissions (e.g., NOx, CO, unburned hydrocarbons, and soot, depending on the technology), and
decrease fuel consumption. Key challenges include managing thermal gradients and ensuring performance during transient
operations. Annular combustors enhance efﬁciency with a compact design suitable for both commercial and military aviation,
while RQL combustors excel in NOx emission control through precise air–fuel mixture management. Despite obstacles
in stabilizing detonation waves and ensuring turbine compatibility, these technologies present promising opportunities for
substantial reduction in fuel consumption and emissions. Although newly developed cooling techniques effectively address
thermal issues, ongoing research is essential for further advancements. Future efforts are expected to focus on hybrid combustor
designs that leverage the strengths of various technologies, integrating sustainable aviation fuels (SAFs) and hydrogen while
utilizing artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) for optimization. This review offers valuable insights into the evolution of jet engine
combustion chambers, highlighting pathways for enhanced efﬁciency and reduced emissions.

1 Introduction
Historically, gas turbines have played a vital role in generat-
ing electricity [1, 2] and powering aerial systems. Aviation
gas turbineengines, knownas jet engines [3], operateaircraft,
drones, and helicopters. Jet engines are classiﬁed as turbojet,
turbofan, turboprop, and turboshaft. Signiﬁcant advance-
ments in jet engines occurred after World War II [4, 5]. The
use of jet engines in civil aviation makes their performance
crucial. Thus, it is necessary to assess the performance of air-
craft propulsion systems not only in light of ecological and
environmental factors but also in light of fuel consumption
and power considerations [6].
Due to their relatively simple design, minimal frontal area,
and rapid exhaust speed, jet engines are used in medium-
range cruise missiles [7, 8]. The primary components of a jet
engine are the diffuser (which typically slows down the inlet
air speed [9, 10]), the compressor (which increases the air
pressure to a high level before the combustion process), the
combustor (in which combustion occurs and chemical energy
is transferred to thermal energy to increase the temperature to
a high level), the turbine (which feeds the fan and compressor
with the needed power), and the nozzle (which maximizes
the ﬂow speed to generate thrust [11]).
The combustion chamber is the central part of the engine,
where chemical energy is converted into useful thermal
energy [12]. Thus, it is crucial to understand the chemi-
cal reactions involved in combustion. Kerosene, a blend of
hydrocarbons, is the most commonly used aviation fuel. It
is delivered through injectors to the combustion chamber,
where it mixes with air to generate a burning mixture. A
consistent temperature distribution at the combustion cham-
ber exit should be ensured. During incomplete combustion,
hazardous components such as sulfur oxides, nitrogen oxides
(NOx), and unburned hydrocarbons (UHCs) are undesirably
releasedintotheatmosphere.SinceNOxisespeciallydanger-
ous and contributes to ozone layer destruction, most efforts
are being made to lower its levels and enhance the efﬁciency
of turbojets [13].
Gas jet engines have used many technologies to enhance
efﬁciency, reduce emissions, and increase durability. Cat-
alytic combustors [14], rich-burn, quick-quench, lean-burn
(RQL) combustors [15], tubular and annular combustors [16,
17], advanced cooling techniques [18], lean-burn combus-
tors [19], thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) [20], advanced
fuel injection [21], high-temperature materials [22], and
detonation-based combustion [23, 24] are the most com-
monly employed technologies in combustion chambers.
Annular combustors are currently essential for modern jet
engines because of their efﬁcient fuel–air mixing and reliable
temperature distribution. The efﬁciency of RQL combustors
in reducing nitrogen oxide emissions is well established,
but catalytic combustors provide signiﬁcant potential for
further emission reductions in CO, UHCs, and NOx. High-
temperature materials and advanced cooling techniques have
enabled higher operating temperatures, thereby improving
engine performance.

However, catalytic and tubular combustors have not been
extensively used due to temperature limitations in catalytic

inside the combustion chamber. Other studies, such as that by

applied to prevent critical parts from thermal damage while

ment of the air–fuel ratio, which directly impacts combustion
efﬁciency and reduction in NOx, CO, and UHC emissions.
Due to stricter environmental rules on emissions, RQL
combustion engines have drawn more attention. Research
on the mechanics of RQL combustion has been conducted
by Feitelberg et al. [29], wherein a rich mixture (fuel-rich)
is employed to boost fuel combustion ﬁrst, followed by a
quenching step to reduce NOx emissions. Recent research
hasexpandedontheseﬁndingsbyintegratingadvancedinjec-

[30]. Under various operating conditions, RQL combustors
are being optimized not only to reduce NOx emissions but
also to maintain high combustion efﬁciency. It has become
clear that RQL combustors offer a balance between reduc-
ing pollutants and maintaining engine performance as the
technology matures.
Research has also been conducted on TBCs to improve
component longevity and reliability by protecting combus-
tor components from high temperatures. Hoque [31] applied
zirconia-based TBCs to turbine blades, showing enhanced
heat resistance and engine longevity. Several recent stud-
ies, including that of Solntsev et al. [32], have investigated
ceramic composite coatings and high-temperature-resistant
materials, showing that advanced TBCs can operate at higher
temperatures. Studies have shown that TBCs reduce thermal
fatigue and permit high-temperature materials that improve
combustion efﬁciency, leading to lower emissions of CO and
UHCs due to complete combustion, while indirectly reducing
CO2 emissions per unit thrust through improved efﬁciency
at elevated operating temperatures.
Developing advanced fuel injection systems has been
key to improving combustion efﬁciency and reducing emis-
sions in modern jet engines. Pitcher et al. [33] focused on
swirl injectors and their impact on air–fuel mixing, improv-
ing combustion stability and efﬁciency. Erfan et al. [34]
have explored the integration of multi-hole injectors and
high-pressure fuel injection systems, which optimize fuel
atomization and distribution within the combustor. These
advancements have led to improved combustion efﬁciency,
enhanced fuel economy, and reduced emissions of CO and
UHCs through complete combustion, while also contribut-
ing to the reduction of NOx in RQL combustors by enabling
precise air–fuel staging. Researchers have also focused on
optimizing the injector designs for various types of annu-
lar combustors, further demonstrating the importance of fuel
injection systems in achieving low-emission, high-efﬁciency
performance.
Detonation-based combustion technologies have emerged
as a promising alternative to conventional deﬂagrative com-
bustion technology in jet engines. The early research in
pulse detonation combustors (PDCs) was directed toward
the cyclic behavior of detonation waves and their potential to
yield increased thermodynamic efﬁciency [35]. Experiments
demonstrated that the rapid, repeated detonations in PDCs
generate a high-pressure pulse that can lead to increased
thrust and speciﬁc fuel consumption compared to steady
deﬂagration processes. Short and Wang [36] examined the
transient dynamics of detonation waves, highlighting the
challenges associated with pressure oscillations and the need
for precise timing in the preparation of the fuel–air mix-
ture. These investigations paved the way for the formulation
of computational models and experimental conﬁgurations to
record the complex behavior of shock-induced combustion
[37], which will ultimately serve as the foundation for opti-
mizing the performance of PDCs in practical systems.
Another ﬁeld of detonation-based combustion research
is the rotating detonation combustor (RDC). Unlike PDCs,
RDCs rely on a continuous detonation mode, where a sta-
ble detonation wave travels along an annular chamber. The
continuous mode has been shown to provide more homoge-
neous energy release and higher-pressure gains, which are
necessary to obtain both improved efﬁciency and possible
reductions in nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions due to mod-
erated ﬂame temperatures [38]. Studies in this area have
reported mainly the advantages of RDCs in the form of
improved fuel utilization and lower cycle-to-cycle variabil-
ity [23]. Researchers have also noted that the detonation
front in RDCs can help temper peak temperatures, thereby
reducing the formation of thermal NOx, a major pollutant
in conventional combustion systems [39, 40]. The literature
also identiﬁes signiﬁcant engineering challenges, including
the stabilization of detonation waves [41] to resist high ther-
mal loads and integration with existing turbine designs [42].
Recent studies have begun to examine the emissions char-
acteristics of detonation-based combustors more in depth
and observe that both PDCs and RDCs can yield secondary
reduction beneﬁts in CO and UHC emissions through more

engines. Thus, this article discusses the role of combus-

and enhanced performance. Different technologies discussed
in this paper include annular combustors, RQL combus-
tors, thermal barrier coatings, high-temperature materials,
advanced cooling methods, advanced fuel injection, and
detonation-based combustors.

2 Combustion Chamber Technologies
2.1 Annular Combustors
Fuel can be burned in the proper amount within a competent
combustion chamber. The combustor is located between the
compressor and axial turbine in aero and jet engines. The dif-
ference is that the aero engine has size and weight limitations,
making the combustor more compact. In jet engines, annular
combustors are often used. They are divided into single [52],
double/twin [53, 54], triple [55], and can [56] annular types.
Table 1 describes the main features of each type; each has
unique characteristics appropriate for achieving performance
and emissions targets.
Single-annular combustors (Fig. 3a) employ a single,
continuous ring of fuel injectors around the chamber to
ensure uniform combustion and simplify construction and
maintenance. A more challenging design is the double-/twin-
annular combustor (Fig. 3b), which has two inner arrays,
concentric to each other, of premixers or injectors. While
in earlier literature “double”- and “twin”-annular types are
occasionally distinguished, these are, in fact, interpretations
of the same concept; these have two stages in combustion,
the differences between them applying primarily to the tech-
nologies used for fuel injection, premixing, and stabilization
of ﬂames. The double-/twin-annular conﬁguration optimizes
emission performance primarily through optimized staging
over broad power settings, thereby reducing nitrogen oxide
(NOx) formation while enabling steady-state performance
over broad ranges.
More recently, triple-annular combustors (Fig. 3c) have
been designed to meet stringent modern emissions require-
ments. These use three concentric zones of combustion,
enabling very ﬂexible staging from idle to full thrust. The
NOx reduction is highly achieved by the triple-annular type,
with excellent efﬁciency under maximum load and very sta-
ble burning under low power. However, overall combustor
complexity is signiﬁcantly increased. A hybrid solution is
represented by can-annular combustors (Fig. 3d), which are
hybrids between annular systems and cans. Multiple cans
are positioned in annular arrays to ensure uniform fuel–air
mixing, excellent temperature distribution, and very high
efﬁciency, though precise can alignment is needed to give
maximum performance.
The conventional combustor features between 12 and
20 fuel injectors, depending on the engine size, whereas
advanced, larger combustors can incorporate up to 75 pre-
mixers. The combustion chambers are designed aerodynam-
ically,resultinginacompactunitthatminimizespressureloss
comparedtotraditional combustor designs [55, 57]. This type
of chamber offers several advantages, including enhanced
performance and increased combustion volume relative to
the exposed metal surface area, which contributes to reduc-
tions in both length and weight. Annular combustors with
slingers are commonly utilized in small- and medium-sized
jet engines. The advantages of slinger annular combustors
include their compact size, superior fuel atomization, high
combustion efﬁciency, and excellent performance at high
altitudes. One of the key factors affecting a jet engine’s per-
formance is the local output (or turbine inlet) temperature,
deﬁned as the maximum gas temperature at the combustion
exit that has an immediate inﬂuence on the thermal loading
and life of heat-exposed components like the turbine guide
vanes at startup. Conversely, the mean exit temperature is the

In contrast to the original design, Habibi et al. [67]
redesigned a gas turbine’s annular combustion chamber to
reduce wall temperature, minimize pressure loss, and lower
the PF. The area of the annulus in the initial combustion
chamber design was reduced to increase the penetration
depth of the diluting jet, after the computational ﬂuid dynam-
ics (CFD) ﬁndings of the combustion chamber’s geometry
demonstrated that the optimization procedure could not pro-
duce the ideal design. Another run of the simulation was
performed. The latest ﬁndings indicated that, compared to the
previous design, there was a 56.07%, 20.52%, and 4.611%
decrease in the PF, pressure loss, and wall temperature of the
combustion chamber, respectively. These improvements are
clearly demonstrated in the temperature and velocity con-
tours shown in Fig. 5. Khandelwal [69] emphasized that a
good combustion chamber design is fundamentally depen-
dent on the three “Ts” of combustion: turbulence for effective
mixing, reactant temperature, and adequate residence time,
along with maintaining low pressure loss.
Another study [70] employed the Dynamic-Q method to
pursue the optimum design for mixing in the combustion
chamber in order to enhance the quality of the OTP. They
proposed two design suggestions. The initial design had two
dilution hole constraints, and the second design incorporated
two dilution hole constraints, two secondary hole constraints,
and one swirler constraint. The outcomes indicated that the
value of PF was reduced from 0.5 in the ﬁrst design to 0.36.
However, in the second design, the pressure loss limitation
was considered, since the pressure loss was 37% greater than
that of the original conﬁguration. The PF increased to 0.55
in the second design, despite the OTP quality being better
than in the ﬁrst. The schematic of these cases is presented in
Fig. 6.
Similarly, another study [71] focused on reducing the liner
length of the gas turbine engine’s combustion chamber. They
discussed how a shorter liner length decreased the maximum
output temperature but simultaneously introduced the pos-
sibility of turbine blade damage, once again demonstrating
the balance required between thermal protection and aerody-
namic losses.
In addition to geometry-focused modiﬁcations and opti-
mization methods, researchers have studied instabilities and
noise phenomena in annular combustors. The link between
temporal amplitude and the overall rate of heat release [72]
was investigated in a simple annular combustor conﬁgura-
tion using experimental and modal dynamic analysis. In this
study,twelve,ﬁfteen,oreighteenpremixedbluff-bodyﬂames
were evenly distributed around a predetermined perimeter in
the combustion chamber. The numerical detection revealed
two sound waves propagating that were moving in oppos-
ing directions and had time-varying amplitudes, indicating
a statistical prevalence that aligned with earlier instability
results reported in the literature. Similarly, numerical analy-
sis [73] sought to forecast the levels of combustion noise in
an actual turboshaft engine. The results on the noise levels
from direct and indirect combustion showed good agreement
with theoretical forecasts as well as experimental ﬁndings.
These works highlight the importance of considering acous-
tic stability and noise reduction in addition to thermal and
aerodynamic optimization.

The role of advanced computational modeling is also sig-
niﬁcant in annular combustor research. Many authors have
analyzed annular combustor behavior using the large eddy
simulation (LES) technique. Based on an appropriate grid
and simulation duration, LES has been utilized to estimate
instabilities and azimuthal modes [74]. LES has also been
applied to forecast combustor performance under steady
operating conditions [75]. The ﬁndings suggest that LES is
valuable during the design phase, as it reduces risks and the
number of required experimental tests. In parallel, advanced
data-driven approaches have been introduced. The radial
basis function (RBF), a kind of artiﬁcial neural network using
feed-forward (ANN), consists of three layers: input, hidden,
and output [76]. RBF-NN speciﬁes the input layer’s outputs
by determining how far the network’s inputs are from the hid-
denlayer’scenters.Itisasecond-ranklinearhiddenlayer,and
its outputs are weighted samples of the input. Every neuron in
thehiddenlayerhasacenter,whichisaparametervector[77].
In addition, “smart modeling” [66], combining ANN, genetic
algorithms, and CFD techniques, was implemented to opti-
mize the proﬁle of turbine inlet temperature (TIT) inside an
annular combustion chamber of a turbojet engine. The study
introduced some geometric changes to the dilution holes to
achieve a more homogeneous TIT proﬁle. Two optimized
designs were compared with a baseline design (BD). The
best-performing design showed a 44.44% reduction in PF
compared with BD, demonstrating better temperature distri-
bution and reduced thermal stress on turbine blades, while
maintaining high accuracy, with only a 2.87% error between
simulation and experimental results.
Further research has been conducted to understand para-
metric variations and explore novel combustor concepts. A
numerical study [78] calculated the ﬂow and heat transfer
efﬁciency of a large annular combustion chamber in a heavy-
duty gas turbine, focusing on how premixed gas temperature,
the fuel–air ratio, and the inlet swirl strength affected the tem-
perature, pressure, and velocity ﬁelds. The results showed
that these parameters had a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on combus-
tion efﬁciency and heat transfer. Another study [79] designed
and computationally analyzed an annular combustion cham-
ber for a turbojet engine using kerosene fuel to generate 1650
N of thrust. This investigation combined RANS-based CFD
with experimental validation, using turbulence and combus-
tion models to assess aerodynamics, air distribution, and
pressure drop. OTP homogeneity at the chamber exit was
conﬁrmed to be critical for performance. The complexity of
combustion chamber design was further highlighted by [80],
who employed three-dimensional simulations using methane
(C H4) as fuel. Their work studied temperature distribution,
species concentration, and velocity proﬁles, using the SST
turbulence model with second-order discretization to resolve
chemical and physical interactions. Finally, a novel combus-
tion chamber with a double-stage counter-rotating swirler
and ﬁxed vane angles of 45º [81] was analyzed. Using RANS-
based CFD and 2D-PIV testing, this study showed that ﬂow
patterns and turbulence structures are strongly affected by
Reynolds number, with a critical value around 21,698 inﬂu-
encing boundary layer dynamics and turbulent mixing.
In summary, the reviewed literature highlights that annu-
lar combustor optimization is inherently multidisciplinary.
Geometry strongly inﬂuences PF, OTP uniformity, and wall
temperature, but these beneﬁts are often offset by increased
pressure loss. At the same time, acoustic instabilities and
combustion noise must be controlled to ensure durabil-
ity. Advances in CFD, LES, and AI-based optimization
now provide powerful tools to address these trade-offs.
Overall, the research underscores that effective combustor
design requires a balance between aerodynamic, thermal, and
acoustic considerations, supported by modern numerical and
data-driven approaches.
2.1.1 Annular Combustors’ Operating Principle
The design creates an annular space where combustion
occurs by combining an inner and outer casing. Entering
through several ports, the air is separated into two ﬂows:
primary and secondary. For combustion, the primary ﬂow
combines with the fuel, and the secondary ﬂow cools the
combustor walls and keeps the ﬂame stable (see Fig. 7).
Fuel is atomized by nozzles in an annular combustor, which
produces a tiny spray that facilitates quick and thorough
burning. The design promotes high turbulence, enhancing
mixing and ensuring the ﬂame remains steady under var-
ious operating conditions [69]. This structure enables more
consistent temperature distribution and reduced emissions of
CO and UHCs than alternative designs, including can-type
combustors [60]. Furthermore, smaller weights and more
compact engine conﬁgurations are made possible by the
annular design, which is essential for the demands of modern
aircraft [17]. The compressor’s inlet temperature and pres-
sure are essential operating conditions affecting combustion
dynamics and ﬂame stability.
To maintain desired temperatures, the airﬂow entering
the combustor is divided into primary, secondary, and dilu-
tion zones [12, 82]. Typically, about 20–30% of the airﬂow
is supplied to the primary zone, where combustion begins
under locally fuel-rich conditions (equivalence ratio greater
than 1.0) to ensure stable ﬂame anchoring. The remain-
ing 70–80% of the airﬂow is directed to the secondary and
dilution zones, where it mixes with combustion products to
complete the burning process and reduce the overall equiva-
lence ratio to lean values [69]. This staged distribution helps
minimize emissions by suppressing excessive NOx forma-
tion and reducing UHCs. The combustor must also provide a
uniform exit temperature proﬁle to limit thermal stresses on

2.1.2 Beneﬁts and Drawbacks of Annular Combustors
In turbojet engines, annular combustors provide signiﬁ-
cant advantages [83, 84], such as compactness and a lower
requirement for cooling air because of their smaller sur-
face area. This improves combustion efﬁciency, enabling a
lighter and smaller design that increases the engine’s thrust-
to-weight ratio. Their ring-shaped geometry promotes a more
uniformﬂameandamoreeventemperaturedistributionat the
combustor exit, which reduces turbine blade thermal stress,
minimizes hot spots, and ultimately lowers emissions, as out-
lined in Table 2. However, as noted in Table 2, there is less
surface area available for heat dissipation due to the compact
form, and cooling issues may arise, necessitating more intri-
cate and expensive cooling methods. Furthermore, compared
to other combustor designs, maintaining combustion stabil-
ity in a range of operational conditions, such as variations in
speed or altitude, can be more difﬁcult, which could have an
effect on performance and reliability in some circumstances
[83, 85, 86].
2.2 Rich-Burn, Quick-Quench, Lean-Burn (RQL)
Combustors
Before describing the RQL concept, it is important to brieﬂy
introduce the lean premixed prevaporized (LPP) combustor,
illustrated schematically in Fig. 8, since it is often used as a
reference technology in low-emission combustion research.
In LPP systems, compressed air from the compressor is
directed into the premixing zone, where fuel is injected
through multiple injectors (primary and secondary stages)
and thoroughly mixed, and in many cases, prevaporized
before entering the combustor. This process creates a uni-
form lean mixture that burns in the primary combustion zone
under lean conditions, as shown in the ﬁgure. The premixed
lean ﬂame leads to lower peak ﬂame temperatures, which in
turn suppresses thermal NOx formation [87, 88]. The com-
bustion products then expand through the turbine to produce
power.
As highlighted in Fig. 8, the primary and secondary injec-
tion stages play a crucial role in stabilizing the ﬂame and
ensuring proper distribution of the fuel–air mixture within
the premix zone. This design achieves very low NOx levels
and has been successfully applied in stationary gas turbines,
where operating conditions are relatively stable, and safety
requirements are less stringent [89, 90]. However, in aero
engine applications characterized by high pressures, high
temperatures, and rapidly changing duty cycles, LPP com-
bustors face critical challenges such as ﬂashback into the
premixing passages, autoignition before entering the main
chamber, and combustion instabilities [91–93]. These draw-
backs limit their adoption in aviation, despite their superior
NOx performance. Against this backdrop, the RQL com-
bustor was developed as a more robust alternative for aero
engines.
The rich-burn, quick-mix, lean-burn (RQL) combustor
concept was introduced as a way to lower NOx emissions
[15], as it is the main focus of the National Aeronautics and
Space Administration (NASA). Technology for Advanced
Low NOx, a product of Pratt & Whitney, currently employs
the RQL as the cornerstone combustor technology in aero
engines [94]. Furthermore, RQL is favored over lean pre-
mixed alternatives in aero engine applications due to safety
concerns and overall effectiveness (e.g., stability) during
the duty cycle. The RQL combustor follows a three-zone
approach. In the rich-burn zone, more fuel than the the-
oretical amount of air–fuel mixture is added to provide
a fuel-rich mixture. This rich mixture lowers peak ﬂame
temperatures and promotes intermediate hydrocarbon forma-
tion to reduce NOx formation. Upon leaving the rich-burn

rapidly introducing additional air. Such air dilution further
reduces NOx emissions by cooling the combustion gases
and lowering their temperature while preventing the com-

gases are mixed with extra air, resulting in a lean combustion
process. This zone ensures that all UHCs are fully oxidized to
enhance overall combustion efﬁciency and reduce particulate
emissions [95, 96].
For RQL combustors, the most signiﬁcant operating con-

turbine entrance locations to maintain proper air distribu-

a rise in temperature while reducing soot production. The
“quench” zone requires quick air injection, which is accom-
plished by slots or jets designed to maximize the mixing
rate. This is usually tracked by regulating the ratios of jet-to-
mainstream momentum ﬂux. In the “lean-burn” zone, lean
mixtures further lower emissions, especially NOx and tem-
perature. Monitoring is also performed to ensure quench air
integration and ﬂame stability using zonal ﬂow rates and
pressure drops [97, 98].
The lean premixed combustion technique is the norm
for stationary applications. Compared to RQL technology,
the duty cycle is more limited, safety concerns are less
severe, and the NOx emission reduction is more signiﬁcant.
Nonetheless, the ability of RQL combustor technology to
[99] process fuels of complex composition more efﬁciently
and [100] process fuels with various compositions is attract-
ing increasing interest in stationary applications. The latter
is growing in importance as a result of heightened competi-
tion for fuels worldwide, growing interest in biomass-based
fuels, expanded use of liqueﬁed natural gas, and “opportunity
fuels” (landﬁll, digester, and well-head gases) to either sup-
ply all or a sizable amount of a nation’s natural gas demand
or to complement domestic sources [101].
Moreover, staged burning is a technique used in the RQL
concept (Fig. 9). By reducing the amount of available oxy-
gen in a fuel-rich zone, combustion begins at equivalence
ratios of 1.2 to 1.8, preventing NOx generation. In a quick-
mix segment, bypass combustion air is injected downstream
at an overall equivalence ratio of 0.5 to 0.7, resulting in lean
combustion. The quick-mix region has a lower geometric
cross-sectional area than the rich-burn zones to improve mix-
ing and prevent backﬂow. The RQL design provides better
stability and eliminates ﬂame ﬂashback, although it is more
complicated than the LPP [102].
The California Energy Commission, in collaboration with
the US Department of Energy, investigated RQL technology
to determine whether RQL techniques could be utilized as
a substitute for combustors in speciﬁc applications involv-
ing stationary electricity generation. RQL is based on the
notion that, as illustrated in Fig. 10, rich mixture ratios are
ideal for the central zone of a gas turbine combustor. First,
a “rich-burn” condition (e.g., ∅= 1.8) in the primary zone
boosts the stability of the combustion event by generating
and maintaining a high concentration of energetic hydrogen
and hydrocarbon radical species. Second, because of the low
number of intermediate species that contain oxygen and the
relatively low temperatures, rich-burn conditions reduce the
formation of nitrogen oxides [94, 103].
Hydrogen, carbon monoxide, and hydrocarbon species
that are partially oxidized and partially pyrolyzed will be
present in signiﬁcant concentrations in the efﬂuent that
emerges from the rich primary zone. Therefore, without

2.2.1 Advantages and Disadvantages of RQL Combustors
In general, the RQL combustor presents both advantages
and disadvantages regarding the application of turbojets,
especially in terms of emissions and efﬁciency. Among the
primary pollutants emitted by jet engines, NOx emissions
have been signiﬁcantly reduced with the RQL combustor.
This is explained by an initial rich combustion stage that lim-
its NOx formation, followed by a quick-quench phase that
inhibits NOx generation by rapidly lowering temperatures.
This is a vital stage for avoiding high-temperature, stoichio-
metric conditions that are favorable for NOx formation. The
ﬁnal stage provides complete combustion and minimizes all
CO and UHC emissions, thereby generally enhancing emis-
sion performance [15, 114, 115].
Despite these beneﬁts, the signiﬁcant drawbacks of RQL
combustors are that they are highly complex in design and
require precise control over fuel–air mixing to ensure stabil-
ity. Although the primary zone is fuel-rich and not prone to
lean blowout, the downstream lean-burn zone can become
susceptible to LBO if the mixing process is not carefully
controlled. Speciﬁcally, poor air distribution or excessive
dilution in the quick-mix section may result in locally lean
pockets with equivalence ratios below the ﬂammability limit,
making it challenging to sustain combustion in the lean-burn
zone under varying operating conditions. Furthermore, the
fast cooling stage may lead to a non-uniform temperature dis-
tribution, which is also detrimental and, again, crucial with
respect to turbine durability and overall engine performance.

2.3.1 Effusion Cooling in Combustion Chamber Design
For combustor liners, effusion cooling (illustrated in
Fig. 14b) has been a widely used method. By applying a
coolant ﬁlm to the hot side, forced convection inside each
hole, as well as convective heat transfer between the cold-side
wall and the coolant, effusion cooling is achieved, protecting
the liner from hot gas and reducing the temperature of the
metal. The primary beneﬁt of effusion cooling for combus-
tor liners is that it uses less air and is highly efﬁcient [118].
The two main measures of effusion cooling effectiveness
are conjugate cooling effectiveness and adiabatic cooling
effectiveness. The combined impact of wall convective heat
transfer, wall thermal conductivity, effusion jets, and coolant
ﬂow on cooling efﬁciency is considered by conjugate cooling
effectiveness. For engineering reference, conjugate cooling
effectiveness provides a more realistic result, although adi-
abatic cooling effectiveness can be used to evaluate ﬁlm
coverage performance at a lower cost [118, 129].
Recent studies have demonstrated that the adiabatic model
canpredict near-wall ﬂowfeatures withnearlythesameaccu-
racy as the conjugate heat transfer model. However, it cannot
predict the actual wall cooling efﬁciency [130]. Numerous
numerical and experimental investigations into the structural
characteristics of the multi-oriﬁce conﬁgurations and aero-
dynamic aspects have been conducted in recent decades to
enhance the performance of effusion cooling systems [128,
130]. The data show that 60° and 90° holes are clearly inferior
to inclined cooling holes, especially those with a 30° inclina-
tion. The cooling advantages of smaller angled inclined holes
are no longer signiﬁcant [128, 131]. Additionally, a study
found that the cooling effect improves with a smaller diame-
ter and a greater number of holes, because the latter reduces
boundary layer mixing [132]. However, due to restrictions in
material strength and production costs, the 30° hole with a
0.5 mm diameter is a key choice in combustion liner cooling.
High freestream turbulence has been shown to promote ﬁlm
coverage and prevent ﬁlm detachment from the wall [133].
Additionally, it has been shown that increasing the blow-
ing ratio enhances cooling performance and the heat transfer
coefﬁcient [122, 134].
2.3.2 Film Cooling Methods
Film cooling (Fig. 14a) is a crucial cooling method employed
to ensure the safe and effective operation of combustion
chambers in liquid-fuel propulsion systems. One of the most
practical and efﬁcient cooling techniques is the application
of ﬁlm cooling in a liquid-fuel propulsion system [135]. It
is widely used to shield metal surfaces from extreme heat.
Through tiny, carefully positioned pores or slots in the com-
bustion chamber’s inner wall, a thin layer of cool air, or
“ﬁlm,” is injected. The metal is shielded from direct expo-
sure to hot combustion gases by the cool air that circulates
along its surface. Film cooling is essential for preserving the
structural integrity of combustion chamber components, as it
effectively reduces surface temperatures and thermal stress.
This approach demonstrates notable efﬁcacy in settings
exposed to extreme heat, with the cooling layer functioning
both as insulation and as a means of managing temperature
variations. The ﬂexibility of ﬁlm cooling offers a notable ben-
eﬁt, enabling engineers to reﬁne hole sizes, arrangements,
and distances for different areas, thus improving efﬁciency
while maintaining a balance between weight and complexity.
Modern high-performance engines require this technology to
extend component life span, boost fuel efﬁciency, and enable
higher operating temperatures, all of which are crucial for
achieving maximum power output and durability in chal-
lenging environments [125, 133, 136].
Figure 14 presents the methodologies of ﬁlm and effusion
cooling. In ﬁlm cooling, coolant is introduced through spe-
ciﬁc openings, forming a slender protective layer over the
surface to shield it from the high-temperature gas ﬂow. The
system provides localized cooling while utilizing minimal
coolant; nevertheless, its performance is greatly affected by
factors such as injection angle and blowing ratio. An exces-
sively high blowing ratio can result in coolant jet liftoff,
thereby reducing its effectiveness. In contrast, effusion cool-
ing, or transpiration cooling, utilizes multiple closely spaced
holes to create a more uniform and continuous cooling layer,
leading to improved cooling efﬁciency and better surface
temperature regulation. Film cooling is often used for tur-
bine blades and nozzle guide vanes where targeted cooling is
crucial, while effusion cooling is preferred for combustor
liners due to its effectiveness in maintaining lower sur-
facetemperaturesandminimizingthermalstresses.Although
effusioncoolingprovidesbetteroverallcoolingperformance,
it requires more complex manufacturing processes due to the
large number of small holes.

2.3.4 Thermal Barrier Coatings for Enhanced Durability
Combustion chambers are part of the hot gas stream. High
temperatures can occur on the combustion chamber wall.
Low-cycle fatigue is a signiﬁcant failure mode because the
materials experience sudden temperature ﬂuctuations when
they start and stop. The nature of the combustion process can
also result in large steady-state thermal stresses. A design
must be both ﬂexible and resistant to thermal fatigue. High-
frequency vibration produced by the combustion process
itself may lead to high-cycle fatigue (HCF) failure. Because
the combustor can and liner have comparatively thin walls,
oxidation is also a signiﬁcant failure mode [141, 142].
There is slightly less pressure inside the combustor than
outside. This pressure differential, where the temperature is
highest, is sufﬁcient for buckling and creep rupture, as the
walls are thin. Nimonic 263.1, RA333, and Hastelloy-X are
examples of nickel-based alloys that have been utilized in the
production of the combustor for GE engines. Thermal bar-
rier coatings are commonly used to optimize the beneﬁts of
outside cooling air, thereby decreasing thermal gradients and
maintaining metal temperatures within creep limits. Thermal
barrier coatings have been applied to combustion chamber
liners to increase the longevity and efﬁciency of engines run-
ning at temperatures higher than their melting points for the
past 20 years [143, 144].
To enhance thermal resistance and protect substrates from
extreme heat and oxidative environments, thermal barrier
coatings (TBCs) play a crucial role as an essential tech-
nology in gas turbine engine combustion chambers and
turbine components. When utilized on the surfaces of com-
bustion chamber components, these coatings, typically made
of ceramic materials such as zirconia, form a protective
layer that signiﬁcantly reduces heat transfer to the under-
lying metal structures. By protecting the substrate from
the high-temperature gases produced during combustion,
thermal barrier coatings allow the engine to operate at ele-
vated temperatures, enhancing performance and efﬁciency
while also prolonging the life span of components [145,
146]. The implementation of TBCs provides engineers with
enhanced design ﬂexibility, enabling the optimization of
engine parameters to boost efﬁciency while maintaining
structural integrity. Furthermore, advancements in TBC tech-
nology, including the development of graded coatings and
the implementation of bond coats, have enhanced durability
and adhesion while simultaneously reducing the likelihood
of wear and spallation in operational environments. Thermal
barrier coatings play a vital role in the ongoing efforts to
improve the efﬁciency and longevity of combustion cham-
bers in modern gas turbine engines. Moreover, TBC systems
function in the most demanding, high-temperature environ-
ments found in industrial gas turbine engines and airplanes,
and they have a highly complicated structure [138, 141, 146].
The increasing demand for higher-temperature operation
in jet engine combustors has led to the exploration of high-
entropy alloys (HEAs) as a potential material for substrates
and bond coats in thermal barrier coating (TBC) systems due
to the thermal, mechanical, and microstructural characteris-
tics of HEAs. Unlike conventional nickel-based superalloys,
HEAs [147] are considered good alternatives due to their
increasedmechanicalstrength,high-temperaturethermalsta-
bility, and oxidation resistance. HEAs enhance bond coat
performance by improving thermally induced oxide con-
tinuity, density, and homogeneity, thereby increasing hot
corrosion resistance. HEAs can control dispersion at the bond
coat–substrate interface at high temperatures. In addition,
their thermal gradient resistance makes them suitable for
ultra-high-temperature future combustors. However, chal-
lenges such as high processing costs, complex processing
techniques, and limited large-scale usage remain obstacles
to widespread application. Recent advancements in additive
manufacturing [148] are being explored to make the integra-
tion of HEAs within TBC systems feasible, with enhanced
thermal degradation and oxidation protection for extended
periods.

Premix/prevaporizing injectors, air-blast, vaporizing, and

2.5 Detonation-Based Combustion
Gas turbines and internal combustion engines rely on the
deﬂagration of fuel/air mixtures to turn chemical energy
into usable work. Deﬂagration is often a slow-moving pro-
cess that requires turbulence to speed up the reactions,
resulting in a compact combustor. Iterative design has signif-
icantly increased system efﬁciency and dependability over
time. However, with the growing emphasis on emissions,
further advances in efﬁciency are required. For that rea-
son, detonation-based combustion is considered a feasible
alternative. In these systems, a shock wave compresses the
fuel/air mixture, accelerating chemical reactions faster than
diffusion-controlled processes. The energy release maintains
the shock wave. Because detonation waves (DWs) travel at
123


--- Page 24 ---

Arabian Journal for Science and Engineering
twice the speed of deﬂagration, novel designs for small com-
bustion devices may be possible. Furthermore, the pressure
gain caused by detonation increases the amount of work that
can be extracted from the combustion process [168–170].
Pressure gain devices have been under development for
almost 60 years [171]. Recent research has concentrated
on pulse detonation combustors [172] and rotating detona-
tion combustors [173]. The fundamental advantage of using
RDEs over PDEs is the near-uniform force supplied by
continually propagating DWs within a detonation chamber.
Purging and reﬁlling the detonation chamber in PDEs limit
the frequency of thrust generation due to mechanical con-
straints. RDEs can cause high-frequency thrust changes due
to shock structures [174]. Rankin et al. [175] found that using
exit nozzles can further reduce variance. Additionally, DW
propagation enables new ﬂow pathways, thereby expanding
design possibilities.
The operating principle of the PDC is based on detona-
tive combustion (Fig. 18), where a fuel–oxidizer mixture is
combusted using a supersonic detonation wave. The PDC
is detonative in nature, unlike conventional combustion sys-
tems, which are subsonic, deﬂagration-based systems [176].
The process in a PDC can be explained in four steps: ﬁll-
ing, initiation, propagation, and blowdown and purging. The
cycle begins with the ﬁlling of the detonation tube with
a premixed fuel–oxidizer mixture. The mixing is carefully
controlled to achieve the desired equivalence ratio, thereby
ensuring the optimum condition for detonation. The ﬁlling
process is comparatively slow, as it involves the physical
movement of gases into the tube. The tube typically con-
tains valves or aerodynamic features to control the inﬂow
and prevent backﬂow. After the tube is charged, a high-
energy ignition source, such as a spark plug or predetonator,
is subsequently used to start combustion. The combustion is
initially a subsonic ﬂame, or deﬂagration. Detonation is not
possible until the ﬂame expands to a supersonic shock wave.
This transition is facilitated by obstacles such as turbulence-
generating devices, which accelerate the ﬂame front and
induce the formation of a detonation wave. This phase is
crucial, as engine efﬁciency relies on a fast and reliable
deﬂagration-to-detonation transition. The detonation wave
subsequently propagates supersonically down the tube, com-
pressing and igniting the fuel–oxidizer mixture. This is a
high-pressure and high-temperature process, during which
energy is released very quickly. The high-pressure gases
behind the detonation wave expand and produce thrust as
they are expelled out of the tube. Once the detonation wave
has exited the tube, expansion waves or rarefaction waves are
created at the closed end of the tube. The waves reduce pres-
sure within the tube so that the combustion products can be
expelled from the open end. The tube is subsequently cleared
of residual gases, usually with puriﬁed air or inert gas, for
the next cycle. This phase is comparable to the ﬁlling phase
in terms of time consumption, as both entail the movement
of gases into or out of the tube. The entire cycle is repeated
at high frequencies to simulate continuous thrust. The PDC
is a highly efﬁcient propulsion system owing to its cyclic
nature of operation, especially for high-speed applications
[176–180].
As shown in Fig. 19, a rotating detonation combustor
(RDC) is also based on the principle of detonative combus-
tion, but with a different geometry and operation. In place
of a pulsed detonation wave, an RDC utilizes a steady-state
rotating detonation wave propagating circumferentially in an
annular combustion chamber. The operating principles of the
PDE are simpler: the long tube contains gaseous fuel (hydro-
gen) and air. When a mixture forms, an electrical spark starts
the combustion process. The mixture’s composition and tube
dimensions should facilitate a quick detonation process. Dur-
ing detonative combustion of the mixture, very high pressure
and thrust are generated. After detonation, the high-pressure
combustion products exit the tube and expand, causing the
pressure inside to decrease below ambient pressure. This ini-
tiatestheﬂowofairintothetubeandmixesitwiththeinjected
fuel. After creating the mixture, ignition is restarted, and a
new cycle begins [182]. Details about such a cycle can be
found in [183, 184]. The ﬁrst pulsed detonation engine was
built and successfully tested at the University of Michigan
[185], but there was no interest in developing such a propul-
sion system at the time; thus, PDE research was abandoned
and later restarted near the end of the twentieth century. PDEs
were created in laboratories across the USA, Russia, and
China. In the early twenty-ﬁrst century, a team from the US
Air Force Research Laboratory built and tested an experi-
mental aircraft driven by PDEs [182].
Both RDEs and PDEs offer signiﬁcant advantages over
conventional propulsion systems. Detonative combustion is
basedontheHumphreycycle,whichismoreefﬁcientthanthe
Brayton cycle used in conventional gas turbines [187, 188].
The quasi-constant volume combustion process in PDEs and
RDEs leads to improved thermal efﬁciency and work out-
put. PDEs and RDEs also eliminate heavy and complicated
components, such as compressors and turbines. Compres-
sion through shock waves in detonation engines simpliﬁes
thedesignandreducesweight[189,190].Detonationengines
are best suited for high-speed applications such as supersonic
and hypersonic ﬂight because of their high thrust-to-weight
ratio and high energy release rate [186, 191]. RDEs and PDEs
can both be scaled from small unmanned aerial vehicles to
large aerospace propulsion systems. They can be operated
using a wide variety of fuels, such as hydrocarbons and
hydrogen [192, 193]. RDEs also beneﬁt from continuous
detonation without cyclic ﬁlling and purging, thus providing
smoother operation and greater thrust than PDEs. Detona-
tion engines can also be combined with conventional gas

out acoustic waves and enabling efﬁcient simulations with
RANS or LES. These approaches are sufﬁcient to capture
swirl stabilization, dilution air mixing, pollutant forma-
tion, and thermoacoustic instabilities. In contrast, PDCs and
RDCs operate in regimes where detonation shocks and com-
pressibility effects dominate, requiring the full compressible
Navier–Stokes equations with shock-capturing schemes. For
PDCs, this includes modeling deﬂagration-to-detonation
transition (DDT) and cyclic blowdown, while RDCs demand
resolution of azimuthal detonation stability and inlet/nozzle
coupling. Similarly, advanced injection systems for non-
conventional fuels impose specialized modeling needs: SAFs
require fuel property-sensitive spray and soot models, while
hydrogen demands fast kinetics schemes and ﬂashback/NOx
prediction [198, 199].

3.1.1 Inﬂow Boundary Conditions
Annular and RQL combustors typically operate with sub-
sonic premixed or partially premixed inﬂows. In simulations,
these conditions are often imposed through ﬁxed mass ﬂow
or velocity inlets at the fuel–air injectors, with prescribed
turbulence intensity, temperature, and species mass fractions
matched to experimental or design values [69, 201]. For
instance, one NASA RQL combustor study employed a mass
ﬂow inlet of 0.3914 kg/s at 394 K, along with speciﬁed tur-
bulence levels, to reproduce test rig conditions [201]. RQL
combustors usually employ multiple feed stages, including
a primary rich-burn zone fuel–air inlet and secondary air
injections (quick-quench jets) for rapid mixing. These are
modeled using multiple inﬂow boundaries; for example, one
study used characteristic-based mass ﬂow inlets at both the
primary swirler and the quench air holes, ensuring each
supplied the prescribed ﬂow while allowing realistic pres-
sure feedback [201]. To reduce computational cost, periodic
boundaries are often applied in annular and RQL sector sim-
ulations, enabling representation of the full 360° combustor
with a smaller modeled sector [202].
By contrast, PDC and RDC simulations involve choked or
time-dependent inﬂow conditions. In a PDC, the inlet may
be closed (wall) during detonation and then reopened during
the reﬁll stage; modeling this process can require moving or
time-varying boundary conditions, such as switching from a
wall to a velocity or pressure inlet to emulate valve opera-
tion [205]. In RDCs, injectors typically deliver choked fuel
and air streams. Simulations commonly prescribe either total
pressure inlets or constant mass ﬂow inlets for each pro-
pellant stream [203]. Recent high-ﬁdelity RDC studies have
shown that constant mass ﬂow inlets yield more accurate
mean pressure distributions than total pressure inlets, since

they preserve the correct mass ﬂux despite back-pressure
oscillations induced by the rotating detonation wave [203].
In all cases, inﬂow boundaries should be formulated to
permit limited outﬂow when local pressure rises, thereby pre-

based inlet conditions are frequently employed for this
purpose, using Riemann invariants to separate incoming
and outgoing waves and to allow pressure disturbances to
transmit upstream without artiﬁcial reﬂection [207]. Such
treatments are particularly important in combustors prone
to pressure oscillations, including RQL combustors during
high-altitude relight and RDCs operating under unsteady det-
onation conditions [202].
3.1.2 Outﬂow Boundary Conditions
Downstream (outlet) boundary conditions govern how com-
bustion products and pressure waves exit the combustor
domain. In conventional annular and RQL combustors, the
ﬂow at the combustor–turbine interface is subsonic; there-
fore, aﬁxed-static-pressureoutlet is commonlyapplied, set to
the design back-pressure or to match experimental conditions
[69, 201]. For instance, simulations of an annular combustor
at takeoff imposed an outlet static pressure of approximately
3.313 MPa to reproduce the downstream turbine pressure
[69]. Such pressure outlet conditions are adequate for steady
or low-amplitude oscillatory cases, but they may artiﬁcially
reﬂect acoustic waves when strong pressure ﬂuctuations
reach the outlet. To mitigate this, RQL combustors that
exhibit combustion instabilities are sometimes modeled with
extended computational domains and non-reﬂecting bound-
ary conditions at the outlet. For example, one LES study of
an RQL combustor added a 30% extended outlet section with
a relaxation zone to absorb oscillations [202], ensuring that
pressure pulsations and convective disturbances dissipated
before reaching the numerical boundary.

detonation simulation, a large external domain was mod-

to damp pressure oscillations and avoid directly imposing a
ﬁxed-pressure boundary at the outlet [206]. If the combustor
exhaust is supersonic, the outlet can instead be treated as a
supersonic outﬂow, where no pressure condition is applied
and ﬂow variables are extrapolated, since information only
travels downstream under supersonic conditions [209].
In summary, deﬂagration combustors are typically mod-
eled with static pressure outlets (occasionally supplemented
by non-reﬂective adjustments), whereas detonation-based
combustors require carefully tuned non-reﬂecting or absorb-
ing boundaries to capture the physics of shock expansion and
avoid numerical instabilities [203, 205].
3.1.3 Wall Boundary Conditions
All combustor simulations enforce a no-slip velocity condi-
tion at solid walls (e.g., combustor liners, injector surfaces),
meaning the gas velocity is zero at the wall in the wall-ﬁxed
frame. This condition is essential for resolving boundary
layer development, wall-bounded heat transfer, and fuel ﬁlm
dynamics. Thermal boundary conditions at the walls, how-
ever, vary according to combustor design and the desired
modeling ﬁdelity. A common simpliﬁcation is the adiabatic
wall assumption (zero heat ﬂux), which treats the walls as
perfectly insulated. For example, this approach was adopted
in the NASA RQL combustor simulation, where all walls
were modeled as no-slip and adiabatic [201]. While adiabatic
walls simplify the solution process, they tend to overpredict
gas temperatures because real engines lose heat through the
liner. At the other extreme, a ﬁxed wall temperature (isother-
mal wall) may be imposed if liner temperatures are known
from measurements. Many studies assume a uniform wall
temperature, such as 700 K in model combustor tests, to
approximate the effect of cooling [200].
In practice, modern combustors employ thermal barrier
coatings (TBCs) and active cooling systems (ﬁlm or effusion
cooling), producing strong temperature gradients across the
liner. To capture these effects, some simulations use conju-
gate heat transfer or coupled CFD–ﬁnite element analyses, in
which wall temperatures are solved rather than prescribed.
For example, one study coupled a CFD combustor model
with a ﬁnite element model of a TBC-coated liner, exchang-
ing heat ﬂux and temperature data at the interface. The results
showed that the TBC induced wall temperature “swing”
effects, with surface temperatures reaching ~ 1200 K, while
reducing the heat ﬂux into the underlying metal [210]. In sim-
pler CFD-only approaches, TBC effects can be approximated
by combining an adiabatic gas-side wall with a speciﬁed out-
ward heat ﬂux. A recent combustor liner study, for instance,
assumed the inner liner surface was adiabatic and applied a
constant heat ﬂux of 10 kW/m2 on the outer surface to repre-
sent cooling; the resulting wall temperature distribution was
then used for stress analysis [211].
Many annular and RQL combustors also incorporate ﬁlm
cooling and dilution holes in the liner. In CFD, these are
implemented as additional mass ﬂow inlets on the wall sur-
faces, representing small angled jets of cool air. The NASA

chosen wall model directly inﬂuences ﬂame quenching and

the combustor’s hardware conﬁguration. For example, if the
liner is ﬁlm-cooled, the simulation should explicitly include
those coolant ﬂows rather than assume an adiabatic wall.
3.1.4 Radiation Boundary Conditions
The high temperatures in gas turbine combustors mean that
radiative heat transfer can contribute signiﬁcantly to the over-
all thermal balance, particularly in fuel-rich ﬂames with soot
formation or in high-pressure, long-residence systems. Radi-
ation effects arise from two sources: (i) gas-phase radiation
within the ﬂow and (ii) radiation exchange between gases
and walls. Many CFD solvers include simpliﬁed radiative
heat transfer models, such as the gray gas model, to compute
volumetric emission and absorption. At boundaries, radia-
tion is accounted for by prescribing wall emissivity and an
external radiative environment (e.g., an ambient temperature
for radiative heat loss). For instance, atmospheric combustor
simulations often assume an external ambient of 300 K with
a liner wall emissivity of ~ 0.8, allowing the solver to com-
pute wall radiative ﬂuxes according to the Stefan–Boltzmann
law.
An illustrative example is provided by an LES study that
incorporated a P-1 radiation model and applied a “radiative
convection” boundary condition at the combustor walls. In
this setup, walls exchanged radiation with the environment
while convective heat transfer was disabled, enabling isola-
tion of radiative effects [204]. The results demonstrated that
radiative cooling inﬂuenced the predicted combustion insta-
bilities, underscoring the importance of including radiation
when analyzing ﬂame-acoustic interactions.
In practice, annular and RQLcombustors operating at high
pressurewithhydrocarbonfuelsexhibitnon-negligibleradia-
tive losses—often a few percent of the total heat release.
Including radiation in such simulations improves wall tem-
perature predictions and better captures thermal loads [200].
In contrast, PDCs and RDCs usually neglect radiation due
to the short timescales and frequent use of hydrogen or lean
mixtures, which produce minimal soot. However, for long-
duration operation or hydrocarbon-fueled RDCs, radiation
may become important for accurate prediction of liner ther-
mal loads.
When radiation is excluded, modelers sometimes com-
pensate by adjusting thermal wall boundary conditions—for
example, by specifying a lower ﬁxed wall temperature to
approximate the effect of radiative cooling. If radiation is
included, the boundary condition setup must deﬁne the rele-
vant wall emissivities and an external sink temperature (often
treated as a black body ambient or as the cooling air temper-
ature) to ensure a consistent energy balance.
3.1.5 Implications for Solution Stability, Realism,
and Validation
The choice of boundary conditions has a critical impact on
both the stability of CFD solutions and their physical ﬁdelity.
Non-physical reﬂections at boundaries can generate artiﬁ-
cial pressure oscillations or sustain modes that would not
occur, leading to convergence difﬁculties and inaccurate pre-
dictions of combustion instabilities [207]. For this reason,
non-reﬂecting boundary treatments are widely employed
in PDC and RDC simulations, and are also recommended
for acoustically sensitive RQL conﬁgurations. Inﬂow and
outﬂow boundaries further inﬂuence mass conservation: A
well-posed combustor model should ensure that the time-
averaged mass ﬂow exiting the domain equals the mass
entering it. Any imbalance may indicate numerical leakage
or spurious reﬂections. Characteristic-based boundary con-
ditions help to maintain this balance by dynamically adapting
to the internal ﬂow state [207].
From a physical accuracy perspective, boundary condi-
tions must represent the actual test or engine environment.
Oversimpliﬁed inlet proﬁles—for example, uniform veloc-
ity and turbulence distributions—can misplace recirculation
zones, affecting ﬂame anchoring and mixing. Similarly, spec-
ifying unrealistically low wall temperatures can exaggerate
ﬂame quenching and the formation of pollutants. To avoid
such discrepancies, experimental measurements (e.g., turbu-
lence intensity, wall temperature, or ﬁlm cooling ﬂow rates)
are often used to set or validate boundary conditions [201].
For instance, in the NASA RQL combustor benchmark study,
boundary conditions were explicitly matched to rig condi-
tions, including ﬁlm cooling ﬂows and outlet pressure, to
ensure meaningful comparison with experimental data [201].
Validation of combustor CFD typically involves assessing
whether predicted outputs such as exit temperature proﬁles,
liner heat loads, pressure drops, and emission indices agree
with measurements. Mismatched or overly simpliﬁed bound-
ary conditions are frequently the source of discrepancies.
Therefore, researchers often conduct sensitivity analyses—-
suchascomparingadiabaticversuscooledwalltreatments,or
varying outlet pressure conditions—to quantify uncertainties
and improve conﬁdence in the results. In summary, boundary

4 Comparative Analysis of Combustion
Chamber Technologies
4.1 Emissions
Recent advancements in combustion chamber technologies
have led to a notable decrease in emissions, especially
concerning NOx, CO, and UHCs [212]. Annular com-
bustors effectively minimize CO and UHC emissions by
promoting complete combustion through uniform fuel–air
mixing, while their ability to maintain a consistent tempera-
ture distribution throughout the combustion zone also helps
inhibit localized hot spots that generate thermal NOx [213,
214]. RQL combustors demonstrate superior performance in
reducing NOx emissions due to their innovative staged com-
bustion process. The rich-burn zone effectively minimizes
NOx formation by sustaining sub-stoichiometric conditions.
Meanwhile, the quick-quench zone swiftly lowers tempera-
tures, which restricts additional NOx production. Finally, the
lean-burn zone guarantees complete combustion, contribut-
ing to further reductions in CO and UHC emissions [94, 96].
The mechanisms of effusion and ﬁlm cooling [215, 216]
are essential for minimizing emissions by ensuring a sta-
ble combustion environment and preventing irregularities
that may lead to incomplete combustion or heightened
NOx production. TBCs enable higher operating tempera-
tures while protecting structural materials, aiding in cleaner
combustion, and reducing both NOx and particulate matter
emissions. Moreover, advanced fuel injection technologies,
including air-blast and pressure-atomizing systems, ensure
precise atomization and uniform fuel–air mixing, signiﬁ-
cantly reducing UHCs and particulate emissions by mini-
mizing incomplete combustion [217, 218].
Due to immediate and complete combustion, PDCs
reduce UHCs and CO. Their short high-temperature duration
reduces thermal NOx emissions compared to conventional
systems. Pressure spikes in detonation can cause transient
NOx spikes if fuel–air mixing is not ideal [35, 36], whereas
RDCs reduce NOx by avoiding steady high temperatures,
as the rotating detonation wave spreads heat evenly. They
minimize UHC and CO through continuous combustion.
Hydrogen-based RDCs have near-zero emissions, although
instability in hydrocarbon-based systems can produce local-
ized pollution [38–40].
4.2 Reliability
The dependability of turbojet combustion chambers is crucial
because of the signiﬁcant thermal and mechanical stress they
experience during operation [219]. Annular combustors sig-
niﬁcantly minimize mechanical stress and thermal gradients,
which enhance the durability of the combustion chamber
and downstream components like turbine blades [86]. On
the other hand, RQL combustors feature a more complex
design; nonetheless, their durability is enhanced by the use
of advanced materials and careful oversight of combustion
processes, which reduce the likelihood of thermal fatigue and
mechanical failure, even under varying operating conditions
[116, 220].
Effusion and ﬁlm cooling mechanisms play a crucial role
in protecting combustor walls from extreme temperatures
and preventing thermal deformation and fatigue. Effusion
cooling offers reliable thermal insulation, enhancing over-
all dependability [221, 222]. Additionally, the regenerative
cooling system [223] utilizes fuel as a coolant to prevent
overheating of combustion chamber walls, thus improving
the durability of components exposed to high thermal loads.
Furthermore, thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) [124] protect
metallic components from oxidation and thermal fatigue,
thereby maintaining operational reliability over extended
periods. Sophisticated fuel injection systems ensure reliable
performance in demanding conditions, signiﬁcantly reduc-
ing cold start problems and the risks associated with lean
blowout [224].
PDCs encounter reliability issues because of their cyclic
mode of operation. Successive detonation waves produce
high-frequency pressure oscillations, leading to mechani-
cal fatigue in combustor walls and downstream turbines
[35, 36]. Thermal gradients resulting from rapid heating
and cooling cycles also degrade materials such as nickel-
based alloys over time [18]. Intermittent combustion also
threatens ﬂame extinction during transient operations (e.g.,
altitude changes), requiring durable ignition systems [172].
Incomplete fuel–air mixing during ﬁlling phases can create
localized hot spots, accelerating wear [178]. While RDCs
improve reliability through continuous detonation, they
reduce cyclic stresses. However, sustaining stable detona-
tionwavesremainschallenging.Waveinstabilitytriggeredby
uneven fuel–air mixtures or acoustic disturbances can cause
combustion oscillations, eroding liners and turbine blades
[38, 41]. Transient pressure peaks caused by wave impact
increase the risk of mechanical failure [40], and high heat ﬂux
demandsadvancedcooling(e.g.,effusionorregenerativesys-
tems) to prevent liner burnout [118, 134]. Hydrogen-fueled
RDCs possess enhanced resistance, whereas hydrocarbons
present risks of injector blockage and carbon deposition [39].

4.3 Combustion Enhancement
Improving the combustion process is a common objec-
tive in various technologies, emphasizing the enhancement
of efﬁciency, stability, and energy output. Annular com-
bustors, characterized by their signiﬁcant turbulence and
effective fuel–air mixing, play a crucial role in attaining
stable and complete combustion. This is fundamental for
optimizing thrust and maintaining consistent operation [225,
226]. However, RQL combustors [227] use a staged combus-
tion approach to stabilize the ﬂame under various operating
conditions, ensuring thorough energy extraction and reduc-
ing blowout risks.
Effusion and ﬁlm cooling mechanisms play a crucial role
in stabilizing the thermal environment within the combus-
tor, enabling consistent and efﬁcient combustion even under
extreme conditions [121, 228]. Furthermore, regenerative
cooling [229] enhances atomization and combustion by pre-
heating the fuel, thereby improving overall energy extraction.
Thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) enable higher operating
temperatures, improve the efﬁciency of the Brayton cycle,
and strengthen combustion optimization [230]. Moreover,
sophisticated fuel injection systems [231] ensure precise and
reliable fuel delivery, promoting optimal fuel–air mixing
and achieving effective and stable combustion across a wide
range of operating conditions.
PDCs enhance combustion through shock-induced turbu-
lence, which improves fuel–air mixing and reduces ignition
delays. The rapid pressure rise rate in detonation compresses
the reactants to generate near-stoichiometric combustion and
minimize UHCs [35, 172]. PDCs also employ deﬂagration-
to-detonation transition (DDT) techniques, such as obstacle
tube arrays, to stabilize detonation waves and maximize com-
bustion completeness [36, 177]. However, cyclic operation
limits residence time, and fuel injection timing must be opti-
mized to maintain efﬁciency at various altitudes [172, 178].
RDCs,ontheotherhand,achievebettercombustionenhance-
ment by propagating detonation waves in a sustained manner
with high-pressure gain. The spinning wave generates high-
intensity shear layers, which enhance turbulent mixing and
ensure near-complete burnout of fuel [38, 39]. Experiments
have demonstrated that RDCs offer better combustion efﬁ-
ciency than traditional systems due to the high-speed energy
release [38]. Hydrogen-fueled RDCs enhance combustion
by eliminating soot and reducing ignition delays, and staged
fuel injection helps reduce NOx formation in hydrocarbon
systems [39, 40].
4.4 Fuel Consumption
Minimizing fuel consumption is essential for enhancing
cost efﬁciency and promoting environmental sustainability.
Annular combustors [17] enhance fuel economy by optimiz-
ing fuel–air mixing and reducing fuel wastage. Conversely,
RQL combustors [232] facilitate effective energy extraction
during the lean-burn phase, leading to decreased fuel con-
sumption without compromising performance. Effusion and
ﬁlm cooling [233] also enhance fuel efﬁciency by stabilizing
combustion conditions and preventing heat loss. Regener-
ative cooling [123] preheats the fuel before combustion,
reducing the energy required for vaporization and improv-
ing fuel economy. Thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) [230]
boost the thermodynamic efﬁciency of the engine, resulting
in lower speciﬁc fuel consumption. Additionally, advanced
fuel injection technology ensures optimal droplet size and
distribution, thereby minimizing fuel wastage and enhanc-
ing overall efﬁciency [234].
PDCs achieve lower speciﬁc fuel consumption compared
to traditional combustors. This is mainly due to pressure
gain combustion, which minimizes compressor work and
increases thermodynamic efﬁciency [35, 172]. The rapid
detonation process helps ensure that fuel burns almost com-
pletely, resulting in less fuel being wasted as UHCs [35,
178]. During ﬁlling phases, cyclic operation and intermit-
tent fuel injection can create pockets of fuel, which increase
transient fuel consumption [36, 172]. On the other hand,
RDCs perform better in terms of fuel efﬁciency, boasting
lower speciﬁc fuel consumption during steady operation.
The continuous detonation wave compresses the incoming
air, which means there is less need for upstream compres-
sors, and it allows for ultra-lean combustion with minimal
fuel waste [38, 39]. Additionally, hydrogen-fueled RDCs
can achieve nearly zero UHC emissions. (Table 8) However,
hydrocarbon-fueled systems must be carefully controlled
to maintain the correct stoichiometric balance, as fuel-rich
zones can lead to increased consumption [40].

improving combustion stability. Nonetheless, their capa-

thermal gradients and providing sufﬁcient cooling within
their compact conﬁgurations. While these combustors effec-
tively lower NOx emissions, their lack of dynamic adapt-
ability to transient operating conditions reveals a signiﬁcant
limitation in their suitability for environments with high vari-
ability. Moreover, ensuring structural integrity when exposed
to elevated temperatures and stress remains a challenge,
underscoring the need for innovative materials and cooling
techniques [235, 236]. RQL combustors demonstrate excep-

PDCs and RDCs are innovative, emerging combustion
technologies; however, their practical implementation is hin-
dered by signiﬁcant challenges. Stabilization of detonation
waves is one of the key challenges. In the case of PDCs,
a stable deﬂagration-to-detonation transition (DDT) must
be controlled with high precision regarding fuel–air mix-
ing and ignition timing because inhomogeneous mixtures
or turbulent ﬂow can deteriorate wave formation and lead to
incomplete combustion or cycle failure [35, 36]. RDCs, when
operating in continuous detonation mode, struggle to main-
tain rotating waves stably under varying conditions, such as
altitude changes or changing loads, which may lead to wave
extinction or unsteady propagation [38, 41]. Both systems
also suffer from extreme thermal and mechanical loading.

Another critical challenge across combustor technologies
is dynamic (acoustic) instability. In annular combustors, the
continuous geometry promotes resonance of longitudinal,
tangential, and toroidal acoustic modes, allowing pressure
oscillations to couple around the circumference and gener-
ate high-amplitude azimuthal waves [74, 243, 244]. Such
thermoacoustic instabilities can induce severe pressure oscil-
lations, leading to injector vibration, liner fatigue, and even
hardware failure if left uncontrolled. RQL combustors are
comparatively more robust due to their rich primary zone,
which enhances ﬂame stability, but oscillations may still
arise in the quick-quench and lean zones if unsteady heat
release couples with chamber acoustics [91, 94]. PDCs inher-
entlygeneratestrongcyclicdetonativepressurespikes,which
can resonate with downstream plenums or turbines, produc-

phasing or plenum damping [35, 245, 246]. RDCs face chal-
lenges such as mode switching between one- and two-wave
operation and thermoacoustic coupling with inlet or exhaust
conditions, which can destabilize the detonation wave and

Fuel injection systems play a vital role in regulat-
ing combustion processes and reducing emissions as the
aviation industry moves toward sustainable aviation fuels
(SAFs) and hydrogen. SAFs present an immediate oppor-
tunity for reducing carbon emissions, although variations in
viscosity, volatility, and atomization characteristics neces-
sitate continued development of multi-hole injectors and
high-pressure spray atomization methods. Hydrogen, a zero-
carbon fuel, also presents challenges in the form of high
ﬂame speed, autoignition behavior, and increased NOx
formation, necessitating the development of new direct
injection combustion systems, staged lean-burn techniques,
and hybrid cooling systems to enable effective control
over combustion dynamics. For SAFs, injector hardware
should be recalibrated for differences in viscosity, volatil-
ity, density, and surface tension; practical measures include
raising rail/injection pressure, using reﬁned multi-hole or
air-assist/air-blast tips, and controlling spray cone angle to
maintain target Sauter Mean Diameter (SMD) values that
suppress CO, UHC, and nvPM. Materials, seals, and elas-
tomers should be veriﬁed for chemical compatibility to
prevent swelling/leaching, anti-coking tip geometries should
be adopted, and mild fuel preheating may be used to stabilize
atomization in cold conditions. For hydrogen, fuel injec-
tion systems should transition to leak-tight gaseous/direct
injection hardware with ﬂashback-resistant premixers (short
residence time, high-swirl or micro-mixing arrays), fast
response metering valves, ﬂame arrestors/backﬂow pre-
vention, hydrogen embrittlement-resistant materials, and
enhanced liner/injector cooling; control strategies should
emphasize very lean staging and rapid mixing to limit ther-
mal NOx while avoiding lean blowout (LBO). In both cases,
model-basedcontrolswithfuelpropertysensingshouldadapt
injection pressure, timing, staging, and fuel–air ratio in real
time to preserve stability and emissions.
As part of future studies, machine learning- and artiﬁcial
intelligence-based control mechanisms should be utilized to
develop adaptive fuel injection systems that can adjust spray
patterns, fuel–air ratios, and injector pressure in real time,
based on an in-depth understanding of fuel properties and
operating conditions.
As aviation continues to move toward fuel ﬂexibil-

annular, and rich-burn quick-quench lean-burn (RQL) fea-
tures can achieve optimal performance across various con-
ditions by leveraging the strengths of each system. Modular
approaches that allow for reconﬁguration to suit particular
missions or fuel types can enhance ﬂexibility and scalability.

This paper reviewed the evolution of combustors in jet
engines and the technologies used to enhance combustor
and engine performance. Innovative technologies, includ-
ing advanced cooling techniques, high-temperature-resistant
materials, and thermal barrier coatings (TBCs), enable com-
bustion chambers to withstand extreme conditions. The

development of rich-burn quick-quench lean-burn (RQL)
and annular combustors has led to strategies that enhance
fuel–air mixing and stabilize combustion processes. These
improvements are crucial for meeting stringent environmen-
tal standards by reducing harmful emissions, such as nitrogen
oxides (NOx). Additionally, advanced fuel injection sys-
tems substantially enhance combustion performance through
better mixing. As the aviation industry shifts toward fuel
ﬂexibility, hybrid combustor designs that incorporate var-
ious system strengths are becoming increasingly relevant.
Modular approaches allow reconﬁguration for speciﬁc mis-
sions or fuel types, enhancing both ﬂexibility and scalability.
Ongoing research into multilayer and graded TBCs is cru-
cial for improving thermal insulation and engine durability.
Reducing emissions of NOx, CO, and particulate matter
remains a central objective, requiring continuous reﬁnement
of staged combustion methods. Detonation-based combus-
tion is promising for future burners. Hybrid power systems,
whichintegratesomeofthesetechnologieswithconventional
gas turbines, offer exciting opportunities for efﬁcient and sta-
ble combustion with lower emissions. Ultimately, the future
of combustion chamber technology relies on innovation and
sustainable solutions. Collaborative efforts across research
and industry are essential for enhancing engine performance
while ensuring environmental responsibility. Simulation and
artiﬁcial intelligence (AI) may play a pivotal role in optimiz-
ing combustion chamber design.
