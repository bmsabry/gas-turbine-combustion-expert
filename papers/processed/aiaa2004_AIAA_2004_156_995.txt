ABSTRACT 
 
• 
Empirical/analytical design tools 
A status summary report is given on the 
development of comprehensive computational combustor 
design (CCD) tool activities of GEAE combustion group 
conducted for the last three years. A “no-fudge factor” pre-
test prediction approach is being pursued for the 
combustors that use rich or lean-dome swirl cups (Single 
Annular and Dual Annular combustors, SAC, DAC); Twin 
Annular Premixing Swirler, TAPS (SAC and DAC), and 
Dry Low Emissions’ DACRS mixers for triple annular 
combustor domes. The pretest prediction capability is 
being gauged with existing data including emissions, exit 
temperature quality and wall temperatures from nine 
different combustors selected from a group of production 
and technology combustors. The agreement between 
predictions and data is good considering this is the 1st 
phase of the “no-fudge-factor” modeling approach. An 
integrated 
diffusion 
system/flame 
tube 
calculation 
approach is recommended for the swirl cup combustors. 
However for combustion systems with little dilution air, 
we can perform flame tube calculations with inlet 
conditions provided from through-the-inlets (e.g., vanes of 
the mixer) calculations. 4 to 10 million adaptive node 
calculations are envisioned for the both types of 
combustors before one could achieve a “grid independent” 
solution.  
• 
Hybrid modeling for analyzing current combustors 
• 
Anchored design tools. 
 
Table 1 and Figure 1 give a partial list of the gas 
turbine combustors that have used variants of empirical/ 
analytical design methodology proposed in Reference 1. 
The biggest deficiency of this methodology was that it 
provided qualitative guidance only. Due to many obvious 
reasons, including the number of grids one could afford, 
the highly simplified combustion models, and lack of 
benchmark quality data to propose and validate advanced 
models, Rizk and Mongia proposed a hybrid modeling 
approach. With a set of empirical constants, this approach 
gave an excellent agreement with 10 different combustors 
in regard to pattern factor (PF), smoke, lean blow-out 
fuel/air ratio, unburned hydrocarbons, CO and NOx 
emissions indices as shown typically in Figure 2.  This 
clearly demonstrates potential for application of this 
modeling approach for designing new combustors. To-date 
there has not been reported however any application of the 
hybrid modeling for designing combustors. 
The anchored design methodology7 has addressed 
many of the limitations of the empirical/analytical design 
methodology. It does a reasonable job of simulating the 
complex combustor geometry with affordable number of 
nodes sometime exceeding one million. The state-of-the-
art combustion models are employed including reasonable 
assumptions about the critical boundary conditions. 
Special emphasis has been placed on modeling the swirl 
cup and validating the analytical results with good quality 
data. The “same process and procedures” are used for 
different combustors so as to validate predictions with 
measured data on NOx, HC and CO emissions, and 
combustor exit profile and pattern factor, shown typically 
in Figure 3 for the selected five combustors. The anchored 
design methodology is used in all GEAE New combustion 
Product Introduction (NPI) programs; some of these are 
shown in Figure 4. 
We are working closely with the advanced 
diagnostics teams of UC, UD/AFRL, NASA GRC and 
Sandia; and LES modelers from Georgia Tech, Penn State, 
Sandia and NRL to further improve pretest prediction 
capabilities for gas turbine combustor design. 
  
INTRODUCTION 
 
Gas turbine combustion modeling for supporting 
design effort was initiated in the early 1970’s,that resulted 
in the first published successful demonstration of the use 
of the Computational Combustion Dynamics (CCD) tools 
in the combustor design process e.g., Mongia et al1-4. 
Several status and overview articles by Mongia and his 
coworkers have since appeared to share the continuing 
improvements in the combustion design tools and 
increased reliance on CCD for designing combustors; e.g. 
the most recent References 5 and 6. As noted in these 
publications, the design tools have progressively moved 
through the following three stages of improvements: 
The anchored design methodology7 has worked as 
well as originally intended. Its major deficiency continues 
to be its reliance on engine, rig and sub-component data 
for anchoring, and consequently its obvious limitation for 
achieving the desired pre-test prediction reliability. Its 
limitation became quite obvious during the design and 
development effort of the GE90 and CFM TAPS8. TAPS 
combustion system aero design and development process 
relied extensively on systematic use of rig (single module,

“No-Fudge Factor” Design Tools 
Therefore, the recent effort of the GEAE combustion 
design tools team is focused on developing the next-
generation combustion design tools for achieving the 
following objective: 
Develop a “pre-test prediction capability” that is as 
good as running the engine; which means model’s 
accuracy and reliability are equivalent to engine data for 
accuracy and σ levels.  
Specifically, this means achieving: 
σTO NOxEI < 0.69 or 2.4% of take-off NOxEI; Idle σHCEI < 
1.25, σCOEI < 4.4; take-off and climb-out smoke number’s 
σ < 1.35. Here σ denotes the root mean square error 
between model prediction and engine data. The subscripts 
identify the particular variable. Specifically σTO NOxEI < 
0.69 or 2.4%; means that the take-off NOx emissions index 
(NOxEI) needs to be predicted within σ of 0.69 EI or 2.4% 
of the takeoff NOxEI. Similarly, idle σHCEI < 1.25, σCOEI < 
4.4 imply the σ values for idle HCEI and COEI should not 
exceed 1.25 and 4.4, respectively. The σ levels for 
liner/dome temperature, lean blowout (LBO) fuel-air ratio 
(FAR), combustor exit temperature profile and pattern 
factor (PF) should be 25OF, 0.0005, 0.01 and 0.03, 
respectively.  
These highly aggressive goals for accuracy and 
pre-test predictive reliability (without supporting rig and 
engine testing) can be achieved by assuming that accurate 
combustion modeling methodology can be developed 
specific to particular types (classes) of gas turbine 
combustors. For example, this means that the GEAE 
combustion design tools team needs to develop models 
specifically for the following 5 types of combustors:

and industrial engines 
(b) Lean-dome swirl-cup combustors 
(c) Trapped Vortex Combustors (TVC)

Grid Generation 
There are many ways of creating grids for 
combustor applications. The two techniques for a typical 
combustor, Figure 4, tried by Hsiao and Vij19 are explained 
briefly in the following paragraphs. 
 
 
 
The first approach uses the hexahedral grid 
generation process used by ICEM which is based on the 
principle that a geometry can be approximated by cutting a 
large brick into several small ones so that all surface 
details are represented by external faces of the collection 
of smaller bricks.  The set of all bricks put together is 
called a multi block topology.  The edges and faces of each 
block in the topology must be projected manually to the 
corresponding faces and edges in the geometry.  Next each 
edge must be assigned an edge count to generate the grid. 
The grid quality is measured by the determinant, which 
represents the closeness of the cell shape to a cuboid.  The 
determinant of a perfect cuboidal cell would be 1.  
 
The following strategy was used for creating grids 
for a one-nozzle sector  of a typical combustion system: 
1. Create a blocking for a single jet and a single vane of 
the swirl cup. 
2. Copy and merge the jets and vanes at the multi block 
topology level. 
3. Create unstructured grids for the jets and vanes. 
4. Create a separate blocking for the swirl cup region 
surrounding the jets and vanes. 
5. Create an unstructured grid from step 4 and merge 
with grids from step 3.  This gives the full swirl cup 
grid. 
6. Create the fuel nozzle stem grid by growing o-grid 
blocks by extrusion from the diffuser swirl cup 
interface. 
7. Create a separate blocking file for the rest of the 
diffuser dome passage excluding the fuel nozzle. 
8. Merge the blocking from steps 7 and 8.  Create a full 
diffuser grid from the merged blocks.   
9. Merge the unstructured grids from steps 5 and 9 to 
obtain the diffuser + swirl cup grid; e.g., 2.2 million 
cells shown in Fig. 11c. 
10. Create a separate blocking for the combustor and 
create an unstructured grid for the combustor typically 
about one million cells as presented in Figure 11d. 
11. Merge the unstructured grids from steps 9 and 10 to 
get the combined combustor + diffuser + swirl cup 
grid. 
 
The diffuser + swirl cup grid was created in about 640 
hr. and the combustor grid in about 200 hr.  Given 
familiarity with the geometry and technique, a similar grid 
creation for future applications would would take 
considerably less effort to create.  This estimate does not 
include time taken for aero-volume creation and geometry 
cleanup. The total grid size for the combustion system 
shown in Figures 11 is ~3.2 million cells.  The determinant 
was > 0.1 and improved to > 0.3 after smoothing in ICEM.  
This grid covered volume within 0.03% and surface area 
within 0.7% for all tagged surfaces, compared to the actual 
geometry. 
 
The 2nd grid generation approach used Gambit of 
FLUENT. A tetrahedral mesh of 2.4 million cells was

included 120 hours for aero-volume creation including

Combustion models 
 
 
The 1st phase of the turbulent combustion 
modeling, which started in 1997, focused on the flames 
relevant to nearly premix DACRS flames. The most recent 
laminar flamelet modeling results are summarized by Held 
etal20 The laminar flamelet modeling effort driven by data 
covered a broad range of engine operation that included 
close to flameout conditions where one encounters a 
sudden increase in unburned hydrocarbons and CO. As 
shown in Figures 15, the predicted contours of CO and NO 
are significantly different at low and high flame 
temperatures of 1750 and 1900K, respectively. The 
qualitative comparison between predictions and data for 
NOx, CO and unburned HC is quite good as shown in 
Figure 15. 
 
The DLE combustion systems modeling effort 
continues including the significant challenges because:  
(a) The flames are operating slightly above LBO fuel-air 
ratios;  
(b) The measured flowfield has turbulent flow structure 
which is more suitably modeled by LES;  
(c) The sensitivity to the inlet conditions is more than the 
conventional diffusion flame combustors.  
 
 Held etal20 started their calculations immediately 
downstream of the mixer exit plane with profiles estimated 
from a limited database. Recent study by Giridharan22 for 
the DACRS mixer through the vanes calculations show  
that of the various turbulence models studied (RLZ, STD, 
RNG and RSM are calculations using the realizable k-ε, 
the standard k-ε, Renormalized k-ε, and the Reynolds 
stress models coded in FLUENT9), the realizable k-ε 
model gave the best agreement with data. For the swirl cup

Held etal20 laminar flamelet model combined with 
through the inlets (including vanes, purge holes and the

Several approaches are currently being pursued 
for improving the use of the laminar flamelet modeling to

fully premixed on the other side. In addition, work 
continues for improving reduced-kinetic and skeletal

Our results from these studies will be coming out in future 
publications. 
 
 
Reacting Spray Combustion models  
 
Most of the prior gas turbine combustor modeling 
activities1-7 has made arbitrary assumptions about the 
quality of spray. Several investigators have proposed 
comprehensive injector modeling approaches typical to

Large Eddy Simulation 
There are many obvious reasons for using Large 
Eddy Simulation, LES for gas turbine combustors29-33. We 
have been working closely with several research groups 
involved in LES. For example, we follow closely Menon’s 
work29 since it focuses on dynamical structure and the 
relating modeling issues that impact gas turbine 
combustion processes for gas turbine combustor designers. 
It relies on RANS solution to provide inlet boundary 
conditions, and will adopt complex geometry simulation 
technology from our experience in gas turbine combustor 
RANS calculations. We have been working closely with 
Grinstein and his coworkers30,31 since their emphasis is on 
the specification of the inlet conditions for LES. We are 
working closely with Professor Yang because the main 
thrust of his LES work32 is characterization of combustion 
dynamics of gas turbine mixers that invariably have 
complex geometrical features. Finally the details of the 
near-wall regions need to be properly simulated with LES 
along with spray, which we rely on the research activities 
of Oefelein and Schefer33.   
These joint activities between GEAE, Georgia 
Tech, Penn Stat, NRL and Sandia have put the proper 
emphasis on addressing both fundamental and design 
driven applied research of particular interest to gas turbine 
combustor design community34-36. Specifically it means 
resolution of complex geometry, proper specification of 
the inlet/boundary conditions, NOx, CO and HC 
prediction. As reported in these papers, there is back-to-
back comparison between RANS and LES for 
• 
Swirl cup nonreacting flow field 
• 
DLE reacting flow. 
As mentioned by Shih and Liu37, LES work needs to 
compliment RANS calculations. We find LES modeling 
effort as typically represented by Menon, Yang, Oefellein 
and their colleagues35-37 bringing us closer to applying 
LES techniques to designing next-generation gas turbine 
combustors for improving performance, operability and/or 
emissions. 
Summary 
 
A status summary is given in this paper of the last three 
years activities of GEAE combustion group for further 
improving the reliability and accuracy of predictive CCD 
tools for gas turbine combustor design. The prime 
objective of the on-going effort is: 
Develop a “pre-test prediction capability” that is as 
good as running the engine; which means model’s 
accuracy and reliability are equivalent to engine data 
for accuracy and σ levels.
