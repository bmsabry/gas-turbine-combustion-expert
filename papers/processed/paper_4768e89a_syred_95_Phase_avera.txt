Phase Averaging of the Precessing Vortex Core in a Swirl 
Burner under Piloted and Premixed Combustion Conditions 
D. FROUD, T. O’DOHERTY, and N. SYRED* 
Cardiff School of Engineering, P.O. Box 917, The Parade, CardiffCF2 
1X4, U.K. 
The flow patterns produced in and past the exhaust of a lOO-KW swirl burner have been investigated 
experimentally 
under piloted premixed combustion conditions. The well-known three-dimensional 
time- 
dependent instability called the precessing vortex core (PVC) dominates the flow and mixing patterns. The 
PVC and its associated cycle time were used to trigger a three-component 
laser anemometry 
system. 
Successive cycles were overlaid and phase averaged to give a three-dimensional 
picture of the rotating flow 
fields. Measurements 
were obtained over successive slices of the flow, extending to X/De 
= 2.5 past the 
burner exit. A description of the flow was thus obtained in terms of phase averaged tangential, axial and 
radial velocities in tangential/radial 
and axial/radial 
planes. The results confirm previously reported work 
on the same burner operated isothermally and show that the center of the vortex flow is displaced from the 
central axis of the burner, creating the PVC phenomena as the center of the vortex precesses around the 
central axis of symmetry. As a consequence 
of this displacement 
the reverse flow zone (RFZ) is also 
displaced, while also partially lagging behind the PVC by up to 180”. The RFZ acts as a feedback mechanism 
for the PVC phenomena. As a consequence of the displaced vortex centre, flow between the PVC centre and 
the wall is squeezed. Thus, due to angular momentum flux consideration, it produces a considerable increase 
in tangential velocity and gives the characteristic PVC signal. The displaced RFZ is both rotating through a 
region of forward flow whilst also being of an intermittent 
nature, giving rise to the excellent flame 
stabilisation and mixing characteristics of these types of burners. Similar results were obtained for isothermal 
and premixed combustion conditions providing the flame was stabilised close to the burner exit nozzle.

APPARATUS 
The swirl burner is of simple design and has 
evolved for burning dirty, low calorific value 
gases [7]. The air/gas mix is fed into the burner 
via two tangential inlets and has a geometric 
swirl number of 1.5 and is of similar design to 
the units reported in Refs. 9, 10, and 15 (Fig. 
2). It forms part of a swirl burner/furnace 
system for efficiently burning low calorific value 
gases [7, 151. The furnace is typically twice the 
burner exit diameter. For this study, and rea- 
sons of experimental 
access, the furnace was 
removed 
and the system run with premixed 
firing discharging 
into the atmosphere. 
The 
purpose 
of these trials was to simulate the 
combustion of premixed air/low calorific value 
gas mixtures 
(calorific 
value 
down 
to 
1.4 
MJ/m”) in this system. Of the natural gas used 
90% was premixed with all the combustion air 
and was used to simulate the combustion 
of 
low calorific value gases. The overall mixture 
ratio relative to stoichiometric 
was 1.64. To 
prevent flashback of the combustible 
mixture 
from the exhaust to the swirl chamber inlets 
the exhaust of the unit was extended 
back- 
wards into the swirl chamber so that the flow 
had to pass radially through a 50-mm annular 
gap before entering the exhaust. The remain- 
ing 10% of the natural gas was fed through the 
axial fuel inlet to provide a pilot flame, as 
normally the furnace section had to be left in 
place for reasons of flame stability. Results 
were obtained for an air flowrate of 1120 l/min 
and natural gas of 82 l/min. This corresponds 
to a simulated low calorific value gas of ap- 
proximately 2.7 MJ/m3. A water-cooled Kistler 
pressure 
transducer 
(type 7261 frequency 
re- 
sponse 2.5 kHz) was mounted flush with the 
burner exhaust rim and gives a direct analogue 
PVC signal as shown in Fig. 1. This was used to 
trigger or mark a system for the associated 
color component 
DANTEC LDV system such 
that the 10,000 Doppler bursts captured at a 
measuring point could be related to the peak 
velocity of the PVC. This system has a fre- 
quency response of over 90 kHz provided suf- 
ficient particles 
are present 
in the flow. In 
terms of the structure and shape of the PVC 
signal the configuration 
of the swirl flow sys- 
tem seems to be of little significance apart 
from the level of swirl generated, or the system 
swirl number [4, 9, 101. 
The analysis software is derived from engine 
analysis work using the timing pulse generated 
from the hot-wire probe or pressure transducer 
to trigger the LDV system. This enables not 
only the conventional 
time mean and fluctuat- 
ing velocities (and shear stress) to be obtained, 
but also the periodic mean velocity profile as 
the PVC rotates about the central axis of sym- 
metry. This is achieved by overlaying succes- 
sive data points onto a scatter diagram (Fig. 4). 
In this diagram the PVC frequency 
was 100 
Hz, i.e., 10 ms for each revolution. When an 
average curve is produced, Fig. 4, the curve can 
be digitized such that the angular variation of 
all 3 phase averaged mean velocities, as the 
PVC rotates, can be obtained. Values of fluc- 
tuating or rms velocities, as well as the mean 
could be obtained, but this was not done owing 
to worries about spatial resolution of the mea- 
suring volume (2 mm long x 0.4 mm diameter) 
and jitter on the PVC signal, which could cause 
uneven triggering of the system by the PVC. 
This can be clearly seen on Fig. 4 where some 
5%-10% of points are not located in the main 
cluster of points around the mean curve. A 
range of data for mean radial, tangential and 
axial velocities at different measuring stations 
has been obtained,

In terms of mixing processes and flame sta- 
bilization, it is clear that the precessing vortex 
core phenomena 
generates an off-centered, 
or 
eccentric, RFZ which rotates through a region
