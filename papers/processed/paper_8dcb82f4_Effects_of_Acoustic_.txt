Effects of Acoustic Excitation on a
Swirling Diffusion Flame
A swirling double concentric jet is commonly used for nonpremixed gas burner applica-
tion for safety reasons and to improve the combustion performance. Fuel is generally
spurted at the central jet while the annular coﬂowing air is swirled. They are normally
separated by a blockage disk where the bluff-body effects further enhance the recircula-
tion of hot gas at the reaction zone. This paper aims to experimentally investigate the
behavior of ﬂame and ﬂow in a double concentric jet combustor when the fuel supply is
acoustically driven. Laser-light sheet assisted Mie scattering method has been used to
visualize the ﬂow, while the ﬂame lengths were measured by a conventional photography
technique. The ﬂuctuating velocity at the jet exit was measured by a two-component laser
Doppler velocimeter. Flammability and stability at ﬁrst fuel tube resonant frequency are
reported and discussed. The evolution of ﬂame proﬁle with excitation level is presented
and discussed, together with the reduction in ﬂame length. The ﬂame in the unforced
reacting axisymmetric wake is classiﬁed into three characteristic modes, which are weak
swirling ﬂame, lifted ﬂame, and transitional reattached ﬂame. These terms reﬂect their
primary features of ﬂame appearances, and when the acoustic excitation is applied, the
ﬂame behaviors change with the excitation frequency and amplitude. Four additional
characteristic modes are identiﬁed; e.g., at low excitation amplitudes, wrinkling ﬂame
with a blue annular ﬁlm is observed because the excitation induces vortices in the central
fuel jet and hence gives rise to the wrinkling of ﬂame. The central jet vortices become
larger with the increase in excitation amplitude and thus lead to a wider and shorter
ﬂame. If the excitation amplitude is increased above a certain value, the central jet
vortices change the rotation direction and pacing with the annular jet vortices. These
changes in the ﬂow ﬁeld induce large turbulent intensity and mixing and therefore make
the ﬂame looks blue and short. Further increase in the excitation amplitude would lift the
ﬂame because the ﬂow ﬁeld would be dramatically modiﬁed. DOI: 10.1115/1.4001768
Keywords: reacting swirling wake, acoustic excitation, nonpremixed ﬂame
1
Introduction
Swirl ﬂow has been employed to enhance mixing and combus-
tion for industrial gas burners suited for boilers, gas furnaces, gas
turbines, etc. The tangential velocity component imparted by the
swirling ﬂow causes a low relative pressure at the central axis and
thus triggers recirculation at the central axis, which expedites mix-
ing if fuel is spurted as a central jet. However, the swirl number
has to be beyond 0.6, or an annular jet Reynolds number has to be
more than 18,000 to make recirculation possible 1. Many of the
modern day industrial gas burners, particularly for small to me-
dium scale purposes, operate at much lower swirl and Reynolds
numbers.
Downstream recirculation naturally occurs behind a ﬁrmly
mounted blockage disk along the ﬂow stream at the right Rey-
nolds number. Behind such a disk is a subatmospheric pressure
that will entice a recirculation of ﬂuids called the bluff-body ef-
fect. But bluff body offers considerable pressure loss to the ﬂuid
ﬂow, particularly when the blockage size becomes large. A recir-
culation bubble was observed by Escudier and Keller 2 imme-
diately behind the cylindrical center body with a recirculation
zone at the downstream area. The wake is enclosed in a cylindri-
cal tube where the center bodies’ blockage ratios are 0.25 and
0.39. Huang and Tsai 3 used 14 blockage disks to investigate the
recirculation of a swirl ﬂow with swirl number ranges from 0.02
to 0.6. The blockage ratio ranges from 0.026 to 0.563, where the
bluff-body effects were found signiﬁcant if the blockage ratio was
more than 0.11. Several ﬂow structures were found, such as single
bubble, dual rings, and dual rings with vortex breakdown. Huang
and Tsai 4 made a follow-up investigation particularly on the
mixing capability coupled with laser Doppler velocimeter LDV
measurements. They concluded that larger central blockage re-
quires least swirl strength to attain better mixing. However, mix-
ing is not satisfactorily high, particularly with a dual ring ﬂow
structure because the central jet is entangled by the jetlike swirling
ﬂow at the central axis.
An alternative way to improve the entrainment of surrounding
air into the combusting jet is by pulsing the fuel supply. Jet puls-
ing is commonly done either by acoustic excitation 5–15 or by a
valve switching mechanism 16–18. Acoustic excitation is car-
ried out by mounting a loud speaker along the fuel supply line,
while valve switching usually employ a solenoid valve or a rotat-
ing perforated disk to periodically cut the fuel supply. Either way,
investigators reported an ampliﬁed jet exit velocity ﬂuctuation es-
pecially when the pulsation is tuned close to the fuel tube resonant
frequency 5,7–11,15–17. Crow and Champagne 5 deﬁned a
preferred mode of excitation at a Strouhal number of 0.3 by which
the jet response is a maximum velocity ﬂuctuation amplitude. At
an excited central jet Strouhal number of 0.4, Strawa and Cantwell
6 discovered a strong coupling with the coﬂowing annular jet by
which the luminous zone pinches off. Their schlieren images trace
the deceleration of the central reacting jet at such pinch-off point.
The large ﬂuctuating amplitude of the pulsing jet tuned at the
corresponding fuel tube resonant frequency exhibits a dramatic
reduction in ﬂame length 7–9,16. Pulsing a conﬁned jet at a
conﬁnement tube resonant frequency, Parikh and Moffat 17 re-

to control ﬂame lifting 11,12,14 and ﬂame blow out 15. The

a big help to reduce NOx emission 14. As the fuel jet pressure
varies during the cycle of ﬂow forcing, the fuel outward vortices
collapse into the entrained ﬂow named by Kim et al. 7 as the

acoustic forcing amplitude, the fuel vortices change their direction
and cause the ﬂame to elongate. The work of Demare and Baillot

However, improper acoustic forcing also offers detrimental com-
bustion effects on the likes of ﬂame extinction, ﬂash back, poor
combustion, and system efﬁciency reduction. Baillot and Demare
11 made a classiﬁcation on the behavior of ﬂames at various
forcing frequencies and fuel jet velocities where some illustrate
the adverse effect on combustion.
Nonpremixed combustors are usually involved in industrial ap-
plication for safety reasons in exchange of the low combustion
efﬁciency due to poor mixing. The present combustor is one of
them, as described by Huang and Tsai 4. It is a double concen-
tric combustor where the fuel jet is spurted at the central axis with
an annular swirling air separated by a blockage disk. Inefﬁcient
burning of fuel from this combustor may give rise to carbon mon-
oxide poisoning; thus, improved mixing at the wake is a para-
mount importance. Huang and Yen 19 diverted the central jet
fuel to the annular rotating vortex by installing a control disk at
the fuel jet exit. Indeed, turbulence intensity was ampliﬁed, mix-
ing was improved, and better combustion was attained manifested
by a blue ﬂame. The present work aims to enhance mixing at the
swirling wake by an acoustic excitation of the central fuel jet. The
entrainment of annular swirling air into the pulsing fuel is some-
thing that was rarely found in the literature. Our speciﬁc objec-
tives are i to ﬁnd the best combination of parameters by which
better combustion is achieved and ii to describe the ﬂame be-
havior and mixing phenomena of ﬂow for some of those chosen
parameters.

Experimental Arrangement
The arrangement of the experimental setup is shown in Fig. 1.
A 3.4 kW ring blower with a maximum static pressure of 98.1
bars is used to provide the annular swirling air ﬂow. To minimize
the ﬂow perturbation caused by the rotating blower blades, acous-
tic ﬁlters are used. Air ﬂows are quantiﬁed using rotameters be-
fore they are fed into the test rig. The rotameters are calibrated
using a microﬂow calibration system, and the accuracy is within
1% at full scale. The test rig is a 600400 mm2 circular cylinder
with an internal cavity proﬁled into a nozzle where the contraction
ratio is 9. At the test rig, quantiﬁed air ﬂow passes through a
settling chamber, honeycomb, and mesh screen and is ﬁnally re-
directed by 12 axis-symmetric guide vanes to generate swirl ﬂow.
Guide vanes are proﬁled with NACA 0012 having chord and span
lengths of 60 mm and 50 mm, respectively. Arranged on a pitch
circle of radius 175 mm, the vane angle can be varied from 0 deg
to 70 deg with a minimum resolution of 0.5 deg. Shown in the
subset of Fig. 1 is the test section located at the exit of the test rig
where a 1 mm thick blockage disk is positioned. The exit diameter
Do is 40 mm, while the blockage disk diameter D is 30 mm,
making a blockage ratio B=D/Do2 equal to 0.563.
The central jet is commercial grade propane, which is com-
posed of 95.0% C3H8, 3.5% C2H6, and 1.5% C4H10. This fuel is
stored in an adjacent pressure tank, conveyed by a ﬂexible high
pressure hose where the line pressure can be efﬁciently controlled
by a pressure regulator. The fuel gas goes through needle valves
prior to ﬂow measurements by rotameters. An acoustic excitation
arrangement by placing the loud speaker at the base of the fuel
tube, as shown in Fig. 1, is adopted for the interaction of sound
waves with the fuel jet. This arrangement can also be found in
Ginevsky et al. 20. The 300 mm length nozzle is proﬁled into a
ﬁfth order polynomial where the contraction ratio is 900. A seam-
less stainless steel tube with a length of 600 mm and an inner
diameter of 5.0 mm is connected to the nozzle exit and the block-
age disk. Fuel jet ﬂuid is vented to the wake of the blockage disk
via a 5.0 mm diameter hole d=5.0 mm located at the center of
the blockage disk. Acoustic excitation is carried out by a 15 cm
face diameter loud speaker. The electrical resistance of the voice
coil is 6.6  measured at the loud speaker terminals. The moving
mass of the loud speaker, which includes the voice coil, dust cap,
surround, spider, and diaphragm, is 22.07 g. A square wave signal
is generated by a function generator, and the output signal is veri-
ﬁed by an oscilloscope. The frequency at the function generator is
varied from 10 Hz to 1200 Hz with a minimum resolution of 1 Hz.
The output voltage from the function generator is ampliﬁed by a
power ampliﬁer. Such ampliﬁed signal oscillates the voice coil of
the loud speaker, and the maximum attainable excitation voltage is
25 V. A multimeter is used to measure the root mean square volt-
age across the terminal of the loud speaker.
Image acquisitions are carried out by three different cameras
intended for three speciﬁc purposes. A Sony progressive scan
camera DCR-TRV 900NTSC is used to capture colored moving
pictures for illustrative purposes. IDT X-Stream Vision high speed
camera model XS-4 is used to capture a black and white moving
picture to investigate the evolution of vortices. Nikon still camera
Nikon COOLPIX 990 is used for conventional photography
technique. The detailed operation of each of these image acquisi-
tion tools will be discussed in the following sections.
Jet velocities were measured with a two-component LDV, while
the ﬂow is seeded with 1 m diameter MgO particles. According
to the approximation of Hjelmfelt, Jr. and Mockros 21, the re-
sponse frequency of these particles in a propane medium is 9000
Hz. The blue and green laser beams, which were supplied by a 6
W argon-ion laser Spectra-Physics’ Stabilite-2017, were sepa-
rated and focused through the Dantec Fiber-Flow optical system.
Typical properties of the optical conﬁgurations were listed in
Table 1. The “X” conﬁguration for the two-component light
beams was used. Such arrangement improved the measurement

As shown in Fig. 3a, at a jet exit Strouhal number of 0.65
Eexc=0.9 V, the acoustic excitation makes the ﬂame base
wrinkle. An annular blue ﬂame surrounds the core region of yel-
lowish ﬂame. Wrinkling with a blue annular ﬁlm near the ﬂame
base characterizes ﬂame mode A, which shows a somewhat in-
creased combustion capability compared with the corresponding
unexcited case of Fig. 2a. The transition period from ﬂame
mode A to B is manifested by a strong coupling 6 between the
excited jet and the annular ﬂow. Periodic ﬂame structures pinch
off from the wrinkled ﬂame surface through the outer annular ﬁlm
and move upstream as excitation slightly increases. Severe violent
wrinkling motions are very obvious in the downstream area of
ﬂame mode B accompanied by a very obvious nonuniform bulk
expansion around the root area. Oh and Shin 8 spotted seven
symmetric azimuthal structures surrounding the central jet during
acoustic forcing. A similar observation was reported by Demare
and Baillot 10 on such azimuthal structures, which they called
ﬁlament. The highest ﬂame length reduction with a slight change
in Strouhal number in ﬂame mode B, as shown in Fig. 3b, is in
good agreement with the work of Crow and Champagne 5. A
strong response of the jet exit velocity amplitude to a minimal
change in excitation level exists in this ﬂame mode. Increasing the
central jet pulsing amplitude pushes the bulky structure at the root
area to the downstream section, leaving a completely blue an-
chored ﬂame at the burner tip. Such behavior is deﬁned as ﬂame
mode B, where the ﬂame length continues to drop with the jet exit
Strouhal number. A typical ﬂame appearance of ﬂame in this
mode is shown in Fig. 3a for the jet exit Strouhal number less
than 0.39 Eexc2.4 V. The ﬂame exhibits a blue diverging base
and looks like an inverted conical ﬂame, which burns a consider-
able amount of mixtures at the base, resulting to a much shorter
ﬂame. Seemingly blue pebbles pinch off at the trailing edge of
soot radiating yellow ﬂames. Severe strain at the burner base
causes the ﬂame to lift 11,12,14 as excitation is increased to a
much higher level Fig. 3. The lifted ﬂame’s leading edge is blue
and exhibits strong turbulent motion, which is characterized as
ﬂame mode D. This kind of ﬂame behavior always happened
when the Strouhal number and swirl strength are pushed down to
their minimum values. Persistent and increasing excitation ampli-
ﬁes the lifted ﬂame oscillation while receding to a far downstream
area until it never comes back and then blows out 15.
3.3
Characteristic Regimes. Similar appearances of ﬂame
during acoustic excitation are mapped in Fig. 4a in the domain
of excitation level and frequencies. The narrow band of bound-
aries represents uncertainty and hysteresis during the transition
period from one ﬂame mode to another mode. There exists a
threshold of excitation amplitude for each excitation frequency.
Exciting the ﬂame with an amplitude lower than the threshold
would cause no effect on the ﬂame appearance. Throughout the
range of excitation frequencies, ﬂame modes A, B, C, and D ap-
pear in an order of increasing excitation level. It means that ﬂame
mode C always has the higher range of excitation level than ﬂame
mode B for the same excitation frequency. If the excitation fre-
quency is equal or close to 180 Hz, or its harmonics, a minimal
amount of excitation level transforms the ﬂame modes due to
strong fuel jet oscillations caused by fuel tube resonance 24.
Dramatic reduction in ﬂame length was reported by several inves-
tigators 5,7–11,15–17 of the axisymmetric jet ﬂame at the fuel
tube resonant frequency.
Logically, the ﬁrst fuel tube resonant frequency is considered in
the present work to investigate the best performance of the system
to improve the combustion. The central jet exit velocity was mea-
sured by a two-component LDV only at the ﬁrst fuel tube resonant
frequency. Better repeatability was obtained with at least 30,000
samples collected with a maximum data rate of 4.2 kHz. The
volumetric deviation of the calculated ﬂow rate at the jet exit
varies from 0.08% to 0.05% compared with the rotameter reading

ﬂame blowout is beyond the scope of this work.
4
Conclusions
A nonpremixed reacting swirling wake has been excited acous-
tically at various ranges of excitation levels and frequencies. The
fuel tube resonance frequency was experimentally obtained at 180
Hz and its harmonics by which minimal excitation level can
modify the ﬂame. Velocity at the jet exit was measured by two-
component LDV at the ﬁrst fuel tube resonant frequency. The
Strouhal number of the acoustic excitation was calculated based
on the ﬁrst fuel tube resonant frequency and the root mean square
of the ﬂuctuating velocity at the central jet exit. Swirl strength
substantially damped the jet velocity ﬂuctuation and at the same
time mitigated the reduction in ﬂame length. In the summarized
representation of ﬂame reduction, the forcing system effectively
reduced the ﬂame length at Rec=2386. Flammability and stability
chart illustrated that ﬂame stabilization is weak at a swirl strength
close to 0.491 Rea=728.
The ﬂame at the swirling wake exhibited four different ﬂame
behaviors during acoustic excitation classiﬁed into ﬂame modes
A, B, C, and D. The mechanics of mixing at each ﬂame mode was
discerned from phase locked Mie scattering images captured by a
high speed camera. At mode A, low excitation amplitudes induce
beadlike vortices in the central fuel jet and make the ﬂame
wrinkles. At mode B, the central jet vortices become larger with
the increase in excitation amplitude and thus lead to a wider and
shorter ﬂame. At mode C, the excitation amplitude is increased
further to exceed the critical value so that the central jet vortices
change the rotation direction and pacing with the annular jet vor-
tices. Large turbulent intensity and mixing are induced due to the
drastic change in ﬂow ﬁeld and therefore make the ﬂame looks
blue and short. At mode D, a lifted ﬂame is formed because the
excitation amplitude is large enough to modify a strong but small
recirculation bubble sitting on the disk.
