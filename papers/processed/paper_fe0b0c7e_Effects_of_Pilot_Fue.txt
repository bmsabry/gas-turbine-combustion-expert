The п¬‚ame structure and the limits of operation of a lean premixed swirl п¬‚ame were

Introduction
Lean premixed swirl stabilized combustion is successfully ap-
plied in gas turbine combustion systems to maintain low NOx and
CO-emissions over a wide operating range. The п¬‚ow patterns in
these combustions systems are characterized by a conical main
п¬‚ow zone and an internal recirculation zone in which hot burnt
gases are recirculated. Stable combustion over a wide operating
range is ensured by the turbulent reacting shear layer formed be-
tween the hot recirculating gases and the unburnt fuel/air mixture
of the main п¬‚ow. Extensive work was performed to understand
swirl п¬‚ows and their effects in combustion processes. The work of
Gupta et al. 1 gives a comprehensive overview, while Meier et
al. 2 and Beck et al. 3 include recent advancements. Require-
ments to modern heavy duty gas turbine systems are low pollutant
emissions, safe operation, and a wide operating range. While low
NOx emissions require air/fuel mixtures close to the lean blow off
limit, the risks of thermo-acoustic instabilities and lean blow off
often can be avoided by choosing richer mixtures.
In the light of this contradiction, multifuel staged systems have
been introduced to increase the operating range of lean premixed
combustion systems. In the burners used in the annular combus-
tion chambers of Siemens heavy duty gas turbine series Fig. 1 a
lean premixed main fuel stage is combined with a central вЂњpilotвЂќ
fuel stage, which provides a locally enriched air/fuel mixture at
the center of the burner outlet. This system extends the operating
range of the swirl п¬‚ame toward leaner conditions 5,6. The prop-
erties of combustion systems based on this staged burner concept
were investigated at various levels of abstraction from п¬Ѓeld tests
7 to atmospheric single burner testing 8 and from simulations
covering speciп¬Ѓc details, such as mixing 9, to extensive large
eddy simulations including the entire combustion system 10.
Although many aspects of swirl stabilized lean combustion sys-
tems are known today, the effects of pilot fuel injection on the
overall п¬‚ow п¬Ѓeld of this exemplary lean premixed combustion
system are not fully understood. Even though emission measure-
ments taken during п¬Ѓeld tests 7 clearly indicate a potential to
reduce NOx emissions considerably by a reduction in the pilot fuel
ratio, the mechanism, which produces the stabilizing effect of the
pilot, is yet to be clearly identiп¬Ѓed. Previous experimental studies
using a quasi-adiabatic atmospheric combustion chamber were fo-
cused on the mechanisms leading to thermo-acoustic instabilities
under conditions richer than the design point 8,11вЂ“13. These
investigations indicated that the pilot neither had a measurable
effect on the thermo acoustic behavior nor on the lean blow off
limits.
In this context, a large scale test rig for a single staged burner
was designed, which has features similar to the quasi-adiabatic rig
studied earlier. The new setup includes a nonadiabatic liner con-
п¬Ѓguration in order to bring the test case closer to the real engine.
The nonadiabatic test rig exhibited the desired effects of pilot fuel
injection 14. In this paper, detailed measurements of the п¬‚ow
п¬Ѓeld inside the nonadiabatic combustion chamber will be pre-
sented in order to highlight some of the effects of pilot fuel injec-
tion. Wall heat п¬‚ux, noise, and emission measurements will be
used to link these effects to system performance.

Changes in the position and shape of the п¬‚ame were also ob-

Figure 13 shows the time-averaged CH-chemiluminescence of
the п¬‚ame close to the burner mouth. The burner and the ceramic

Conclusion
It was demonstrated that the positive effects of pilot fuel injec-
tion on the stability and the lean blow off limit of an industrial
lean premixed combustion system can be reproduced using a dis-
tinctively nonadiabatic test setup. A similar but quasi-adiabatic
combustion system tested earlier 13 did not show effects of pi-
loting on the п¬‚ame properties. As the current nonadiabatic test rig
exhibits such effects, the importance of wall heat п¬‚ux for investi-
gations on stability of lean premixed п¬‚ames is emphasized.
The shape of the reacting п¬‚ow п¬Ѓeld within the nonadiabatic
combustor was severely affected by pilot fuel injection. Two п¬‚ow
patterns were detected. They differ in the width of the cone angle
of their main п¬‚ow zone and the resulting size of the inner recir-
culation zone. The wide п¬‚ow pattern occurred at high load, sufп¬Ѓ-
cient piloting, and/or reduced leakage air п¬‚ow. It did coincide with
a more stable and more silent combustion process. For lower load,
reduced piloting and/or increased leakage air, a sudden transition
to the narrow п¬‚ow п¬Ѓeld was observed. This transition results in
changes of the pressure loss in the system, the wall heat п¬‚ux and
the pollutant emissions. The studies revealed that the wall heat
п¬‚ux is an easily accessible indicator for the shape of the п¬‚ow
pattern and hence for the stability of the п¬‚ame. It was seen from
chemiluminescence imaging that piloting shifts the reaction zones
toward the burner. As lean blow off did only occur if the narrow
п¬‚ow п¬Ѓeld was present before the LBO, it was concluded that the
п¬‚ame stability is severely affected by the change in the п¬‚ow struc-
ture.
