A review of recent advances 
in the application of machine learning 
algorithms for gas turbine combustion
Da Mo1,2, Yuzhen Lin2,3, Yixiong Liu4, Yuchen Wang2, Ziyu Qin2 and Xiao Han2,3* 
Abstract 
With the rapid advancement of computing power and significant progress in machine learning (ML) algorithms, ML 
has shown immense potential across a wide range of fields, particularly in simulation, experimental analysis, and con‑
dition monitoring. In the realm of combustion science and engineering, ML techniques have driven substantial 
advancements, particularly in gas turbine engine combustion. The integration of intelligent algorithms in combustion 
modelling and analysis has led to more accurate predictions, enhanced performance, and the potential for more effi‑
cient and environmentally friendly combustion processes. This paper provides a comprehensive review of the current 
state of research on intelligent algorithms applied to combustion chemical reaction kinetics, combustion simula‑
tion, performance prediction, combustion state, and instability monitoring. The review highlights the progress made 
and offers valuable insights for improving the performance of gas turbine engines. Additionally, the paper discusses 
the challenges and prospects of applying intelligent algorithms in combustion research, including issues related 
to data quality, model interpretability, and computational complexity, while identifying avenues for future develop‑
ment and innovation.
Keywords  Machine learning, Chemical reaction kinetics, Instability prediction, PINN
1  Introduction
In the current data-driven era, machine learning (ML) 
algorithms are continuously advancing technological 
development. These algorithms, which simulate intelli-
gent behaviors observed in nature, such as learning, rea-
soning, and perception, are designed to solve complex 
problems. Examples of such algorithms include linear 
regression (LNR), logistic regression (LGR), decision tree 
(DT), random forest (RF), neural network (NN), convolu-
tional neural network (CNN), recurrent neural network 
(RNN), support vector machine (SVM), and generative 
adversarial network (GAN). Due to their versatility, these 
algorithms hold significant potential in a wide range of 
applications, such as prediction, classification, pattern 
recognition, and data generation [1, 2].
LNR and LGR serve as fundamental statistical mod-
eling approaches due to their computational efficiency 
and model transparency. LNR establishes linear corre-
lations between independent variables and continuous 
responses, making it particularly effective for quantita-
tive predictions such as temperature forecasting. In con-
trast, LGR employs a sigmoid transformation to estimate 
class probabilities, demonstrating superior performance 
for classification tasks with limited datasets or high-
dimensional sparse features.

Decision trees and RFs excel in handling nonlinear 
relationships and high-dimensional data, though they 
may face the risk of overfitting. Decision trees partition 
the feature space into regions using a series of decision 
rules. Each internal node represents a decision based 
on a feature, and each leaf node represents a prediction. 
RF is an ensemble method that constructs multiple DTs. 
Each tree is trained on a random subset of the data and a 
random subset of features. The final prediction is aggre-
gated by majority voting (classification) or averaging 
(regression).
NNs consist of layers of interconnected neurons that 
process input data through weighted sums and activation 
functions. NNs, particularly deep learning (DL) models, 
have become a research focus due to their ability to han-
dle complex data relationships, though they require vast 
amounts of data and computational resources. SVMs 
identify the optimal hyperplane that maximizes the mar-
gin between different classes. For non-linearly separable 
data, they employ kernel functions to map the data into 
higher-dimensional spaces, enabling effective classifica-
tion and regression. This approach allows SVMs to pro-
cess data indirectly via kernel functions, thereby avoiding 
the computational complexity of direct high-dimensional 
operations. It is especially advantageous for handling 
problems where data cannot be separated by a linear 
boundary.
NNs are computational models that simulate the struc-
ture and function of the human brain, capable of process-
ing complex data relationships, particularly in fields such 
as image recognition, natural language processing, and 
speech recognition. NNs predict and classify by learn-
ing the intricate patterns and relationships between input 
data. Numerous variants of NNs exist, with CNNs achiev-
ing revolutionary advancements in image recognition [3]. 
The introduction of convolutional and pooling layers in 
CNNs effectively addresses the dimensionality curse, 
spatial feature loss, and gradient issues encountered by 
artificial neural networks (ANNs) when processing high-
dimensional data. These innovations enable CNNs to 
excel in image classification and computer vision tasks, 
especially in analysing complex flame structures. RNNs 
process sequential data by updating hidden states based 
on current inputs and previous hidden states. They are 
prone to vanishing or exploding gradients, which are 
mitigated by variants, such as long short-term memory 
(LSTM) networks. They perform exceptionally well in 
processing natural language and time-series predictions. 
GANs consist of a generator that produces synthetic data 
and a discriminator that distinguishes between real and 
fake data. They are trained adversarially until the genera-
tor produces data indistinguishable from real data. This 
process enables GANs to generate high-quality, realistic 
data samples, particularly in image synthesis.
The development of ML algorithms marks a significant 
advancement in computational technology, as presented 
in Fig. 1. Machine learning techniques have evolved from 
fundamental algorithms (including linear regression [4] 
and neural networks [5]) to more advanced approaches 
like deep learning [6, 7] and reinforcement learning [8], 
with applications extending to domains such as chemi-
cal kinetics modeling via Arrhenius equations [9]. The 
field has advanced significantly with the rise of big data, 
GPU acceleration, and DL frameworks. The development 
of transformer architectures and foundation models has 
further advanced ML capabilities, enabling a broad range 
of applications. Transformers, with their powerful atten-
tion mechanisms, have revolutionised natural language 
processing and have also found applications in other 
domains such as computer vision and multimodal learn-
ing. Their ability to capture long-range dependencies 
and parallelize computations makes them highly effec-
tive, though they come with challenges like high compu-
tational complexity and memory requirements. On the

The development of ML algorithms has brought revo-
lutionary changes to various fields, showcasing immense 
potential in improving efficiency, reducing costs, and 
uncovering new knowledge. With the growth in computa-
tional power and the explosion of data, ML algorithms will 
continue to play a crucial role across many domains, driv-
ing technological progress. In the field of combustion, the 
application of ML plays a critical role in turbulence model-
ling, chemical reactions, and experimental research.
The following provides an overview of the develop-
ment and application of these technologies. For turbu-
lence modelling, ML and DL techniques have proven to 
be effective tools. Researchers have used these methods 
to predict the characteristics of turbulent flows, such as 
Reynolds stress models [10]. Spalart introduced turbu-
lence models and simulation strategies and presented a 
new version of the detached eddy simulation (DES) that 
is resistant to grid resolution variations [11, 12].
Regarding chemical reaction modelling, ML technolo-
gies are used to simulate and predict complex chemical 
reactions [13–15]. Key areas of focus include combus-
tion mechanism optimization [16, 17], chemical reaction 
kinetics modelling, and computational acceleration [18–
21]. ANNs have been successfully utilized to simulate 
chemical reactions [22]. These networks have shown the 
capability to capture the nonlinear functions of tempera-
ture and concentration. Additionally, unknown reaction 
pathways have been autonomously discovered through 
the use of chemical reaction NNs [23]. Moreover, com-
plex reaction processes have been revealed by employing 
molecular dynamics simulations [24].
Concerning numerical simulations, ML techniques are 
employed to improve the accuracy of combustion effi-
ciency predictions and optimize combustion processes. 
Buchheit et al. [25] proposed a stabilized explicit varia-
ble-load solver that accelerates the solution of stiff chem-
ical kinetics problems using ML. Chen et al. [26] applied 
ML to the closure of the filter density function in moder-
ate or intense low-oxygen dilution (MILD) combustion.
As for experimental research, ML technologies are 
also used to assist with sensor data augmentation and 
combustion process control [27–30], as well as perfor-
mance prediction, fault diagnosis, health management, 
and performance optimization [31–33]. ML further aids 
in analyzing vast amounts of combustion test data to 
identify key parameters in the combustion process, ena-
bling prediction, diagnosis, and management [34, 35]. To 
address high noise in industrial data and improve robust-
ness under varying operating conditions, various tech-
niques could be used, such as regularization methods like 
L1 and L2 regularization. Feature engineering and selec-
tion allow models to focus on the most relevant features, 
minimizing the influence of noisy or irrelevant data. Data 
augmentation techniques, such as adding noise, rotation, 
and scaling, enhance the model’s ability to generalize by 
exposing it to diverse variations during training. Dropout 
and batch normalization in NNs further improve robust-
ness by preventing overfitting and stabilizing the training 
process. Ensemble methods like RF reduce variance and 
improve generalization by averaging multiple models. 
Kernel functions in SVMs and attention mechanisms in 
RNNs help handle non-linearities and focus on relevant 
parts of the data, enhancing performance under varying 
conditions.
This paper aims to provide a comprehensive review 
of the application of intelligent algorithms in the field 
of gas turbine engine combustion. It explores how these 
algorithms assist researchers in better understanding 
and predicting combustion phenomena and addresses 
the challenges and future directions for their practical 
implementation.
2  Combustion modelling
ML with combustion models is crucial for enhancing the 
accuracy, adaptability, and optimization of complex gas 
turbine combustion processes. By learning from large 
datasets, they refine predictive accuracy, adapt to vary-
ing conditions, and reduce model uncertainties. This 
combination offers significant advantages over traditional 
models, enabling more efficient operation in practical 
applications.
2.1  Turbulent combustion models
ML in turbulent combustion models primarily improves 
prediction accuracy and computational efficiency, 
addressing the limitations of traditional models in han-
dling complex nonlinear and high-dimensional data. 
Techniques such as DL and neural networks enable the 
optimization of reaction rate predictions, turbulence-
combustion coupling, and low-dimensional data mod-
elling. Recent studies have made significant progress in 
developing efficient turbulent combustion models, real-
time simulations, and multi-scale modelling.
The integration of ML into the combustion model has 
proven effective in improving prediction accuracy and 
computational efficiency [36, 37]. The ANN model out-

2.2  Combustion kinetic model
In the context of chemical reaction kinetics modeling 
[55–58], ML techniques have been employed to construct 
and simplify complex chemical reaction mechanisms, 
thereby reducing computational costs and enhancing 
computational efficiency. For instance, optimizing chem-
ical reaction mechanisms using neural networks can 
significantly lower computational expenses while main-
taining high prediction accuracy. Using optimization 
algorithms, the accuracy of ANNs can reach the level of 
medium-to-fine resolution tabulated methods [59]. The 
ANN-based approach could accelerate global sensitivity 
analysis of combustion kinetics models and require only 
a fraction of the original samples [60].
Reaction rate constants are essential parameters in 
chemical kinetics models, and their accurate prediction 
is crucial for combustion simulations. Lu et al. [61] uti-
lized machine learning to predict the rate constants of 
hydroxyl radical reactions with alkanes. By constructing 
machine learning models and combining extensive 
experimental data with theoretical calculations, they 
achieved efficient prediction of reaction rate constants. 
Li et al. [56] also employed machine learning to predict 
rate constants for various reactions in combustion kinetic 
models, further demonstrating the potential of machine 
learning in rate constant prediction, which can signifi-
cantly improve prediction accuracy and reduce com-
putational time. Additionally, KiNet [62], a deep neural 
network representation of chemical kinetics, models 
chemical reaction kinetics by automatically learning the 
complex relationships between reaction rate constants 
and reaction pathways, offering a new tool for combus-
tion chemical kinetics modeling and simulation (Figs. 11 
and 12).
Optimizing combustion kinetic mechanisms is a key 
step in improving combustion simulation accuracy. A 
study [57] proposed a machine learning-based opti-
mization method for hydrogen combustion kinetic 
mechanisms. By analyzing and mining extensive experi-
mental data using machine learning algorithms, the 
kinetic parameters were optimized, resulting in a more 
accurate representation of hydrogen combustion pro-
cesses. Liu et  al. [63] developed Reaction Mechanism 
Generator v3.0, which has made significant progress in 
automatic mechanism generation. This tool uses machine 
learning to intelligently select and optimize reaction 
pathways and kinetic parameters, enhancing the accu-
racy and reliability of generated mechanisms and pro-
viding strong support for the development of kinetic 
mechanisms in complex combustion systems.
Deep learning, a significant branch of machine learn-
ing, has also been widely applied in combustion chemi-
cal kinetics. A study [64] explored the application of 
deep learning in analyzing and reducing dynamic laten-
cies in combustion chemical kinetics. By analyzing

Combustion chemical kinetics models often contain a 
large number of reactions and species, resulting in high 
computational costs. Therefore, model simplification

research directions in combustion kinetics. Haghshe-

kinetics problems were transformed into low-dimen-

tational efficiency while maintaining model accuracy. 
Pulga et al. [67] employed machine learning to improve 
the accuracy of reduced chemical kinetics mechanisms 
in laminar flame simulations. Their study optimized the 
simplified mechanisms using machine learning, result-
ing in higher accuracy in simulating laminar flame 
propagation speeds and temperature distributions, pro-
viding a new approach for efficient combustion simula-
tions (Fig. 14).
Machine learning techniques can also be used to 
integrate kinetic mechanisms from different sources, 
enhancing model applicability and accuracy. For exam-
ple, by analyzing multiple experimental datasets and the-
oretical calculations using machine learning algorithms, 
more comprehensive and accurate kinetic models can 
be generated. This approach not only reduces the influ-
ence of human factors on models but also better adapts 
to the kinetic requirements under different combustion 
conditions. The application of artificial intelligence in the 
modeling of chemical reaction kinetics [68] has also been 
explored, highlighting the potential of artificial intelli-
gence (AI) techniques to enhance the development and 
integration of kinetic models.
2.3  Conditional scalar dissipation rate modeling
Conditional Sscalar dissipation rate (CSDR) is an impor-
tant parameter for describing mixing and reaction pro-
cesses in turbulent flow fields, particularly in the study of 
combustion and pollutant formation. Traditional CSDR 
modelling methods typically rely on empirical formu-
las or similar approximations. With the ML method, 
researchers can leverage large datasets from DNS or 
experimental data to train models, capturing complex 
nonlinear relationships and identifying the connec-
tions between CSDR and other flow field variables. ANN 
trained on carrier-phase DNS data could model CSDRs 
in turbulent spray flames, and a  few droplet-related 
parameters were sufficient for accurate predictions [69, 
70], as shown in Fig. 15.
For modelling the reactive flows in LES, the DL model 
outperformed the analytical β-β PDF model and linear 
regression models in accuracy, while being as accurate 
as the RF model but with fewer trainable parameters 
and faster inference speeds [71]. CNN could effectively 
extract topological features of the flame and predict sub-
grid-scale wrinkling as well as reaction rates in turbulent 
premixed flames [39, 72]. ANNs are capable of directly 
simulating an important unresolved term in the unre-
solved stress tensor [73]. Meanwhile, DNN can predict 
the dissipation rate of the progress variable in turbulent 
premixed flames [74].

3  Numerical simulation
Numerical simulation analysis plays a crucial role in vari-
ous engineering fields, especially in applications involv-
ing combustion. They help to predict flame propagation, 
combustion states, pollutant emissions, and extinguish-
ment processes without the need for physical experi-
ments. With the rapid advancements in computational 
power and ML technologies, intelligent algorithms pro-
vide innovative approaches and tools for addressing 
complex engineering challenges. These algorithms are 
particularly effective in tasks such as optimization, pre-
dictive modelling, and real-time control, further enhanc-
ing the accuracy, efficiency, and capabilities of traditional 
numerical simulations.

tion techniques in pollutant prediction enhances our 
understanding of complex combustion phenomena, ulti-
mately aiding in the transition towards cleaner and more 
sustainable energy systems [79, 80].
RF classifier could be applied for dynamic submodel

3.3  Extinction prediction
Accurate extinction prediction is essential for combustor 
design, ensuring safety in industrial applications. Unlike 
traditional methods, ML models can handle high-dimen-
sional data, adapt to varying conditions, and provide 
real-time predictions, making them highly suitable for 
complex combustion systems [82–84]. Moreover, they 
can enable early detection of lean blowout (LBO) [85]. 
For instance, an SVM model has been shown to iden-
tify the critical flame location and predict LBO 20  ms 
in advance, demonstrating its potential for online flame 
health monitoring and engine management [85], as 
shown in Figs. 18 and 19.
3.4  New emerging methods
Transformer, GNN, and physics-informed neural net-
work (PINN) are advanced machine learning techniques 
that have revolutionized various domains by addressing 
complex data structures and incorporating domain-spe-
cific knowledge.
Transformer methods have demonstrated remark-
able potential in optimizing the performance of avia-
tion engines and gas turbine combustion chambers. 
Specifically, a dual-stream network that integrates 
CNNs with Transformers has proven highly effective 
in classifying the combustion state of sintering flames. 
By seamlessly integrating these two streams, the model 
combines granular local insights with comprehensive 
contextual information, thereby enhancing its ability 
to accurately classify combustion states. The preci-
sion of this classification is further refined through the 
optimization of attention weights within the Trans-
former, as well as the implementation of multi-scale 
feature fusion techniques [86].
Additionally, Transformer-based deep neural net-
works have been employed to predict combustion 
reaction source terms, which are crucial for accurate

PINNs have rapidly emerged as a transformative tool 
in the modeling of combustion processes, particularly 
for solving complex, multiphysics problems governed 
by partial differential equations (PDEs) [90]. Their 
applications span a wide range of combustion-related 
fields, including turbulent combustion, reactor design, 
and chemical kinetics. PINNs have demonstrated 
remarkable capabilities in accurately predicting key 
combustion parameters such as temperature distribu-
tions, species concentrations, and emission levels [91–
94]. By integrating physical laws such as conservation of 
mass and energy into the model, PINNs provide a way 
to solve governing equations, like the Navier–Stokes 
equations and species conservation equations, with 
both data and physics constraints. These capabilities 
make PINNs well-suited for modelling the multiphysics 
phenomena inherent in combustion processes.
PINNs [95–97] offer several advantages in combus-
tion modelling. Their ability to efficiently integrate 
sparse experimental or simulation data with physical 
laws leads to a more data-efficient and computationally 
cheaper approach compared to traditional methods. 
PINNs also ensure physically consistent predictions by 
enforcing governing equations during training. How-
ever, there are challenges to their widespread adoption. 
Training PINNs can be computationally expensive, 
particularly for large-scale combustion problems, and 
obtaining high-quality experimental data for training 
can be difficult. Furthermore, PINNs may struggle with 
generalization across diverse combustion conditions, 
requiring careful training to handle a wide range of sce-
narios effectively.
Hybrid models that combine PINNs with tradi-
tional simulation techniques like LES could enhance 
predictive accuracy, especially for turbulent combus-
tion regimes. Adaptive PINNs, capable of refining dis-
cretization based on problem complexity, may lead to 
improved performance in diverse scenarios. Addition-
ally, PINNs could be adapted for real-time combustion 
monitoring and control, offering faster and more accu-
rate predictions in dynamic systems. As research pro-
gresses, the ability of PINNs to handle multi-scale and 
multi-physics combustion phenomena would further 
expand their applicability, making them a valuable tool 
in the design and optimization of more efficient and 
sustainable combustion systems.
PINN for solving combustion reaction kinetics inte-
grates physical principles to regularize the ANN, as 
demonstrated in Fig.  20. It can also infer intermedi-
ate species and temperature, and significantly reduce 
data dependence, achieving up to 14.6 times speedup 
in simulations and improving accuracy in reacting flow 
simulations [98].
Raissi [99, 100] introduces PINNs for solving super-
vised learning tasks while respecting physical laws. 
It presents two algorithms: continuous-time models 
for data-efficient spatiotemporal approximations and 
discrete-time models using high-accuracy Runge–
Kutta schemes. The framework’s effectiveness is dem-
onstrated in classical problems in fluids, quantum 
mechanics, reaction–diffusion systems, and nonlinear 
wave propagation.

extending the lifespan of combustion equipment.
NN methods effectively extracted and replicated flame

then leverages these refined features to improve predic-
tion accuracy, effectively identifying combustion instabil-

4.5  Emission prediction
Accurate prediction of emissions ­(NOx, CO) is critical 
for environmental compliance, process optimization, and 
emission control. Zhang  et al. [128] proposed a hybrid 
multi-output least-squares support vector regression 
(MLS-SVR)-NN model for multi-emission prediction in 
biodiesel-hydrogen fueled engines, demonstrating supe-
rior accuracy, while Li  et al. [129] demonstrated ANN’s 
effectiveness for multi-parameter combustion monitoring 
­(NOx, dynamics, vibration, and temperature) (Fig. 35).
Potts [130] evaluated ML-based emission predic-
tion performance by comparing self-attention and 
inter-sample attention transformer (SAINT) and extreme 
gradient boosting (XGBoost) against conventional 
chemistry-based models, demonstrating superior accu-
racy with ML methods. Wang et al. [131] optimized NN 
architecture for emission prediction, identifying 24 as the 
optimal network width (R2 > 0.986), which maximized 
both prediction accuracy and generalization capability.
4.6  Autoignition prediction
Autoignition poses significant risks in lean-premixed 
combustion systems, potentially causing flashback and 
hardware damage during fuel–air mixing. To address this 
operational challenge, Li et al. [132] developed a hybrid 
ML model integrating LGR and RNNs for autoignition 
prediction. The model, validated through experimental 
fuel–air mixing simulations, successfully identified criti-
cal autoignition drivers while achieving robust predictive 
accuracy.

5  Conclusions
The application of ML algorithms in the field of gas 
turbine combustion has shown significant potential 
as technology continues to advance. ML algorithms 
enhance the accuracy of combustion process predic-
tions, especially in capturing complex nonlinear behav-
iours that traditional physics-based models often miss. 
This leads to improved simulation efficiency and more 
accurate performance predictions. Additionally, ML 
algorithms play a crucial role in optimizing combustion 
efficiency, reducing harmful emissions, and enhancing 
engine performance.
However, the application of ML algorithms also faces 
several challenges and limitations. Firstly, ML models are 
highly dependent on large amounts of high-quality train-
ing data, which is particularly difficult to obtain in the 
complex and extreme conditions of aviation engine com-
bustion. The cost and difficulty of data collection are sig-
nificant hurdles. Secondly, overfitting is a common issue, 
especially when data samples are limited or of low qual-
ity. In such cases, ML models may perform well on the 
training set but fail to generalize effectively to new and 
unseen operating conditions. Furthermore, ML models 
are often criticized as “black boxes” due to their lack of 
interpretability and transparency, which can hinder trust 
and practical application, particularly in safety–critical 
domains like combustion stability.
In conclusion, while data-driven models hold immense 
promise for revolutionizing combustion science, their 
practical implementation faces significant hurdles. A pri-
mary challenge is the acquisition of high-fidelity experi-
mental data under extreme combustion conditions, such 
as supersonic flames or sooting pyrolysis. In these envi-
ronments, harsh temperatures and obscuration often 
compromise optical diagnostics, making it difficult to 
obtain reliable and comprehensive data. Additionally, 
reconciling purely data-driven frameworks with funda-
mental physical principles remains a critical issue. Mod-
els that fail to account for mass and energy conservation 
or chemical kinetics may violate thermodynamic con-
straints and lack interpretability, thereby undermining 
their credibility and applicability.
