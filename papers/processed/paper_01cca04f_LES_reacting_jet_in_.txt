Today environmental issues play a vital role in the viability of modern, low-emission 
power plants in which gas turbine combustors need to be operated under lean premixed 
conditions. However, successful designs require a detailed knowledge of the combustion 
process. The present study focuses on studying reacting and non-reacting jet. Firstly, an iso-
thermal confined jet has been studied using Large Eddy Simulation. The flow is highly 
turbulent and a precession of the jet core was detected. The comparison of the numerical 
results and the experimental velocity data measured with Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) 
showed an excellent agreement. Secondly, the influence of the combustion on the flow field 
was investigated. The jet core was found to be lengthened due to the density jump across the 
flame. The slow jet core precession was not found to be affected by combustion.

I. 
Introduction 
UE to stricter emission regulations, environmental issues of power generation play an important role in the 
economic viability of modern power plants. In order to reduce harmful emissions, the current trend is to design 
gas turbine combustion devices to operate at premixed, extremely fuel lean conditions. Consequently, the peak 
temperature and NOX emissions are reduced but combustion instabilities leading to flash back, flame blow out, and 
high CO/UHC emissions may occur1. The challenge therefore is to get an accurate knowledge of the unsteady 
combustion process in order to design high-efficient, safe, low-emission combustors. 
D 
The phenomena are very complex as many physical and chemical scales are involved, therefore turbulent 
combustion instabilities are a multi-scale 4D problem. Simulation of turbulent combustion is particularly difficult, as 
the phenomena involved are highly non-linear and unsteady. In addition, a direct full simulation of the problem is 
beyond our computing capability. However, the recent increase in computational power enables to use Large Eddy 
Simulation (LES) tools for resolving directly the large scale flame fluctuations and capture the possible instabilities. 
In the present paper, LES of a confined jet and jet flame were performed. Turbulent free jets have been studied 
numerically using LES for about one decade e.g.2 3 4. In the initial part of the jet, close to the nozzle exit, the axial 
velocity along the jet axis remains constant. Further downstream the axial velocity starts to decrease. The decrease 
corresponds to the end of the jet core. LES studies showed the influence of vortex rings that result from Kelvin-
Helmholtz type of instability. They are formed in the shear layer around the jet core and are convected downstream 
until they merge. These vortex rings contribute to the momentum exchange between the jet and the surrounding. 
Since vortex pairing is an efficient way of momentum exchange an increase in momentum transfer is visible after 
pairing. DaSilva and M√©tais4 showed that the inflow conditions can trigger vortex pairing. Consequently, one can 
shorten the vortex core by adjusting the turbulence properties upstream of the expansion. 
Turbulent jet flames are simpler to model than swirling flames but are already very complex systems. 
Additionally to the physical phenomena described in the previous paragraph the thermal expansion due to 
combustion has to be taken into account. Laboratory experiments related to premixed free jet flames (often called 
Bunsen burner) using laser techniques are quite common. However, detailed measurements of both velocity and 
flame properties are much less common. For example Chen et al.5 measured the velocity and the concentration in a 
piloted stoichiometric Bunsen-type flame at ambient temperature and pressure. They observed that the thermal 
expansion decreased the momentum transfer between the core and the surrounding and therefore the jet core became 
longer. An experimental study at gas turbine relevant operating conditions were performed by Griebel et al.6 7 8. He 
investigated preheated lean premixed jet flames at pressures up to 15 bars. 
The present paper uses the setup studied by Griebel et al. 6 7 8 to investigate in detail the influence of combustion 
on a confined jet flame. Firstly, the LES methodology is presented. In the third section, we present the LES 
combustion model used for the simulations. The fourth section describes the combustion chamber and the runs 
performed. The fifth section discusses the accuracy of the LES tool using comparison with experiments and 
sensitivity analysis. It also includes a comparison between different operating conditions. Finally conclusions are 
drawn regarding the flame dynamics and the influence of combustion on the jet turbulence.

The spatial filtering corresponds to a вАЬlow-passвАЭ filter which removes all the Fourier components that have 
shorter length scale than the filter size. The filtering operator is linear and is assumed to be commutative with time- 
and space-derivatives. The filtering operation is not commutative with non-linear terms. Thus, non-linear terms lead 
to expressions that cannot be expressed in terms of the filtered quantities. These terms are gathered on the right-hand 
side in the equations above and called subgrid scale (SGS) term. Expressing these SGS terms in terms of filtered 
quantities is required for вАЬclosingвАЭ the system.  The main role of the SGS terms is to account for the interaction 
between the resolved and the unresolved scales. In the momentum equations, the SGS terms should account for the 
dissipative character of turbulence on the small (unresolved) scales as well as for the transfer of energy among the 
resolved and unresolved scales. Similar SGS properties one may attribute to the SGS in the energy- and the species-
transport equations. 
A computational grid can support only Fourier components that have longer wavelengths than the grid size. 
Thus, a dependent variable that is represented on a grid that is used together with a discrete approximation for the 
derivatives leads to an implicit filtering. If no explicit SGS terms are added, then the numerical scheme should 
account at least for the small scale dissipation. This is attained for any numerically stable scheme. However, too 
dissipative numerical schemes are highly in-appropriate for LES since in addition to dissipation on small scales, they 
may be also dissipative on the larger scales. This effect can be avoided by choosing appropriate (higher order) 
discretizations. In addition, the spatial resolution has to be fine enough, of the order of magnitude of the Taylor 
micro-scale. With such a resolution, the energy transfer among the scales is dissipation independent and therefore 
the numerical scheme may act implicitly as a SGS model. However, one should keep in mind that this implicit SGS 
approach requires a better resolution than a corresponding LES which uses advanced SGS models. Nevertheless, 
even with explicit SGS (such as the dynamic model), the spatial resolution has to be adequate (usually may be 
coarser by a factor of at most 2 compared to the implicit SGS approach). In the present paper, we use a third-order 
upwind scheme10. Using the same scheme, Revstedt et al..11 showed that traditional SGS models do not perform 
significantly better than the implicit approach for simulations of a turbulent jet. Therefore, the implicit approach will 
be used in this paper for closing the filtered momentum transport equations.

IV. 
Computational tools and burner setup 
A. The LES tool 
 
The present formulation has been implemented into a high-order finite difference code that solves the 
incompressible Navier-Stokes equations with variable density on Cartesian grids. The code is third-order accurate 
for the convective terms and forth-order accurate for the other terms. A second order finite difference scheme is used 
for time discretization. Locally refined grids can be employed in regions with large gradients. Multi-grid iterations 
are used to solve the system of equations. More details can be found elsewhere13. Using Cartesian finite differences 
yield easily coded, fast and accurate results. These advantages make the approach most suitable for LES of reacting 
flows.  
 
B. The burner 
 
Non-reacting and reacting flows in a dump combustor where studied. The geometry corresponds to an 
experimental high-pressure test rig located at the Paul Scherrer Institut (Villigen PSI, Switzerland). The generic, 
high-pressure combustor consists of an inlet pipe diameter of Dp=25¬Ј10-3 m discharging in a coaxial pipe of 
diameter 3Dp. The flame is stabilized aerodynamically by the recirculation of hot combustion gases induced by the 
geometry with sudden expansion. The combustor is equipped with high pressure windows to provide an optical 
access to the entire length of the combustor chamber. The turbulence intensity (uвАЩ) and the integral length scale at 
the inlet can be varied by using different turbulence grids (defined by hole diameter and blockage ratio) or by 
mounting these grids at different axial positions within the combustor inlet section. In this study a perforated plate 
with hexagonally located 3 mm (0.12Dp) holes and a blockage ratio of 50 % was used. The grid was located at the 
axial distance of 1.2Dp upstream of the expansion. The turbulent flow field was measured with PIV in the 
isothermal flow field (Griebel et al.7). Detailed experimental data are available for the present jet flame6 8. 
The computational domain starts 1.2Dp upstream of the expansion and ends 12.4Dp downstream of the 
expansion A steady Neumann inflow boundary conditions is used i.e. the inflow is modeled as a collection of small 
jets. Modeling of the incoming turbulence is a difficult but important task for LES computations. Steady inflow

conditions ensure a correct incoming mass flow but do not mimic many features of turbulence. However, the strong 
shear imposed at the inlet generates some turbulence. As we will see later, the turbulent fluctuations level at the 
expansion is predicted correctly. Despite of injecting no turbulence in the domain, we obtain an accurate turbulent 
field upstream of the expansion. Zero gradient outflow boundary conditions are used.  
The computational domain includes the whole 3D geometry. Three different computational grids were tested. 
The coarser grid consists of 1000000 points giving a mesh spacing of ~~1¬Ј10-3 m in the expansion region. The 
medium size grid contained 1500000 points with a refined region close to the expansion with mesh spacing of 
~0.6¬Ј10-3 m. Finally the finer grid contains 2500000 points. It is similar than the medium grid but the locally refined 
region includes a larger part of the domain. The time step was computed using a CFL number ~0.2. 
In the non-reacting case, the inflow bulk velocity was set to 40 m/s giving a Reynolds number of 64000. The 
operating temperature and pressure where respectively 293K and 101325 Pa. 
In the reacting cases, the bulk velocity was kept constant to 40 m/s. A methane/air mixture of equivalence ratio 
0.5 was used. The fresh gas temperature was set to 673 K. The operating pressure is 101325 Pa corresponding to a 
Reynolds number of 24000. The Karlowitz number is ~5. The flame is in the so-called extended flamelet regime but 
not in the flamelet regime. However, Duwig and Fuchs12 showed that the filtered flamelet model presented in 
section III can be also used for accurate simulations within the extended flamelet regime. 
For both reacting and non-reacting cases, the velocity quantities are dimensionless by scaling with the bulk 
velocity of 40 m/s.

A. Non-reacting case

normalized mean axial velocity profiles at 5 different axial locations in the combustor. The jet penetrates into the

the root-mean-square of the axial velocity. The fluctuation levels predicted by all three grids are very similar both in 
magnitude and in shape. In principle one could conclude that all these three grid are suitable for simulating the non

cases that require higher resolution). Consequently, we decide to use the medium grid for the rest of the study.

The predictions performed with the medium grid were compared to available PIV measurements7. Figure 3 
shows radial profiles of the axial velocity. The LES predictions match very well the PIV measurements. The jet 
spreading rates as well as the outer recirculation zone are well captured by LES. The discrepancy between the 
predictions and the experiments is below 5 % which is in the order of the estimated accuracy of the PIV results.  
Figure 4 shows the radial profiles of the RMS of the normalized axial velocity. In general the predictions agree very 
well with the measurements. At the axial positions close to the combustor inlet and at a radius of r/Dp=0.5, the RMS 
values are very high and the profiles show steep gradients. In the shear layer, turbulence is generated due to high 
velocity gradients between the jet core and the recirculation zone leading to high velocity fluctuations in this region. 
In the outer region (recirculation zone) the fluctuations are low. In the core of the jet, the fluctuations are almost 
constant and the fluctuation level is close to 15% of the bulk velocity. Since the core region is not influenced by 
strong shear stresses, the fluctuations originate upstream i.e. are generated by the turbulence grid and are convected 
downstream by the flow. Figure 4 shows that the fluctuation levels predicted by LES are in good agreement with the 
experimental data. This validates our modeling of the inflow boundary condition.

Figure 5 shows a snapshot of the jet core. The core surface is wrinkled by the turbulent structures. Close to the 
expansion, the vertical structures are relatively small and located in the shear-layer. They are responsible for the 
peaks seen on figure 2 and 4. Further downstream, the size of the vertical structures and the characteristic length of 
the wrinkling increase. Therefore the momentum exchange between the jet core and the surrounding fluid is 
amplified and results in an increase of the jet spreading rate as seen in figure 3. Figure 6 shows that the fluctuating 
kinetic energy is concentrated around a finite number of characteristic frequencies. A typical shear-layer instability 
is located for St~0.3. It is characteristic for the turbulence generated in the shear layer and corresponds to the 
structures visualized on figure 5. In addition two peaks at St=0.035 and St=0.07 are visible in figure 6. It is believed 
that these peaks correspond to a slow precession of the jet core around the combustor axis. Since the period of this 
phenomenon is very slow, a phase averaging procedure is not feasible.

The presence of a slow large scale mode is confirmed by studying the axial velocity PDF. Figure 7 shows two 
points located at x/Dp=3. The simulation results are shown on the left hand side whereas the experimental results are 
listed on the right hand side. Firstly, it can be concluded that the LES computation capture very well the shape of the 
measured velocity PDF indicating that the present computation does not only capture the averaged quantities but 
also the nature and magnitude of the fluctuations. The first point is located at the centerline in the jet core. The width 
of the PDF is very narrow and the PDF has a Gaussian shape, indicating that the instantaneous velocity fluctuations 
are simply distributed around the mean value. The fluctuations at this location originate from the grid and are 
convected downstream. The second location lies within the shear-layer close to the end of the jet core. The shape of 
the PDF is rather complex with three peaks. The broad width of the PDF shows also a large fluctuation amplitude of 
the velocity. The particular shape can be explained considering the slow precession of the jet core. During the 
precession, the shear-layer will move and location 2 will be sometimes in the core of the jet (relatively high 
velocity) and sometimes outside of the jet core (relatively low velocity). This particular motion results in two 
distinct states. The superposition of the two states results in the broad PDF with three peaks as presented in figure 7.

B. Influence of combustion on the flow field 
 
Firstly, we consider the reacting case at atmospheric pressure. Figure 8 left shows the instantaneous flame front 
wrinkled by vortical structures. The flame surface is enlarged and the turbulent flame speed is increased by 
turbulence. Close to the expansion, the flame wrinkling is due to small scales. Further downstream, large scale 
wrinkling is seen and the flame shape changes from quasi-cylindrical to a spiral-type. Figure 8 right shows the 
vortex tubes. Close to the expansion, the vortex tubes are relatively small and do not modify significantly the global 
flame pattern. Further downstream, large scale wrinkling occurs as large vortical structures appear. The vorticity 
dynamics is similar to what was observed in the non-reacting case. 
Figure 9 shows a comparison of the axial velocity field for a reacting and a non-reacting case. At the exit of the 
nozzle, the profiles are identical. Farther downstream, the reacting jet core is slightly longer and wider. The 
momentum transport from the jet to the recirculation zone is therefore lower in the reacting case. This phenomenon 
is well known as вАШcounter-gradient transportвАЩ (CGT). It describes the effect of a thermal expansion within a shear-
layer. The differential acceleration of the вАЬcoldвАЭ gases (the jet) and the combustion products (recirculation zone) 
lowers the entrainment of the jet. Consequently in this particular setup CGT slightly decreases the axial expansion of 
the jet core. This phenomenon has also been observed in free jets5. 
Figure 10 left shows the axial velocity PDF in the shear-layer at an axial distance of 3Dp from the expansion. 
The shape of the PDF is not Gaussian and again 3 peaks are visible. Comparing this PDF with the corresponding one 
in figure 7, it is somewhat obvious that the peak at the negative velocity is dominant in the reacting case but not in 
the cold case. The negative velocity peak corresponds to the moments where hot gases are found at this location.

VI. 
Summary 
The present paper reports a numerical investigation of a turbulent confined jet. Numerical simulations of non-
reacting and reacting conditions were performed. The reacting conditions correspond to a lean premixed flame 
relevant for gas turbine applications. Large Eddy Simulation was used to investigate the flow and was supplemented 
with a LES flamelet model for capturing the flame dynamics. The velocity results of the non-reacting case were 
averaged and compared to experimental PIV results. The agreement was excellent for both the mean and the RMS 
values. The numerical simulation showed that the jet core exhibits a slow precession. 
The simulations of the reacting cases showed that the thermal expansion across the flame front lowers the 
momentum exchange between the jet core and the recirculation zone. Consequently, the jet core was found to be 
slightly longer and broader in the reacting case. However, this change in the aerodynamics did not suppress the slow 
precession of the jet core but shifted its frequency. One may argue that the preheating of the fuel/air mixture reduced 
the density jump across the flame front and consequently lowers its effect on the flow field. It highlights the 
relevance of isothermal experiments/simulations for understanding the flow dynamics in combustors and shows the 
possibility to extend this understanding to reacting cases.
