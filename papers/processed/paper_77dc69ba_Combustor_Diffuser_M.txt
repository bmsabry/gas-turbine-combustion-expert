Combustor Diffuser Modeling 
Part V: Validation with a Three Passage Diffuser Rig Data 
 
H. MongiaC, and G. Hsiao, GE Aircraft Engines, Cincinnati,  
MVV Ravindra and PSVS Sreedhar, EACoE, Bangalore 
 
ABSTRACT 
A full-scale 3-passage combustor diffusion system 
has been analyzed with FLUENT code using its grid 
generation package, the standard k-ε model and the 
nonequilibrium wall function. Inlet conditions were 
calculated using through the rig inlet, the three profilers 
that provide center-, inner- and outer-peak profiles at the 
prediffuser inlet. 3-D model predictions were compared 
with data from a full-scale annular diffuser rig. In 
general, CFD gave good agreement with data as shown 
in the following table. 
 
INTRODUCTION 
 
2-D and 3-D CFD calculations are generally done 
during the gas turbine combustion system design process 
to aid in the optimization of the diffusion system. Early 
work by Karki et al1 as well as the follow-on 
publications, e.g., Reference 2 have shown that the 
standard k-ε model with wall functions overpredicts CP 
by 5-15% compared to data. A preliminary assessment of 
various advanced turbulence models indicates3 that SST 
k- ω turbulence model has shown potential for giving 
better agreement with data for flows subject to adverse 
pressure gradients including separation. However due to 
lack of systematic validation of CFD-based approach 
with data of combustor diffusion systems of practical 
interest to designers, the current design process relies on 
empirical data collected on simulated full-scale 3-D 
diffuser rigs coupled with qualitative guidance from 
CFD.  
Prompted by recent advances in CFD simulation 
technology, turbulence modeling4, 5 and availability of an 
excellent data set6 from a typical single passage full-scale 
annular 
diffuser 
rig, 
we 
have 
undertaken 
a 
comprehensive investigation to study the effect of the 
following on CP and Ω: 
 
1. Diffusion system design parameters including 
inlet profiles, pre-diffuser area ratio, L/H, 
cowling, blockages due to dome swirlers, nozzle 
stem and tip, airflow splits between the dome, 
inner and outer annuli of the combustor liner.

Part V, the present paper extends 3-D simulation 
technique of Reference 7 for single passage diffuser to a 
typical 3-passage full-scale annular diffuser rig, Figure 1. 
Similar to the single passage study, the standard k-ε 
model with non-equilibrium wall function5 is used for 
making back-to-back comparison between the three 
prediffuser inlet profiles, namely center-, inner- and 
outer-peak. The diffuser inlet conditions were calculated 
by using through-the-diffuser inlet/profiler calculations 
described in Reference 6. The 3-passage diffuser rig, 
instrumentation and data sets etc. are similar to those of 
single passage set-up of References 6 and 7 which also 
give details on the inlet conditions, turbulence models, 
wall functions, CFD simulation etc.

CFD calculations and comparison with the data 
when available are presented in Figures 2 through 14 for

velocity profiles at the diffuser inlet. Figures 2, 3 and 4 
present results for the prediffuser and dome with the

the combustor outer and inner passages. Comparison 
between data (along x-axis) and CFD calculation along

Discussion on these results follows in the following 
paragraphs. 
Prediffuser Exit and Dome CP and Ω 
 
 
Similar to 3-D calculations for a typical single 
passage combustor diffusion system7, here the data and 
calculations were done for three different prediffuser 
inlet profiles, namely center-, inner- and outer-peak 
profiles. Similarly for a given rig set-up, the flow rates 
through the combustor outer passage, dome and inner 
passage are controlled to document performance of the 
diffusion system. Unlike the single passage prediffuser, 
here one has to estimate the flow splits between the three 
passages of the prediffuser. It is unlikely that 
experimentally estimated splits for the prediffuser 
passages would be identical to those calculated by the 
CFD simulation. Table 4 compares these splits in 
addition to listing the range of the flow splits for the 
combustor dome, outer and inner passages. For example 
for the center-peak profiler, the experimentally estimated 
range for inner passage of the prediffuser is between 
29.74% and 37.65% compared to CFD calculated range 
of 31.00% and 34.72%. Consequently when comparing 
data with CFD for the prediffuser passages in Figure 2, 
one would usually see different ranges of the flow splits 
respectively for the data and model calculations.  
The diffusion system performance with the 
center-peak profiler was measured over a wide range of 
airflow rate through the dome, ranging from ~9%Wa3 to 
~37%Wa3, approximately a factor of 4 or a change of 
74%. Similarly, airflow splits for the inner and outer 
passages 
around 
the 
combustor 
were 
varied 
approximately a factor of 2 or changes of 58% and 56%, 
respectively. However, the resulting airflow split 
changes for the inner, center and passage of the 
prediffuser were only 10.7, 3 and 11.2% respectively. 
Similar observations can be made with the inner- and 
outer-peak profilers. The airflow split changes in the 
center passage of the prediffuser of ~3, 3 and 1% are 
relatively insignificant compared to large changes in the 
airflow splits for the dome, outer and inner combustor 
annuli. On the other hand, the airflow split changes in 
both the outer and inner passages of the prediffuser are 
approximately comparable with average value being 
~9%; which is approximately 1/7th of the flow split 
changes for the dome or 1/5th of that of the passages.  
Figure 2 shows comparison between data and 
CFD predictions for the static pressure recovery 
coefficient CP of the prediffuser exit outer, center and 
inner passages, respectively going from top to bottom 
figures; x-axis is the flow split expressed as % total 
combustor flow, and y-axis value of CP.  In general, 
CFD predictions tend to agree qualitatively except for 
the center-passage where perhaps data is somewhat more 
scattered than desirable. As summarized in Table 3 and 
pictorially presented in Figures 11 and 12, 3-D CFD 
simulation for the center-peak inlet profiler overpredict 
CP for the prediffuser exit inner passage by 14.5%, 
underpredicts for the center passage by ~9% and 
overpredicts the outer-passage CP by ~17%. However, 
for the single passage diffuser7 the corresponding 
overprediction is only 2.2%, Table 2.The model 
discrepancy from the data could be reduced by using an 
advanced turbulence model that accounts appropriately 
for the boundary layer growth in adverse pressure 
gradient. It is expected that the shear stress transport k-ω 
model might be the one to improve prediction capability. 
However, it continues to be difficult to achieve the 
desirable level of numerical convergence with SST. Even 
though experimental accuracy of the total pressure loss 
coefficient Ω is not as good as in 3-passage diffuser as in 
the single passage diffuser7, it is usefully instructive 
anyway to make comparison with CFD as it calculates Ω 
correctly.

From Tables 5 and 6 follow the observations: 
1. Single passage diffusion system is more robust 
to the variations in the prediffuser inlet profiles 
than a typical 3-passage diffusion system. 
2. Prediffuser total pressure losses are higher in 
the 3-passage prediffuser along with lower static 
pressure recoveries in addition to having to 
address the variations among the three passages. 
This means for the same geometric prediffuser area ratio, 
a single-passage diffusion system would give higher CP 
and lower Ω than its counter-part multi-passage diffusion 
system.

Similar to Fig. 2, Figures 3 and 4 present the 
results with inner- and outer-peak profiles. There is no 
need to provide any further elaborations of these figures 
except to refer to the summary provided in Table 3. 
Prediffuser exit CP with inner-peak inlet profile is 
underpredicted by ~14%, 26% and 1% for the inner, 
center and outer passages, respectively; see also Figure 
11. On the other hand for the outer-peak profile, CFD 
overpredicts CP of inner and outer by ~11% and 2%, but 
underpredicts the center-passage by ~14%. When 
averaged for all the three inlet profiles, prediffuser CP 
for the inner and outer passages are overpredicted by 
~4% and 6%, and underpredicted by ~16% for the center 
passage. Even if one questions the experimental accuracy 
of 3-passage diffuser’s CP compared to that of single 
passage, one couldn’t ignore the obvious concern about 
using of the standard k-ε model for such a complex flow 
field. 
 
 
Similar observations and discussion would lead 
to the following summary for the combustor dome CP. 
As summarized in Table 3 and pictorially shown in 
Figures 11 and 12, CFD overpredicts diffuser recoveries 
with the center- and outer-profilers by ~11% and 6%, 
and underpredicts by ~12% with the inner-peak profiler. 
Overall average overprediction for the three profiles is 
~6%.

SUMMARY 
 
A full-scale 3-passage combustor diffusion 
system has been analyzed with Fluent code using its grid 
generation package, the standard k-ε model and the 
nonequilibrium wall function. Inlet conditions were 
calculated using through the rig inlet, the three profilers 
that provide center-, inner- and outer-peak profiles at the 
prediffuser inlet. Predictions were compared with data 
from a full-scale annular diffuser rig. In general, CFD 
gave good agreement with data as shown in the 
following table. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
2-D6 and 3-D calculations7 for single passage diffuser are 
compared with the 3-passage diffuser in regard to errors 
(defined as 1-CFD/Data), average and its standard 
deviation, and root-mean square error for CP of the 
prediffuser, dome, outer and inner combustor annuli 
(passages). Further model validation work is planned 11-
14 to extend the range of application as well as 
improving accuracy.
