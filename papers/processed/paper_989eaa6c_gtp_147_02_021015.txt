Development of a Hydrogen
Microgas Turbine Combustor:
NOx Emissions and Secondary
Air Injection
On the way to defossilization, green hydrogen is a promising way to substitute natural gas
(NG) and oil in the gas turbine industry. In the scope of the H2mGT project, a microgas
turbine (mGT) burner with 100% hydrogen ﬁring is developed and validated. The project is
funded by the German BMWK, and it is a collaboration between Technische Universit€at
Berlin (TUB) and the manufacturer Euro-K GmbH. It consists of three phases: (1)
atmospheric pressure tests with a fused silica combustion chamber; (2) atmospheric
pressure tests with counterﬂow-cooled steel ﬂame tube and secondary air injection; (3)
validation ofthe burner in the mGT atelevated pressure levels. The current study will present
the results of phase 2. The hydrogen burner used in the project is based on a swirl-stabilized
burner of TUB and was scaled to 36 kW thermal power at atmospheric conditions. The
burner design features a variable swirl intensity, additional axial momentum of air in the
mixing tube, a movable central fuel lance, and pilot nozzles at the front plate. Furthermore,
the steel ﬂame tube is exchangeable, which allows the evaluation of different dilution hole
patterns and, thus, the variation of the ratio of primary and secondary air. The study presents
temperature, pressure, and emission measurements. It is found that the ﬂame can be
operated over a large range of equivalence ratios and preheating temperatures up to 500 C.
As expected, the NOx emissions are mainly inﬂuenced by the local equivalence ratio, which
can be controlled by the fuel mass ﬂow or the dilution hole pattern in the ﬂame tube.
Furthermore, the results show a decrease of NOx when the power density is increased at
constant equivalence ratios, and a rise of NOx during the fuel transition from natural gas to
hydrogen. The results indicate certain differences to the ﬁndings of phase 1.

Introduction
The current developments inthe global climate crisis make a rapid
shift away from fossil fuels inevitable. Nevertheless, combustion
engines—especially gas turbines—will continue to be an essential
part of our energy supply thanks to their ﬂexibility and reliability. A
CO2-free alternative to fossil fuels is “green hydrogen” produced
from renewable energies. The joint H2mGT project of Technische
Universit€at Berlin (TUB) and the microgas turbine manufacturer
Euro-K GmbH aims to develop a hydrogen (H2) combustor for a
microgas turbine (mGT).
In the industry, almost all major manufacturers are developing
various combustion systems for syngas and H2-enriched natural gas.
In general, increased NOx emissions and problems with ﬂame
stability are reported, so the H2 capability is still limited at the
commercial level. A recent overview about the developments in the
industry is given by the ETN Global’s Hydrogen Gas Turbines
report [1] and Cecere et al. [2].
The mGT is a perfect platform to investigate H2 combustion on a
machine level since the necessary investment, the operating
pressure, and the exhaust gas temperatures are much lower than
for heavy-duty gas turbines. In addition, mGTs usually offer good
accessibility to the combustion chamber. They provide an
alternative to gas piston engines in the small and medium combined
heat and power segment. They are very ﬂexible and emit
signiﬁcantly less pollutants than piston engines [3]. Modern mGT
combustor systems achieve NOx emissions of 15 ppm, while current
lean-burn engines in the medium power class with oxidation catalyst
emit NOx values of 125–250 ppm. The German Aerospace Center
[3] predicts a large potential for efﬁciency improvements and a
rising share on the combined heat and power market for mGTs. A
review of numerical studies with respect to different mGT-based
energy systems is given by Reale and Sannino [4]. On the
experimental side, mGT combustors have been investigated for a
Turbo Expo, June 24–28, 2024. GT2024.

(1–3 kWel), du Toit et al. [7] measured emissions of H2/CH4/CO2
fuel blends. Adding 15%vol H2 to CH4 showed no signiﬁcant effect
on the NOx emissions. The study also reports the inﬂuence of the fuel

(Capstone C30, 30 kWel), de Santoli et al. [8] tested H2 addition in
natural gas up to 10%vol. In this study, the H2 was used to
compensate the engine derating due to increased inlet temperature in
the summertime. A slight increase of the NOx is reported for the H2
enrichment. The UCI Combustion Laboratory retroﬁtted a Capstone
C60 mGT [9,10] for the operation with H2. Despite adjustments to
the fuel injection, the tests showed signiﬁcantly increased NOx
emissions. Devriese et al. [11] also found high NOx emissions for an
mGT burner with H2 ﬁring in numerical studies. Recently, a T100
microgas turbine was successfully tested with H2 enrichment and
even pure hydrogen by Banihabib and Assadi [12] in Stavanger. The
original combustor was replaced by a fuel-ﬂexible jet-stabilized
burner, which was developed and lab-tested by the German
Aerospace Center in Germany [13]. Stable operation in the mGT
was achieved up to 100% H2. An incline in NOx was observed
resulting in 62 ppm (15% O2) at base load [14].
In summary, there are many challenges in the combustion of H2.
On the one hand, H2 has a lower volumetric energy density (Table 1),
which requires a higher fuel ﬂow. On the other hand, H2 has a high
gravimetric energy density and a signiﬁcantly higher reactivity
compared to natural gas, which might increase the ﬂame
temperature and speed. The former can have a negative impact on
NOx emissions, while the latter imposes a risk of ﬂashback for
premixed ﬂames. These challenges are to be tackled in the present
project with a swirl-stabilized dry low NOx burner with axial air
injection. The TUB has investigated a similar burner in numerous
projects with H2 and enriched natural gas and was able to achieve
very good results regarding the prevention of ﬂame ﬂashbacks and
the minimization of NOx emissions [15–23]. The burner design of
the current project is presented in detail in Sec. 2.
The H2mGT project is divided into three phases. First, the base
combustor is scaled and redesigned according to the requirements of
the mGT. It is then tested in an atmospheric combustion test rig with
a fused silica combustion chamber using OH* ﬂame recording and
emission analysis. In phase 2, the transparent chamber is replaced by
a steel ﬂame tube with counterﬂow cooling and secondary air
injection. Using this setup, the burner is optimized in the test rig
regarding emissions and stable operation. Once this phase is
complete, the burner is adapted for installation in the MTG35
microgas turbine at the Euro-K test facility. The mGT has 130 kW
thermal power (by fuels lower heating value (LHV)), 3.6 pressure
ratio, up to 28% electrical efﬁciency and is recuperated to 500  C
combustor inlet temperature. In phase 3, the pressure is increased
and the new burner is validated under nominal operating conditions.
A previous paper presented the results of the project’s phase 1 [24],
which is followed by the current study, in which the results of phase
2 are analyzed.
2
Burner and Test Rig Design
The burner designed for this project is based on a generic swirl-
stabilized burner, which was already extensively studied at TUB
[15–23]. It was scaled to the thermal power of the mGT by
maintaining similar volumetric ﬂows in the burner. Geometrical
parameters in the burner design are supposed to allow for an
optimization of the operation range and NOx emissions.
Figures 1 and 2 show a schematic of the burner in phase 2. The
preheated air is fed to a preplenum via four inlets downstream of the
combustion chamber. The air then streams as counterﬂow along
the outer ﬂame tube wall. The stainless steel ﬂame tube replaces the
fused silica combustion chamber from phase 1 and allows secondary
air injection through a pattern of dilution holes. Consequently, the
air mass ﬂow is divided into primary air, which takes part in the
combustion, and secondary air, which dilutes the high-temperature
exhaust gas. The primary air ﬂows through the mixing tube
(Dmt ¼ 31 mm) into the combustion chamber (Dcc ¼ 100 mm,
Lcc ¼ 180 mm). It is fed from the plenum in three ways: a tangential
ﬂow (via the swirler), an axial ﬂow in the center (left orange arrows
in Fig. 1) and small wall jets further downstream to lean out the
boundary layer. In the case of the secondary air, the largest part is
directly ﬂowing into the combustion chamber through the dilution
holes in the ﬂame tube. The ﬂow turns 180 deg at the holes and
penetrates as jets in the combustion chamber ﬂow as shown in Fig. 3.
A smaller part is injected parallel to the liner to support the cooling
of the ﬂame tube wall. The actual distribution of the air will be
discussed in Sec. 4.
The fuel can be injected through three different injection points
(numbers in Fig. 2):
(1) Premixed: 16 nozzles (DP1 ¼ 1:4 mm) at the bottom of the
mixing tube, which is regarded as technically premixed,
(2) Central: an axial nozzle (DP2 ¼ 3 mm) fuel lance
(Dcb ¼ 6 mm) in the mixing tube as central injector,
(3) Pilot: eight pilot nozzles (DP3 ¼ 0:8 mm) at the front plate
around the mixing tube exit.
The latter are supplied by a cavity behind the burner plate. The
pilot is intended for ignition in the mGT and as emergency backup
for ﬂashback recovery. The idea of the central injection via the fuel

The three fuel injectors are supplied from a H2 bundle via two mass
ﬂow controllers, one for premixed and one for either central or pilot

The burner features four parameters to alter the ﬂow ﬁeld and fuel
distribution. These parameters are depicted in Fig. 1 and can be
adjusted manually from outside without disassembling the burner.
Hence, the design points in an optimization can be set during the
operation of the burner, which allows for the evaluation of much
more conﬁgurations. The parameters are (letters in Fig. 2):
 S—swirler blockage in mm,
 A—axial injection restriction in # of blocked channels,
 L—length of the center body in mm, and
 P—share of either central or pilot fuel mass ﬂow %.
The swirler consists of eight tangential slots (Ls ¼ 27 mm), which
can be blocked partially (S). The axial air injection ﬂows from a
small subplenum through an annulus around the center body into the
mixing tube. The subplenum is fed by eight radial channels from
the main plenum. These channels can be separately blocked (A). The
fuel lance is movable in the axial direction (L) and can be positioned
between 0 mm and 90 mm, the latter corresponding to the end of the
mixing tube. The variation of this position has two effects. On the
one hand, it changes the ﬂow ﬁeld in the mixing tube and
the combustion chamber because the lance body creates a wake in
the ﬂow. On the other hand, the point of the central fuel injection can
be moved downstream leading to a stronger impact on the fuel
distribution in the combustion chamber. The parameter P is the share
of the central or the pilot fuel injection. Hence, P ¼ 0% corresponds
to the mode, when only the premixed nozzles are activated, while
P ¼ 100% means that all the fuel ﬂows through either the central or
the pilot injection. Please note that central and pilot injections are
never used at the same time.
The ﬂame is ignited by a spark plug in the combustion chamber
56 mm downstream of the front plate. A nozzle at the end reduces the
diameter from the 100 mm combustion chamber to a 80 mm fused
silica tube, which is followed by a water-cooled exhaust tube. Four
type K thermocouples at the end of the nozzle measure the exhaust
temperature at different radii. A small portion of the hot gas is
sampled downstream in the exhaust tube by a water-cooled
multihole probe. A gas analysis unit measures the NOx content
(61 ppm) for emission evaluation.

Measurement Settings
The mGT has a thermal power of 130 kW and a nominal air mass
ﬂow of 1280 kg/h. However, these values are scaled down by
pressure ratio (constant Ma number) since the tests are conducted at
atmospheric pressure. The air mass ﬂow is ﬁxed to _mair ¼ 368 kg/h
for most of the experiments. In the mGT, the total air mass ﬂow at
baseload conditions is ﬁxed by the output of the compressor, but the
air is split into primary and secondary air. Only the primary air ﬂows
through the combustion zone and takes part in the reaction. The local
equivalence ratio of the ﬂame can be varied by changing the splitting
ratio (e.g., changing the hole pattern in the ﬂame tube). In contrast,
the global equivalence ratio remains the same for all ﬂame tubes, but
can be varied via the hydrogen mass ﬂow, as shown in Fig. 4. The
bounds are set to /global ¼ 0:06 and 0.15 or _mH2 ¼ 0:7 and 1.6 kg/h,
respectively. The base load conditions of the mGT correspond to a
/global of 0.10–0.12. The air is preheated to 500  C in the plenum to
match the conditions of the recuperated mGT. The fuel is supplied at
room temperature but the fuel heats up while ﬂowing through the hot
metal body of the burner. The actual fuel temperature of the
premixed nozzles is measured by a thermocouple in the fuel plenum
(Fig. 2) and is shown exemplarily in Fig. 5. The operating conditions
are summed up in Table 3.
The mass ﬂows and temperatures of a typical measurement series
are shown in Figs. 4 and 5. Please note that the air is preheated by two
separate electrical heaters, of which one reaches Tin ¼ 500  C and
the other only 450–460  C. Therefore, Tair refers to the uniﬁed air
temperature in the plenum, which can be even higher than 500  C
for certain operating conditions due to the heat transferred through
the ﬂame tube.
At high exhaust temperatures, the thermocouples start glowing.
Hence, the exhaust temperatures had been corrected for the radiative
heat loss using a model proposed by Prıkopsky [25]. The adiabatic
exhaust temperature shown in Fig. 5 is calculated from the chemical
equilibrium of the global mixture using GRI-MECH 3.0.

Air Distribution
The ﬂow in the burner and the combustion chamber has been
analyzed within phase 1 of the project. Please refer to Tanneberger
et al. [24] for a detailed description of the ﬂow ﬁeld and its
dependence on the geometrical parameters. Figure 2 depicts the
absolute velocity of a sample ﬂow ﬁeld in the combustion chamber.
From the left, the swirled fuel—air mixture enters the combustion
chamber. The ﬂow undergoes vortex breakdown forming an
opening jet and a central recirculation zone. The premixed ﬂame
is supposed to stabilized in the strong shear layers between the
recirculation zone and the high velocity jet (50–80 m/s). The
secondary air injection consists of an axial cooling jet at the ﬂame
tube wall and the ﬂow through the dilution holes, which can be seen
as high velocity streaks in Fig. 3.
The splitting ratio of the air ﬂow into primary and secondary air is
determined by the effective cross sections of different ﬂow paths
inside the combustor, i.e., burner, dilution holes, and cooling slits.
These cross sections are variable due tothe setting of the geometrical
parameters A and S (L has only minor effect) and due to the design of
the ﬂame tube, speciﬁcally the pattern and size of its dilution holes.
To evaluate the ﬂow distribution, the pressure of the cold ﬂow is
measured at the positions p0 to p3 in Fig. 6 with blockages of certain
ﬂow paths. From the measured pressure losses of the different paths,
a model is set up to calculate the ﬂow distribution for each parameter
set and each ﬂame tube. Please note that the model simpliﬁes the real
geometry and neglects the effect of the temperature rise due to the
heat release in the ﬂame. Hence, it resembles only an estimation of
the air distribution. An example distribution is shown in Fig. 6.
In the example, 21% of the air is ﬂowing through the mixing tube
and the reaction zone of the ﬂame as primary air. The remaining 79%
is secondary air and does not take part in the reaction. This
estimation can be used to calculate the local equivalence ratio /local,
which only takes into account the primary air ﬂow, as opposed to
/global that takes the total air mass ﬂow. In phase 1, /P1 is not
differentiated between local and global as there is no secondary air
and therefore both are equal.
The ﬁrst design of the ﬂame tube (V1) is based on the original
ﬂame tube of the mGT and has six lines of dilution holes at different
axial positions. In order to vary the ratio of primary and secondary
air, six different ﬂame tubes have been tested. For V2 to V5 some
lines of holes have been closed to reduce the secondary air. The
selection was done using computational ﬂuid dynamics simulations.
Flame tube V5* features the same open lines as V5 but the double
line of small holes is further upstream. V5** is the same ﬂame tube
as V5* but the cooling slits in the front plate have been closed
resulting in _mK ¼ 0% (Fig. 6). Table 4 lists the ﬂow distribution for
each ﬂame tube along with the resulting local equivalence ratio for a
ﬁxed global one.
The inﬂuence of the geometrical parameters is as follows: The
swirler blockage S, which is used to increase the swirl intensity,
raises the pressure loss and therefore reduces the primary air mass
ﬂow as shown in Fig. 7. The same applies for the reduction of the
axial air injection A, even though the effect is small up to A ¼ 5.
The ﬂow distribution model will be used in Secs. 6 and 7 to
estimate the ﬂow through the mixing tube and, hence, the local
equivalence ratio. However, the ﬂow coefﬁcients have been
recorded at cold ﬂow conditions and without a ﬂame. Therefore,
the local equivalence ratio must be seen as estimation.
A direct inﬂuence of the secondary air injection can be seen in the
temperature proﬁles of the exhaust gas shown in Fig. 8. The proﬁle
of phase 1 without secondary air shows the most homogeneous
temperature distribution. In phase 2, the proﬁles are much more
uneven, with the exception of ﬂame tube V5, which has the lowest
secondary air ﬂow and therefore the lowest ﬂame temperature. It is
also the only ﬂame tube where two rows of downstream holes are
closed, which means that more of the secondary air is injected

Flame Shape and Stability Range
Due to the mGT like setup, there is no direct optical access to the
ﬂame in phase 2 of the project. Therefore, a library of ﬂame shapes
has been recorded in phase 1 using a fused silica combustion
chamber and an OH* ﬁltered camera (see Ref. [24]). Please note that
no secondary air (neither dilution nor cooling) was applied in phase
1. However, since the ﬂames are rather compact, the inﬂuence on the
ﬂame shape should be small. The previous publication [24]
presented a parameter study of the ﬂame shape regarding the
equivalence ratio, the swirler blockage, the axial injection, center
body position, and the share of central fuel injection.
The technically premixed H2/air mixture generally burns in a V-
or M-shape depending on the operating conditions and parameter
settings. Figure 9 shows the shape of two stable ﬂames for different
equivalence ratios. However, geometry parameter changes can alter
the ﬂow ﬁeld as described in Ref. [24], which may trigger ﬂame
ﬂashback. Then, the ﬂame propagates quickly upstream into the
mixing tube. The ﬂame stabilizes itself in the wake of the center
body and keeps burning as a jet ﬂame in the mixing tube and the
combustion chamber. In the event of a ﬂashback under laboratory
conditions, the burner was not damaged and the ﬂame could be
restored by switching to the pilot ﬂame [24].
The ﬂame shapes of the other two fuel injection modes can be seen
in Fig. 10. On the left, 50% of the fuel is injected via the central fuel
lance, which increases the ﬂame intensity in the center. The right
image shows the pilot mode, which consists of eight individual jet
ﬂames.
Figure 11 depicts the operational range of the ﬂame depending on
three of the parameters. The different markers symbolize increasing
levels of fuel share via the fuel lance (P). Hence, points with all ﬁve
markers feature stability for a variety of fuel distributions and are
very robust against ﬂashback. The main driver of instability is the
positioning of the fuel lance (L). For L <¼ 10 mm all ﬂames are
stable, while for higher L the stability declines with increasing swirl
intensity as shown by the dashed line in Fig. 11. Due to ﬂow ﬁeld
changes, L varies the fuel distribution and thus the local equivalence
ratio and ﬂame speed. However, these ﬂow-induced changes in the
concentration ﬁeld are smaller than for a variation of P. Since the
receptivity of ﬂashback is still higher for L than for P, it can be
concluded that the ﬂashback is dominantly triggered by hydro-
dynamic effects. A more detailed description of Fig. 11 includingthe
behavior close to the stability line can be found in Ref. [24].
The utilization of the burner in the mGT does require not only
static but also dynamic stability. During the experiments in phase 1,
the sound pressure levels had been monitored by a microphone in the
exhaust tube. There are no indications of thermoacoustic insta-
bilities under normal conditions at any of the tested parameter
settings.

premixed to central injection increases NOx quickly ﬁrst and then
saturates at approximately P ¼ 50%. Shifting it to the pilot leads to a
slower and more steady increase (Fig. 13). The parameter A and L
have only little inﬂuence on NOx when varied individually.
In phase 2, the burner features secondary air in the form of cooling
and dilution injection as described in Sec. 3. The pattern of dilution
holes in the ﬂame tube determines the air distribution (see Table 4)
and, hence, the local equivalence ratio of the ﬂame. Figure 14 shows
the inﬂuence of ﬁve different ﬂame tubes on the NOx emissions over
the global equivalence ratio. All the emissions increase as expected
with /global. However, the gradient depends strongly on the ﬂame
tube version, i.e., the dilution hole pattern. For high fuel ﬂow the
NOx level of V1 is three times higher than for V5. The change can be
mostly attributed to the declining primary air ﬂow. Figure 15 shows
that the lines for different ﬂame tubes nicely collapse when plotted
over the local equivalence ratio (calculated from the estimated ﬂow
distribution). The collapsed emission lines also align with the
equivalent burner conﬁguration in phase 1, except for the last point.
This indicates that the dilution of the exhaust ﬂow plays a greater
role at large /local, where the adiabatic ﬂame temperature is very
high and secondary air injection leads to rapid cooling and
quenching of NOx generation. The level of dilution does not appear
to have any inﬂuence, as V1, V2, and V4 have the same NOx
emission over /local. The location of injection, on the other side, has
a (small) effect. The ﬂame tubewith the highest emissions over /local
is V3, the only ﬂame tube in which one of the three upstream rows of
holes is closed. This means that, compared to the other ﬂame tubes, a
higher proportion of the secondary air is injected further down-
stream. V2, for example, has a similar primary air ﬂow as V3, but
lower emissions, as more secondary air is blown in closer to the
ﬂame, which leads to faster cooling of the hot exhaust gases. This
effect is more pronounced with larger /local due to the higher
adiabatic ﬂame temperature.

Besides the ﬂame tube design, the burner itself features
geometrical parameters. The swirler blockage shows an interesting
behavior compared to phase 1. All points in Fig. 16 were measured at
constant thermal power and constant global equivalence ratio. An
increase in emissions can be seen at higher swirl, which clearly
contradicts the results from phase 1 (Fig. 12). In phase 1 the NOx
emissions show a reduction from S ¼ 5 mm to S ¼ 19 mm, which
goes along with an increase in the pressure loss of 4 kPa (/P1 ¼ 0:4)
due to the larger swirler blockage. For the same parameter change in
phase 2, the pressure loss only increases by 0.9 kPa (V5) because the
blockage restricts only the ﬂow through the mixing tube and the air
can divert to the secondary air holes. Thus, the further blockage of
the swirler leads to a decline in primary air as shown in Fig. 7. This
increases the local equivalence ratio and, hence, the NOx emissions.
For example, with ﬂame tube V5, /local rises from 0.51 at S ¼ 5 mm
to 0.63 at S ¼ 20 mm with nearly constant emissions. Without the
positive effect of the swirl intensity, such a step in the equivalence
ratio would correspond to a signiﬁcant increase in NOx (Fig. 15).
This effect is stronger the larger the dilution holes in the ﬂame tube,
because the / variation takes place in a higher range of the local
equivalence ratio with a larger gradient of emissions. A similar
effect can be seen with the blockage of the axial air injection
(parameter A). Please note that the effect does not occur in phase 1
because there is no secondary air and all the air ﬂows through the
mixing tube even when the pressure loss increases. This explains the
opposite trend in Fig. 12.
Shifting the fuel from the premixed nozzles to pilot and central
injection in Fig. 17 shows a similar trend as in phase 1 (Fig. 13). For
the central injection the NOx emissions increase quickly due to the
richer center of the ﬂow. The growth saturates at approximately
P ¼ 50%. For the pilot injection, NOx rises slowly ﬁrst, followed by
a sharp step up close to P ¼ 100%. The step appears approximately
at the point, when the air/fuel mixture in the mixing tube reaches the
blow off limit and would not be able to burn on its own anymore. The
remaining hydrogen must be consumed by the pilot ﬂames.
In terms of NOx, the central injection is always worse than
premixed, while the relation between pilot and premixed depends
strongly on the primary/secondary air splitting because the pilot
ﬂame is not inﬂuenced in the same way by the primary air as the
premixed ﬂame. The 100% pilot ﬂame can have even lower
emissions than the premixed ﬂame for high local equivalence ratios.
The reason for the relatively low pilot emissions is to be seen in the
miniaturization into eight single ﬂames with high momentum
injection in the pilot mode (see, e.g., micromixing concept [28]).
The operating conditions offer a second set of parameters besides
the geometrical variations. The relevant parameters for the gas
turbine are the operating pressure, the thermal power and the
preheating temperature. While the pressure is ﬁxed in the
atmospheric pressure test rig, the thermal power can be varied in
two ways. First, the fuel mass ﬂow is increased at constant air mass
ﬂow resulting in a rise of the thermal power level and the global/
local equivalence ratio. This type of variation is shown in Figs. 14
and 15 and displays increasing NOx emissions. The second way is to
hold the equivalence ratio constant and increase the air and fuel mass
ﬂow at the same time. Figure 18 shows that this way the emissions
drop with rising thermal power. The oxygen concentration measured
in the exhaust indicates that the equivalence ratio was not perfectly
constant during the measurement series. However, this can be
corrected (dashed line) and has only a small share in the observed
decline. One reason for the decreasing NOx could be that the higher
ﬂow velocity improves the premixing of air and hydrogen leading to
a more uniform mixture proﬁle in the ﬂame which would reduce
NOx. The Reynolds numbers in the mixing tube increase from
35, 000 to 76, 000 for the 500  C series. However, the same
declining NOx trend also appears for the pilot and central ﬂame,
which makes it unlikely that it is solely a mixing phenomenon.
Another factor for the NOx generation is the residence time in the hot
zone. The large amount of dilution air in this combustor
conﬁguration (78%) conﬁnes the hot zone spatially. The higher
transport velocity at higher power reduces the residence time in the
hot zone and possibly leads to earlier quenching of the NOx
generation.

Emissions Fuel Mixtures
The scope of the project is to fuel the entire run of the mGT with
H2 in phase 3. However from a scientiﬁc and technological
perspective, it might be also interesting to test mixtures of H2 and
NG in the mGT. Therefore in phase 2, the burner is tested in the test
rig with natural gasas well. H2and NG are mixed inthe supply lineto
the burner with respect to a constant thermal power level and
constant exhaust temperature. These tests have been done in phase 1
and phase 2 with different results.
The ﬁndings of phase 1 regarding fuel mixtures have been
reported in Ref. [24]. Figure 19 shows the evolution of NOx
emissions from 100% NG to 100% H2 for a constant burner
conﬁguration. When normalized using the fuel-corrected method
(Eq. (1)), the NOx emissions show no signiﬁcant incline from NG to
H2. The three points with 100% H2 correspond to /P1,H2 ¼ 0:4, 0.5,
and 0.6, the three 100% natural gas points to /P1,NG ¼ 0:52, 0.64,
and 0.79.
A similar series was recorded in phase 2 with ﬂame tube V5* and
the same burner settings as in phase 1. It is shown in Fig. 20. Please
note that the exhaust temperatures are lower due to the secondary air
injection in phase 2. Nevertheless, the local equivalence ratios are
higher with /local,H2 ¼ 0:53, 0.61, and 0.76 and /local,NG ¼ 0:71,
0.77, and 0.96, respectively. In contrast to phase 1, the emissions are
now rising. Even though all three curves indicate higher NOx for H2
compared to NG, they show slightly different behaviors. For the
coldest ﬂame, the emissions decline ﬁrst before rising. The middle
one increases linearly and the hottest ﬂame picks up strongly up to
20%m and then similarly to the others.
The three measurement series lie in different ranges of
equivalence ratios, which might be the reason for the different
behavior. For clariﬁcation, additional series with constant mixtures
but varied equivalence ratios have be measured by increasing the
fuel mass ﬂow at constant air ﬂow for each line. Since H2 and NG
have different stoichiometry, these lines are plotted in Fig. 21 over
the adiabatic exhaust temperature instead of the equivalence ratio.
However, each line still presents an increase in the local equivalence
ratio. For H2 and the H2 enriched mixtures, Fig. 21 shows the
expected nonlinear rise of NOx. The pure NG curve, however,
exhibits a linear increase, which cannot be expected, especially
when reviewing literature data for similar burners [16,17]. One
possible explanation is that the size of the natural gas ﬂame, which is
less compact than the enriched ﬂames (see Ref. [24]), interacts with
the dilution air leading to reduced NOx at high power. This is also
indicated by a comparison of ﬂame tube V5 and V5* (earlier dilution
holes), which show the same NOx values with H2 but lower NOx for
V5* with NG (Fig. 21). Please note that the NG ﬂames could not be
measured for lower Tad than shown in the plot due to blow-off, while
the H2-enriched ﬂames could be operated even well below
Tad ¼ 650  C, showing the large operating range of the H2 ﬂame.
To investigate the opposing H2–NG behavior of phases 1 and 2, it
was tried to reproduce the ﬁndings of phase 1 by testing a ﬂame tube
without dilution holes (expect one for the spark plug) and without
cooling air in the phase 2 setup. The increase in NOx for H2
enrichment is still present for this conﬁguration and has a similar
gradient as the 743  C line in Fig. 20, which means that the
secondary air injection seems not to be responsible for the different
behavior between phase 1 and phase 2. Since this conﬁguration and
the phase 1 measurements differ mainly in the combustion chamber
wall material and its surrounding, the difference might be caused by
heat transfer and radiation properties. However, this point needs
further investigation.
Figure 20 shows an increase in NOx over the whole range of H2
mass fraction. In the literature, H2 enrichment often refers to the H2
share involume fraction. Ifthe same data are plotted overthe volume
fraction, the NOx increase occurs mainly above 80%v, which many
studies do not reach. Hence, the mass fraction with a steadier
gradient might be the better metric to investigate the inﬂuence of H2
enrichment.

Conclusion
The paper presents the results of the second phase of the H2mGT
project, which aims to develop and validate a hydrogen burner for a
microgas turbine. In phase 2, the burner is investigated at
atmospheric conditions in a machine like setup featuring the
injection of dilution air. It is shown that the burner can be stably
operated for multiple parameter settings with 100% hydrogen
fueling. The inﬂuence of four geometric parameters as well as the
ﬂame tube’s dilution hole pattern on the ﬂame is presented. The size
of the holes deﬁnes the ratio of primary and secondary air and, thus,
the ﬂames local equivalence ratio. This has a large inﬂuence on the
NOx emissions and the exhaust temperature proﬁles, both getting
better with increasing primary air ﬂow. The limiting factor here is
the pressure loss of the burner, which also increases due to the
smaller dilution holes. Reviewing the temperature proﬁles and
emission data, ﬂame tube V5 seems to be best suited for the
integration into the mGT in phase 3. It features around one-third less
dilution hole area than the original ﬂame tube of the mGT, which is
used tofor the combustionof NG anddiesel (with adifferent burner).
Its pressure loss is about 1.3 kPa higher than ﬂame tube V1.
An interesting aspect of the project is the comparison of phase 1
and phase 2: the fused silica combustion chamber setup (often used
in combustion research) and the burner with secondary air injection.
The NOx analysis showed signiﬁcant difference between both
setups. First, the inﬂuence of the swirler blockage is reversed
because it triggers a shift in ﬂow distribution, which has a larger
effect on NOx than the swirl intensity. A second change can be found
in the fuel transition from natural gas to hydrogen. While in phase 1,
NOx remained nearly constant, an increase was measured in phase 2.
The magnitude of this increase depends on the local equivalence
ratio and gets larger with higher fuel mass ﬂow. In each case, the
strongest gradient occurs above 80%v H2 by volume.
At the end, the ignition and start-up process was successfully
conducted at atmospheric conditions in preparation of the mGT
tests, which will take place in phase 3 of the project.
