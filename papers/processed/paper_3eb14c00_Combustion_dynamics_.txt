Abstract
The methodology for the measurement of dynamic combustor behavior has never been clearly established,
due to the complexities associated with unsteady premixed ﬂames and the difﬁculties in their measurement. The
global and local distribution of Rayleigh index and the ﬂame response functions are the main parameters normally
employed to quantify and describe combustion dynamics. The Rayleigh index quantiﬁes the thermoacoustic cou-
pling, while the ﬂame response function is a measure of the response of the system to outside disturbances. The
primary objective of this work is to investigate the combustion dynamics of a commonly used low-swirl burner
and to develop tools and methods for examining the dynamics of a combustion system. To this end, the effect of
acoustic forcing at various frequencies on ﬂame heat release behavior has been investigated. The current work
uses OH–PLIF imaging of the ﬂame region to produce phase-resolved measurements of ﬂame behavior at each
frequency. The response of the ﬂame to the imposed acoustic ﬁeld over the range of 22–400 Hz is then calcu-
lated from the processed images. This provides a starting point for an extension/extrapolation to practical acoustic
ranges (∼5000 Hz). It was found that the thermoacoustic coupling was mainly evident in the shear mixing zone,
producing a toroidal Rayleigh index distribution pattern. The phase shift of the ﬂame ﬂuctuation from the imposed
acoustic wave seems to be very closely coupled to the vortices generated at the ﬂame boundary due to shear mix-
ing (Kelvin–Helmholtz instability), thus inducing the alternating toroidal structures. The peak value of the ﬂame
response function coincides with the peak absolute value of the Rayleigh index.

Most experimental work characterizing combus-
tion instability has been performed on naturally un-
stable systems. A study of the acoustic coupling be-
tween fuel injectors and an applied acoustic ﬁeld was
carried out by Torger et al. [4], but it only consid-
ers cold-ﬂow experiments. Work by Chen et al. [5]
with premixed ﬂames was speciﬁcally designed to
simulate solid rocket propellants. Results were lim-
ited to one spatial dimension and only two forcing
frequencies were used. The study by Cadou et al. [6]
employed a two-dimensional dump combustor that
showed little response to nonresonant forcing. Durox
et al. [7] and Schuller et al. [8] measured the ﬂame
response to forced acoustic oscillations at frequen-
cies up to 400 Hz, but the burner was placed in the
open air, and the response function was based on the
pressure and velocity measurements. They measured
the CH chemiluminescence without attempting any
phase resolution. Despite these efforts, work is still
required to understand the physical underpinnings of
this process and to provide industry with guidelines
that will be useful in designing stable combustion sys-
tems.
In works by Dowling et al. [9], Bloxsidge et al.
[10], and Langhorne [11], a general theoretical model
was constructed based on experimental measurements
of the “reheat buzz,” which is a combustion instabil-
ity observed in a combustor with conﬁned geometry.
These studies laid the foundation for the next step—to
use the data as the fundamental reference for theo-
retical modeling. However, these experiments did not
include a forced acoustic excitation, which is crucial
in determining the dynamic response of the system.
Also, the lack of spatial resolution in previous mea-
surements means that the resulting data are primarily
useful only for one-dimensional models, which are
too simplistic for many of the issues of interest.
Previous measurements of ﬂame intensity (chemi-
luminescence from all species) has been used for
both control and modeling purposes. It is based on
a line-of-sight measurement rather than a spatially
resolved local measurement. Since radiation may be
absorbed, the ﬁnal intensity at the observation point
does not in general represent the local reaction rate.
Pun et al. [12], showed that misleading results can be
obtained from chemiluminescence data.
Chemiluminescence speciﬁcally from the CH rad-
ical has been used to mark the reaction zone (al-
though it suffers from line-of-sight limitations) and
has been used by a number of researchers to study
heat release in unsteady ﬂames. These studies can be
categorized into two groups: measurements using a
photomultiplier tube with a slit that obscures a por-
tion of the ﬂame to obtain some spatial resolution,
typically in the axial direction [5,13,14], and fully
two-dimensional imaging using CCD-based cam-
eras [1,15,16]. Of these works, only Chen et al. [5]
acoustically forced a ﬂame, but they used a photo-
multiplier tube with a slit conﬁguration that obtained
only one-dimensional information.
One of the ﬁrst demonstrations of planar laser-
induced ﬂuorescence (PLIF) of the hydroxyl radi-
cal (OH) in a ﬂame was performed by Dyer and
Crosley [17]. PLIF has been used to identify a variety
of species in unsteady reacting ﬂows, including OH
(to measure the heat release rate [18,19]) and NO that
was seeded into the fuel in order to measure the tem-
perature ﬁeld [6]. Cadou et al. [6] and Pun et al. [12]
applied OH–PLIF to achieve spatial resolution in their
investigation of the dynamics of a forced combustion
system.
Najm et al. [20] showed that the concentration of
OH is not directly correlated with the heat release
rate. However, for the ﬂame considered in the present
study (described below), and for the case of small per-
turbations to local ﬂame intensity, there is reason to
believe that use of the OH–LIF signal is an appropri-
ate way to quantify heat release rates. This belief is
due to (1) the assumption that small changes in the
OH–LIF signal are proportional to the small changes
in local heat release and (2) the assumption that the
data processing involved in calculating the Rayleigh
index uses only the changes in the OH–LIF signal
that occur in the ﬂame zone and not the steady post-
ﬂame signal. The logic of Part 1 is as follows: the
intensity of the OH–LIF signal is proportional to the
ﬂuorescence quantum yield, which in turn is propor-
tional to the number density of the OH radical. The
local number density is proportional to the reciprocal
of the local temperature. Therefore the spatial distri-
bution of the OH–LIF signal is representative of the
local heat release, since the OH–LIF signal is stronger
where the local temperature is higher. The analysis
method (Part 2) is based on calculating the spatially
ﬁxed temporal ﬂuctuation in the OH signal. The post-
ﬂame regions are unchanged on the time scale of the
pressure ﬂuctuation used to identify changes in ﬂame
intensity/heat release. This means that the Rayleigh
index will have a value of zero for all of the purely
thermally produced OH regions.

OH–PLIF measurements, the current series of experi-

measurement of a spatially resolved Rayleigh index

the combustion process under acoustic excitations is

quantiﬁed. The ﬂame base behavior and NO produc-

uses phase-resolved OH–PLIF measurement of the
ﬂame region to measure ﬂame behavior at each fre-

of the ﬂame to the imposed acoustic ﬁeld over the
range of 22–400 Hz is then determined, to provide
data that are expected to serve as a starting point for
combustion system designs and for numerical estima-
tions of combustor behavior up to the higher practical
levels (∼5000 Hz). OH–PLIF is believed to give the
best temporal and spatial information available at the
present time, and it is superior to chemiluminescence
techniques [12]. The current work is limited to 400 Hz
due to experimental limitations, but it is expected that
the ﬂame response will decrease above 400 Hz and
the trends observed at low frequencies are expected
to yield insight about the trends expected at higher
frequencies.
The objective of this work is to determine the
global and local values of the ﬂuctuations of local
energy release rate (based on the OH–PLIF measure-
ments) and to demonstrate the effectiveness of the
experimental methodology. The measurements pro-
vide the basis for constructing a model of the source
term ˙Q′r, which is a primary parameter in combustion
dynamics models (Culick [21]). By “primary para-
meter,” it is meant that ˙Q′r appears as a source of
unsteady motions in the equations for ﬂow in a com-
bustor and therefore can be given a direct physical
interpretation. An accurate representation of ˙Q′r is es-
sential to understanding the presence of combustion
instabilities and, accordingly, how they can be either
avoided or eliminated in practical systems. In general,
˙Q′r must be known to analyze any problem of com-
bustor dynamics. To examine the impact of other key
parameters on this problem, the behavior of ﬂames at
different equivalence ratios is also compared. Three
different equivalence ratios (0.5, 0.6, and 0.75) were
used, which correspond to leaner, desired, and richer
operation conditions for the swirl burner, respectively.
All three values of equivalence ratio are within the
normal operating range of the burner.
2. Experimental conﬁgurations
2.1. Laser system
The experimental layout is shown in Fig. 1. The
PLIF system employs an Nd:YAG pulsed (10-Hz)
laser (Continuum Powerlite 9010) pumping a tunable
dye laser (Continuum ND6000), which in turn drives
a custom-made mixer/doubler system. The 532-nm
beam out of the doubler pumps the dye laser. Use
of Rhodamine 590 for the dye laser dissolved in
methanol optimizes the conversion efﬁciency near
564 nm (∼200 mJ/pulse), which is then doubled
to approximately 282 nm to excite the A–X (1, 0)
band of OH. Slightly over 35 mJ/pulse of energy is
provided by this system, resulting in saturated ﬂuo-
rescence.
2.2. Detection system
The detector for the ﬂuorescence signal is an in-
tensiﬁed CCD camera (Princeton Instruments), which
uses a 512 × 512 Thomson CCD array, operated
with a gate width of 100 ns. Attached to the cam-
era is a catadioptric, all-reﬂective f/1.2 UV lens with
a focal length of 105 mm, providing a spatial res-
olution of 174 µm. The lens provides exceptionally
high light throughout, as well as minimizing spheri-
cal and chromatic aberrations. A lattice electro optics
(LEO) bandpass ﬁlter (312.6F10-10) was installed
in the ICCD camera to cut off all undesired light,
such as that from the scattered pump laser beam,
while transmitting the ﬂuorescence that occurs at
300–320 nm. One thousand eight hundred images
were taken for each set (single equivalence ratio) of
data and these were phase-binned to give a phase-
resolution of 10◦.
2.3. Postprocessing
Since the images are taken at random values of the
phase angle of the driving pressure wave, the cam-
era gating signal is recorded by the data acquisition
system along with the pressure signal to enable appro-
priate postprocessing. Postprocessing involves sort-
ing of the images according to the phase of imposed

2.4. Chamber and acoustics
A schematic view of the acoustic chamber is
shown in Fig. 1. The acoustic chamber is made of
an aluminum central section and a stainless steel top,
with the top housing the loudspeakers (Cerwin-Vega,
Vega 124) used for system excitation and enabling di-
rect exhaust of the product gases. The exhaust port
is open to the atmosphere which creates an acousti-
cally open exit condition for acoustic wave resonance.
The bottom of the chamber creates a closed-end
acoustic condition. The speakers are 30 cm in di-
ameter and powered by a 1000-W power ampliﬁer
(Mackie M1400i). A function generator (Wavetek
171) provides the input signal. The speakers create
pressure and velocity oscillations in the combustion
chamber (Fig. 1) with a peak-to-peak pressure inside
the chamber of 0.1 ± 0.01% of the atmospheric pres-
sure.
The amplitude of the fundamental driving mode
(the acoustic waves) is actively controlled by custom-
designed electronics, which measure the pressure in
the acoustic chamber at the burner height with a
pressure transducer (PCB 106B50) and then scale
the power provided to the speakers. The signal from
the transducer is notch-ﬁltered to ensure that the in-
tended driving mode is correctly ampliﬁed or attenu-
ated.
2.5. Fuel/air feed system
A Linde air-ﬂow meter/controller unit (18C-202-
4125; ∼50 slpm) and a Hastings ﬂow meter/controller
unit (HFC-203A; ∼300 slpm) were used to provide
a steady ﬂow of air and methane to the premixer.
The equivalence ratios, φ = 0.5, 0.6, and 0.75, were
selected based on the recommendation of the de-
signer (Dr. Robert Cheng, Lawrence Berkeley Liver-
more Laboratory). The total volume ﬂow rate is about
120 slpm, which corresponds to an exit velocity of
4 m/s out of the nozzle (see Table 1 for experimental
details).

Axial velocity distribution of a jet ﬂow out of
the swirl burner was measured by Cheng et al. [22]
at locations indicated in Fig. 4 (that work used a

the dotted line (the ﬂame border on Fig. 4), the

pected that vortex structures form due to the differ-

and combustion-induced density and baroclinic ef-

Figs. 6–8 show the circular patterns in the spa-
tial distribution of the Rayleigh index in the region
that are expected to be the shear mixing zone (from
comparison with the axial velocity distribution [22]).
These circular structures are believed to be slices of
toroidal structures in 3-D. Each toroid is character-
ized by the same sign of Rayleigh index thus forming
islands of similar tendency toward instability. It is in
the leaner part of the ﬂame that the toroidal struc-
tures are observed due to the shear entrainment of
the outer ﬂow (air in this case). Where the ﬂow is
lean, it is more susceptible to combustion instabil-
ities because the values of the Rayleigh indices are
larger.
For this system, it seems that the thermoacoustic
coupling (Rayleigh index) is linked to shear-layer in-
stability (Kelvin–Helmholtz). Vortex ring structures
have been observed to grow in the jet boundary
[26–29]. The driving force for formation of these
vortical structures is the axial component of the ﬂuc-
tuating ﬂow velocity due to acoustic excitations [26]
and the shear layer formed with the outer ﬂow.
Ganapathisubramani et al. [30] performed PIV mea-
surements of a cold-swirl-jet ﬂow and showed that
there exists an alternating circular pattern of vortical
cores and straining region. Numerical simulations of
Ghoniem et al. [29] showing that the unstable shear
layer, rather than the acoustic ﬁeld, acts as the ther-
moacoustic resonator. They report patterns similar to
those observed in Figs. 6–8 in their shear regions.
The toroid structure is located in the shear layer,
where the fuel/air mixture is mixed with the outer
air ﬂow. The mixture becomes even leaner and forms
weaker heat release rate zones at the ﬂame bound-
ary. This would result in the Rayleigh distribution
following the shape of the vortical distributions.
Though the ﬂame intensity is greater toward the cen-
ter (Fig. 4), the central region is not much affected by
the driving acoustic waves, in terms of the Rayleigh
index (Figs. 6–8).
The global Rayleigh index (Fig. 9) has signiﬁ-
cant values only when the jet core region is ther-
moacoustically coupled such as at 210 Hz (bottom
right, Figs. 6–8). The majority of the ﬂow is the axial
direction (hence “low” swirl), and thus most of the
energy is released in the central region. The major
contribution toward instability comes from the fuel jet
core; the global Rayleigh index and ﬂame response
functions are signiﬁcant only at frequencies at which
the fuel jet core is thermoacoustically coupled to the
imposed acoustic oscillations. At this conditions the
contributions from the toroidal structures are likely to
cancel each other.

The occurrence of the observed toroid structures is

by Figs. 10–13. If these toroidal structures are actu-

ﬂow ﬁeld measurements in the future), then the for-

to acoustic excitation. That is, the vortex structures
created by the shear mixing are modulated by the im-
posed acoustic excitations and they appear to control
the combustion response for this type of low-swirl
burner.
The size of the toroidal structures becomes smaller
and smaller until the acoustic excitation reaches a
given frequency of about 100 Hz. The number of
toroidal structures in the ﬂame region is an indica-
tor of the frequency of the toroid formation in the
ﬂow ﬁeld. It does not appear that these structures are
the result of vortex shedding, since they initially grow
in strength before weakening downstream. As shown
in Figs. 6–8, the spatial frequency of toroid occur-

of the equivalence ratio. Values of the local Rayleigh
index were determined at locations along the peaks
and troughs of the toroidal patterns (the dotted line in
Fig. 4), and these values are presented in Figs. 10–12.
Figs. 10–12 show how the spatial distribution of

variables q′ and qmean are calculated from the signal
recorded at each pixel.

3.4. Distribution of local ﬂame response function
Using Eq. (3), the local response function was cal-
culated in the same manner as the global response.
The primary difference is that instead of integrating
over the entire imaged region, the values at each pixel
are reported. The local maximum of the Rayleigh in-
dex coincides with the phase shift of 0◦or 360◦, while
the local minimum coincides with a phase shift of ap-
proximately 180◦, as was seen in Fig. 15.
Comparing the local Rayleigh index distribution
and the spatial distribution of the magnitude of the
ﬂame response function, we conclude that (1) large
values of the local response function are concentrated
in the shear mixing zone, which is along the ﬂame
boundary, and (2) the local phase distribution coin-
cides with local peaks (at 0◦or 360◦) and troughs (at
180◦) of the Rayleigh index.
4. Conclusion
Two-dimensional images of the local Rayleigh in-
dex were measured using OH–PLIF in a low-swirl
burner developed by Cheng. The Rayleigh index im-
ages were found to have a distinct feature: a series
of toroidal structures was observed in the shear layer
mixing zone. This indicates that thermoacoustic cou-
pling is signiﬁcant in the shear-layer region near the
ﬂame boundary. The coupling is believed to be due to
the shear entrainment of ambient air, which dilutes
the already lean initial fuel/air mixture and makes
those regions more unstable. The toroidal structures
become wider as the equivalence ratio is increased
and appear to grow outward and extend to locations
that are leaner and more susceptible to instabilities.
The toroidal structures do not appear to be caused
by the swirl created by the burner; if they were cre-
ated by swirl they would be asymmetric and would be
convected by the spiraling ﬂow ﬁeld. The structures
are observed to be perfectly symmetric over all exci-
tation frequencies and equivalence ratios. This leads
to the conclusion that the driving force for the for-
mation of this structure is not the swirling tangential
and radial ﬂows, but is the axial component of the
ﬂow ﬁeld that exerts shear stress on the outer air ﬂow.
The toroidal structures appear to be closely coupled to
the excitation frequencies. For this reason, the mea-
sured trends in the local and global ﬂame response
function, as acoustic forcing frequency is varied, are
similar to the trends measured for the Rayleigh in-
dex.
