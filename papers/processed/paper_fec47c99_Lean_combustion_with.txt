Lean Combustion with Near-Critical Swirling Flows
By Zvi Rusakl and Ashwani K. Kapila2
Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, Troy, New York 12180-3590
Abstract
The dynamics of a lean-premixed combustion
state in a straight circular tube with an un-
derlying low Mach number near-critical ax-
isymmetric swirling flow is studied. A small-
disturbance approach is developed to carefully
explore the nonlinear interaction between the
flow swirl and the heat transfer through the
combustion. The analysis shows that in the
leading order the reaction problem can be de-
coupled from the flow problem. However, the
effect of the heat addition on the flow behavior
is important and has a nonlinear influence. It
is found that a small but finite heat addition to
the flow changes the transcritical bifurcation of
solutions of the Euler equations for a cold (non-
reactive) flow into two branches of solutions of
the reactive flow equations. These branches
fold at limit swirl levels near the critical swirl
resulting in a finite gap of swirl that separates
the two branches. This suggests that no near-
columnar equilibrium state can exist for an in-
coming flow with swirl close to the critical level
and the flow must develop large disturbances
and a vortex breakdown process in this swirl
range. Also, the critical swirl for a reactive flow
is less than that for a cold flow. Beyond this
range of swirl, two equilibrium states may ex-
ist under the same inlet/outlet conditions and
combustion state. One which describes a near-
columnar state, and another which shows the
appearance of a large-amplitude solitary wave.

I. Introduction
Combustion is susceptible to a variety of desta-
bilizing influences (Williams,l Kapila,2 Gut-
mark et al.,3'4 McManus et al.,
5 
Paschereit
et al.6). These can either be intrinsic to the
mode of combustion, or arise as a result of in-
teraction between the combustion process and
its flow environment. A manifestation of in-
trinsic instability is the spontaneous develop-
ment of cellular structures in flames. Instabili-
ties induced by the coupling between combus-
tion and the underlying flow are exhibited by
reactive shear layers and jets, while acoustic
instabilities in propulsion devices, and flame
acceleration prior to possible transition to det-
onation in closed vessels, are examples where
confining boundaries and obstacles in the path
of flames play crucial roles. In technological
applications, instabilities can have an adverse
effect (e.g., damage or flame extinction), a fa-
vorable effect (e.g., enhanced burning) or be of
no consequence.
Instabilities in which combustion interacts
with the underlying flow, the boundaries, or
with processes occurring in other parts of the
system can involve a broad range of phys-
ical mechanisms, including acoustic oscilla-
tions, vortex-shedding, and deformation of the
flame zone. One may not have a simple, basic,
steady configuration that can be subjected to
the standard techniques of stability analysis.
The problem is particularly challenging when
the underlying inert flow is itself susceptible

to instabilities, such as a swirling flow. It is
not at all clear then what the role played by
combustion is, and how the process of combus-
tion itself is affected as a result of the intrinsic
instability of the underlying flow.
Swirling flows are of practical importance
in enhancing the efficiency of certain lean-
premixed combustion systems. A promising
technique is to induce axisymmetric vortex
breakdown in the incoming flow of a mixture
of air and fuel (Lefebvre7). This technique is
used in various industrial combustors such as
those designed by Pratt and Whitney, Ellison,
and ABB (see, for example, McVey et al.,8
Snyder et al.,9 Stephens et al.,10"12 Wang
and Yang,13 and Paschereit et al. 14~15 ). The
technique is based on the idea that when the
swirl ratio of the flow is above a certain criti-
cal level, a large and nearly stagnant axisym-
metric separation region appears near the in-
let of the combustion chamber. In the case
of a premixed flux of air and fuel, the burned
particles are trapped in the breakdown zone
where they create a region of high tempera-
ture. Heat transfer from this hot region to the
surrounding swirling flow apparently helps to
stabilize the flame and burn more of the re-
actants, thereby improving combustion effec-
tiveness (Sivasegaram and Whitelaw16). In
the cases where liquid fuel is injected into a
swirling flow of air, the hot breakdown zone
serves as a noninvasive flame holder where fuel
particles are trapped for longer residence time
and mixing between air and fuel is improved.
However, a major problem in inducing the
axisymmetric vortex breakdown in combus-
tion states is the appearance of flow insta-
bilities inside the large separation zone and
in its wake (see, for example, Syred et al.,17
Gupta et al.,18 Gupta et al.19) At certain
critical situations, the breakdown zone itself
may become unstable and disappear because
of changes in the distribution of swirl of the
incoming flow, variations in the temperature
field, or large perturbations in the back pres-
sure at the downstream of the combustor. In
such situations, the fluid mechanical instabil-
ities also induce flame instabilities and affect
the combustor performance. Gupta et al.18'19
discussed different modes of instabilities gen-
erated in combustion with swirl.
It is therefore clear that a detailed study of
the dynamical behavior of combustion systems
with swirl is needed. A theoretical study of
such systems is crucial for a better physical
understanding and mathematical modeling of
the mechanisms underlie the instabilities and
the critical conditions for their appearance. It
may also provide new strategies to improve the
control of the combustion system.
As a topic in fluid mechanics, vortex break-
down boasts an extensive literature, including
reviews by Hall,20 Leibovich,21 Escudier,22
Sarpkaya,23 Althaus et al.,24 and Rusak.25
However, a consistent description of the phe-
nomenon has proved elusive. Several possible
explanations have been advanced, each clari-
fying some aspects of the problem. The re-
lationship between various theoretical and nu-
merical solutions has never been fully clarified.
Until recently, no clear criteria for the occur-
rence, stability and dynamics of vortex break-
down states were given.
Recently, Rusak and co-authors have devel-
oped a novel theoretical framework for predict-
ing the axisymmetric vortex breakdown pro-
cess (see Wang and Rusak,26"29 Rusak and
Wang,30 Rusak et al.,31-33 and Rusak34'35).
This approach, employing the Euler and
Navier-Stokes formulations, examines the dy-
namics of axisymmetric swirling flows in an
axisymmetric chamber of finite length. The
results, established through a rigorous nonlin-
ear global analysis, provide a fundamental and
nearly complete mathematical description of
the dynamics and stability of inert axisymmet-
ric swirling flows. The results show a good cor-
relation with numerical computations and ex-
perimental studies (Malkiel et al.36 and Rusak
et al.32'33). 
It is also found that the the-
ory naturally unifies the major previous the-
oretical and numerical approaches to the ax-

The approach of Wang and Rusak 28 shows
that in the inviscid flow limit, there exist two
critical swirl ratios of the incoming flow to the
pipe, o;o and uj\, where UQ < u\. Columnar
flows with a swirl ratio less than the thresh-
old level, ujQ , are unconditinally stable to any
axisymmetric disturbance. In the range UQ <
uj < ui the flow may evolve into one of two
steady states, depending on the size of the
initial disturbances. When disturbances are
sufficiently small they decay in time and the
flow returns to be columnar but, when distur-
bances are large, they grow and evolve into a
large and steady, semi-infinite stagnation zone,
similar to the breakdown states found in very
high Reynolds number flows. When u > cui,
any initial disturbance grows and evolves into
a breakdown zone.
These special stability characteristics are re-
lated to the upstream propagation of both
small- and large-amplitude disturbances and
their interaction with the flow conditions
downstream of the vortex generator. The dis-
turbances tend to propagate upstream with a
speed that increases with uj. When u < Ui,
small disturbances are convected by the axial
flow out of the pipe and the columnar flow is,
therefore, stable. However, when u > uo\,
small disturbances tend to move upstream.
Since the flow out of the vortex generator at
steady operation is fixed, the disturbances can-
not move through it. They are trapped, grow,
and stabilize as a large and steady stagnation
zone. These large-amplitude zones also tend to
grow and move upstream as uj changes. How-
ever, when uj < LJQ , they are convected by the
axial flow out of the pipe and the flow returns
to be columnar.
The theory also presents for the first time
a consistent explanation of the physical mech-
anism leading to axisymmetric vortex break-
down in a pipe as well as the conditions for
its occurrence. 
The results show that this
phenomenon is a necessary evolution from an
initial columnar concentrated vortex flow, to
another relatively stable lower-energy equilib-
rium state describing a swirling flow around a
large breakdown zone. This evolution is the
result of the interaction between waves propa-
gating upstream, and the incoming flow, which
leads to an absolute loss of stability of the
base columnar state when the swirl ratio of
the incoming flow is near or above a critical
level. It should be noted here that the theory
also shows that the fundamental characteris-
tics which lead to vortex instability and break-
down in high Reynolds number vortex flows
in a pipe, as well as the conditions where the
breakdown state itself becomes unstable (the
critical conditions), may be calculated from a
single (reduced-order) nonlinear ordinary dif-
ferential equation.
To the best of our knowledge, fundamen-
tal studies on combustion with swirling flows
are quite limited to experimental and numeri-
cal simulations (Sivasegaram and Whitelaw,16
Wang and Yang13 , Paschereit et al. 14~15 and
Gupta et al.18'19). Specifically, there is no
consistent analysis of the interaction between
the combustion and the vortex breakdown pro-
cesses. The effect of heat release on the stabil-
ity and dynamics of swirling flows is unclear.
In the present paper we develop a first
step toward an understanding of the dynam-
ics of combustion states with swirl. We study
the dynamics of a lean-premixed combustion
state in a straight pipe with an underlying
near-critical (with swirl level around u;i) and
near-columnar axisymmetric swirling flow. To
achieve this goal, an asymptotic analysis is
proper as well as necessary. The analysis is
based on the low Mach number theory for re-
active flows. A small-disturbance approach is
developed to carefully explore the nonlinear in-
teraction between the flow swirl and the heat
transfer through combustion. The analysis re-
veals the possible states that may develop at
various swirl levels and combustion parame-

V. Conclusions
The dynamics of a lean-premixed combustion
state in a straight tube with an underlying
low Mach number near-critical axisymmetric
swirling flow can be studied by asymptotic
methods. A small-disturbance approach is de-
veloped to carefully explore the nonlinear in-
teraction between the flow swirl and the heat
transfer through the combustion. The analy-
sis shows that in the leading order the reaction
problem can be decoupled from the flow prob-
lem. However, the effect of the heat addition
on the flow behavior is important and has a
nonlinear character. We find that a lean reac-
tion in the flow breaks the transcritical bifurca-
tion of solutions of a cold flow in a straight tube
into two branches of solutions. These branches
fold at limit swirl levels near the critical swirl
with a finite gap between the two branches.
This means that no near-columnar axisymmet-
ric state can exist in a finite range of incom-
ing swirl around the critical level; the flow
must develop large disturbances in this swirl
range which are related to the vortex break-
down states and also strongly affect the com-
bustion state. It also suggests that the critical
swirl decreases with the increase of the reac-
tant amount. Beyond this swirl range, two
equilibrium states may exist under the same
inlet/outlet conditions. One describes a near
columnar flow and the other a large-amplitude
disturbance. However, when the amount of the
reactant is increased this special behavior uni-
formly changes into a branch of solutions with
no fold. These describe a gradually increasing
steady disturbance as the swirl is increased.
