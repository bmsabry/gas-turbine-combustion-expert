The present work studies the effect of utilizing counter swirling flow on pollutant 
emission characteristics at different equivalence ratios and inlet air temperatures. A PIV 
system is employed to study the aerodynamic interactions between the recirculation zone of 
a model gas turbine combustor and the primary air jets. Centerline measurements of 
emissions generated during combustion of Jet-A liquid fuel in a swirl cup assembly provide 
more insight into nitric oxide and carbon monoxide formation/destruction processes. Results 
showed that misplacement of the primary air jets may cause production of higher amounts 
of carbon monoxide and nitric oxides. Besides, over penetrating primary air participates in 
thermal quenching for carbon monoxide.

I. 
Introduction 
ILUTION jets play a vital role to control the combustion temperature, enhance mixing, and achieve satisfactory 
combustion performance within a gas turbine combustor. In the primary zone, dilution jets are required to 
provide more uniform mixing, promoting efficient combustion and minimizing pollutant formation. The attainment 
of a satisfactory temperature distribution in the exhaust gases is mainly dependent on the degree of mixing between 
air and combustion products in the dilution zone.  
Extensive work have been carried out to study and characterize the dilution jet mixing processes in the dilution 
zone of the combustor [1-5] but very few studies were concerned with the primary zone which is believed to be the

main contributor for pollutant emissions in all combustor zones. Unfortunately, there is no clear strategy for the 
placement of the transverse air jets in the primary zone. Dodds and Bahr [6] emphasized that interactions between 
swirler, primary jets, and cooling flows are known to be important, but the effects of these interactions on emissions 
and performance have not been systematically studied. Lefebvre [7] stated that as a general rule, maximum stability 
is achieved by injecting primary air through a small number of large holes. Although no firm guidelines have been 
laid down for the optimal number of liner holes for annular combustors, one opposing pair of holes per fuel injector 
should be regarded as the absolute minimum; twice that number would be preferable.  
Gogineni et al. [8], pointed out that many aircraft engine combustors use transverse air jets introduced through 
the liners to provide a nominal termination of the primary zone. Richards and Samuelsen [9, 10] showed that the 
relative positions of the transverse jets to the swirl injection plane are of paramount importance. They suggested 
placement of the transverse jets in the region of half to one duct diameter for optimum injection. Mellor et al [11] 
suggested that the placement of the first row of penetration air jets will largely determine the emissions within the 
combustor. Noyce et al [12] showed that the primary air addition methods have a dominant influence on pollutant 
levels at the combustor exit. On the contrary, in a study by Gogineni et al [8], it was concluded that most of the NOx 
was generated from stoichiometric burning in interfaces around the combustion air jet system. Minimum total 
emissions were produced when all of the reactants are introduced through the combustor dome. However air jets 
were necessary for improving lean blowout stability and shorter flame length. Sturgess et al [13] concluded that 
minimum overall pollution is generated when air jets are positioned 0.5 dome heights downstream from the dome.

II. 
Experimental Setup 
A. Test Facility 
A schematic diagram of the test facility is shown in Fig. 2. The air system consists of a compressor, a dryer, a 0.5 
micron particle filter, a pressure regulator, and an air storage tank. An ASME orifice flow meter is utilized to 
measure airflow rate into the system. A 39 kW heater can be used to preheat the air per test requirements. The air 
passes through a honeycomb flow conditioner to the test section. A separate air line equipped with a flowmeter, 
regulator, and pressure transducers is utilized to control the amount of air flow through the transverse jets.  The fuel 
system includes a fuel tank, a fuel pump, a fuel filter, a coriolis flowmeter, and a needle valve for controlling the 
fuel flow rate. Fuel is injected by a hollow cone simplex atomizer with 90-deg spray angle mounted in the center of 
the swirler. The swirler used in this study is designed to generate two coaxial, counter swirling airstreams. The 
swirler flare exit diameter D is 40mm. The detailed design of this swirl-cup has been fully described elsewhere 
(thesis).  
The combustion chamber used in this study is made of a combination quartz glass, for optical access, and 
stainless steel plates. The cross section of the chamber is a square with a side dimension of 76.2mm. The chamber 
height is about 330mm. In this study, the chamber is designed such that it can:  
• 
Allow for the study of the effect of transverse air jets on aerodynamics and emissions characteristics, 
• 
Allow a gas sampling probe to traverse inside the chamber in order to study the effect of different 
parameters on emissions within the primary zone. 
A combustion chamber with the swirl-cup assembly is shown in Fig.2. In this case the chamber is designed such 
that primary air jets location as well as jet diameter can be varied. Eight jet heights can be examined; four of them 
are shown in the figure and the other four locations can be obtained by turning the primary jets holder 180°. Thus, 
jet heights can be varied in the range of 25mm to 114mm with a step of 12.7mm. Moreover, the air jet diameter can 
be varied by using jet connectors with different inner diameters. However, in this study only transverse air jets with 
a diameter of 12.7mm were utilized. When no dilution air is allowed to be injected to the chamber, the primary jets 
holder is plugged. 
In order to study the transient as well as average flow aerodynamics within the primary zone of a gas turbine 
combustor, a Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV) system is utilized. The current system can provide two- or three- 
dimensional velocity maps in flows using whole field techniques based on imaging the light scattered by small 
particles in the flow illuminated by a laser light sheet. For non-reacting flow measurements, the flow was seeded, 
using a six jet TSI flow seeder, with propylene Glycol which provides particles in the size range of 1-3 µm.   
Double Pulsed Laser ND: YAG laser (NewWave, Solo PIV) with pulse energy of up to 120 mJ per pulse at 15 
HZ and a wave length of 532 nm was utilized to illuminate the seeding particles with short time difference.  
A double frame-double exposure CCD Camera (Lavision - Image Intense) is placed perpendicular to the 
illuminated swirl-cup center plane in the case of 2D-PIV measurements as depicted in Fig. 2. The Camera delivers 
12 bit digital images with a resolution of 1376 x 1040 pixels per frame. It features a built in electronic shutter with

focal length and maximum aperture of f/1.8 was placed in front of the CCD camera. A bandpass filter with 532 
±3nm is placed in front of the camera lens to suppress the background light in the second frame.  
One CCD camera was setup to capture a field of view of 75mm width x 100 mm height. However, a larger field 
of view was required to cover the whole recirculation zone region and part of the post recirculation zone. To achieve 
this large field of view without altering the resolution, it was decided to use two CCD cameras side-by-side as 
presented in Fig. 2. In this case a special calibration for both cameras in which the calibration of camera 2 is mapped 
to the calibration of camera 1.  The resulting vector fields from both cameras are then merged together to form one 
enlarged vector field.    
The scattered light from first and second exposure of the particles within this field of view is recorded in two 
different images which are then divided to small sub-areas called interrogation windows. Using statistical correlation 
techniques (cross correlation) one local displacement vector is determined for each interrogation window. The 
window size selected for image processing is 32 x 32 pixels with a 50% overlap in each direction. As a result, this 
approach led to a measurement grid with a spatial resolution of about 1.5 mm x 1.5 mm. 
Both the main flow through the swirl-cup and the flow through the primary jets are seeded using propylene 
glycol. Frame straddled pairs are acquired with laser pulse delay (dt) in the range of 5-8 µs. The main flow structure 
is evaluated by ensample-averaging 200 successive double frames.

III. 
Results 
Since coupling between fluid dynamics and chemical processes within the combustion chamber provide a major 
factor governing the production of pollutant emissions, it was decided to check the aerodynamic influence of the 
primary jets on the main swirling flow. Non-reacting flow measurements were considered because the main 
objective is to investigate the trends of the flow-field at different primary jets conditions. The experimental setup for 
this case was presented in Fig. 2.14.  
Two main parameters are studied these are the jet location and mass ratio. Since there is no combustion, the mass 
flow through the swirler is kept fixed to a value corresponding to a pressure drop of 4.0% across the swirler. The 
mass ratio is varied by controlling the mass flow rate through the primary jets. The test parameters are provided in 
table 5.5.  
 
PIV results for the base line case, where no air is allowed to flow through the primary jets, is presented in Fig. 
5.13. The swirling air departing from the swirler remains attached to the swirl-cup conical sleeve expands away 
from the centerline and impinges on the chamber walls at about 25 mm downstream of the swirler exit plane. Upon 
impingement, the main flow velocity weakens; the outward radial velocity decays rapidly and the axial velocity 
drops by about 40% if compared to that just departing the swirler. Due to confinement, the recirculation zone is 
extended to about Z =104 mm and it occupies about 45% of the chamber size at about Z =35 mm

These results also clarify why there is a trade off between CO emissions and primary jets mass ratio. Tracking 
the primary jets path at each mass ratio studied, it can be observed that for low mass ratios (or high deflection 
angles) the jet air takes longer time prior to penetrating the recirculation zone thus mixing is enhanced between the 
jets and the main swirling flow. This primary air becomes part of the combustion process and its amount is not 
sufficient to form a spot with cold temperature that can deteriorate the oxidation process of CO to CO2. On the other 
hand, for high jet mass ratios (MR>0.2), the primary air overpowers and does not take time to mix with the main 
flow. This primary air penetrates through the recirculation zone forming a region of cold temperature that quenches 
the reaction and suppresses the oxidation of CO to CO2.   
Based on these results one can suggest that the optimum mass ratio in this case is 0.15 because the recirculation 
zone is still preserved, and the two opposing jets are not impinging on each other forming a cold spot that might 
quench the chemical reaction.   
Referring to the baseline case where no jet air was admitted, Fig. 5.13, the main swirling air velocity decay very 
rapidly upon impingement on the chamber walls which happens at about Z=25 mm. It is expected that if the primary 
air jets are located far from the impingement location it will face less resistance by the main flow to penetrate 
through the recirculation zone. This anticipation is demonstrated in figures 5.15 and 5.16 where the primary air was 
injected at Z =50 mm and Z =76mm respectively.  
The jet deflected angle can be used as a measure of the resistance of the primary jet to the main flow. As shown 
in table 5.7, the deflection angle drops significantly as the jet is placed farther from the plane where the main flow 
impinges on the chamber. As the deflection angle decrease indicating a strong jet, mixing with the main flow 
deteriorates. In combustion cases this is not a favorable situation because the jets might act only as a heat sink that 
can quench the reaction leading to higher CO and UHC at the combustor exhaust.
