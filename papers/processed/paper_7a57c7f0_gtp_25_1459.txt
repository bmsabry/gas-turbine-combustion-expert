Measurements of CO and NOx 
Emissions From Premixed 
Turbulent Methane/Hydrogen 
Flames
This paper presents measurements of the sensitivity of NO and CO emissions from H2=CH4 
combustion to variations in turbulence intensity. Existing studies of exhaust emissions either 
focus on laminar modeling and measurements or report device-level measurements from 
practical combustors. However, there are limited fundamental studies of emissions 
characteristics of turbulent systems, the focus of this study. Because of the strong stretch 
sensitivity of these mixtures, local temperatures/reaction rates along the turbulent front can 
differ markedly from mixture-averaged properties, and they are very sensitive to turbulence. 
Single-point emissions measurements are presented for turbulent premixed H2=CH4 flames 
from a contoured nozzle Bunsen burner with variable turbulence. Emissions are measured at 
varying residence times and incoming flow turbulence intensities. Data show the clear 
benefit of H2 blending on CO emissions, both due to the expected reduction in C atoms, but 
also due to faster relaxation of flame generated CO toward equilibrium. It also shows 
negligible impact of turbulence intensity on NO emissions at constant CO for CH4.

Keywords: NO, CO emissions, H2/CH4 blends, gas turbine relevant conditions, premixed 
turbulent flames 
1 Introduction
Development of hydrogen combustion strategies for gas turbine 
combustors is critical to achieving net zero emission goals set for 
2050 [1]. Hydrogen-blended methane combustion systems present 
unique design challenges, including achieving a broader operability 
range with higher turndown, minimizing emissions, and implement­
ing effective flashback prevention mechanisms [2,3]. These 
challenges arise from the significant differences in fundamental 
properties between hydrogen and methane fueled systems, such as 
flame propagation speed, stretch sensitivity, and adiabatic flame 
temperature [4–6] at the same equivalence ratio. Lean H2 flames 
exhibit high stretch sensitivity, with local flame temperatures 
exceeding mixture-averaged values by over 200 K at points of strong 
positive curvature [7]. For this reason, local laminar burning 
velocities can surpass unstretched values by an order of magnitude 
or more [8]. NO and CO emissions are byproducts of hydrocarbon 
combustion. Hydrogen-blended premixed flames offer a notable 
advantage in reducing CO emissions. For instance, Singh et al. [9] 
reported a significant decrease in CO levels as the H2 concentration 
in H2/CH4 blends increased. This reduction was attributed not only 
to the lower atomic carbon content in the fuel but also through 
increased relaxation rates of superequilibrium CO levels toward its 
equilibrium. It is likely that these relaxation rates would be further 
influenced by turbulence levels, as H2-blended flames show strong 
sensitivity to turbulence [10–12], but we are not aware of studies of 
this nature. Two primary contributors to NOx production in 
hydrocarbon flames are (1) flame or prompt NOx—formed within 
the exothermic region of the flame through various fuel-based 
reaction pathways, and (2) thermal NOx—produced in the postflame 
region with sustained high temperatures. Numerous studies have 
been performed to understand the effect of H2 levels on the NOx 
emissions [13–15]. First, it is important to emphasize that using 
volumetric measures (like ppmvd) are problematic for comparing 
across variable H2 composition, and comparisons are better done 
using metrics like mass NOx produced per unit of heat input [16]. In 
addition, it is crucial to carefully consider what else is being changed 
with H2 content, particularly if adiabatic flame temperature is being 
changed, since H2 has a higher adiabatic flame temperature as 
compared to methane for the same equivalence ratio. For example, 
some studies report a rise in NOx levels as the H2 content in fuel 
increases [17,18], which probably reflects this thermal influence. 
What is of more interest for premixed applications is H2 effects at 
constant temperature. A detailed numerical analysis by Breer et al. 
[19] at constant adiabatic flame temperature explored the influence 
of hydrogen addition on NOx emissions in a 1D flat flame and found 
that NO emissions at a fixed temperature were relatively insensitive 
to H2 content, although it could go slightly up or down depending 
upon pressure and temperature. As practical combustion systems

operate in highly turbulent conditions, understanding the impact of 
incoming flow turbulence on flame structure and chemical reactions, 
which in turn influence flame emissions, is crucial. Numerical study 
[20] performed using perfectly stirred reactors with syngas fuel and 
coupled with a turbulent mixing submodel found that increased 
mixing frequencies amplify both CO and NO levels. This was 
primarily associated with the influence of mixing frequencies on 
oxyhydrogen radicals. Experimental investigations using low-swirl 
burners fueled by syngas and operating at gas turbine relevant 
conditions [21] showed a slight increase in NOx levels as hydrogen 
content in the fuel increased. Furthermore, numerical studies 
comparing NOx production between laminar and turbulent counter­
flow configurations fueled by syngas found that turbulent counter­
flow systems produced significantly lower NOx levels than the 
laminar case for the same fuel consumption [22]. This reduction in 
NOx levels was attributed to turbulence-induced enhancements in 
heat and mass transfer, which are hypothesized to suppress NOx 
production rates. Direct numerical simulations by Day et al. [23] 
noted the influence of the cellular structure of lean H2 flames on NOx 
production, emphasizing the role of localized hot spots formed 
within these flames. These hot spots, exhibiting temperatures well 
above the average adiabatic flame temperature, arise due to 
enhanced preferential diffusion in lean premixed H2 flames. The 
study identifies these hot spots as a contributor to elevated NO 
production, with the effect being particularly pronounced at leaner 
equivalence ratios where NO production increased by nearly 
threefold compared to laminar flame counterparts. At higher 
equivalence ratios, the relative variation from laminar flames was 
much smaller, likely due to the rapid mixing of these elevated- 
temperature regions, driving them closer to the mixture-averaged 
temperature. Given the varied results observed regarding the effects 
of H2 blending and flow turbulence on NOx and CO emissions, this 
paper aims to address these influences individually. To facilitate 
meaningful comparisons across H2 blends, the data are constrained 
by maintaining a constant adiabatic flame temperature by varying 
H2% and equivalence ratios simultaneously. This study has two 
primary objectives: first, to assess the impact of incoming flow 
turbulence on NOx and CO emissions from turbulent premixed 
flames, and second, to analyze the influence of H2 content in fuel on 
emissions from these flames.
2 Materials and Methods
Measurements of CO and NOx emissions from atmospheric 
premixed turbulent hydrogen blended methane flames were made in 
a piloted Bunsen burner configuration with a nozzle diameter (D) of 
12 mm and variable turbulence generator (see Fig. 1). This system 
comprises of H2=CH4 fuel metered with critical orifices and 
premixed with air two meters upstream from the point of ignition, 
enabling sufficient premixing of reactants. A premixed H2/air 
annular pilot was used to stabilize the flame. The turbulence 
generator system comprises two radially slotted plates, enabling the 
adjustment of turbulence intensity by altering the blockage between 
them. The blockage ratio ranges from 69% to 97%, corresponding to 
angular slot openings from 30 deg to 2 deg. This system allows for 
independent control of bulk flow velocity and turbulence intensity 
while being remotely operable, making it a critical design element of 
the facility. Flow characteristics under isothermal conditions were 
assessed using laser Doppler velocimetry, with detailed findings 
reported in a previous study [24]. A quartz tube, 305 mm in length 
with an inner diameter of 52 mm and a wall thickness of 3 mm, was 
mounted at the nozzle exit to prevent dilution of the product gases 
with ambient air. Product gas samples were extracted using a clear 
fused quartz probe with the following dimensions: 4 mm ID, 6.3 mm 
outer diameter, and 100 mm length, tapered to a 1.5 mm diameter 
opening. This tapered design facilitates aerodynamic quenching to 
suppress chemical reactions in the sampled product gases [25]. The 
probe was mounted on a traverse system, allowing collection of gas 
samples at various axial locations, corresponding to different 
residence times. The acquired samples were then transported to 
Testo 350 emissions analyzer via a gas filter and dryer. The dryer 
was used in line to dry the product gas sample before passing it to the 
analyzer, as the presence of water in the sample is detrimental to both 
the functioning of the analyzer and the accuracy of the measure­
ments. The Testo 350 system is a portable fast exhaust gas analyzer 
that offers high-accuracy measurements of NOx and CO emissions, 
with an uncertainty of 62 ppm below a measured value of 40 ppm 
and 65% of measured value above it. It has a full-scale range of 
300 ppm for NOx and 500 ppm for CO. Table 1 lists the range of test 
conditions from this study.
2.1 Equilibrium Properties. Equilibrium flame properties, 
such as Tad and equilibrium product gas composition, were 
computed using CANTERA’s chemical equilibrium solver [26]. The 
results from these simulations were postprocessed using PYTHON for 
further analysis.

measurements. The acquired images are temporally averaged and 
processed using a 3  3 Gaussian filter. The resulting averaged

3 Results and Discussion
3.1 Baseline CO and NO Plots. In Figs. 3 and 4, measured NO 
and CO levels (in ppmvd), respectively, from a CH4–air flame at 
u ¼ 0:9 (Tad ¼ 2130 K) is presented as a function of sres (in 
milliseconds). The color bar in these plots indicates the turbulence 
intensity (urms=U0) values. Figure 3 illustrates typical behavior of 
NO production with residence time—an initial jump (when 
extrapolating back to zero residence time) driven by flame NO 
formation, followed by a linear rise associated with thermal NO 
production. As also expected, CO levels decrease with increasing 
residence time due to the oxidation of CO to CO2 in Fig. 4.
Next, constant Tad slice is obtained across varying H2 content. 
Here, both H2 content and the equivalence ratio are varied 
simultaneously to achieve a constant Tad. Figure 5 plots the CO 
emissions as a function of residence time across different H2 blends 
at Tad ¼ 1800 6 20 K, U0 ¼ 10 m/s, and turbulence intensity of 
20%. In the case of CO, a significant reduction is observed as a 
function of H2%. For a fixed Tad, the variation in NO levels across 
H2 blends falls within the uncertainty bounds making it insufficient 
to ascertain influence of H2 blending and hence not shown here.
3.2 CO Emissions at Fixed Residence Time. In this section, 
CO emission levels are examined at a constant residence time (sres). 
This analysis underscores the impact of turbulence intensity on 
flame height and the influence of H2 content on flame kinetics. These 
factors, in turn, affect the distribution of total sress between the in- 
flame and postflame regions as discussed in Sec. 2.2. Figure 6 shows 
the CO emissions from a CH4–air flame at u ¼ 0:9 at fixed 
sres ¼ 0–0.5 ms and 15 ms. At a fixed residence time, CO levels show 
a slight decrease as turbulence intensity increases. As the residence 
time sres has been corrected for the variation in flame height across 
turbulence intensities, the observed reduction in CO levels can be 
attributed to enhanced oxidation of CO to CO2 facilitated by 
turbulent transport.

4 Conclusions
To advance the development of next-generation gas turbine 
combustors designed for high-hydrogen fuel blends, we conducted 
a fundamental study exploring the effects of incoming flow 
turbulence and fuel hydrogen content on NOx and CO emissions. 
H2 exhibits markedly distinct combustion characteristics compared to 
hydrocarbons, including significantly higher flame temperatures at 
equivalent equivalence ratios. To enable systematic comparisons, 
measurements were performed at constant adiabatic flame temper­
atures across varying conditions. Additionally, the postflame 
residence time was corrected to account for the influence of hydrogen 
content and turbulence intensity on flame height, as increases in either 
parameter result in reduced flame height. Data from this study reveal 
that NO levels (in ppmvd) at fixed CO levels do not exhibit significant 
variation with increasing turbulence intensity. Therefore, while local 
NOx production may increase with higher H2 concentrations— 
potentially due to superadiabatic zones forming at flame front cusps— 
this effect is expected to be offset by regions of lower NOx production. 
As a result, overall NOx emissions remain unchanged with increasing 
turbulence intensity. Additionally, both increased turbulence inten­
sity and higher H2 content in the fuel lead to a reduction in CO levels at 
a constant residence time. The decrease in CO with increased 
turbulence intensity is attributed to enhanced oxidation of CO to CO2, 
while the reduction in CO with higher H2 content is due to both a 
kinetic effect as well as a decrease in the carbon atoms.
