ABSTRACT
Acoustic liners are key passive dampers for reducing self-sustained combustion oscillations in gas turbine combustors and afterburners. This
study experimentally and numerically explores how acoustic liners mitigate longitudinal combustion oscillations in a combustor featuring a
bluff-body diffusion flame. Combustion tests measured oscillation suppression using liners with varying porosities, complemented by acoustic
simulations clarifying liner operating mechanisms. Results indicate that optimizing the maximum sound absorption coefficient alone does
not yield optimal oscillation attenuation. Instead, lowering the acoustic impedance (dimensionless impedance <0.5) significantly enhances
suppression effectiveness. Additionally, attenuating combustion oscillations reduces their frequency by up to 40 Hz, explaining why maximiz-
ing absorption and minimizing oscillation intensity cannot be simultaneously optimized. Liners with larger aperture ratios exhibit better sup-
pression performance under identical backing cavity conditions, owing to reduced oscillating velocity within the holes, which lowers the
nonlinear acoustic resistance. When the peak sound absorption frequency is approximately 70 Hz lower than the self-sustained oscillation fre-
quency, the liner can reduce the pressure oscillation amplitude by 81% (equivalent to 14.5 dB). This work offers new insights into acoustic
liner design for passive control of combustion oscillations in afterburners.

I. INTRODUCTION
Since Rick1 first conducted experimental research on combus-
tion oscillation in 1958, the issue of combustion oscillation has
remained a hot and challenging topic. In the past, an afterburner
of a certain aircraft engine exhibited strong combustion oscilla-
tion.2 In recent years, the problem of combustion oscillation has
still not been entirely avoided in the development of the latest
engine. Acoustic liners are one of the key components of after-
burners, used for the passive suppression of combustion oscilla-
tion. In the afterburner, the dominant frequency of vortex
shedding behind the flame stabilizer is close to the radial acoustic
resonance, so early acoustic liners were used to absorb high-
frequency oscillations, with the oscillation direction perpendicular
to the liners. Nowadays, tangential mode combustion oscillation is
more prominent, with the oscillation direction parallel to the liners,3
often resulting in poorer suppression effectiveness.
In the past, numerous studies have explored methods to suppress
combustion oscillation. Some research introduced air jets,4,5 sliding
arcs,6 electric heaters,7 and out-of-phase interference sound waves8
into the combustion chamber under laboratory conditions. Other
studies modified fuel injection.9 Although these methods achieved
effective suppression, they are challenging to implement in aircraft
engines. Combustion oscillation can be suppressed from two perspec-
tives: flame dynamics and acoustic characteristics. For the dynamic
response characteristics of the flame, controlling the convective time of
the flame is an important parameter for suppressing combustion oscil-
lation.10–12 In contrast, sound-absorbing equipment is a widely
adopted passive suppression method. This sound-absorbing equip-
ment mainly includes Helmholtz resonators, perforated acoustic liners,
quarter-wavelength tubes, and half-wavelength tubes.
Previous research proposed that the principle of passive suppres-
sion is to reduce the growth rate of thermoacoustic instability by

impedance matches the characteristic impedance of air, the sound-

Therefore, the research goal of most scholars is to optimize acoustic
impedance to achieve a smaller reflection coefficient or optimal acous-

Generally, the aperture size significantly affects the acoustic
impedance of liners. To achieve suitable acoustic impedance, a perfo-
rated plate can be installed at the neck of a Helmholtz resonator.16
It can also be obtained by changing the shape of the perforation17
or designing double-layer perforated plates.18,19 Additionally, airflow
can also adjust the acoustic impedance. Jing et al.20 first pointed out
that appropriate bias flow can increase acoustic resistance, thus opti-
mizing sound absorption performance by adjusting the bias flow
velocity. However, an increase in grazing flow can deteriorate the
damping performance.21,22 Lawn23 described a method for calculat-
ing the absorption coefficient of perforated acoustic liners with both
bias and grazing flow. Yu and Yang24 experimentally found that the
effective length of the perforations, viscous wall dissipation, and
vortex-shedding coefficients need to be recalibrated under bias flow
conditions. Recently, Meng et al.25 discovered through the Large
Eddy Simulation method that perforated acoustic liners can effec-
tively suppress thermoacoustic instability at certain bias flow veloci-
ties.

In actual aircraft engine afterburners, the pressure pulsation
amplitude is quite high, leading to significant oscillation velocities
within the perforations and prominent nonlinear issues in acoustic
impedance. There are two different damping mechanisms in perfora-
tions: linear acoustic resistance and nonlinear acoustic resistance.
When the pressure pulsation amplitude is small, linear acoustic resis-
tance dominates, primarily caused by thermal losses and viscous losses
of the boundary layer. Flow instability can lead to vortex shedding at
the perforation shear layer.27,28 When the pressure pulsation amplitude
is large, significant vortex shedding and jet phenomena occur at the
perforation edges, which are the main causes of nonlinear acoustic
resistance.29 Subsequent research has found that the edge chamfering
of perforations30 and special structures in the neck31 can significantly
affect nonlinear acoustic resistance. Dai,32 using the discrete vortex
model33,34 and a vortex/acoustic flow coupling model, investigated
the mechanism of vorticity generation in Helmholtz resonators.
Additionally, boundary layer flow issues might also be key parameters
leading to nonlinear effects.35 It is noteworthy that the nonlinear
effects of acoustic liners are generally concentrated near the primary
absorption frequency, while the nonlinear terms of acoustic impedance
are relatively small when far from the absorption frequency.36
Combustion oscillation problems involve the coupling of flame
response and acoustics. Some studies have pointed out that the suppres-
sion effects on combustion oscillation cannot be predicted solely based
on absorption characteristics. Kim et al.,37 in their research on suppress-
ing high-frequency combustion oscillation in liquid rocket engines
and gas turbine engines, found that the absorption coefficient does
not adequately represent acoustic damping, and more emphasis should
be placed on acoustic power loss. Meng et al.38 also pointed out that per-
forated plates have two mechanisms: noise reduction and redistribution
of acoustic modes in the combustion chamber. Some researchers, com-
bining acoustic mode analysis, have indicated that acoustic liners can
reduce the sound pressure amplitude within the combustion cham-
ber,39–41 thereby disrupting the coupling between sound waves and
unstable heat-release rates. Gullaud et al.,42 using a Helmholtz solver
developed by Howe,43 noted that the position of the sound-absorbing
structure significantly impacts the acoustic characteristics of the com-
bustion chamber. Therefore, reducing the acoustic feedback intensity of
the combustion system using sound-absorbing structures is a research
approach for suppressing combustion oscillation.
The acoustic liner in afterburners is similar in structure to
Helmholtz resonators. Given the special application environment, the
issue of nonlinear acoustic impedance of acoustic liners is prominent.
The suppression of combustion oscillation differs from traditional
sound absorption studies, which emphasize the optimal sound absorp-
tion coefficient. However, for suppressing combustion oscillation in
confined spaces, appropriate sound wave reflection may achieve better
suppression effects, such as the reflected sound waves being out of
phase with the original incident waves. Combustion oscillation has
strong self-sustained characteristics, with the oscillating frequency
changing according to the implementation of suppression. Although
extensive research has been conducted on the absorption coefficient of
perforated acoustic liners, this did not achieve a good attenuation effect
in this study. Therefore, this study combines the investigation of non-
linear acoustic impedance and self-sustained combustion oscillation
tests to research the suppression of combustion oscillation by acoustic
liners.

acoustic liners, which are installed on the sidewall corresponding to

III. NONLINEAR ACOUSTIC IMPEDANCE OF THE
ACOUSTIC LINERS
The acoustic pressure generated by combustion oscillation is
generally high. When acoustic waves pass through small holes, they
are affected by viscosity and separation vortices, resulting in nonlinear
effects. Figure 3 shows the standing wave tube measurement system.
Using the dual-microphone method,17 the acoustic impedance at point
A is calculated through the measured acoustic pressure at points B and
C. The sound pressure amplitude at the entrance of the acoustic liner
is obtained through measurement at point A.
The acoustic impedance of the acoustic liners has a minimum value
near the absorption frequency fa. As pressure waves pass through the
perforated plate, there is a significant velocity pulsation, especially under
high SPLs. Figure 4 shows the Reynolds number inside the acoustic lin-
ers. The perforation rates for acoustic liners with hole diameters of
d¼ 2mm, d¼ 3mm, d¼ 4mm, and d¼ 10mm are 2.09%, 2.36%,
2.93%, and 7.85%, respectively. Different perforation rates were designed
to ensure an approximately identical absorption frequency. Although the
Reynolds number increases with increasing hole diameter, the oscillatory
velocity amplitude inside the perforations actually decreases. It should be
noted that the SPLs in this study are up to 146dB, and the Reynolds
numbers inside the holes generally exceed 300. This causes flow separa-
tion and vortex shedding at the entrance and the exit of the hole.44
Recently, Temiz et al. provided a nonlinear acoustic impedance
expression for circular perforated plates under high SPLs.44 Figure 5
shows the nonlinear acoustic impedance (Zs) of the acoustic liners.
The acoustic impedance exhibits significant differences under varying
pressure amplitudes, revealing the nonlinear effects. As the amplitude
increases, the acoustic impedance near the absorption center frequency
increases significantly. This is because the pulsation velocity is higher,
making viscous effects and vortices more pronounced, thus leading to
greater acoustic resistance.
When the acoustic impedance of the liner matches the characteris-
tic impedance of air (Zsair¼ q0c0, where q0 is the density and c0 is the
sound speed), the sound absorption coefficient reaches its maximum
value, i.e., a ¼ 1, indicating that the sound wave is entirely absorbed by
the liner without any reflection. Figure 6 presents the acoustic imped-
ance and absorption coefficient for acoustic liners with different hole
diameters under a 400Pa pressure amplitude condition. The results
show that increasing the hole diameter can rapidly reduce acoustic
impedance. The sound absorption coefficient is maximized for
d ¼ 4mm near the fa, and its acoustic impedance is comparable to Zsair.
Either decreasing or increasing the hole diameter leads to a deviation in
acoustic impedance, thus reducing the sound absorption coefficient.
The oscillation investigated occurs in a confined space with the
flame acting as the sound source. The acoustic liners serve as one of
the acoustic boundary conditions, with the upstream and downstream
reflective surfaces being equally important boundary conditions. This
study suggests that the acoustic impedance of the acoustic liners
should be minimized. In the extreme case where Zs ¼ 0, the reflection
coefficient of the liners would be 1, meaning that the reflected wave
and the incident wave have the same amplitude but opposite phase.
This results in significant attenuation when the incident and reflected
waves superimpose.

After installing the acoustic liners, combustion oscillation was
effectively suppressed, and the dominant frequency also decreased by
approximately 40Hz. The phase space trajectory of the pressure pulsa-
tion still exhibits a distinct annular structure, with additional outer
curves indicating instability of pressure amplitude. The significant
change in the dominant frequency is quite interesting, reflecting the
flexibility of combustion oscillation. For the low-speed flow case in this
study, this can be understood as follows: the occurrence of combustion
oscillation requires the phase synchronization of pressure pulsation and
heat-release rate pulsation. The phase delay of the heat-release rate in
response to incoming velocity disturbances increases with frequency
and is also related to oscillation amplitude. Therefore, thermoacoustic
oscillation can only occur within certain frequency ranges that meet the
Rayleigh criterion. Combustion oscillation does not necessarily occur
only at fixed frequency points but can stabilize within a frequency
range. After installing the acoustic liners, the oscillation system seems
to have found an additional optimal state (160Hz). This means that a
slight reduction in the dominant frequency can still ensure considerable
acoustic feedback, enabling the heat-release rate pulsation and pressure
pulsation to satisfy the Rayleigh criterion at the current amplitude level.
By adjusting the perforation diameter d and the absorption center
frequency fa, Fig. 9 shows the oscillating pressure spectra. Although
the acoustic liner with d ¼ 4 mm had the optimal absorption coeffi-
cient, overall, smaller perforation diameters had poorer suppression
effects on combustion oscillation. Only when the acoustic liners had
larger perforations did they exhibit acceptable suppression perfor-
mance. Considering the absorption coefficient results in Fig. 6(b),
although the acoustic liner with d ¼ 10 mm had a lower absorption
coefficient, it exhibited excellent suppression performance, indicating
that lower acoustic impedance is more beneficial.
Smaller acoustic impedance can generate out-of-phase reflected
pressure waves, which are one of the key factors in suppressing oscilla-
tion. Therefore, the acoustic liners with a perforation diameter of
10 mm produced excellent suppression effects. By reasonably design-
ing the backing cavity volume, the oscillating pressure amplitude can
be suppressed by more than 80%, causing the oscillation to exit the
limit cycle state. Moreover, the insert-type perforation design achieved
the same suppression effects, providing greater flexibility in the wall
thickness design of the acoustic liners. The reduction of the dominant
frequency after suppression is a common phenomenon. This is
because the acoustic feedback intensity at the original oscillating fre-
quency has been significantly reduced. Appropriately lowering the
oscillating frequency can provide sufficient acoustic feedback intensity
while still satisfying the Rayleigh criterion.
The spectrum in Fig. 9 is a snapshot at a specific moment (sam-
pling time is 1 s). It should be noted that pressure pulsations are unsta-
ble and can be evaluated by averaging over long-term sampling, for
instance, with each sampling time being 1 s and the total sampling
time not less than 10 s. Based on long-term statistics of oscillating pres-
sure, Fig. 10 shows the suppression levels of oscillating pressure ampli-
tude. The results for the insert-type structure are consistent with the
common structure and are not listed separately. DSPL represents the
sound pressure level reduction. The figure also shows the reduction
ratio of the oscillating pressure amplitude. It is noted that the original
oscillating dominant frequency was 208 Hz, with an amplitude of
1106 Pa and a corresponding SPL of 154.9dB. The acoustic liners were
not designed to match the oscillating frequency; all the absorption cen-
ter frequencies are lower than the original oscillating frequency. A
matching oscillating frequency design did not result in effective sup-
pression, as explained later through acoustic analysis. The larger diam-
eter scheme (d ¼ 10 mm) presented the best suppression effect, which
is also observed to have the lowest acoustic impedance. The optimal
suppression was achieved at fa ¼ 130Hz, with an amplitude reduction
ratio of 81.1% (14.5 dB).

response and the acoustic characteristics. Disrupting either of these
factors can achieve effective suppression of oscillation. In this study,
the combustion conditions and flame stabilizer dimensions remain
unchanged, meaning the flame response remains constant. Therefore,
the change in the acoustic characteristics of the combustion system is
the key factor in suppressing oscillation. The acoustic characteristics of
the combustion system are analyzed using acoustic simulation
methods.

C. The acoustic principles of passive suppression
Combustion oscillations arise from thermoacoustic coupling
between the acoustic feedback and the unsteady heat-release rate
response. First, the suppression physical mechanism is that reducing
acoustic feedback diminishes the self-sustained oscillation, even if the
two reductions are not strictly proportional. Second, the liner reduces
acoustic feedback by adjusting its acoustic impedance (equivalently,
acoustic reflection coefficient). When the liner’s reflection coefficient
approaches 1, the reflected wave interferes destructively with the
incident wave, which is the most effective route to lowering acoustic
feedback—this is the second physical mechanism demonstrated here.
Third, while reducing feedback at the dominant frequency, there is a
risk of amplifying feedback in adjacent bands—a key pathway to sup-
pression failure.

combustor’s acoustic feedback response changes after installing differ-

oscillations. From this, these results inform the selection of the liner’s
absorption frequency and perforation scheme. Figure 14 presents the
acoustic feedback and the SPL reduction. Solid dots in the figure indi-

do not focus here on the direct relationship between perforation diame-

oscillatory velocity inside the perforations is one of the key factors lead-
ing to differences in nonlinear acoustic impedance, and the magnitude
of this velocity is linked to vortex-shedding losses. In the early stage of
this study, to achieve the same absorption frequency while varying hole
diameters under identical back-cavity conditions, liners with larger hole
diameters were designed with higher porosity; this reduced the oscilla-
tory velocity and thus lowered the acoustic impedance.
Acoustic liners have a certain absorption bandwidth (over 50 Hz)
around the fa. An important fact noted is that the oscillating frequency
significantly decreases after suppression, up to 40 Hz. Therefore, after
implementing suppression, the oscillating frequency may differ signifi-
cantly from the original oscillating frequency, limiting the suppression
capability. Additionally, it is noteworthy that after installing the liners,
a new peak appears on the left side of the fa. Combustion oscillation
may stabilize near this frequency. Therefore, the designed fa of the lin-
ers should be appropriately lower than the oscillating frequency to
ensure that the new peak is far from the potentially oscillating fre-
quency band. Figures 14(g)–14(k) indicate that appropriately lowering
the fa can push the new peak toward lower frequencies. However, it is
not advisable to excessively lower the fa, as this would reduce the DSPL
on the right side of fa.
VI. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
This study conducted suppression experiments on self-sustained
combustion oscillations occurring in confined spaces. The results show
that reducing the acoustic impedance of the liner can achieve more
effective suppression. The main conclusions are as follows:
(1) For the self-sustained combustion oscillations generated by flames
in confined spaces, the sound absorption coefficient should not be
used as the optimization target for acoustic liners. The acoustic
impedance of the liners should be reduced, with dimensionless
acoustic impedance lower than 0.5 providing better suppression.
(2) In this investigation, the oscillating frequency decreases by up
to 40 Hz after attenuation. The change in oscillating frequency
limits the suppression effects, requiring the liners to have a cer-
tain suppression bandwidth.
(3) After installing the liners, a new acoustic feedback peak appears on
the left side of the absorption center frequency (fa). Combustion
oscillation may stabilize at this new peak after suppression, so the
fa of the liners should be appropriately lower than the original
oscillating frequency, such as reducing it by 70Hz.
(4) Under the large-amplitude oscillating pressure generated by com-
bustion oscillations, the resulting high oscillating velocity inside the
perforations significantly increases the nonlinear acoustic resistance
of the liner. Therefore, under the premise of consistent backing cav-
ity conditions and matching peak absorption frequency, the
