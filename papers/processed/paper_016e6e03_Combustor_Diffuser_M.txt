Combustor Diffuser Modeling 
Part II: Inlet Profiles & 3-D Calculations 
 
H. MongiaC, G. Hsiao, and D. Burrus, GE Aircraft Engines, Cincinnati,  
PSVS Sreedhar, and A. Rao, EACoE, Bangalore 
 
ABSTRACT 
3-D simulation of a typical gas turbine diffusion 
system is conducted with the standard k-ε model along 
with the non-equilibrium wall function of FLUENT 
software.  The diffuser inlet conditions were estimated 
by using through-the-rig inlet 3-D calculations including 
the bow-tie profilers. Calculations were compared with a 
typical 3-D diffuser rig data for the three inlet profiles, 
namely center-, inner- and outer-peak velocity profiles. 
Back-to-back comparison with 2-D calculations clearly 
showed superior performance achieved with 3-D 
simulation.  
 
INTRODUCTION 
 
2-D and 3-D CFD calculations are generally done 
during the gas turbine combustion system design process 
to aid in the optimization of the diffusion system. Early 
work by Karki et al1 as well as the follow-on 
publications, e.g., Reference 2 have shown that the 
standard k-ε model with wall functions overpredicts CP 
by 5-15% compared to data. A preliminary assessment of 
various advanced turbulence models indicates3 that SST 
k- ω turbulence model has shown potential for giving 
better agreement with data for flows subject to adverse 
pressure gradients including separation. However due to 
lack of systematic validation of CFD-based approach 
with data of combustor diffusion systems of practical 
interest to designers, the current design process relies on 
empirical data collected on simulated full-scale 3-D 
diffuser rigs coupled with qualitative guidance from 
CFD.  
 
Prompted by recent advances in CFD simulation 
technology, turbulence modeling4,5 and availability of an 
excellent data set6, we have undertaken a comprehensive 
investigation to study the effect of the following on CP 
and Ω: 
 
1. Diffusion system design parameters including inlet 
profiles, pre-diffuser area ratio, L/H, cowling, blockages 
due to dome swirlers, nozzle stem and tip, airflow splits 
between the dome, inner and outer annuli of the 
combustor liner.

3.  2-D versus 3-D simulations. 
4.  Different turbulence models and near-wall treatments

The prediffuser exit angle, exit height, L/H and

annulus) are 39.4 and 30.5 in2, respectively. The airflow 
splits between the three streams (dome, inner and outer 
annuli) are controlled independently in order to evaluate 
the effect of the flow splits on CP and Ω. 
 
A total of 118 static and total pressure taps were 
installed on the diffuser model test rig, shown 
schematically in Fig. 1a. Of these 64 were located in the 
rig inlet and prediffuser sections, the remaining 54 were 
located in the dump regions, the combustor dome, and 
upstream of the metering orifices. As part of the inlet 
instrumentation there were four 5-element total pressure 
rakes along with wall static pressures for each rake. The 
elements of these rakes were located at y/h = 0.1033, 
0.3081, 0.5095, 0.7078, and 0.9031, bottom to top. At 
the diffuser discharge plane another set of four 5-element 
total pressure rakes along with associated wall statics 
were located.  The radial positions of these total pressure 
elements were at y/h = 0.1045, 0.3136, 0.5227, 0.7365, 
and 0.9233, bottom to top. As part of the combustor 
dome instrumentation two mixer cups were each 
instrumented with five total pressures and five static 
pressures equally spaced around the cup circumferences. 
These taps were placed to quantify the pressure variation 
around the mixer cups. Pt3 is based on the calculated 
value determined from the measured diffuser inlet static 
pressure and the known inlet mass flow rate, assuming 
CD of 1.0. The dome total pressure is taken from the 
average of the dome pressures mounted onto the dome 
plate facing upstream, assuming stagnation flow 
conditions.   
 
For the selected series of 3-D diffuser rig testing to 
be compared with predictions in this paper and 
References 7-9, a total of 10 good quality runs  (Table 1) 
are used to quantify the effect of the following on 
pressure recovery and total pressure loss coefficients, CP 
and Ω: 
1. Diffuser inlet profiles, Runs 3, 4, 5 and 8   
Tables 2 and 3; 
2. Diffuser area ratio and length, Runs 3 and 9; 
3. W/ and w/o combustor dome cowling, Runs 3

4. Cowling inlet area, Runs 3 and 7; 
5. Nozzle tip diameter, Runs 3 and 11; 
6. Blockage due to nozzle stem, Runs 3 and 12; 
7. Airflow splits between dome, inner and outer 
passages that was conducted for all the 10 test 
cases. 
Here static pressure recovery and total pressure loss 
coefficients CP and Ω are: 
CP = (Ps-Ps3)/q3 
Ω  = (Pt3-Pt)/q3 
Subscripts s, t and 3 denote static and total pressures, 
combustor inlet station, respectively; q3 is the diffuser 
inlet dynamic head. 
Since the measurements were available only at 
five points across the passage height, reasonable and 
consistent assumption for the near-wall treatment needs 
to be made for center, inner and outer peaked profiler 
runs. The calculated results are therefore strongly 
influenced by the assumptions made for estimating the 
inlet conditions.  
Gas turbine combustor designers reliance on 
CFD for pre-test predictions can not be realized without 
tackling the issues of inlet conditions for the entire 
combustion system including diffuser inlet, fuel nozzles, 
mixers, primary/dilution, liner and dome cooling air. 
Significant progress has been made in this area in the last 
three years4. A similar process has been used to deal with 
analyzing the bow-tie profiler geometries and attendant  
3-dimensional flow field characteristics (Fig. 1b), of each 
of center, inner and outer peaked profilers were taken up 
by simulating the test conditions. GAMBIT was used for 
griding the inlet geometry through the prediffuser. For 
the through the profiler (TTP) analysis, an all hexahedral 
mesh comprised of ~512k cells was used, Fig. 1d.  A 
total of 245 grid points were used in the axial direction 
over a physical length of 9.2” starting from the upstream 
dump exit to the diffuser inlet. Similarly 20×96 cells 
were employed at the inlet section (fig. 1c) over a 180 
sector with a channel height of 0.76”.  A very fine mesh 
was employed near the profiler walls with the minimum 
cell size of 0.0155” and 0.033” in the axial and radial 
direction respectively.  The dimensions of the upstream 
dump were 8h*5h in the radial and axial direction 
respectively, where h is the height of the profiler 
(0.764”).

Description of Models & Grids 
 
A commercially available code, FLUENT was 
used in our investigation since it already has the 
turbulence models we had investigated in our earlier 
studies, e.g., References 1 and 3, in addition to having an 
excellent 
griding 
strategy 
suitable 
for 
the 
test 
configuration of interest to us. The reader should refer to 
FLUENT’s documentation5 and the Part I paper6 for 
details on the two turbulence models and the two types 
of wall functions, (standard k-ε and shear stress transport 
SST k-ω models; standard and non-equilibrium wall 
functions) used in this study.  
Gridding was done in the FLUENT’s pre-
processor, GAMBIT. An excellent quality all-quad 2d 
mesh, with 40k cells, was generated for the 2-D axi-
symmetric simulation (fig. 1e) ensuring y+ ~40 that is 
required for using with standard k-ε model with wall-
functions. ~10k cells were distributed in the pre-diffuser 
region, ~6k cells in the inner and outer annulus passages 
each, and the rest for the dump region. For 3-D 
simulations, the same 2-D mesh (of ~40k cells) was 
rotated to give a total of 2.64 million cells to resolve the 
geometrical details of the fuel nozzle and the concentric 
simulated mixer as shown in Figure 1e. Some of the 
critical details of the grids are summarized in the 
following table. 
RESULTS 
 
Table 2 and Figures 2 through 9 show back-to-
back comparison between 2-D and 3-D simulations with 
the standard k-ε model that uses non-equilibrium wall 
function described in References 5 and 6. For example in 
Table 2 for Runs 3 and 4 that correspond to the profilers 
that gave respectively center- and inner-peak velocity 
profiles at the diffuser inlet.  Here the agreement for the 
prediffuser data is excellent with both 2-D and 3-D 
simulations, average errors are less than 2.5% for CP, the 
static pressure recovery coefficient. However, the total 
pressure loss coefficient Ω is reduce by ~50% with 3-D 
compared to 2-D modeling approach.   
 
Since it is difficult to simulate 3-D features of 
fuel nozzles and the concentric mixers properly by 2-D, 
2-D modeling does not give good agreement with dome 
total pressure recovery or loss coefficient being off by as 
much as 25% for CP and 150% for Ω. On the other hand, 
the 3-D modeling approach reduces these errors to less 
than 8% and 50% respectively for CP and Ω. Similarly 
for both the combustor outer and inner annuli (passages), 
the average errors by 3-D modeling are ~50% lower than 
those with 2-D modeling. Moreover, the model’s 
agreement with data in regard to Ω is reasonably well 
which among other things could be attributed at least 
partly to the way it has been estimated experimentally.

Whereas the modeling does good job for Runs 3 
and 4, it does not show higher prediffuser CP and lower 
Ω values observed experimentally in Run 5, the outer-
peak inlet profile. Consequently, the average CP error 
with the 3-D approach is slightly higher than that with 2-
D simulation, being respectively 11.1% and 8.6%. 
Consequently, the advantage of 3-D over 2-D approach 
is somewhat less pronounced compared to Runs 3 and 4. 
Nevertheless, the composite effect for all the three runs 
as shown in the bottom part of Table 2 still favors 3-D 
simulation over 2-D modeling approach which is the 
current design practice for its obvious expediency. 
Further elaboration is shown in Figure 10 where 
comparison between data (along x-axis) and model 
predictions along y-axis for  CP and Ω is shown for the 
prediffuser, dome, outer and inner passages. This figure 
should be compared with similar Figures 12 and 13 of 
Reference 6 for the 2-D simulation.  
From visual representation, one can conclude the 
following main points of comparison in Table 3. 
1. On an overall basis 3-CFD reproduces data well 
for Runs 3 and 4, average errors being 1 and 
1.5%, respectively, for CP; the range of error 
being -8 to 10% for Run 3, and  -7 to 10% for 
Run 4. The corresponding errors with 2-D 
simulation are 7.5%, 6%, (0 to15%) and (-3 to 
15%). 
2. On the other hand for Run #5, the agreement is 
not as good; being –7.5% and (0 to –15%) with 
3-D, and -2.5 and (5 to –10%) with 2-D 
simulation. 
3. Similar statement can be made for CP of the 
prediffuser and dome in that the model do better 
correlation job for Runs 3 and 4 than Run #5. 
However, for the outer and inner passages the 
average errors are comparable. 
4. 2-D simulation with SST k-ω model is not as 
good as with the k-ε model, and the both are 
inferior to that of 3-D simulation. 
5. Nevertheless, there is enough disagreement 
between predictions and CP data from the 
simulated 3-D diffuser rig, an integrated 
diffuser and combustor liner analysis for 
predicting performance, profiles, emissions and 
wall temperatures should not be expected 
without applying proper correction at the critical 
inlets around the combustor to account for 
deviations between data and model predictions. 
 
 
Dome Total Pressure Recovery and Ω 
 
Figure 2 shows typical total pressure recovery 
versus dome flow rate, % compressor flow rate, %Wa3. 
Data shows that CP decreases from ~0.9 at ~30% flow 
through the dome to ~0.75 at ~70%Wa3; ~17% decrease 
in CP. It may be noticed that this paper as well as others 
in this series of investigation the calculations are also 
done for several test conditions so that it is possible to 
draw a trend line to compare with the data. 2-D 
predictions are considerably lower than data whereas the 
3-D simulations for Runs 3 and 4 are quite close to data, 
achieving qualitative trend rather very well. On the other 
hand for Run 5, 3-D predictions fall in between 2-D 
calculations and data. Figure 3 shows comparison 
between measured and predicted total pressure loss 
coefficient for the dome. 3-D predictions even though 
higher than data reproduce experimental trend rather 
very well.

CFD PROVIDES INSIGHT 
 
 
Even with less than majority consensus on the 
usefulness of 3-D CFD model-based diffusion system 
design approach, one can’t overestimate the importance 
of having the ability to characterize 3-D flow field, as 
shown in Figures 11, 12 and 13. When this information 
suitably combined with a most recent combustion 
modeling approach4, it will be possible to explain the 
occurrence of isolated liner and dome hot spots in 
addition to quantifying the effect of fuel nozzle 
manufacturing tolerances on predicting pattern factor. 
 
Finally, comparing 2-D diffusion modeling 
based predicted distribution of Pt, Ps, CP and turbulence 
kinetic energy contours, Figure 14 are significantly 
different from 3-D predictions, Figures 11 and 12. 3-D 
diffusion 
system 
modeling 
therefore 
strongly 
recommended over 2-D diffuser modeling. 
 
SUMMARY 
 
A systematic evaluation of 3-D modeling 
approach including the diffuser inlet conditions for a 
given profiler has been conducted to assess its limitations 
as a design tool for future diffuser design activities. 
Experimental data from a typical 3-D diffuser rig with 
three different profilers was compared with both 2-D and 
3-D modeling approaches. In both the cases, the diffuser 
inlet conditions were calculated by doing realistic 3-D 
simulation of the experimental set-up. Based on earlier 2-
D work6, the standard k-ε model with nonequilibrium 
wall function was used. Average errors in predicting 
static pressure recovery coefficient CP for prediffuser 
(PD), dome (D), outer passage (OP) and inner passage 
(IP) for the three runs Runs 3 (center-peak), 4 (inner-
