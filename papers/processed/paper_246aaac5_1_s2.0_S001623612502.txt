A B S T R A C T
This paper depicts the results and analysis of experimental investigations on the effect of hydrogen enrichment 
on the combustion characteristics of premixed C3H8/air flames in a Dual Annular Counter Rotating Swirl 
(DACRS) combustor. The novelty of the burner, developed for this experimental study, lies in its ability to 
independently regulate swirl and jet flows in concentric annuli. This design enables operation in multiple modes: 
swirl, jet, partially swirling, or stratified, thus effectively mimicking practical gas turbine conditions. In this 
work, the burner was operated under fully premixed swirl conditions. Hydrogen enrichment (0–50 % by volume) 
and global equivalence ratios (from blowout limits up to 1.0) were systematically evaluated. The primary ob­
jectives were to quantify the effects of hydrogen addition on flame stability, thermoacoustic dynamics, tem­
perature distributions, and emissions performance. Experimental results demonstrated that hydrogen enrichment 
significantly extends lean blowout limits up to 21 %. This improvement is attributed to increased flame speed, 
diffusivity, and OH radical production. Thermoacoustic analysis, utilizing Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) and 
Phase Space Reconstruction (PSR), revealed a clear transition from stable to unstable combustion regimes with 
increasing hydrogen content. This instability was characterized by enhanced pressure-heat release coupling and 
limit-cycle oscillations. Temperature profiles exhibited limited sensitivity to hydrogen fraction, showing stronger 
dependence on equivalence ratio. Emission analysis revealed substantial reductions in CO2 (10.5 %) and CO 
(33.3 %). However, these benefits were accompanied by a moderate increase (21 %) in NOx emissions. This 
increase in NOx is attributed to elevated flame temperatures and radical formation. Overall, these findings 
highlight hydrogen’s potential to improve flame stability and combustion efficiency. They provide critical 
experimental benchmarks for modeling and advancing low-emission, hydrogen-enriched combustion 
technologies.
1. Introduction
The global drive toward decarbonization has accelerated the devel­
opment of cleaner and more efficient combustion technologies. 
Hydrogen, a zero-carbon fuel at the point of combustion, offers high 
energy density and rapid reaction kinetics, producing only water vapor 
as a combustion byproduct [1–3]. Blending hydrogen with hydrocar­
bons like propane or methane not only reduces carbon emissions but 
also enhances flame stability and extends lean combustion limits, 
making it particularly suitable for retrofitting existing infrastructure in 
power generation and transportation [4–8]. Nevertheless, the practical 
implementation of hydrogen-enriched fuels faces significant challenges, 
including elevated flame temperatures, increased NOx emissions, and 
greater susceptibility to flashback and flame instability [9,10]. To 
address these challenges, hydrogen is typically blended gradually with 
conventional fuels, a strategy that improves flame characteristics and 
ensures safety and emission control [11]. This approach is particularly 
beneficial in lean premixed combustion (LPC) systems, widely utilized in 
gas turbines for their high efficiency and low NOx emissions [12,13]. 
However, LPC systems operating under ultra-lean conditions can expe­
rience flame blowout and thermoacoustic oscillations [14]. Previous 
research indicates that hydrogen addition in swirl-stabilized combustion 
enhances flame anchoring and propagation due to its high laminar flame 
speed, diffusivity, and reactivity, but simultaneously necessitates

Extensive studies have investigated hydrogen-enriched combustion 
across various burner geometries, fuel types, and conditions. Gee et al. 
[17] showed that hydrogen addition to low-swirl natural gas flames 
reduced radiant heat loss by 33 % and improved flame stability by 
decreasing lift-off height, although NOx emissions increased by up to 30 
%. De Persis et al. [18], using laser-induced fluorescence (LIF), found 
that hydrogen enrichment increased flame temperature and NO pro­
duction due to intensified thermal and NNH pathways; however, it 
enabled leaner operation, potentially reducing NO emissions at com­
parable temperatures. Guo et al. [19] and Schefer [20] demonstrated 
that hydrogen enrichment promotes key radicals (OH, H) formation, 
accelerating reaction rates, stabilizing flames, and anchoring them 
closer to stable flow regions. Collectively, these findings highlight hy­
drogen’s significant potential to enhance reactivity and stability in lean 
premixed combustion systems, provided that NOx emissions are effec­
tively managed.
Complementing the discussion on flame stability, several studies 
have highlighted the effects of hydrogen enrichment on thermoacoustic 
instabilities in premixed flames. Day et al. [21] identified that higher 
hydrogen contents in turbulent premixed flames lead to increased flame 
curvature, intensified heat-release fluctuations, and cellular flame 
structures, primarily due to differential diffusion rates of hydrogen 
relative to methane. Similarly, Patel and Shah [22] reported shorter 
flames and notable changes in acoustics, noise generation, and emission 
profiles for both swirling and non-swirling inverse diffusion flames 
depending on the hydrogen blending strategy. Additionally, Zhang et al. 
[23] demonstrated that hydrogen enrichment enhances turbulent 
burning velocities and flame wrinkling due to stronger flame turbulence 
interactions, ultimately improving flame propagation and stability in 
lean premixed combustion. These findings collectively underscore the 
necessity of managing thermoacoustic phenomena when employing 
hydrogen-enriched fuels.
Fuel staging and stratification significantly influence flame dy­
namics, affecting local equivalence ratios, anchoring positions, and 
combustion intensity. Wang et al. [24] investigated staged methane 
combustion and identified various flame topologies, such as lifted, 
attached, and V-shaped flames, influenced strongly by stratification. 
Although their study did not directly consider hydrogen, it provides 
valuable insights into how mixing strategies affect flame stability in 
swirl-stabilized systems. Cozzi and Coghe [25] showed that adding 
hydrogen to non-premixed natural gas flames enhanced lean stability, 
enabling operation at lower equivalence ratios but increasing NOx and 
soot emissions due to higher flame temperatures. Kim et al. [26] simi­
larly found that hydrogen enrichment improved blowout limits and 
reduced CO emissions in swirl-stabilized methane flames, though this 
was accompanied by elevated NOx emissions and upstream shifts in 
flame anchoring locations. These studies collectively emphasize the 
importance of carefully balancing hydrogen enrichment strategies with 
emission control and flame stability objectives.
From safety and operational perspectives, hydrogen blending 
significantly impacts flammability limits and flame resilience. Miao 
et al. [27] reported that hydrogen enrichment substantially increases the 
upper flammability limit. Jackson et al. [28], and Gauducheau et al. 
[29] found that hydrogen enhances strain resistance in laminar pre­
mixed flames, a critical advantage for stable combustion in high-strain 
environments like gas turbines. Their studies indicate that even small 
hydrogen additions improve flame robustness under challenging con­
ditions without significantly increasing ignition delays or exhaust tem­
peratures. Similarly, El-Ghafour et al. [30] demonstrated improved 
flame attachment and notable reductions in CO2 emissions in natural gas 
hydrogen flames, attributed to enhanced combustion efficiency and 
flame stabilization near the burner exit, supporting the practical adop­
tion of hydrogen-enriched fuels.
In swirl-stabilized combustion systems, hydrogen enrichment results 
in more compact and brighter flames, thereby broadening the limits of 
flame stability. These effects are particularly pronounced when 
hydrogen is premixed rather than separately injected into the primary 
fuel stream. Several studies have emphasized the critical importance of 
DACRS burner configuration and flow aerodynamics in enabling effec­
tive hydrogen enrichment strategies. Kim et al. [31] demonstrated that 
swirl-stabilized combustors operating with independently controlled 
dual swirlers significantly improve flame stabilization by shortening 
flame lengths and enhancing combustion intensity under hydrogen 
enrichment conditions. Han et al. [32] further showed the beneficial 
impact of controlled stratification ratios in DACRS configurations, 
illustrating how precise stratification influences flame anchoring, 
structure, and overall stability. The DACRS configuration’s ability to 
create distinct recirculation zones and finely controlled mixing regions 
strongly contributes to stable combustion and effective flame anchoring. 
These burner features are particularly advantageous for mitigating 
flashback risks and managing thermoacoustic instabilities associated 
with highly reactive, hydrogen-rich fuel blends. Collectively, these 
recent studies underscore the DACRS combustor’s suitability for diverse, 
fuel-flexible, and low-emission combustion applications [24,33].
To address the emerging challenges of hydrogen-enriched combus­
tion, recent studies have explored innovative strategies such as Colorless 
Distributed Combustion (CDC) and staged fuel injection, which show 
potential in mitigating thermoacoustic instabilities and reducing emis­
sions. These methods redistribute heat release and acoustic coupling 
within the combustor, minimizing hotspots and pressure fluctuations 
and consequently reducing thermal NOx formation. Alabas et al. [34]

This study presents a comprehensive experimental investigation into 
the effects of hydrogen enrichment on premixed propane-air swirl 
flames using a Dual Annular Counter-Rotating Swirl (DACRS) burner. 
Although hydrogen enrichment in methane-air and natural gas com­
bustion systems is well-documented, propane-based swirl combustion 
remains relatively unexplored, particularly regarding flame stability, 
thermoacoustic behavior, and emissions under varying hydrogen frac­

• Characterize the influence of hydrogen enrichment (0–50 %) on lean 
premixed propane flames across equivalence ratios spanning from 
blowout to near stoichiometric (ϕg = 1.0).
• Investigate the impacts of hydrogen enrichment on flame macro­
structure, lean blowout limits, thermoacoustic instabilities, and 
emissions formation.
• Provide comprehensive experimental benchmarks essential for vali­

bustion strategies for advanced gas turbine applications.
To achieve these objectives, a multi-diagnostic approach is adopted, 
incorporating high-fidelity sound pressure measurements, CH* and OH* 
chemiluminescence diagnostics, spectral analysis (FFT), and nonlinear 
phase-space characterization. This robust methodology provides 
multidimensional insights into flame dynamics and combustion acoustic 
interactions. The findings from this research fill critical knowledge gaps 
and offer practical guidelines for developing stable, efficient, and low- 
emission propane-based combustion technologies utilizing hydrogen- 
enriched fuels.
2. Experimental setup and methodology
2.1. Combustor configuration and instrumentation
The experimental investigation was conducted using a DACRS 
burner, a configuration that closely replicates modern gas turbine 
combustor designs. The DACRS combustor enables the formation of 
strong recirculation zones, promoting enhanced fuel–air mixing and 
robust flame stabilization in lean premixed combustion. Its design is 
particularly well-suited for investigating flame stability, low-emission 
combustion strategies, and fuel flexibility scenarios, including 
hydrogen enrichment. The configuration has been detailed in prior 
studies involving jet and swirling flames [36,37].
A schematic of the experimental setup and detailed burner layout are 
shown in Fig. 1 and Fig. 2. The combustor consists of three concentric 
stainless-steel tubes. The combustor comprises three concentric 
stainless-steel tubes that create inner, middle, and outer channels. The 
inner tube (12.7 mm) does not convey any fluid but supports a ceramic 
bluff body, which acts as a flame stabilizer. The middle tube (25.7 mm) 
and the outer tube (38.1 mm) deliver premixed fuel–air mixtures into 
the inner and outer annuli, respectively.
A key feature of this combustor is its dual-swirling capability, in 
which both annular streams are imparted with a swirl collar. Each collar 
contains eight equally spaced guide channels, each inclined at 30◦to the 
horizontal. The channels have a diameter of 2.5 mm and are designed to 
enable either co-swirl or counter-swirl operation, depending on their 
orientation. Notably, the novelty of this dual annular burner lies in its 
ability to operate in three distinct aerodynamic modes: (i) jet flame 
mode, where both annulus supply axial (non-swirling) flows; (ii) swirl

Fuel and oxidizer delivery is controlled by ALICAT mass flow con­

reading. Propane and hydrogen (purity ≥99.999 %) are used as fuels, 
while air is supplied by a compressor and passed through a moisture 
removal unit to maintain consistent humidity levels. All gases are pre­

pipelines. The controllers were operated through a LabVIEW interface. 
This LabVIEW was integrated with MATLAB and EES back-end routines 
to enable automated flow setpoint computation and real-time adjust­
ment. Thermoacoustic pressure oscillations are captured using Kulite 
MIC-093 microphones. Two microphones are positioned 50 mm and 90 
mm upstream of the bluff body, while a third serves as a reference 
microphone placed remotely to account for background noise. Micro­
phone signals are amplified using a Kulite signal conditioner and ac­
quired through a high-speed DAQ system synchronized via LabVIEW. 
Heat release rate fluctuations are captured using a Photomultiplier Tube 
(PMT) equipped with narrow-band optical filters targeting CH* and OH* 
chemiluminescence (centered at 430 ± 5 nm and 310 ± 5 nm, respec­
tively). The PMT is aligned to the flame axis to monitor radical emissions 
correlated with unsteady combustion.
The flame images were captured using a Nikon D5200 DSLR camera, 
configured with a 1/40 s shutter speed, f/2.2 aperture, and ISO 250. This 
setup enabled time-resolved imaging of flame structure and dynamics 
across different operating regimes. Temperature measurements were 
obtained using a B-type thermocouple (Pt-30 %Rh, Pt-6 %Rh). The 
thermocouple was mounted on a precise traversing mechanism, allow­
ing accurate axial and radial positioning within the flow field. The 
thermocouple was integrated with a LabVIEW data acquisition system 
that recorded and averaged temperature readings once steady-state 
conditions were reached. Temperature readings were corrected for 
radiative heat loss to the surroundings following the well-established 
method developed by Shaddix [38,39]. Flue gas emissions are 
analyzed downstream using a Testo 350 gas analyzer, providing real- 
time measurements of CO2, CO, NOx, and O2 under varying hydrogen 
fractions and equivalence ratios. The sensor specifications were as fol­
lows: CO (0–500 ppm ± 0.5 ppm with 1 ppm resolution), NO (0–4000 
ppm ± 5 ppm with 1 ppm resolution), O2 (0–25 vol% ± 0.8 % FSV with 
0.01 vol% resolution), and CO2 (0–50 vol% ± 0.3 vol% with 0.1 vol% 
resolution). This setup allowed for the accurate evaluation of combus­
tion performance and pollutant formation throughout all test conditions. 
All flow controllers, data acquisition systems, and thermocouples were 
fully integrated and connected to a central PC-based control system. 
Monitoring and real-time adjustments were performed through a Lab­
VIEW interface, ensuring synchronized operation and automated data 
logging across all subsystems.

2.2. Test conditions and parameter definitions
All experiments were conducted under ambient conditions of 1 atm 
pressure and room temperature (25 ◦C). The main objective was to 
investigate how hydrogen enrichment (HF%) and global equivalence 
ratio (ϕɡ) affect combustion performance in premixed C3H8/H2/air swirl 
flames. Key combustion parameters examined included flame stability 
(lean blowout limits), flame macrostructure, thermoacoustic behavior, 
and pollutant emissions under controlled flow conditions.
Experiments were designed to cover a broad range of hydrogen 
enrichment levels and equivalence ratios, ensuring consistent flow dy­
namics by fixing the inner annulus Reynolds number (Rein) and main­
taining fully premixed conditions (SR = 1). While hydrogen enrichment 
is defined by volume fraction (HF%), it corresponds to varying energy 
contributions; for example, a 50 vol% hydrogen blend represents 
approximately 10.6 % of the total fuel energy (power) input based on the 
lower heating values of hydrogen and propane. Detailed equations and 
methodologies used for calculating flow rates and mixture compositions 
are provided in Appendix A. A concise summary of the complete set of 
experimental conditions, including parameter values and ranges, is 
presented in Table 1.
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Effect of hydrogen enrichment on static stability
The static stability of premixed flames in swirl-stabilized combustors 
is primarily governed by the balance between flame propagation speed 
and local flow velocity at the stabilization zone. The blowout was 
determined by performing an equivalence ratio sweep test, in which the 
global equivalence ratio (ϕɡ) was gradually decreased in increments of 
0.01 from a stable condition until blowout [40]. Blowout occurs when 
the flame cannot propagate upstream to anchor within the recirculation 
zone, a phenomenon strongly influenced by fuel composition and 
mixture properties [41]. Hydrogen, as a highly reactive additive, alters 
this balance through its effects on laminar flame speed, diffusivity, and 
chemical kinetics. In this section, the impact of hydrogen enrichment on 
the static stability of propane/air mixtures is analyzed using experi­
mentally determined blowout limits against power and adiabatic flame 
temperature contours (AFT).
Static stability was assessed by systematically determining the lean 
blowout limits across varying hydrogen fractions and global equivalence 
ratios, under a fixed Reynolds number. A consistent experimental pro­
cedure was followed to generate the stability maps presented in Fig. 3(a) 
and (b). The flame initially stabilized at a high global equivalence ratio 
ϕɡ, which was then gradually reduced until the blowout occurred. This 
was identified by visible flame detachment and extinction. This process 
was repeated for each hydrogen fraction (HF) by gradually adjusting ϕɡ 
to determine the corresponding blowout limit.
As hydrogen content increases, the blowout limits shift toward 
leaner equivalence ratios. The minimum ϕɡ for stable flame operation 
decreased from 0.52 at 0 % HF to 0.41 at 50 % HF, representing an 
approximate of 21 % extension in the lean stability margin. This 
improvement is attributed to hydrogen’s high laminar flame speed, 
diffusivity, and enhanced radical formation, particularly OH, which 
collectively enhance flame propagation under lean conditions [20,26]. 
The enhanced lean stability limits observed with hydrogen enrichment 
in our propane-air swirl flames qualitatively align with previously re­
ported results for methane-air flames. For example, Mohammadnejad 
et al. [42] observed substantial extension of lean blowout limits (ϕ ≈
0.45–0.50) with hydrogen enrichment (up to 40 vol%) in methane-air 
turbulent premixed flames. Although our propane-air flames exhibit 
similar qualitative improvements, quantitative differences arise from 
propane’s inherently lower flame speed. Additionally, while a compact 
active turbulence generator employed in [42], our DACRS burner 
maintains controlled turbulence conditions (fixed Re and constant swirl 
number), similarly benefiting flame anchoring.
Fig. 3a shows that for a given power level, hydrogen addition permits 
flame stabilization at lower ϕɡ, enhancing lean stability and thermal 
efficiency. As shown in Fig. 3b, hydrogen-enriched flames remain stable 
even at significantly lower AFT. At the blowout limit, AFT decreases 
from approximately 1550 K at 0 % HF to 1357 K at 50 % HF, a reduction 
of nearly 193 K (12.5 %). While such a temperature drop would typically 
signal a loss in flame stability, this trend primarily reflects the associated 
decrease in the ϕɡ from 0.52 to 0.41. This reduction in ϕɡ is enabled by 
hydrogen enrichment, which allows combustion to occur under leaner 
conditions. The lower equivalence ratio reduces the total chemical en­
ergy release and, consequently, results in a lower AFT. However, hy­
drogen’s high laminar flame speed, diffusivity, and ability to enhance 
radical production compensate for the thermal deficit. These properties 
enable stable combustion even under cooler, leaner conditions. As a 
result, the flame is able to propagate and remain anchored, effectively 
extending the lean flammability limit despite the reduction in thermal 
energy [20].
These findings emphasize that AFT alone is not the sole determinant 
of flame stability in multi-fuel swirl-stabilized systems. Instead, flame 
speed, diffusivity, and radical chemistry, all positively influenced by 
hydrogen, play dominant roles in anchoring the flame under lean con­
ditions. The consistent downward shift of the blowout contours across 
both power and AFT maps underscores hydrogen’s effectiveness in 
broadening the stable operating limit, without requiring higher thermal 
power input. These results underscore hydrogen’s viability as a practical 
flame stabilizing additive for advanced, low-emission combustion 
applications.
3.2. Effect of hydrogen enrichment on thermoacoustic instability
Thermoacoustic instability arises from destabilizing coupling be­
tween pressure oscillations and heat release fluctuations, wherein their 
in-phase alignment leads to acoustic energy amplification. This inter­
action can result in self-excited oscillations, intensifying acoustic en­
ergy, noise, and vibrations, which may potentially cause structural 
damage [43]. The introduction of hydrogen into the fuel mixture 
significantly impacts these dynamics by altering fundamental flame 
characteristics, including laminar flame speed, reaction kinetics, and 
diffusivity. Consequently, hydrogen enrichment influences not only 
static stability but also the flame’s susceptibility to dynamic instabilities 
[44].
In this study, the influence of hydrogen enrichment on thermoa­
coustic behavior was experimentally examined over a range of hydrogen 
fractions (0–50 %) and global equivalence ratios (ϕɡ = 0.6–1.0). A 
combined analytical approach was employed to characterize the dy­
namic response: Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) was used to evaluate the 
spectral content of acoustic pressure and chemiluminescence signals 
(CH*, OH*), while Phase Space Reconstruction (PSR) was applied to 
assess the temporal evolution and nonlinear dynamics of the oscilla­
tions. Together, these techniques offer a comprehensive perspective of 
the system’s behavior in both frequency and time domains, enabling 
detailed insight into the onset and progression of thermoacoustic 
instability. The FFT spectra are presented in arbitrary units (AU),

To explore the complex nonlinear dynamics of the thermoacoustic 
system, Phase Space Reconstruction (PSR) was applied to the time-series 
signals of acoustic pressure and chemiluminescence (CH*, OH*). This 
analysis aimed to gain deeper insight into the evolution of pressure heat 
release coupling under varying operating conditions. PSR is a nonlinear 
analysis technique that reconstructs a system’s state space using delayed 
versions of a single observable. This approach enables the visualization 
of transitions between different dynamical regimes, such as stable, 
quasi-periodic, or limit-cycle (unstable) behavior [46]. In this study, 
three representative operating conditions were selected to capture the 
full spectrum of dynamic behavior: a fully stable regime (HF = 0 %, ϕɡ =
0.8), a transitional regime (HF = 30 %, ϕɡ = 0.8), and a strongly un­
stable case (HF = 50 %, ϕɡ = 0.8), as shown in Fig. 8. These cases were 
chosen to capture the full spectrum of system responses, ranging from 
random, non-periodic fluctuations to coherent oscillations, as identified 
in the FFT spectra. As shown in Fig. 7, hydrogen enrichment progres­
sively increases the spectral amplitude of both pressure and chem­
iluminescence signals. It also causes the dominant frequencies to align, 
which are clear indicators of the onset and intensification of flame 
acoustic coupling. The time delay for PSR was selected based on the first 
minimum of the average mutual information, following standard prac­
tice in nonlinear time series analysis [46].
At HF = 0 %, the reconstructed phase-space patterns appear as 
diffuse, cloud-like structures. These structures reflect noise-dominated, 
nonperiodic behavior, consistent with acoustically stable combustion 
and minimal coherent interaction between heat release and acoustic 
perturbations. With an increase to HF = 30 %, the acoustic pressure 
pattern transitions into a more structured toroidal shape. This indicates 
the emergence of low-dimensional limit cycle periodic oscillations rep­
resented as coherent, repetitive loops within the phase-space domain.

3.3. Effect of hydrogen enrichment on flame macrostructure
The flame macrostructure is a critical characteristic influencing 
combustion stability, heat transfer, and emission formation in swirl- 
stabilized premixed flames. The shape and structure of the flame 
significantly impact how the flame interacts with flow dynamics, heat 
distribution, and pollutant formation mechanisms. Stable and compact 
flames anchored close to the combustor inlet promote effective flame 
stabilization by resisting turbulent fluctuations, thereby reducing the 
risk of flame blowout. This close anchoring ensures stable combustion 
through consistent flame propagation and anchoring points within the 
recirculation zones [52]. Additionally, compact flames offer improved 
flame stability and concentrated heat release, though their reduced 
surface area may decrease overall convective heat transfer efficiency. 
Flame shape also directly influences emissions; stable and compact 
flames typically have optimized temperature profiles that facilitate 
complete combustion, thus minimizing carbon monoxide (CO) and un­
burned hydrocarbons. However, these flames may increase thermal NOx 
formation at elevated temperatures, which requires careful optimization 
[53].
Fig. 9 illustrates the effect of hydrogen enrichment on flame 
macrostructure across a range of global equivalence ratios (ϕɡ= 0.5, 0.6, 
0.7, and 0.8). Systematic changes in flame shape, length, stability, and 
luminosity with increasing HF are evident. At higher equivalence ratios 
(ϕɡ= 0.8 and 0.7), flames exhibit a stable and compact conical structure, 
firmly anchored at the burner exit. Increasing HF from 0 % to 50 % 
slightly reduces flame height, accompanied by enhanced luminosity and 
a more intense blue color. This indicates increased combustion intensity 
and reaction rates, aligning with known characteristics of hydrogen- 
enriched flames such as higher laminar flame speeds, improved flame 
stability, and increased heat release rates. The flame shortening with 
hydrogen addition is primarily due to accelerated combustion kinetics, 
as hydrogen significantly enhances radical formation (OH and CH rad­
icals). These radicals actively participate in propagating combustion 
reactions, as discussed in earlier sections. These observed macrostruc­
tural trends are consistent with previous findings in the literature. Link 
et al. [54] demonstrated similar structural transformations for the CH4/ 
H2 flame, noting that flames transitioned from elongated and partially 
lifted conditions to compact and well-anchored states with increased 
hydrogen content. In addition to this recent publication, Agostinelli 
et al. [55] observed analogous flame shortening and improved upstream 
anchoring in hydrogen-enriched CH4/air swirl flames. Similarly, 
Abdelhalim et al. [52] reported enhanced compactness and luminosity 
in stratified flames stabilized within a dual annular counter-rotating 
swirl (DACRS) combustor. Collectively, these studies reinforce the crit­
ical role of hydrogen enrichment in enhancing flame stability and 
compactness across various combustor configurations.
At intermediate equivalence ratios (ϕɡ= 0.6), flames initially show a 
slightly elongated, partially lifted flame at low HF (0–20 %), suggesting 
marginal stability conditions. As hydrogen enrichment exceeds 30 %, 
the flames become visibly shorter, more compact, and brighter, with 
improved stabilization at the burner rim. These visual changes reflect 
the increased combustion reactivity and stability associated with 
hydrogen addition, consistent with the trends discussed in earlier sec­
tions. Furthermore, as indicated in Fig. 9, hydrogen addition increases 
the AFT, enhancing thermal buoyancy and promoting stronger recircu­
lation. This facilitates the transport of hot gases and reactive species to 
the stabilization zone, especially beneficial under lean conditions.
At the leanest condition studied (ϕɡ= 0.5), pure propane flames were 
unable to sustain stable combustion, resulting in flame extinction or 
blowout. However, incremental hydrogen enrichment (HF = 10 % to 50 
%) enabled the flames to transition from lifted and unstable conditions 
to progressively anchored and stabilized states. Hydrogen enrichment 
effectively extended the lean flammability limits as illustrated in section 
3.1, allowing stable combustion at significantly leaner mixtures due to 
its higher reactivity, broader flammability range, and enhanced ignition 
potential through increased radical formation.
Overall, the observed flame macrostructural trends in Fig. 9 illustrate 
hydrogen’s significant role in enhancing flame stability and compact­
ness across varying equivalence ratios. The resulting increase in com­
bustion intensity and improved resistance to extinction not only 
highlight practical benefits in combustion performance but also align 
with the emission reductions detailed in Section 3.5, emphasizing the 
viability of hydrogen-blended fuels for low-emission operation in swirl- 
stabilized combustors.
3.4. Effect of hydrogen enrichment on axial and radial temperature 
profiles
Temperature profiles measured along the axial centerline and

dynamics, flame stability, and pollutant formation processes in swirl- 
stabilized combustors. Accurate temperature measurements are essen­

operational conditions [26]. Moreover, the measured temperature pro­
files provide valuable data that can support and improve numerical 
models used to simulate combustion behavior. In the present study, 
temperature measurements were conducted at a fixed Reynolds number 
(Rein = 3000), capturing the effects of hydrogen enrichment (HF = 0–50 
%) and global equivalence ratios (ϕɡ= 0.55–0.75). Due to limitations 
associated with thermocouple placement, axial and radial measure­

maintaining data accuracy.

4. Conclusion
The effects of hydrogen enrichment on premixed C3H8/air flames 
were investigated in a Dual Annular Counter Rotating Swirl (DACRS) 
combustor. The emphasis was placed on flame stability, thermoacoustic 
behavior, temperature field, and exhaust emissions. The combustor 
configuration mimics practical swirl burner designs, offering opera­
tional flexibility across various flow configurations. Hydrogen was 
premixed with propane at volumetric fractions (HF = 0–50 %), and 
experiments were conducted under globally lean conditions near 
blowout (ϕɡ= 0.5–1.0) at a fixed Rein = 3000. Key findings from the 
tested combustor configuration can be summarized as follows: 
• Static stability analysis demonstrated that hydrogen enrichment 
markedly extends the lean blowout limit, reducing the minimum 
stable equivalence ratio by approximately 21 % as HF increased from 
0 % to 50 %. This enhancement is primarily driven by hydrogen’s 
high laminar flame speed, superior diffusivity, and increased OH and 
CH radical production, enabling flame anchoring even at reduced 
adiabatic temperatures.
• Thermoacoustic characterization using FFT and Phase Space 
Reconstruction (PSR) revealed a distinct transition from stable to 
unstable combustion regimes with increasing hydrogen enrichment. 
At low HF levels, pressure and chemiluminescence signals exhibited 
weak, broadband fluctuations. As HF increased, coherent narrow­
band oscillations emerged, indicating Rayleigh-driven coupling be­
tween pressure and unsteady heat release. PSR attractors confirmed 
the development of nonlinear limit-cycle dynamics at elevated HF, 
highlighting strong acoustic–combustion interaction. These results 
offer comprehensive temporal and spectral insights into the onset of 
thermoacoustic instability in partially stratified swirl flames.
• Temperature measurements exhibited limited sensitivity to 
hydrogen enrichment, as the global heat release was predominantly 
governed by the ϕɡ. Nevertheless, hydrogen addition led to steeper 
radial temperature gradients and locally intensified reaction zones. 
In contrast, variations in ϕɡ exerted a more substantial influence on 
both axial and radial temperature distributions, confirming its pri­
mary role in enhancing combustion intensity and promoting flame 
compactness.
• Emission analysis at ϕɡ = 0.75 highlighted a dual benefit of hydrogen 
enrichment. CO2 emissions decreased by approximately 10.5 % due 
to the carbon-free nature of hydrogen. CO levels dropped by 33.3 %, 
reflecting improved oxidation kinetics and more complete combus­
tion. However, this benefit was accompanied by a 21 % increase in 
NOx emissions. The rise in NOx is attributed to higher flame tem­
peratures and enhanced radical formation, consistent with thermal 
NO formation mechanisms.
In conclusion, hydrogen enrichment improves lean stability, com­
bustion efficiency, and reduces CO/CO2 emissions in propane-air swirl 
flames. These findings support the use of hydrogen-enriched propane as 
a transitional fuel in clean energy systems and provide reliable experi­
mental data for future combustor modeling and design. Future
