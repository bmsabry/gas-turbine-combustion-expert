Effect of Fuel Injection Location on Combustion Instability 
in a Dump Combustor 
Roopa D. Desai1 ,S. Ramgopal2 and S. R. Chakravarthy3 
Indian Institute of Technology Madras, Chennai 600036 India 
The acoustic behavior of a model combustor with backward facing step is studied, to 
examine the effect fuel injection location on the combustion instability.  Six different 
injection locations, such as far upstream of the step, near upstream of the step, injection at 
the step, near downstream to the step, in the recirculation zone, and far downstream to the 
step, are investigated.  Acoustic characteristics of the combustor over a wide range of 
equivalence ratios and Reynolds number based on step height are carried out.  Liquefied 
petroleum gas (LPG) is used as fuel for the acoustic characterization.  The effect of injection 
location on the blow off limits is presented, to identify specific conditions corresponding to 
unstable regions of combustion.  Simultaneous unsteady pressure measurement, fuel flow 
fluctuations (by Mie scattering) and chemiluminescence imaging are done for conditions 
corresponding to local maxima and local minima of acoustic amplitude as the air flow rate is 
varied.  Methane is used as fuel for these studies.  The fuel injection location dictates the 
effect of fuel flow fluctuations (equivalence ratio fluctuations) on heat release and the 
corresponding acoustic oscillations.  It is shown that within an optimum distance upstream 
of the step, the characteristics of vortex-shedding dominated acoustic excitation are 
predominated by those of the equivalence ratio fluctuations.  Local maxima and minima of 
acoustic amplitude with air flow rate when fuel is injected downstream is from constructive 
and destructive interference of oscillations propagating along vertical and acoustic 
streamlines. 
I. Introduction 
ow NOx emission, noise reduction and fuel economy are the current focus of the gas turbine community.  
Operating combustors with lean mixtures are preferred on these grounds.  Combustion of lean mixtures is prone 
to combustion instabilities.  The primary concerns over combustion instability are the low frequency and high 
amplitude oscillations that lead to reduction in operating envelopes and hence performance, and structural failures.  
The complexity arises because of its interdisciplinary nature.  The primary mechanism through which the 
combustion dynamics could be understood is the coupling between fluid dynamics, chemical heat release, and 
acoustics.  Acoustics plays a major role by providing the feedback between flow and the unsteady chemical heat 
release.  Through acoustic feedback, the vortex shedding in the flow modulates the unsteady heat release [1].  The 
heat release acts as a source of sound.  In confined flows such as ducts, the location of the source of sound, i.e., the 
heat release, is one of the determining factors of combustion instability [2,3].  Vortical structures grow and detach in 
presence of external perturbations, causing the flame to flap as the vortices are shed past it. 
 The location of fuel injection relative to the flame holder is quite varied in gas turbines depending on the 
application.  In land based gas turbines, the fuel is injected upstream of the flame stabilization zone to create a 
reasonably premixed mixture of reactants.  In flight gas turbines, the fuel is injected downstream of the dump 
diffuser and the fuel and air are intensely mixed and burned within a short distance downstream.  In either case, the 
location of fuel injection is within the acoustic field produced in the combustor.  In the case of injection upstream of 
the flame stabilization zone, oscillatory injection of fuel in response to the prevalent acoustic field in the air inlet 
leads to equivalence ratio fluctuations – a prominent mechanism of combustion instability [2].  In this case, the 
distance between the fuel injection location and the flame stabilization zone dictates the delay of mixture fraction 
pockets being convected along the air flow.  In the case of injection downstream of the dump diffuser, it has recently

The instabilities are also caused by interactions of combustor pressure oscillations with reactants supply rates,

equivalence ratio arriving at the flame exhibits oscillations [7].

The blow-off limits for every length and fuel flow

equivalence ratio versus Reynolds number domain.  
Hyperbolae in this domain correspond to constant fuel 
flow rate.  Lower hyperbolae correspond to lower fuel 
flow rates and vice versa.  In each of these graphs, as one 
traverses a constant fuel flow rate line from the top left 
corner, one encounters a data point for the given length 
of the combustor when the flow blows off.  So, the 
bottom right part of the graphs corresponds to the blow-
off condition.

rates, a narrow critical range of Reynolds number exists 
beyond which the flame blows off over a wide range of 
low equivalence ratio, whereas for high fuel flow rates, a 
narrow critical range of equivalence ratio exists below 
which the flame blows off over a wide range of high

IV.  Conclusion 
A systematic study on role of fuel injection location over combustion instability of a model combustor with 
backward facing step as flame holder is carried out. Fuel injection is done upstream to the step, through the step and 
downstream of the step. Two injection location upstream to the step and three injection locations downstream to the 
step are identified. The study is carried out in two phases. In the first phase the unsteady pressure measurements are 
done for different combustor configurations (based on total length and fuel injection scheme) over a wide range of 
inlet Reynolds number and equivalence ratios. LPG is used in this phase. Based on the acoustic data, Blow off limits 
is identified.  Blow off limits provides information on the operating equivalence ratio regions of the combustor and 
effect of configuration on the variation of blow off equivalence ratio. Blow off limit maps throws light on the effect 
of fuel injection locations on stability of the combustor.  Recirculation and near downstream injection cases exhibit 
stable flame near low Reynolds numbers, indicating the extent of unmixedness. Far upstream and far downstream 
injection cases are considerably unstable. The optimum injection location for stable flame may be located close to 
near upstream to the step. Near upstream injection case exhibits acoustic mode with Reynolds number, indicating 
the dominance of fuel flow fluctuations over flame vortex interactions. In other cases vortex mode is present with 
modulation by the fuel flow fluctuations.  In downstream injection cases both vortex and acoustic modes are present.  
The second phase is to carry out simultaneous acoustic measurement, fuel flow fluctuations (Mie scattering) and 
chemiluminescence for specific flow conditions and configuration. Conditions corresponding to Local minima and 
local maxima of acoustic amplitude are chosen.  Methane is used as fuel in the second phase. The instantaneous

In practical cases the location of the fuel injector in combustors are most important as far as the stability of the 
combustor is concerned. The upstream injection cases show that the fuel flow fluctuations play a vital role in a 
stable flame zone. The discussion implies that the near upstream injection leads to a stable flame zone, which can 
also be seen from the blow off limits. This is useful for the practical combustors to locate the injector at the 
upstream of the flame stabilization so as to maintain the premixedness. The location of the injector should not be too 
near to the flame stabilization zone leading to a non premixed flame and high flame vortex interactions. So an 
optimum distance, which leads to less noise at a lean equivalence ratio, should be opted for stable combustor 
conditions.
