Properties of the iodine molecule relevant to laser-induced 
fluorescence experiments in gas flows 
B. Hiller* and R.K. Hanson 
Stanford University, Dept. of Mechanical Engineering, High Temperature Gasdynamics Laboratory, Stanford, CA 94305-3032, USA 
Abstract. Laser-induced fluorescence of iodine seed molecules can 
be used for both flow visualization and accurate measurements of 
gasdynamic properties. This paper gives an introductory review of 
the use of iodine in experimental fluid mechanics, including basic 
formulas for saturated and non-saturated fluorescence (excited with 
narrowband or broadband lasers), line shape, line strength and 
quenching behavior. Techniques for the seeding of the molecules 
into the gas flow and the safe handling of the gas are discussed. 
Finally, a simple numerical example is given for the calculation of 
absorption and fluorescence signals with discussion of the measure- 
ment of gasdynamic properties. 
1 Introduction 
In modern gasdynamics, there is a continuous demand for 
more versatile and accurate experimental techniques for flow 
visualization and measurements of gasdynamic properties. 
Laser-induced fluorescence (LIF) is among the most attrac- 
tive techniques since it can provide time-resolved, 2-dimen- 
sional information about temperature, pressure, velocity and 
density. 
Iodine is a uniquely suited seed molecule for LIF mea- 
surements. It conveniently absorbs in the visible region of 
the spectrum (e.g. at the green 514.5 nm line of the argon ion 
laser), fluoresces in a visible yellow, has reasonable values for 
vapor pressure, absorption cross-section and fluorescence 
yield, and it is only mildly toxic. Its utility for flow measure- 
ments is demonstrated. 
The most attractive alternative is oxygen (Lee et al. 1986; 
Massey and Lemon 1984). Oxygen requires strong ultra- 
violet lasers (low fluorescence yield due to predissociation) 
and its use for measurements of gasdynamic parameters is 
still subject to extensive research. However, it may become 
the molecule of choice in the future. 
A second, very useful molecule is the non-toxic biacetyl 
(2,3-butanedione) which has been successfully utilized by 
Epstein (1977) for density measurements via its fluorescence

and by Hiller et al. (1984) for velocity measurements through 
its long-lived phosphorescence. Other flow properties ap-

Sodium has been used successfully for flow visualization 
and velocity measurements by Miles (1975) and Zimmer- 
mann and Miles (1980). However, it requires heating of the 
gas and experimental care due to its reactivity. NO has been 
used for the visualization and temperature measurements of 
nonreacting and reacting flows (Seitzman et al. 1985), but it 
is very toxic. Among the other halogens, chlorine has been 
used as an absorbing species in tomography by Faris and 
Byer (1986), but its fluorescence yield is small due to predis- 
sociation. 
This list shows that iodine is a very attractive seed 
molecule at least in noncombusting gas flows (iodine is not 
chemically stabe in a reactive environment, another reason 
for the interest in oxygen). In addition to its use in flow 
visualization by Teshima (1983) and McDaniel and Graves 
(1986), iodine has beeen utilized to measure pressure 
(McDaniel 1984; Hiller and Hanson 1988), temperature 
(Fletcher and McDaniel 1987), density (McDaniel 1982, 
1983), and velocity (McDaniel 1983; Hiller et al. 1983; Hiller 
and Hanson 1985, 1988) both in single points of the flow field 
and simultaneously at multiple points in a plane. 
The motivation for this paper is to review the properties 
of iodine and to provide a resource for anyone who wishes 
to use laser-induced fluorescence of iodine in experimental 
gas dynamics. The experimental section (Sect. 3) of this 
paper is written based on our experience with a small wind 
tunnel (length scales on the order of centimeters) but it 
should provide a good starting point for readers with larger 
facilities in mind. The spectroscopy described is universal. 
After an introduction to the basic principles of laser- 
induced fluorescence, a simplified set of formulas will be 
listed which enables the calculation of the signal intensities 
that can be expected in a given flow situation (Sect. 2). Ex- 
perimental techniques for the seeding of the iodine molecules 
to the flow as well as the safe handling will be discussed 
(Sect. 3). Section 4 will focus on the spectroscopy of iodine

2 Absorption/fluorescence model 
A similar setup is employed in all laser-induced fluorescence 
schemes (Fig. 1). Gas molecules absorb a portion of the inci- 
dent laser radiation, and a fraction of the absorbed radiation 
is subsequently emitted as fluorescence in all directions. By 
detecting the fluorescence in a direction which is perpendic- 
ular to the exciting laser beam, a high depth resolution is 
achieved which is only limited by the thickness of the laser 
beam. While the physical process is the modulation of the 
absorption by concentration, temperature, pressure or ve- 
locity, its effect is detected by collecting the emitted fluores- 
cence. The lateral resolution is limited by the collection op- 
tics and the size of the detector.

3.1 Seeding technique 
The iodine is seeded to the gas flow by simple sublimation 
from iodine crystals (e.g. Baker 4-2208) and forms a stable 
diatomic molecule in the gas phase at the conditions of 
interest. The carrier gas should not contain water vapor for 
two reasons. Iodine gas reacts with water to form iodic acid 
(HI) which attacks metals faster than iodine itself. Secondly, 
the iodine crystals appear to form a surface coating under 
the influence of water which inhibits the sublimation process 
and leads to reduced fluorescence signals. 
The choice of the carrier gas is an optimization between 
quenching behavior (Sect. 4.3), effect on the speed of sound 
through the ratios of the specific heats (relevant for transonic 
and supersonic applications), velocity slip, toxicity, reactivi- 
ty and price. In consideration of these factors, nitrogen was 
chosen as the carrier gas in our experiments, although oxy- 
gen, dry air, argon are also suitable. It was shown experi- 
mentally by Teshima (1983) and theoretically by Hiller 
(1986) that velocity slip is not a problem in nitrogen-based 
flows up to moderate Mach numbers (< 5) where the veloc- 
ity is equilibrated within about 10 collisions. 
In our facility, the entire nitrogen flow was sent through 
the mixing chamber containing the iodine crystals. The mix- 
ing-chamber should be isolated with inlet and outlet valves, 
not only to contain the iondine safely during down time, but 
also to enable the adjustment of the mixing chamber pres- 
sure to select the iodine seeding fraction. Assuming satura- 
tion of the carrier gas, the seeding fraction can be estimated 
from the ratio of the iodine vapor pressure at reservoir tem- 
perature (0.3 Torr, Fig. 3) and the mixing chamber pressure 
indicated by a pressure gauge. For larger facilities, tempera- 
ture control to a few degrees via a heating tape may be 
necessary. This value establishes the upper limit. Absorption 
measurements proved that seeding fractions within 10% of 
the saturation value were achieved regularly.

sufficiently low to avoid significant absorption over a 1 em 
long path is given in Fig. 4. Since absorption scales linearly 
with distance in the unsaturated case, the acceptable mole 
fraction can be easily estimated for different path lengths.

For optimum signal strength and control of the seeding 
fraction via the mixing chamber pressure, saturation of the 
carrier gas with iodine is desired. This requires a large sur- 
face area of the crystals and a sufficiently long residence time 
in the mixing chamber. The best solution appears to be a 
fluidized-bed mixing chamber with a cross-section that in-

height to ensure that the crystals fall back and are not 
carried into the flow chamber (cyclone-type flow). 
Many techniques to measure flow properties rely on rela- 
tive measurements (Sect. 5) so that uniform seeding is not 
required and wall losses are not too critical if a reasonable 
signal strength can be maintained. If uniformity is a concern 
(for instance in 3-D visualization) baffle plates and stirring 
devices may have to be used to mix the flow. Diffusion helps, 
iodine diffuses in nitrogen at about 3 mm/s at STP (Table 4). 
Unfortunately, we have no information on the rate of loss of 
iodine to walls. In mixing studies it is generally desirable to 
inject the iodine-laden flow into a pure main flow (McDaniel 
and Graves 1986). 
3.2 Fluorescence detection 
As indicated in Fig. 1, the emitted fluorescence is detected at 
90 ~ with a single detector for point measurements or a detec- 
tor array for linear or planar measurements. A commercial 
lens (in our case a Nikon 50 mm f/1.4 lens) can be used to 
image the collection volume onto the front of the detector. 
The lateral extent of the collection volume is determined by 
the size of the detector and the resolution of the imaging 
arrangement. In general, geometric optics equations are fully 
adequate to determine the lateral extent of the collection 
volume. Focussing errors can degrade the spatial resolution 
significantly if low-f/number lenses are used. The depth of 
the collection volume is determined by the thickness of the 
laser beam (or sheet), usually measured at the 1/e 2 intensity 
points. 
Iodine fluorescence, when excited with an argon ion laser, 
appears yellow to the eye. A close look at the fluorescence 
spectrum (sketched in Fig. 5) reveals a regular structure. One 
can identify the sharp spikes of the primary vibrational spec- 
trum, caused by the radiative decay from the excited molec- 
ular energy level to all possible ground states, and the signif- 
icantly weaker vibrational transfer spectrum: collisions 
between molecules transfer the excited iodine molecules to 
adjacent energy levels, from which they can also decay to 
lower levels. Since most of these collisions result in quench- 
ing of the fluorescence, the vibrational transfer spectrum is 
much weaker than the original spectrum. Figure 5 shows 
that the strongest fluorescence can be expected from a decay 
to the original ground level. This kind of fluorescence is 
termed resonance fluorescence since it is in resonance with 
the optical frequency of the exciting laser radiation.

Unfortunately, elastically scattered radiation from sur- 
faces or particles interferes with this resonance fluorescence 
and produces an undesirably large and nonuniform back- 
ground noise. It is, therefore, desirable to block all elastically 
scattered radiation (including the resonance fluorescence) 
and to detect only the red-shifted fluorescent decay to higher 
energy levels than the ground state. The potential to employ 
spectral filtering is an inherent advantage of nonresonant 
laser-induced fluorescence techniques and allows the com- 
plete elimination of elastic background scattering. 
Spectral filtering is best done with an optical long-pass 
filter which only passes radiation above a certain wave- 
length. We chose a 560 nm longpass filter while using the 
514.5 nm line of our argon ion laser as the excitation source. 
If the material of the longpass filter is absorbing glass (which 
is the least expensive kind), luminescence of the glass under 
background radiation is possible but can be circumvented 
by using an additional laser notch filter. The filters should be 
placed directly on the detector and all potentially reflecting 
surfaces in the facility should be black. 
3.3 Protection of the facility 
The iodine molecule is very reactive. It forms a powdery 
corrosive surface on metals such as aluminum, copper and 
steel. After the initial coating, further corrosion is sufficiently 
slow to eliminate concerns about leaks on a time scale of 
several years. Uncoated brass fittings can be used where 
surface quality is not important. The surface has to be coated 
if smoothness is required, e.g. in the nozzle. Flat black paint 
(Ferrothane no. 31, Flecto, Inc.) has proven to be effective, 
although black-anodization of aluminum surface (i.e. coat- 
ing with an aluminum oxide layer) results in a superior 
finish. Stainless steel reacts only weakly with iodine, but 
forms a powdery surface. Glass, teflon and PVC do not react, 
but are eventually coated with a layer of condensed iodine 
which can be easily removed with ethanol. The flow system 
should contain teflon tubing and stainless steel valves. The 
sealing of the facility can be accomplished with O-rings and 
iodine resistant fluorocarbon vacuum grease (Fisher Fluro- 
lube grease GR-90).

3.4 Health effects 
Iodine is harmful when inhaled. Irritations of eyes, nose, 
mouth (sore throat) and the respiratory tract occur. The 
maximum accepted concentration is 0.1 ppm, which corre- 
sponds to 1 mg/m 3 STP (Bruin 1980). Fortunately, the de- 
tection limit of the receptors in the nose lies below this limit. 
The enclosure of all iodine vapors in the experiment is abso- 
lutely necessary. Reported lethal doses in adults range from 
a few tenths of a gram to more than 20 g. Probably, the 
mean lethal dose lies between 2 and 4 g (Gosselin et al. 
1976). The most effective treatment of chronic iodine over- 
doses (resulting e.g. in sneezing, skin rashes or bronchitis) is 
discontinuation of the exposure. After discontinuation, no 
long-term effects of chronic overdoses have been observed 
(Gosselin et al. 1976).

5 Example 
The following numerical example will illustrate how the ma- 
terial in this paper can be used to calculate the fluorescence 
intensity that can be expected from a given flow situation 
and experimental arrangement. Many of the numbers calcu- 
lated here are also listed in Table. 4. 
A typical medium-size argon ion laser has an output 
power of 4 W. At least 1 W can be expected in single axial 
mode operation of the green 514.5 nm line. With a beam 
diameter of 1 mm, measured at the 1/e 2 intensity points, the 
average intensity is 130 W/cm 2' Let us assume that the laser 
is tuned to center of the overlapping P13/R15 absorption 
lines (either by temperature or angle tuning of the intra- 
cavity etalon). Figure 6 shows that the laser power will de- 
crease to 75% (or 100 W/cm 2) of the peak value because the 
center of the laser line and the center of the absorption lines 
do not coincide. 
Let us further assume that the laser beam travels through 
a static cell with iodine gas at 125 Torr and 298 K and a path 
length of 1 cm. What fluorescence signal can we expect if a 
section of the beam path of 1 mm is imaged onto our detec- 
tor? 
With any fluorescence measurement, two essential checks 
should be made first: (1) Will the laser intensity saturate the 
absorption? In that case the absorption line will broaden 
[Eq. (21)] and the signal will no longer be linear with laser 
intensity [Eq. (2)] so that quantitative results may be difficult 
to obtain. (2) Will there be significant absorption across the 
beam path ? The fluorescence signal will then depend on the 
flow conditions along the laser beam prior to reaching the 
observation point. Quantitative fluorescence results can be 
only obtained if there is no significant absorption, i.e. the 
medium is optically thin. 
From Fig. 2, the saturation intensity at the conditions set 
above is 100 kW/cm 2, three orders of magnitude higher than 
the actual laser intensity. Hence, saturation is not a problem 
in this case, I/Is ~-0 and e = e0. However, if the beam is tightly 
focussed to achieve high spatial resolution (e.g. to a diameter 
of 30 gm), saturation is easily achieved. 
To calculate the optical thickness, the pressure of the 
mixing chamber containing the iodine crystals needs to be 
known. For safety reasons, the mixing chamber is best kept 
at 1 atmosphere to minimize gas exchange with the outside 
through possible leaks. From Fig. 4 we read an iodine mole 
fraction Za of 450 ppm, for which the absorption along the 
I cm pass is significantly less than 1%. Hence, the medium 
is optically thin. 
The next step is to calculate the power absorbed in a 
1 mm long section of the beam path (defining a collection 
volume of 0.8 mm 2) with Eq. (1). This requires the evaluation 
of the absorption cross section a and the number density of 
absorbing molecules n,. [The product of both is the absorp- 
tion coefficient e Eq. (7).] 
For the absorption cross section, we need the line 
strength value (more precisely, the Einstein coefficient of 
absorption) B12 (Sect. 4.2). This value is based on a Franck- 
Condon factor of 0.01779 (Table 2). Section 4.4 shows how 
to compute the line shape function. The value x for the Voigt 
function (Eq. (20)] is 0 since we are tuned to the center of the 
absorption line. Value y [Eq. (21)] is 1.6. The Voigt function 
is then V (x, y)= 0.306. If we consider that we are effectively 
dealing with two lines and if we include line strengths, line 
positions and fractional populations of adjacent lines, the 
values for the line shape function g at line center is 0.76/GHz 
and the absorption cross section becomes 3.5 x 10-16 cm 2. 
[The product of line shape, line strength and fractional pop- 
ulation are simply summed up, taking in consideration the 
different relative line positions - the Voigt value x is only 0 
for the two overlapping lines but needs to be evaluated with 
Eq. (20) for all adjacent lines !] 
The total number density (computed from the gas equa- 
tion n = P/k T, k being Boltzmann's constant) is in this case 
4 x 1018 cm -3. Only 450 ppm of these molecules are iodine 
molecules. And only a fraction fv of these molecules is in a 
ground energy state that can be excited to resonance with 
the single-mode argon laser. This fractional population can 
be computed with Sect. 4.1 to be 0.77%. The number density 
of absorbers is then 1.4 x 1013 cm 3. 
With these values, the absorption coefficient is about 
5 x 10- 3 cm- 1 or 0.5%/cm (supporting the estimate above 
using Fig. 4). The absorbed power in the control volume is 
then approximately 0.5 roW. To calculate the power of the 
fluorescence, we need to evaluate the fluorescence efficiency 
and the optical collection efficiency. 
The fluorescence efficiency is calculated with Eqs. (6) and 
(18) to be 0.8%. With an imaging magnification ofM = 2 and 
an f/l.4 lens, the collection efficiency t/c is 1.4%. The use of 
a long-pass filter in combination with a laser notch filter 
(recommended in Sect. 3.2) will reduce this value to approx-

How will the fluorescence signal behave as we raise the 
pressure to one atmosphere? The saturation intensity will 
increase (Fig. 2) since there are now more absorbing 
molecules in the beam path so that saturation is even less a 
problem. The optical thickness is larger now but still below 
1%, as an extrapolation of the curves in Fig. 4 would show. 
The fluorescence signal will change in the following way. 
Quenching will increase at the same rate as the number 
density increases - both linearly in this situation, so that the 
net effect is zero. The only net difference will be introduced 
by the line shape function. As the pressure increases, the line 
broadens and the influence of adjacent absorption lines be- 
comes larger. A calculation of the line shape function has to 
take this effect into account. Computer computations indi- 
cate a reduction of the line center value by approximately a 
factor of 3 as the pressure is raised from 125Torr to 
750 Torr. That is, the fluorescence signal itself will decrease 
by only a factor of 3 which indicates that iodine fluorescence 
should still be useful for flow visualization purposes at atmo- 
sphere pressures. However, the absorption lines then start to 
significantly overlap and accurate measurements of gas- 
dynamic properties become more difficult. 
In what follows we will discuss how this calculated signal 
relates to the measurement of flow properties. The shot- 
noise limit SNR was better than 200 in our example so that 
the precision, barring all other influences, should be better 
than 1%. 
Temperature is best measured by taking the ratio of line 
center measurements at two different lines. By taking ratios, 
number density, seeding fraction and quenching cancel out 
if the two signals are collected in the same volume in quick 
succession. This scheme should still leave a precision of 1% 
but other systematic errors and experimental reality should 
limit the quality to an error of about 5%. Fletcher and 
McDaniel (1987) achieved an accuracy of 2%. 
Velocity can be measured by tuning the laser over the 
entire line and by measuring the Doppler shift between flow 
and a static cell (McDaniel 1983; Hiller and Hanson 1988). 
If the tuning is done fast enough so that density and quench- 
ing are 'frozen', a relative measurement is again sufficient. A 
better, faster way is to fix the laser wavelength in the wing of 
the line and measure signal changes due to the Doppler shift. 
Several self-calibrating schemes were demonstrated by Hiller 
and Hanson (1988). Although the precision is similar to 
above, the remaining error due to the influence of the non- 
linear line shape and other non-ideal behaviors is about 5%. 
Pressure can be also measured with above fixed-wave- 
length scheme with a similar accuracy. McDaniel (1984) 
suggests a method based on the inverse line center fluores- 
cence value, but here the seeding fraction has to be constant. 
Density is best calculated from pressure and temperature 
measurement, or should be attempted to be measured with 
a saturating broadband laser. 
The photon flux from other spectroscopic methods 
(Raman, Rayleigh) is orders of magnitude lower. Laser- 
induced fluorescence is, therefore, also the best candidate for 
measurements in turbulent flows. To 'freeze' the flow, 
nanosecond-scale measurements are necessary which can be 
only accomplished with pulsed lasers. At these short illumi- 
nation times, saturation will be necessary to achieve reason- 
able signal levels. The measurement of gasdynamic proper- 
ties in turbulent flows is still very much in its infancy and 
most work to date has been done in flow visualization and 
saturated density measurements.
