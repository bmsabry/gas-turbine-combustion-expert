Flame characteristics of turbulent lean
premixed methane/air ﬂames at high pressure:
Turbulent ﬂame speed and ﬂame brush thickness
P. Griebel *, P. Siewert, P. Jansohn
Paul Scherrer Institut (PSI), Combustion Research, 5232 Villigen PSI, Switzerland
Abstract
The eﬀects of operating conditions and turbulence on ﬂame front position, turbulent ﬂame speed and
ﬂame brush thickness of lean premixed methane/air ﬂames at high pressure are investigated experimentally.
A comparison of the measured turbulent ﬂame speeds with existing correlations is presented and discussed.
The measurements were performed in an axis-symmetric, generic combustor at pressures up to
p = 1.44 MPa, equivalence ratios in the range U = 0.43–0.56, preheating temperatures T = 673–773 K
and for an inlet bulk velocity range of uBULK = 30–60 m/s. The turbulence intensity and the integral length
scale at the combustor inlet were varied by means of turbulence grids with diﬀerent geometry and by
changing the grid position in the inlet channel. The isothermal ﬂow ﬁeld was characterized by two-dimen-
sional particle image velocimetry (PIV). The characterization of the ﬂame front was based on the statistical
analysis of two-dimensional instantaneous images of the laser induced ﬂuorescence of the OH radical
(OH-PLIF). The analyses revealed that pressure has no eﬀect on the ﬂame front position, the turbulent
ﬂame speed, or on the ﬂame front ﬂuctuation (ﬂame brush thickness). Decreasing the fuel concentration
from U = 0.43 to U = 0.56 results in a ﬂame elongation by a factor of 2 and a corresponding decrease
of the turbulent ﬂame speed by a factor of 3. For the same variation of the fuel concentration the ﬂame
brush thickness increases by a factor of 2. Additionally, the ﬂame brush thickness was consistently
observed to vary proportionally to the square root of the ﬂame front position. The experimental values
of the turbulent ﬂame speed are in satisfactory agreement with the existing correlations.

1. Introduction
Currently, lean premixed combustion is the
favored method for low-emission power genera-
tion from natural gas used in stationary gas tur-
bines. Homogenous mixing of the fuel and air
combined with ultra-lean operation provide a
reduction of ﬂame temperature and are key to
its success in minimizing NOx formation. Increas-
ing environmental concerns will lead to heavier
restrictions on NOx emissions from gas turbines
prompting a need to exploit this combustion tech-
nique to its maximum potential. Further reduc-
tion of NOx will require extremely lean mixtures,
therefore a better understanding of the turbu-

interaction

mental investigation of the turbulent ﬂame speed
and thickness (also referred to as mean ﬂame

ticular, a better understanding of the eﬀects of
pressure and turbulence on sT and dT is needed.
Turbulent ﬂame speed data from current liter-
ature are quite scattered and attempts to derive a
universal empirical correlation that can predict sT
for a broad range of thermo-chemical and turbu-
lence conditions have not been successful [2,7].
For ﬂames typical for gas turbines there are, how-
ever, several correlations that have gained a rela-
tive popularity [7,8]. The agreement between
diﬀerent experimental data remains unsatisfactory
because the turbulent ﬂame speed does not only
depend on turbulence and the operating condi-
tions but also strongly depends on the determina-
tion method and, in particular, depends on the
ﬂame conﬁguration [1].
The majority of the literature data dealing with
turbulent premixed ﬂames are not representative
for gas turbine conditions. The work of Abdel-
Gayed et al. [9] comprises a large data set
measured in a fan-stirred bomb but only for atmo-
spheric conditions. Turbulent ﬂame speeds at
atmospheric pressure were also measured by Ples-
sing et al. [10] in a low-swirl burner. Recent mea-
surements of Bradley et al. [11] were performed in
a fan-stirred bomb up to 0.5 MPa and diﬀerent
gaseous fuels but for methane only at stoichiome-
tric mixtures. The inﬂuence of pressure on the
ﬂame front curvature was studied by Soika et al.
[12] for pressures up to 1.1 MPa and equivalence
ratios between U = 0.7–1.1. Because of the limited
range of equivalence ratios these results are also
not completely representative for gas turbine con-
ditions. Recent work of Kobayashi et al. [13] com-
prises measurements of turbulent ﬂame speeds in
a Bunsen-type burner for pressures up to 1 MPa.
These ﬂames belong to the corrugated ﬂamelets
regime rather than to the thin reaction zone
regime typical for gas turbines as classiﬁed in the
Borghi/Peters diagram [14,15].

bulent premixed ﬂames at gas turbine relevant
conditions motivated the current research which
aims at the characterization of the turbulent lean
premixed methane/air ﬂames in the thin reaction
zones regime (or thickened ﬂames regime). These
ﬂames are characterized by high turbulent Rey-
nolds numbers (ReT > 103), Damko¨hler numbers
lower than 10, and Karlovitz numbers higher than

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Most probable ﬂame front position
In Fig. 3 the dimensionless ﬂame front posi-
tions (xMP/d) are shown for a pressure range of
0.5–1.44 MPa at a preheating temperature of
673 K, an inlet velocity of 40 m/s and for the grid
g350 · g10. The ﬂame positions for pressures
below 0.5 MPa are not presented because of the
inﬂuence of the high combustor heat losses rela-
tive to the thermal power at these conditions.
Over the entire pressure range the ﬂame front
position has a roughly constant value xMP/
d  7.4 (±7%) indicating that the turbulent ﬂame
speed sT is independent of pressure. This raises the
question why sT is independent of pressure
whereas it is well known that the laminar ﬂame
speed of methane/air ﬂames decreases with pres-
sure approximately as sL  p1/2 [17,20]. The
explanation is that the decrease of sL with pressure
is compensated by an increase of the instanta-
neous ﬂame front surface area. This increase is
attributed to a higher ﬂame front wrinkling
induced by smaller turbulent eddies [12,21] with
increasing pressure. It is known that the spectrum
of turbulence broadens towards smaller scales as
the pressure rises because the ratio of the integral
length scale to the Kolmogorov scale g depends
on pressure as LT=g  Re3=4
T
 p3=4. This broad-
ening of the turbulence spectrum induces higher
ﬂame front wrinkling and in this way leads to an
increase of the instantaneous ﬂame front surface.
Theoretically,
a
weak
pressure
dependence
(p1/4) of sT would be the result, however, this
was not observed in the experiments.
Further analysis of Fig. 3 reveals that the ﬂame
front position (xMP/d) strongly depends on the
fuel concentration. Decreasing the fuel concentra-
tion results in an increase of xMP/d by a factor of 2
from 5.5 at U = 0.56 to 11.2 at U = 0.43. This is
caused by a decrease of the adiabatic ﬂame tem-
perature from 1860 to 1650 K leading to a drop
of the global reaction rate and consequently to a
lower sT i.e. longer ﬂames.

The eﬀect of turbulence on the ﬂame front
position is shown in Fig. 5, where xMP/d is plotted

ﬂame speed increases with ReT. This increase of
ReT (u0, LT,xx) may lead to an intensiﬁed turbulent
mass and heat transport of the hot ﬂue gas from
the recirculation zone into the fresh gas mixture.

3.2. Flame brush thickness
The development of the ﬂame brush thickness
dT/d along the axial distance from the combustor
inlet x/d is presented in Fig. 6 for 673 K, 1 MPa,
U = 0.43, 40 m/s and the grid g365 · g10. The cal-
culation method of dT is illustrated in Fig. 2 in
which the black dashed lines mark the radial
spreading of the ﬂame brush. These lines corre-
spond to the iso-contours of 10% of the maximum
counts level of the entire image.
The mean ﬂame brush thickness grows with the
axial distance from dT/d  0.2 at the inlet to dT/
d  0.8 at x/d = 6. This growth is approximately
proportional to the square-root of x (see the black
dotted line in Fig. 6). The explanation is based on
the conclusion that the mean ﬂame brush thick-
ness grows according to the turbulent diﬀusion
law [2]. This law states that the spatial distribution
of the turbulent ﬂuctuations (e.g. u0) and the ﬂame
front ﬂuctuation must be the same. Based on the
classical Taylor theory of turbulent diﬀusion
(e.g. [22]) it can be shown that dT  x1/2. Such a
close relation between spatial distribution of u0
and dT has already been reported in previous liter-
ature [2,19,23,24].
In order to present the eﬀects of operating con-
ditions and turbulence on dT only the ﬂame brush
thickness dT(xMP) at the centerline is considered.
In Fig. 7 the dimensionless ﬂame brush thickness
at the centerline dT(xMP)/d is plotted as a function
of the most probable ﬂame front position xMP/d.
All measurements were performed at 0.5 MPa.
The points along the black solid line represent
results for the grid g365 · g10. It can be seen that
the decrease of the equivalence ratio (U = 0.56–
0.43 at 673 K and 40 m/s) results in a simulta-
neous increase of the ﬂame front position (xMP/d
by a factor of 2.7) and the ﬂame brush thickness
(dT(xMP)/d by a factor of 2.4). This increase of
dT(xMP)/d is mainly caused by the decrease of
the stability (more susceptible to ﬂow distur-
bances) of ﬂames close to the lean blow-out limit
[25]. Given the fact that the turbulence is not
homogenous [26,27], the change of the ﬂame
brush thickness may be also caused by diﬀerent
local turbulence intensities at diﬀerent ﬂame front
positions. In the present study, these eﬀects can-
not be quantiﬁed because the ﬂow ﬁeld was mea-
sured at non-reacting conditions. However, there
seems to be a close relation between the ﬂame

3.3. Turbulent ﬂame speed
One of the major problems in calculation of
the turbulent ﬂame speed sT is the deﬁnition of
the ﬂame front surface area A used in Eq. (2). In
this paper, an approach based on the progress
variable, illustrated in Fig. 8 with quantities sum-
marized in Table 2, is used to calculate the ﬂame
front surface area. The mean progress variable
Æcæ = 0 (black) refers to the fresh gas mixture
and Æcæ = 1 refers to completely burned gases
(white). The black solid line corresponds to an
iso-contour of Æcæ = 0.5 which coincides with the
most probable ﬂame front contour (MP) repre-
sented by the white dashed line. The iso-contour
of Æcæ =0.05, indicated by the solid white line,
marks the leading edge of the ﬂame front. The vir-
tual rotation of this contour line (Æcæ = 0.05)
around the x-axis leads to a three-dimensional
surface deﬁned as the ﬂame front surface area A.
The turbulent ﬂame speeds calculated according
to Eq. (2) and A based on the progress variable
approach are larger than the ones based on the
most probable ﬂame front contour (MP) [16] by
a factor of 1.5–2.0. The iso-surface Æcæ = 0.05
represents the ﬂame front leading edge where the
temperature and density of the fresh gas mixture
are regarded as unaﬀected by combustion. In this
way the turbulent ﬂame speed sT and the laminar
ﬂame speed sL consistently refer to the same con-
ditions (unburned mixture). Thus, the calculation
based on the ﬂame front surface at the leading
edge is a more appropriate way of deﬁning sT/sL
when comparing to literature and correlation
data.
The experimental turbulent ﬂame speed values,
derived from the progress variable approach, are
compared to correlation data (Zimont [8], Peters
[7]) in Table 2. The sT calculation was based on
the turbulence parameters (u0, LT,xx) at the com-
bustor inlet as commonly done in literature [13].

calculated at Æcæ = 0.05 show a reasonable agree-
ment with the data of both correlations (deviation

4. Conclusions
Turbulent ﬂame speeds of lean premixed meth-
ane/air ﬂames at high pressure have been mea-
sured and the eﬀects of operating conditions and
turbulence on ﬂame front position and ﬂame
brush thickness have been investigated experimen-
tally. A comparison of the measured turbulent
ﬂame speeds with existing correlations is given.
The major results are the following:
1. No inﬂuence of pressure on the most probable
ﬂame front position, on the ﬂame brush thick-
ness, or on the turbulent ﬂame speed was found
in the pressure range of 0.5–1.44 MPa.
2. An increase of the turbulence intensity and the
integral length scale at the combustor inlet
leads to shorter ﬂames i.e. to higher sT. The
maximum variation of the turbulent ﬂame
speed due to grid variation was 40%.
3. A close relation between the ﬂame front posi-
tion xMP and the ﬂame brush thickness dT(xMP)
was observed (dTðxMPÞ  x1=2Þ
MP Þ.
4. The comparison of the experimental turbulent
ﬂame speed data with the correlation of Zimont
[8] shows satisfactory agreement.

Wolfgang Meier, German Aerospace CenterDLR, Ger-
many. From the OH PLIF image you showed, I expect
that there is some local ﬂame extinction. In that case,
you get a bit of mixing of exhaust gas with fresh gas. Have
you estimated the inﬂuence of this eﬀect on your results?
Reply. We have observed some local ﬂame extinc-
tion in our OH PLIF single shot images. However,
for the statistical analysis this is not of a high rele-
vance because local ﬂame extinction occurred rather
seldom. In principle, local ﬂame extinction may lead
to several counteracting eﬀects. On one hand, ﬂame
front extinctions may lead to a decrease of the fuel
consumption (i.e., to lower sT). On the other hand,
locally quenched ﬂame fronts may be responsible for
the creation of unburned gas pockets. These pockets,
transported to the fresh gas mixture, may lead to an
increase of the preheating temperature and/or to an
increase of the radical concentration. As a conse-
quence, this would increase the global reaction rate,
i.e., sT. To investigate and to quantify these eﬀects it
would be necessary to measure, for example, the local
gas temperature which was not possible in the present
study.
