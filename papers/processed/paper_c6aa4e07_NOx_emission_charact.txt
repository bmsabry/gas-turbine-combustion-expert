NOx emission characteristics of conﬁned jet
nonpremixed ﬂames
Susumu Noda *, Junya Inohae, Zaki S. Saldi
Department of Mechanical Engineering, Toyohashi University of Technology, Toyohashi 441-8580, Japan
Abstract
The ﬂame characteristics of conﬁned ﬂames are investigated for propane and hydrogen jet nonpremixed
ﬂames in cylindrical furnaces. The ﬂame characteristics can be strongly dominated by the combination of
the burner and furnace geometries. In the present study, the eﬀects of the inner diameter of the cylindrical
furnace D1, the turbulence at the ﬂame boundary, and the global equivalence ratio / are examined in terms
of the emission of NOx. The emission index of NOx, EINOx, decreases roughly with these parameters. The
decrease in EINOx is thought to be related to the dilution of mixtures by the burned gas and the ﬂame
stretch. The dilution is attributable to vortices formed at the bottom of the furnace, and the ﬂame stretch
is attributable to the air velocity diﬀerence DUa created by two air nozzles. The EINOx characteristics are
evaluated by the furnace Reynolds number Re, c based on the inner diameter of the furnace reﬂecting the
dilution and the strain rate S  Da1 based on the air velocity diﬀerence, where Da indicates the Damko¨h-
ler number. The EINOx is well characterized by the factor D1UFDU, which is proportional to Re, cDa1.
This result indicates that the conﬁned nonpremixed ﬂame is characterized by the furnace volume, not the
ﬂame volume, which is related to the reaction rate of the jet nonpremixed ﬂame with coﬂowing air based on
the ﬂamelet model. This may imply that the furnace combustion should be modiﬁed from the ﬂamelet con-
cept owing to the dilution.

based on both the mean velocities of the higher-
velocity and lower-velocity air ﬂows and reﬂects
the turbulence intensity at the ﬂame boundary.
The Reynolds number Re in Table 1 is based on
the fuel mean velocity and the inner diameter of
the fuel nozzle.
For the conﬁned combustion, the dilution of
fuel and air by the burned gas becomes important
as mentioned above. This eﬀect should be consid-
ered in the formulation of the furnace combus-
tion. We tried to characterize the NOx emission
using various parameters, including the parame-
ters explained in Section 1, and two parameters
were ﬁnally determined to be appropriate: the
Reynolds number Re, c = D1UF/m based on the
furnace inner diameter and the Damko¨hler num-
ber Da based on the air velocity diﬀerence, which
is inversely proportional to the stretch rate
S = DUa/DL, where DL is the distance between
the two air nozzles. The Reynolds number Re, c
denotes the turbulence in the furnace and the
strength of circulation of the vortex formed at
the bottom of the furnace. Consequently, Re, c
reﬂects the level of dilution by the burned gas.
The stretch rate S indicates the ﬂame stretch and
the mixing strength through the enhanced turbu-
lence mixing.

4. Results and discussion
4.1. Propane ﬂame
Figure 2 shows photographs of propane ﬂames
at an air velocity diﬀerence DUa = 10 m/s. The
increase in D1 leads to a contraction of the lumi-
nous ﬂame region and an expansion of the blue
ﬂame region. This tendency is enhanced with /,
and with DUa (not shown herein). In particular,
in the case of / = 0.8 and D1 = 182 mm, the lumi-
nous ﬂame region vanishes at DUa = 12 m/s and
14 m/s (not shown herein). The ﬂame size is
enlarged with / because of the increase in the fuel
ﬂow rate at the ﬁxed total airﬂow rate. In the case
of D1 = 95 mm, the ﬂame touches the wall with an
increase in luminosity. On the other hand, in the
case of D1 = 182 mm, the increase in / decreases
the luminosity, but increases the ﬂame size. The
increase in DUa (not shown herein) showed a
decrease in the ﬂame length, being attributable
to the contraction of the luminous region.

EINOx characteristics depend strongly on the
dilution through the recirculation of the burned
gas in the furnace. In addition, the losses with
respect to the heat radiation and the heat conduc-
tion must be considered. The increase in the lumi-
nosity that is mainly attributable to soot produced
in the ﬂame enhances the radiation heat loss. The
ﬂame interaction with the present pyrex wall also
enhances the heat loss owing to the conduction.
Both of these eﬀects lead to a decrease in ﬂame
temperature and thus a decrease in the EINOx.
In the case of D1 = 95 mm, the radiation and con-
duction eﬀects may be conﬁrmed by a slight
decrease in the EINOx with /, but are not large.
In the case of D1 = 182 mm, however, the EINOx
decreases with the decrease in luminosity. This
also reveals that the present NOx characteristics
are dominated primarily by dilution, rather than
heat losses. However, these eﬀects must appear
diﬀerently in the conﬁguration of the adiabatic
wall. This remains a subject for future investiga-
tion. On the other hand, the eﬀect of DUa is slight-
ly complicated. In the cases of D1 = 95 mm and
D1 = 182 mm for / = 0.4, the EINOx increases
with DUa in the lower range of / and decreases
in the higher range of /. This is explained by
the two factors of the ﬂame stretch and the mix-
ing. Lower ﬂame stretch enhances the reaction,
and higher ﬂame stretch diminishes the reaction.
The increase in the stretch eventually leads to
the extinction of the ﬂame [9]. Therefore, the acti-
vation of reaction by the lower stretch leads an
increase in the EINOx. The higher ﬂame stretch
causes ﬂame cooling and dilution by the burned
gas, because of the enhanced turbulent mixing.
As mentioned above, the NOx emission is
strongly related to the ﬂame appearance and the
dilution. This fact may imply that the furnace
combustion is modeled by some dominant param-
eters. However, we failed to characterize the pres-
ent
furnace
combustion
according
to
the
conventional parameters. Thus, we introduced
two
new
parameters;
the
furnace
Reynolds
number Re, c = D1UF/m and the air strain rate
S = DUa/DL (Da1). Figures 5 and 6 show the
EINOx with respect to Re, c and S, respectively.
Generally, the EINOx tends to decrease with both
of the new parameters, with a slight increase in the
lower region of both of the parameters where the
turbulence is relatively weak. The present Rey-
nolds number reﬂects the turbulence intensity in
the furnace and/or the intensity of the vortex
formed at the bottom of the furnace, because
D1UF in Re, c should be proportional to the circu-
lation of the vortex. Consequently, the Reynolds
number indicates primarily the dilution by the
burned gas and, to a lesser degree, the ﬂame
stretch. The strain rate S reﬂects mainly the ﬂame
stretch, and the dilution is reﬂected to a lesser
degree through the enhancement of the turbulent
mixing. The increases in EINOx in the range of
both lower Re, c and lower S are caused by the
temperature increase due to the activation of reac-
tion by the ﬂame stretch, as mentioned above. On
the other hand, in the range of both higher Re, c
and higher S, both the parameters aﬀect the
EINOx with some certain tendencies to decrease.

4.2. Hydrogen ﬂame
The above results for propane ﬂames may
depend on the fuel species. The chemistry of the
hydrogen ﬂame is believed to be faster than the
propane ﬂame because the burning velocity of
the hydrogen/air mixture is approximately seven
times faster than of the propane/air mixture.
Therefore, the characteristics of the hydrogen
ﬂame are discussed in the following.
Figure 9 shows photographs of hydrogen
ﬂames. The ﬂames are highly turbulent and
obscure at the ﬂame boundary, diﬀering from pro-
pane ﬂames. This is due to high exit velocities and
a high burning velocity. The ﬂame volume is
depressed compared to that of the propane ﬂames,
but increases with /, in the same manner as pro-
pane ﬂames. Not every ﬂame touches the wall.
This should facilitate the formation of vortices
in the furnace. The luminosity increases with /
in the case of D1 = 95 mm, but decreases in the
case of D1 = 182 mm. The increase in DUa short-
ens the ﬂame length and diminishes the ﬂame col-
or, in the same manner as propane ﬂames (not
shown herein). The eﬀect of each parameter on
the ﬂame appearance is very similar to the eﬀects
on the propane ﬂame. Figure 10 shows the EINOx
with respect to D1, DUa, and /. The EINOx
decreases with DUa monotonically, in contrast to
the propane ﬂame. This may be caused by the
dilution enhanced by the vortex, which is easily
generated and scaled up because of the smaller
ﬂame size compared to that of the propane ﬂame.
The increase in D1 also decreases the EINOx. In
addition, the increase in / tends to decrease the
EINOx to a greater degree in the range of smaller
DUa. The eﬀect of / is however diminished in the
case of D1 = 95 mm, and disappears between / =
0.4 and 0.6. This change is not explained herein.

and hydrogen jet nonpremixed ﬂames were inves-

diameter of the cylindrical furnace D1, the turbu-

of NOx. The emission index of NOx, EINOx,
decreases roughly with these parameters, but the

in the case of the smaller-inner-diameter furnace.

and the ﬂame stretch. The dilution is attributable

and the ﬂame stretch is attributable to the air
