Abstract 
The lean flammability limit of an aid 
propane premixed flame was studied 
experimentally, using Schlieren photogra- 
phy synchronized with OH-imaging done 
with the Planar Laser Induced Fluorescence 
(PLIF) technique. 
The flame was studied in 
a large range of equivalence ratios. 
Various 
steps in the process of the flame destahiliza- 
tion were investigated, including partial 
lift-off, stable lift-off, and final blow-out 
conditions. 
The flame structure was visual- 
ized for each stage showing the transition 
from a flame held at the nozzle to a flame 
held by the flow structures. 
In order to 
study the later condition in more detail the 
flame was forced at the preferred mode fre- 
quency generating large, stable, coherent 
The changes 
in the mean and turbulent flow fields as a 
result of this forcing were studied, and the 
modified flame structure was visualized to 
understand the interaction between the 
flame and vortical flow dynamics.

Design trends of modem airbreathing 
propulsion systems, such as gas-turbines or 
ramjets, include optimized airblast atomiza- 
tion systems with enhanced mixing, 
requirements for low idle power for reduced 
emission, design of a "stoichiometric com- 
bustor" for high overall fuel to air ratio 
with improved fuel consumption, reduced 
smoke limits for pollution control, and a 
general quest for expanded operating 
envelope. 
All these treads push the engine 
to operate in marginal lean stability limits. 
The problem of combustion of extremely 
lean mixtures and related problems of 
flammability of diffusion and premixed 
flames were discussed extensively in the lit- 
erature.1-5 
There were also studies of 
- 
methods to increase flame stability, such as 
using swirl,6 for example. 
Various theories have been proposed to 
explain flame extinction, such as effects of 
convection, chemical kinetics, flame 
stretch, diffusion of reactants, and instabil- 
ities. 
All these theories are somehow related 
to thermal balance in the flame between the 
heat release rate and the heat loss rate. 
The 
burning velocity, flame thickness, and flow 
dynamics seem to be the most fundamental 
parameters governing the complicated 
processes in the flame. 
At the Naval Weapons Center (NWC) we 
have concentrated on the interaction 
between flow dynamics and the flame 
structure. 
Using the Planar Laser Induced 
Fluorescence (PLIF) technique we studied 
diffusion and premixed flames.7-11 
When 
the flame was forced at the preferred mode 
frequency of the jet.12 or its harmonics, the 
reaction occurred inside vortical coherent 
structures.9 
vortices, the so-called "braids" , the reaction 
was mostly quenched. 
Similar observations 
were reported by Lewis et a1 (1987)13 and 
simulated numerically by Givi et a1 (1986).14 
This quenching was attributed to the high 
strain rates in the flow at these locations, 
associated with high velocity gradients. 
The 
flame temperature drops locally and the 
reaction rate reduces to zero. 
When local 
quenching occurs in the braid region 
between the burner and the first vortical 
structure it may lead to flame lift-off.

The stability characteristics of the flame 
could be related to the air/propane shear 
layer structures, which are governed by the 
nonreacting premixed jet instability. 
The 
frequency at which the jet is most respon- 
sive to forcing is the preferred mode fre- 
quency.12 
When normalized by the jet exit 
diameter (D) and velocity (U,) it yields a 
Strouhal number, St, in the range of 0.2 to 
0.5. 
For orifice jets the Strouhal number is 
even higher due to the flow acceleration 
downstream from the nozzle. 
The preferred 
mode frequency of the present jet was mea- 
sured on the jet centerline at x/D = 2 for a 
wide range of Reynolds numbers 
(Re = UoD/u, where u is the air kinematic 
viscosity). 
The preferred mode frequency 
was determined from the broad band peak in 
the velocity power spectrum as measured by 
a hot-wire anemometer. 
The results, given 
in Figure 7 showed that the preferred mode 
Strouhal number for this orifice jet varied 
between 0.47 to 0.64.

This behavior can be attributed 
to the fact that the flame studied here has an

stoichiometric ratio. 
The right mixture ratio 
is obtained at the jet edges after mixing with 
the entrained ambient air. 
Since the 
entrained air is engulfed by the vortices, 
the stoichiometric ratio can also be reached 
inside the vortices allowing the flame to 
propagate into the vortices. 
Local quench-

the vortices, as a result of the local high 
strain rates, as observed in Figure 12b and 
discussed in Gutmark et a1 (1987).9

Forcing the premixed flame at the pre- 
ferred mode frequency generates large- 
scale structures in the flame, starting close 
to the flameholder surface. 
The roll-up of 
vortices generates, as discussed above, 
regions of flow reversals and stagnation 
points, where the flame can penetrate the 
flow and be held. 
But also, regions between 
vortices and outside the large structures, 
have high strain-rates that can lead to local 
quenching of the flame. 
This combination 
causes unsteadiness in the flame 
characteristics, when the flame is seen to be 
held alternatingly at different locations 
between the vortical structures. 
The effect of these structures on stabi- 
lization and destabilization of the flame at
