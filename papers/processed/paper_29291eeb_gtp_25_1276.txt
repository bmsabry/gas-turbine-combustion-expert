Structure of High-Pressure 
Premixed Ammonia, Hydrogen, 
and Methane Flames With 
100 kHz OH-PLIF Measurements
The impact of fuel composition on flame dynamics is investigated at 10 bar using 100 kHz 
hydroxyl planar laser induced fluorescence (OH-PLIF) measurements. The PLIF images are 
synchronized with high-frequency pressure measurements at distinct locations in the 
chamber to investigate mechanisms for thermo-acoustic feedback with the flame heat release 
and resonant acoustic modes of the combustor. Experiments were conducted over a range of 
fuel compositions spanning a hydrogen fuel fraction (v) of 30% to 90%, and an ammonia 
decomposition efficiency (g) from 40% to 100%. In predominantly hydrogen-rich fuel 
mixtures (v, g > 0.8), flame stabilization occurs along the shear layer, and flame holding is 
observed closer to the burner face. As methane composition increases (v < 0.8), significant 
flame corrugation and commensurate high heat release rate are observed as chemical 
kinetic rates and thermodiffusive effects decrease. In these cases, OH radical production and 
transport between neighboring injector elements are observed. The flame heat release and 
transport of combustion products in this region occur during the compression phase of the 
acoustic cycle and are responsible for ignition of fresh reactants. These periodic ignition, 
heat release, and flame displacement at the resonant acoustic mode frequency of the 
combustor result in self-excited longitudinal combustion instabilities (p0
RMS=pCC  1–7%). 
With significant concentrations of methane and ammonia in the fuel blend (v < 0.9, g < 0.6), 
the amplitude of this instability increases (p0
RMS=pCC > 7%), and periodic global extinction 
and re-ignition of flames are observed with acoustic expansion and compression.

1 Introduction
Concerns of increasing global greenhouse gas emissions have 
sparked rapid development efforts in carbon-free combustion 
technology [1]. In stationary power generation, fuels such as 
hydrogen (H2) and ammonia (NH3) have potential as carbon-free 
alternatives to natural gas but present unique challenges that inhibit 
widespread utilization. Hydrogen has established methods for 
renewable production and possesses the highest gravimetric energy 
density of any fuel. However, its low volumetric energy density 
makes it prohibitive for efficient storage, transport, and distribution 
at an industrial scale. The availability and cost of hydrogen derived 
from carbon-free sources also remain challenging, especially at the 
scale necessary to replace natural gas. Hydrogen also presents 
several operability concerns as a drop-in alternative to natural gas. 
The flame speed of hydrogen is an order of magnitude greater than 
that of methane, the primary constituent of natural gas. The large 
increase in kinetic timescale of hydrogen flames leads to an 
increased risk of flashback [2]. Ammonia, on the other hand, 
contains a larger density of hydrogen atoms than liquid hydrogen. 
Pre-existing facilities for the production, storage, and transport of 
ammonia can also reduce the economic complexity of the fuel’s 
large-scale implementation [3]. However, the slow kinetic rates of

NOx emissions [5]. Blending of ammonia with hydrogen can 
mitigate undesirable properties of the individual fuels [6]. Never­

Lean-premixed combustion is common for low NOx natural gas- 
fired power generation gas turbine combustors. Successful retrofit­
ting of these combustors to operate with hydrogen and ammonia fuel 
blends requires understanding of the physical processes that govern 
the structure and propagation of the flame. Premixed hydrogen 
combustion has distinctly different underlying physics governing 
flame propagation compared to methane [7–10]. One such differ­
ence is the response to flame stretch, a phenomenon often described 
by the Markstein length. The Markstein length is a characteristic 
length scale that quantifies the sensitivity of local flame speed to 
flame curvature [11]. The Markstein number is a nondimensional 
parameter defined as the ratio of Markstein length to flame thickness. 
At lean conditions, Al-Khafaji et al. [12] reported a decrease in 
Markstein number and subsequent increase in stretch sensitivity 
with hydrogen addition to methane flames. For ammonia–hydrogen 
fuel blends, Ichikawa et al. [9] noted a nonmonotonic trend of 
Markstein length with variation in hydrogen mole fraction. The 
effect of hydrogen addition in lean ammonia flames is less 
understood, especially at engine relevant conditions. At elevated 
pressure, the operability space of these fuel mixtures has been 
characterized for several injector designs [4,13]. However, these 
studies are also scarce, and further experimental efforts at high 
pressure are necessary to resolve the fundamental processes 
affecting the structure and dynamics of these flames.
Despite the lack of empirical data, first principles analysis can still 
provide insight into the effect of hydrogen addition on the 
propagation of ammonia flames. Incommensurate rates of thermal 
and mass diffusion (nonunity Lewis number) have a prominent 
effect on flame stretch sensitivity [14]. When the Lewis number is 
less than one, typical of hydrogen combustion, mass diffusion 
occurs at a faster rate than thermal diffusion [15], leading to a 
thermodiffusive mechanism for intrinsic flame instabilities to 
develop [16]. In contrast, Lewis numbers closer to unity are typical 
for methane combustion, and preferential thermodiffusive insta­
bilities are less likely to occur. In premixed turbulent flames, this 
effect is believed to greatly increase the flame speed as turbulent 
intensity enhances wrinkling and leads to the development of 
smaller-scale structures in the flame [10,17,18]. A recent numerical 
study by Gaucherand et al. [8] investigated this effect on H2/NH3 
flames and determined that ammonia addition led to reduced stretch 
sensitivity for the examined operating conditions.
Lean-premixed combustors are notably susceptible to combus­
tion instabilities due to an increased sensitivity of flame thickness, 
flame speed, and reaction rates to variations in equivalence ratio near 
the lean blowout limit [19–23]. Thermo-acoustic instabilities result 
from in-phase coupling of heat release and acoustic pressure 
fluctuations. Several intermediary coupling mechanisms exist to 
close the feedback loop between pressure oscillations and heat 
release. In technically premixed combustors, differences in acoustic 
impedance between the locations of fuel and air introduction can 
cause pressure fluctuations to perturb local equivalence ratio and 
thus heat release [24]. Velocity fluctuations, be it from acoustic 
disturbances or turbulent eddies, lead to fluctuations in flame area and 
ensuing heat release. These flame area distortions can result in 
different timescales of heat release depending on the fuel composi­
tion. Stretch sensitive flames can see rapid annihilation of flame 
curvature through kinematic restoration [25]. Over an acoustic period, 
this acts to broaden the flame heat release in the frequency domain, 
which can reduce the coherent response to acoustic fluctuations [26].
Characterization of the underlying physical mechanisms that 
result in significant changes to flame structure with small changes in 
fuel composition requires spatiotemporally resolved measurements 
of heat release. Laser induced fluorescence is a nonintrusive 
diagnostic capable of detecting key combustion radicals linked to 
heat release in the flame. Planar laser induced fluorescence of the 
hydroxyl radical (OH-PLIF) is one such application that has been 
previously employed in hydrogen flames [27–30]. Lantz et al. [27] 
performed the diagnostic at 10 Hz in two industrial gas turbine 
combustors and observed a reduction in flame front perturbations 
with hydrogen addition to methane. In radially stratified NH3=H2
flames, Jin and Kim [29] used OH-PLIF in conjunction with 
OH=NH
2 chemiluminescence to identify disparate flame structures 
and timescales. They noted that the interaction between the 
kinetically slow ammonia flame zones and much faster hydrogen 
flames prevented the development of coherent flame undulations 
that can couple with acoustic perturbations. Despite the advantages 
of OH-PLIF, repetition rate remains a key practical limitation. Care 
is also necessary in the interpretation of planar measurements in a 
turbulent three-dimensional flow-field. Investigation of the structure 
and dynamics of these flames in response to acoustic disturbance 
requires deployment of the technique at rates capable of resolving 
the timescales of interest.
The effect of fuel composition on the structure and propagation of 
self-excited high-pressure ammonia/hydrogen/methane lean- 
premixed flames is studied in the current work. The directional 
sensitivity of combustion dynamics to fuel composition has been 
characterized in previous work utilizing this combustor [13]. An 
increase in pressure fluctuation amplitude (p0
RMS) with natural gas 
addition was observed. The frequency corresponded to a longitu­
dinal full-wave (2L) mode of the combustion chamber. In the 
absence of natural gas, the instabilities were lower in amplitude and 
largely insensitive to ammonia addition. Ammonia addition with the 
inclusion of natural gas however resulted in increased p0
RMS. A mode 
transition from 1100 Hz to 550 Hz was also observed for sufficient 
ammonia addition to these fuel mixtures. This frequency corre­
sponds to the fundamental half-wave (1L) longitudinal mode of the 
combustion chamber. Changes in turbulent flame structure and its 
response to acoustic disturbances are characterized with 100 kHz 
OH-PLIF measurements. The novel application of this diagnostic at 
this repetition rate, at elevated pressure, and with NH3=H2=CH4 fuel 
blends facilitates analysis over a broader range of timescales 
relevant to unsteady heat release. Oscillations at frequencies 
associated with the longitudinal modes of the combustor are fully 
resolved. Additionally, further resolution of element to element 
interaction and small scale flame corrugation reveals how small 
scale structures respond to global combustor dynamics.
2 Experiment Description
2.1 Combustor. A multi-element premixed combustor operat­
ing at a mean chamber pressure (pCC) of 10.8 bar is used in the 
study. Figure 1 depicts a schematic of the experiment. The 
combustor uses heated unvitiated air with a fuel blend of ammonia, 
methane, hydrogen, and nitrogen. A plenum supplies uniform air to 
each micromixer element through a choked orifice to isolate the air 
supply from pressure fluctuations in the combustion chamber. The 
micromixer is comprised of three segments, one each for ammonia, 
natural gas, and hydrogen injection. Combustion chamber and 
micromixer lengths are designed to promote self-excited longitu­
dinal combustion instabilities in the combustor [20]. The 19 
micromixer elements are distributed in a uniform hexagonal array. 
The separation between neighboring elements is twice the 6.22 mm 
element diameter. A fixed area sonic nozzle located at the end of the 
combustion chamber pressurizes the combustor. Critical-flow sonic 
venturi nozzles are close-coupled to the experiment and are used to 
meter the flowrate of all reactants [31]. Hydrogen and methane are 
supplied to the experiment from bulk storage at 400 bar. Liquid 
anhydrous ammonia is pressurized to target conditions in a piston 
accumulator assembly and vaporized for gaseous supply through a 
54 kW electrical heater. Typical test durations are 1 s, during which 
all flow conditions and geometric parameters are maintained within

3.1 Effect of Methane Addition. Within the flamelet regime, 
hydrogen enrichment of methane–air flames enhances turbulent 
burning velocity for consistent turbulence intensity [18,37]. For 
flames with an effective Lewis number less than unity, the flame 
front is governed by molecular diffusion, and perturbations lead to 
increased wrinkling through thermodiffusive instabilities. The 
increased flame surface area leads to increased turbulent burning 
velocity. To quantify this effect, a volume weighted effective Lewis 
number (Leeff) is computed for all g ¼ 1.0 fuel blends between 
v ¼ 0.3 and v ¼ 1.0 in 0.1 intervals [7]. Results are depicted in Fig. 6. 
The derivative of Leeff is observed to increase as v decreases, leading 
to larger changes in turbulent burning velocity and flame stretch 
sensitivity as hydrogen is substituted for natural gas.

length-scales and timescales of the cascading turbulent structures

natural acoustic modes of the combustor.
A similar set of simultaneous OH-PLIF and p0 measurements are 
shown for the M-2 case in Fig. 8. In contrast to Fig. 7(b), p0
fluctuations as large as 10% of the mean chamber pressure are 
observed. The largest contribution to this amplitude occurs at a 
frequency consistent with the 2 L acoustic mode of the combustion 
chamber, which corresponds to a full-wave mode shape. Assuming 
small acoustic disturbances, low mean flow Mach number, and 
uniform gas composition, a positive pressure antinode at one end of a 
closed duct will result in the same antinode at the other end for this 
mode shape. Thus, p0 measurements at C2 will be closely in-phase 
with pressure fluctuations in the flame zone. Selection of 
instantaneous images represented in Fig. 8(a) was determined to 
highlight different phases of the acoustic cycle. Snapshots are 
distributed across a single acoustic period with uniform 100 ls 
spacing for all frames except the first. Strong fluctuations in OH 
intensity are observed across the acoustic period. Large changes to 
the length and diameter of the premixed jets are accompanied with 
significant distortion of the flame front. The first frame occurs at a 
local minimum in p0 and depicts reactant jets that are elongated from 
the expansion of the acoustic wave (i). This is followed by rapid 
burning and a reduction in jet length during the compression phase 
of the cycle on frames 2 and 3 (ii). The recovery of reactant jets is 
observed from frame 4 to frame 7 as the expansion phase of the next 
cycle begins (iii).
Throughout the cycle, regions of high OH intensity are observed 
in the regions between reactant jets (iv). The interactions between 
jets and resulting recirculation zones are able to sustain combustion 
for this fuel mixture, which provides a source of ignition for 
incoming reactants. This observation is distinct from the depicted 
images of the M-1 case. The substitution from hydrogen to methane 
alters several aspects of flame propagation that coincide with the 
highlighted differences. The reduced effective Lewis number 
indicates a reduction in thermodiffusive effects that contribute to 
local flame speed [12]. The subsequent effect of flame curvature 
annihilation must also be considered. As flame propagation occurs 
normal to the surface, the flame front is smoothed through kinematic 
restoration [39]. The timescale of this restoration and subsequent 
heat release is sensitive to variations in local flame speed caused by a 
decrease in hydrogen. By reducing the effects of preferential 
thermodiffusive instabilities and flame curvature reduction through 
kinematic restoration, the frequency response of the flame is 
narrowed [25,26]. Unsteady heat release is thus more coherent with 
the timescales of acoustic pressure disturbances, and the 2 L mode of 
the combustor is excited.
Figure 9 shows a set of OH-PLIF snapshots and coinciding p0
measurements for case M-3. The amplitude of pressure fluctuations 
is notably higher for this case than M-2. Unlike the M-2 case, the 
dominant frequency corresponds to a half-wave mode shape, and 
pressure fluctuations in the flame zone will have a phase offset near 
180 deg compared to the measurements at the C2 location [13]. The 
original C2 measurements are plotted alongside a y-axis reflection 
(labeled p0
FOV) in Fig. 9(b) to more closely resemble the expected 
pressure fluctuations at the flame. Instantaneous images in Fig. 9(a)
are again selected to span a single acoustic cycle and are distributed 
equally with 200 ls separation. Global oscillations in OH intensity 
are once again observed, but with several key distinctions. The 
expansion of the acoustic cycle, beginning on frame 2, corresponds 
to a sharp increase in bulk velocity which draws unburned reactants 
into the combustor. Growth in the extent and diameter of the 
premixed jets is observed in frame 3 and frame 4, as bulk velocity 
continues to increase (i). Gradients in OH intensity around the jets 
also reduce, indicating a reduction in reaction progress rate 
occurring with the acceleration of the flow. At a local minimum in 
p0, the fluctuating velocity reaches a maximum. Unburned reactants 
fill the entire field of view and suppress reactions, leading to flame

4 Conclusions
The impact of fuel composition on the structure and dynamics of 
lean-premixed H2/CH4 and H2/NH3/CH4 flames was investigated in 
a high-pressure multi-element combustor. Time resolved images of 
the flame response to acoustic disturbances were obtained using 
100 kHz OH-PLIF along with simultaneous measurements of 
chamber pressure fluctuations. Two unstable modes of the 
combustor were studied with and without the inclusion of ammonia, 
as well as a case of stable operation. The following conclusions are 
noted:
(1) The increase in instability magnitude for test cases with 
methane addition can be correlated to increased corrugation 
of the flame front and to commensurate heat release 
fluctuations. Variations in flame speed, stretch sensitivity, 
and thermodiffusive effects with fuel composition alter the 
timescales of unsteady heat release, allowing in-phase 
coupling with the natural acoustic modes of the combustor.
(2) Unstable cases corresponding to the 2 L mode of the 
combustor see regions of OH between reactant jets persisting 
through the expansion phase of the acoustic cycle. In contrast, 
flame pinch-off, extinction, and re-ignition are seen globally 
in cases that excite the 1 L mode.
(3) Ammonia addition results in weaker gradients in OH 
intensity, which indicates a greater dispersion of reactions 
and a corresponding increase in flame thickness. The 
resulting flame structure sees significant corrugation as a 
result.
These findings highlight the disparate chemical and mixing 
timescales of ammonia, hydrogen, and methane combustion and 
provide understanding of the resulting effects on the structure and 
dynamics of these flames in turbulent, high-pressure environments. 
For retrofit gas turbine combustor applications, it is clear that 
significant consideration of the injector geometry is necessary for 
fuel flexible operation with these fuels. However, the unique 
properties of premixed hydrogen flames could be leveraged for 
flame stability with ammonia and methane in these devices.
