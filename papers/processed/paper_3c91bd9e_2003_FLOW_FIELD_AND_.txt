ABSTRACT 
The present study focuses on the flow field characterization of 
highly turbulent premixed flames, typical for stationary gas 
turbines. Mean flame front position and flame front structure at 
high inlet temperatures, lean mixtures, and high pressures are 
studied, too. Turbulence intensities and integral length scales 
have been measured in an isothermal flow field with the help of 
Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV). Mean flame front position 
and flame structure have been studied using Planar Laser-
Induced Fluorescence (PLIF) of the OH radical. 
Turbulence intensities and integral length scales have been 
measured for different turbulence generating grid geometries 
and operating conditions. The results show that the combustor 
flow field can be divided in a region close to the combustor 
head, where grid-generated turbulence is dominant, and a 
region further downstream, strongly influenced by turbulence 
generated in the shear layer. In general the measured turbulence 
intensity scales well with the bulk velocity. 
For a systematic variation of the turbulent Reynolds number, 
Damköhler number, and Karlovitz number the mean flame 
front position and the flame front structure were investigated. 
Increasing the pressure and thereby mainly increasing the 
turbulent Reynolds number only slightly affects the mean flame 
front position but increasingly corrugates the flame front. 
Increasing the bulk velocity and thereby the turbulence 
intensity does not affect the mean flame front position but due 
to the higher turbulence the flame front is increasingly 
corrugated. 
Keywords: 
High Pressure Lean Premixed Flames, Turbulence, Flow 
Field, Flame Structure 
INTRODUCTION 
Turbulent premixed flames are widely spread in technical 
applications and are used especially in stationary gas turbines 
for a high-efficient and low emission energy conversion of 
gaseous fuels. Although lean premixed combustion of gaseous 
fuels is a well-established technique for power generation, there 
is still not sufficient knowledge of turbulence – chemistry 
interaction. Especially high-pressure experimental data at gas 
turbine relevant conditions is missing. 
Turbulent premixed flames can be classified in different flame 
regimes with the help of the so-called Borghi diagram, 
presented in Figure 1 [1].

Typical flames in stationary gas turbines are characterized by 
extremely high turbulent Reynolds numbers (ReT > 104), 
Damköhler numbers about 1, and Karlovitz numbers higher 
than 10. Therefore these flames belong to the so-called 
thickened turbulent flames regime. According to theory the 
flame front in this regime is thicker than the sizes of the 
smallest eddies, therefore these eddies can penetrate into the 
flame front and thereby influence the reaction rate. In the last 
years Peters [2, 3] has proposed a new theory for the regime 
ReT > 1 and 1 < Ka < 100. In this theory, the flame front in the 
thin reaction zones regime is characterized by a thick 
preheating zone and a thin inner reaction layer, which is thinner 
than the sizes of the smallest eddies so they cannot penetrate 
into this inner layer. 
A number of experimental and theoretical studies dealing with 
flame structure and turbulent flame speed of turbulent lean 
premixed flames can be found in the literature, but most of 
them are limited to atmospheric pressure, e.g. [4, 5]. Because of 
the influence of pressure on the characteristic parameters, 
especially on the turbulent Reynolds number, these results 
cannot be transferred to high-pressure gas turbine combustion. 
Other studies e.g. [6] were performed at higher pressures but 
either in SI engines, in which the turbulence intensities and the 
turbulence scales change during the compression period, or in 
constant volume vessels, in which the pressure change during 
the flame propagation is inherent, or the operating conditions 
were at near stoichiometric equivalence ratios [7]. Therefore 
again all these results are not representative for gas turbine 
combustion.

electrical heater. The fuel (methane) and the combustion air are

hot flue gases, induced by the combustor geometry with sudden 
expansion. The liner consists of two coaxial quartz glass tubes, 
being convectively cooled with cooling air. The diameter of the 
inner quartz glass cylinder D is 75 mm. The diameter of the

The turbulence parameters u’ and LT are varied by using 
different turbulence grids (hole diameter dH, blockage) or by 
mounting these grids at different axial positions within the

plates with hexagonally located holes, two different blockage 
ratios and two different grid positions (xg = 10 and 30) were

position to the combustor inlet (x = 0 mm), normalized by the

was mounted 10 hole diameters upstream of the combustor

PIV Set-up and Post Processing 
The velocity field was measured with the help of 2D Particle 
Image Velocimetry (PIV). The laser beam generated by a 
double pulse Twin-YAG laser (Quantel) passed a set of lenses 
creating a 75 mm high light sheet, which was coupled through 
the axial quartz window. The glass tube was shielded with 
black screens to lower the amount of light reflections. All PIV 
measurements were performed in isothermal flow. The images 
were acquired with a 12 bit camera (PCO) placed on a trans-
lation stage. The flow was seeded with aluminum oxide Al2O3 
solid particles generated in a specially designed seeding gene-
rator. The primary particle size was in the range of 1-3 m. 
The quality of the 12 bit images was enhanced in the SensiCam 
software and dumped into 8 bit bitmaps for post processing 
using the commercial software Visiflow. A preliminary study 
showed that  400 images are sufficient for flow field statistics. 
The camera image was 72 mm high and 86 mm wide. The flow 
field was imaged at 4 to 5 different axial positions with an 
overlap of 7 – 30%. During the PIV measurements bulk 
velocities have been varied between 20 and 40 m/s. The images 
were acquired with a frequency of 3 Hz in double exposure 
mode giving 2 images for the cross correlation algorithm of 
Visiflow. The time separation of the exposures was varied 
between 7 and 15 microseconds according to bulk velocity.  
The images were analyzed with 3232 pixel interrogation spot 
size and an overlap of 75 %. These settings were found to be 
optimal for the desired high spatial resolution (high number of 
vectors in the flow field). Therefore the flow field consists of 
125 vectors in span wise and 157 vectors in stream wise 
direction. The resulting spatial resolution is 0.55 mm. The 
algorithm uses Gaussian fitting of the displacement peak in the 
cross-correlation function, allowing for detection of sub-pixel 
displacements. Calculated velocities were validated by means 
of their magnitude from 0 to twice the bulk velocity. Vectors 
outside of this range were rejected and replaced with vectors 
interpolated from the neighboring ones. As the commercial 
software did not provide a satisfactory algorithm for vector 
validation, an additional validation method was used to obtain 
more reliable values of the root mean square deviation 
(u(RMS), v(RMS)) velocities. In the following, to simplify 
matters, these values will be named RMS velocities.  
In Figure 3, in which a representative histogram of the axial 
velocity is shown, spurious vectors can be seen, which are 
responsible for unrealistic high values of the RMS velocities. 
These spurious vectors were rejected using a standard deviation 
iterative method described in the following part. In the first step 
the mean velocity, and the RMS velocity were calculated. In 
the second step velocities outside of a velocity range  3 
u(RMS) (99.7 % for a normal distribution) were rejected. These 
filtering loops were applied twice. It was found by analyzing 
the influence of the filtering factor on the axial RMS velocity 
that a filtering factor of 3u(RMS), equal for both loops, was 
the best. Usually the percentage of rejected vectors due to this

The instantaneous velocity field data gives an opportunity to 
calculate the integral length scale LT. These calculations were 
done numerically according to equations (3) and (4), and only 
values for the integral length scale in the axial direction will be 
presented in the following. A maximal correlation length rmax of 
30 vectors (16.5 mm) was used in the calculation. The 
correlation coefficient RL was plotted versus the x-coordinate 
and the raw data were fitted with a 6th order polynomial. 
According to equation (4), LT is theoretically the integral of the 
correlation coefficient from 0 to infinity, but in the calculations 
the point at which the correlation curve crosses 0 was used to 
determine LT. 
RESULTS 
Flow Field 
The mean flow patterns are explained with the help of 
Figure 4, which shows the axial velocity (mean) together with 
streamlines and the turbulence intensity. These results of the 
g350 grid at the grid position xg10, for an inlet temperature of 
293 K, atmospheric pressure, and a bulk velocity of 40 m/s are 
representative for other grids and other operating conditions. 
The maximum values correspond always to the red color. In the 
most upstream position of the camera, close to the entrance of 
the combustor the laser light sheet had to be restricted in height 
to get rid of the reflections from the combustor head. 
The core flow region (1) with high axial velocities and 
relatively low turbulence intensities can be seen clearly in 
Figure 4. This region, with a shape like a frustum, is located 
close to the axis of symmetry with an extension up to an axial 
distance of x/d  3. Due to the geometry with sudden 
expansion, a recirculation zone (2) is created. At an axial 
distance of x/d  8 the flow reattaches. Between the core flow 
and the recirculation zone turbulence is generated due to high 
velocity gradients. Close to the combustor entrance this shear 
layer zone (3) with high turbulence intensities has an annular 
shape and its radial extension is small. The zone spreads in 
radial direction with increasing axial distance x/d and at 
x/d  5, the high turbulent zone extends almost over the whole 
combustor diameter. This zone with high turbulence intensities 
almost over the whole diameter is named high turbulent zone 
(4) and it is about 3 diameters d long. For higher axial distances 
(x/d  8), towards the combustor exit, the turbulence intensity 
decreases due to dissipation. This region is named dissipation 
zone (5). 
The Figures 5 and 6 show a comparison of the axial and radial 
RMS velocities. In Figure 5 axial profiles along the axis of 
symmetry (y/R = 0) and in Figure 6 radial profiles at different 
axial positions are plotted. All results are again for the same 
grid, grid position and operating conditions as in Figure 4. 
In the region close to the combustor head (x/d  3) the values 
of u(RMS), v(RMS), and u’ along the axis of symmetry slightly 
decrease with increasing axial distance because of the 
dissipation of the grid-generated turbulence. Between x/d = 3 
and x/d = 7 the values increase with increasing axial distance, 
reach their maximum at x/d  7, and decrease further 
downstream. This typical shape which is the same for all grids, 
grid positions and operation conditions is caused by the

The first part (x/d  3) represents a region of grid-generated 
turbulence, in which no influence of the turbulence generated in 
the shear layer is present. Because the grid is mounted far 
upstream of this region (x > 15 dH), the dissipation rate of this 
grid generated turbulence is already quite small. In the second 
part (3 < x/d < 7) the values increase with increasing axial 
distance. Due to turbulent transport the turbulence generated in 
the shear layer is increasingly influencing the turbulence 
intensity in the region close to the axis of symmetry. This 
broadening of the shear layer in radial direction can be seen in 
the radial profiles of u(RMS) for x/d up to 5 in Figure 6. At the 
axial position of x/d  7 the values of u(RMS), v(RMS), and u’ 
reach their maximum, indicating the balance of shear layer 
generated turbulence, turbulent transport, and dissipation of 
grid and shear layer turbulence. Farther downstream (x/d > 8), 
the dissipation dominates and therefore the values of u(RMS), 
v(RMS), and u’ decrease.  
In the core flow region (x/d  3, -0.15  y/R  0.15) the axial 
RMS velocity is only approximately 20-30 % higher than the 
radial RMS velocity. Therefore the turbulence in the core 
region is almost isotropic. This is not true for the shear layer, in 
which the axial RMS velocities are approximately 40 - 50 % 
higher than the radial RMS velocities v(RMS).

Figure 8 shows profiles along the axis of symmetry of the mean 
axial velocity umean and the turbulence intensity u’ of the g350 
and g450 grid, for 1 bar, 293 K, and the grid position xg = 10. 
The bulk velocity, calculated with the air flow rate and the inlet 
cross section, has been varied from 20 m/s to 40 m/s. For axial

constant for a constant airflow rate. The values at the axis of 
symmetry are approximately 30 % higher than the calculated 
bulk velocities. This is consistent with the theory of partly 
developed turbulent flows in pipes [10]. Because of the 
increasing influence of the expansion farther downstream, the 
axial velocities decrease. At higher bulk velocities due to 
higher flow rates, the turbulence intensity is higher. Almost no 
difference can be seen between the two different grids g350 and 
g450.

has been already more dissipated. Farther downstream where 
the influence of the grid-generated turbulence is low in 
comparison with the shear layer generated turbulence, no 
differences between the xg10 and xg30 series can be observed. 
For different flow rates (bulk velocities), the relative turbulence 
intensity u’/ubulk along the axis of symmetry is the same. This 
indicates the proportionality of the turbulence intensity to the 
flow rate, which can also be seen in Table 3, in which the 
results of the velocity variation are summarized for all grids 
and grid positions. 
In Table 3 the maximum values for the relative turbulence 
intensity u’/ubulk at the axis of symmetry, the relative turbulence 
intensity u’/ubulk at x/d = 2, and the normalized reattachment 
length xr/d are summarized for all grids, grid positions and bulk 
velocities. For a constant grid position, the maximum values of 
u’/ubulk and the values at x/d = 2 are approximately constant for 
each grid and for different bulk velocities, thereby indicating 
the proportionality of the relative turbulence intensity to the 
flow rate.  
The maximum values of the relative turbulence intensity, 
reached at x/d  7, are for all grids in the range of 20 % and do 
not depend on grid position or blockage ratio, because at the 
axial distance, where the values reach their maximum, the 
turbulence is dominated by the shear layer generated turbulence 
and not by the grid-generated turbulence.  
For the core flow region, here represented by the values at 
x/d = 2, mounting the grid further upstream (xg30 instead of 
xg10) results in a decrease of the relative turbulence intensity. 
Because of the more upstream position of the grid the grid-
generated turbulence is already dissipated to a larger extend. 
The value of the g465, xg30 grid is probably too high, but no 
explanation can be given so far. For grids with a lower 
blockage ratio (g350, g450) the relative turbulence intensity in 
the core flow is lower than for the grids with higher blockage 
ratio (g365, g465). The relative turbulence intensities at x/d = 2 
for the g350 and g450 grid are about 4 - 6 %, whereas for the 
g365 and g465 grid the values are in the range of 6 - 11 %. 
Grids with a higher blockage ratio generate more turbulence in 
the region close to the grid. Farther downstream, where only a 
part of this turbulence has been dissipated, this results in higher 
turbulence levels.  
The normalized reattachment length xr/d are for all other grids 
in the range of 8 ± 10 % and do not depend on the airflow rate 
(bulk velocity), which is consistent with literature data [11]. 
The deviation of the reattachment length of ± 10 % is probably 
within the accuracy limit of the presented PIV measurements.

Increasing the pressure from 1 to 10 bars and thereby 
increasing the turbulent Reynolds number by about one order of 
magnitude up to 12460 and increasing the Karlovitz number by 
a factor of about 5 from 6 up to 30, results in a 60 % decrease 
of Damköhler number down to 4 (see also Figure 1). Increasing 
the pressure results in a slight shift of the mean flame front 
position upstream towards the combustor inlet, which could be 
interpreted as a small increase in turbulent flame speed. The 
flame front is thereby increasingly corrugated, which can be 
seen in the single shot images on the right side of Figure 13. 
The value of mean flame front position at the axis of symmetry 
at 10 bars is about 12 % smaller than the value at 1 bar. The 
reason is probably slightly higher flame temperatures at higher 
pressures (higher thermal loading) due to lower relative heat 
losses. Therefore, especially in consideration of the small 
change in flame temperature, no significant influence of 
pressure on mean flame front position has been observed. This 
indicates that the turbulent flame speed is independent of 
pressure, which is contrary to laminar flames, which show a 
strongly decreasing laminar flame speed with increasing 
pressure.  
Finally the reason for the use of the specific values of the 
relative turbulence intensity u’/ubulk = 20 % and the integral 
length scale LT = 10 mm has to be given. This is elucidated 
with the help of Figure 14, which shows the turbulence 
intensity together with the contour of the mean flame front for 
the g350, xg10 grid, for T = 673 K, 1 bar, and a bulk velocity of 
40 m/s. 
The flame front contour has been derived by the determination 
of the flame front position at the axis of symmetry according to 
the previous description. The same OH-LIF signal intensity is 
used to obtain the whole flame front contour in the averaged 
OH-PLIF image. Figure 14 shows, that the flame front is 
located where the turbulence intensity reaches its maximum. At 
the axis of symmetry this is at the axial distance of x/d  7. 
Therefore the values of u’ and LT at this axial position had been 
used for the calculation of ReT, Da, Ka, which is at least for 
classification purposes in the Borghi diagram a valid approach. 
CONCLUSIONS 
In the present experimental work turbulent premixed 
methane/air flames have been studied at gas turbine relevant 
conditions. The influence of turbulence grid parameters 
(geometry, grid position) as well as of the operating conditions 
(bulk velocity, temperature, and pressure) on turbulence 
intensity and integral length scale have been investigated at 
isothermal conditions with the help of Particle Image 
Velocimetry (PIV). Combustion experiments with a systematic 
variation 
of 
the 
characteristic 
parameters, 
turbulent 
Reynolds number, Damköhler number, and Karlovitz number, 
by changing the pressure and bulk velocity have been 
performed to study the mean flame front position and the flame 
front structure with the help of planar Laser-Induced 
Fluorescence of the OH radical (OH-PLIF). 
The presented PIV results show that the turbulence intensity is 
proportional to the bulk velocity, with a maximum value 
u’/ubulk of 20 % reached at an axial distance of x/d  7. In the 
core flow region close to the combustor head, the turbulence is 
influenced by the grid geometry and position. Grids with higher 
blockage ratios or grids, mounted farther downstream, result in 
higher turbulence intensities in the core flow region. Farther 
downstream the turbulence generated in the shear layer is 
dominant and no influence of the grid geometry or the grid 
position have been observed. These results of the turbulence 
intensity measurements can also be assigned to the integral 
length scale results. Because of a low dissipation rate in the 
core flow region the integral length scale increases only 
moderately, whereas in the region farther downstream, where 
the influence of the shear layer turbulence due to turbulent 
transport is growing and the dissipation rate is high, the integral 
length scale increases more rapidly. At the combustor entrance 
the values of the integral length scale correspond approximately 
with one half of the according grid hole diameter dH. Farther 
downstream, at the position on the axis of symmetry where the 
turbulence intensity is maximal, the integral length scale 
reaches a value of about 10 mm.  
Results of the OH-PLIF measurements show that increasing the 
pressure and thereby primarily increasing the turbulent 
Reynolds number shifts the mean flame front position only 
slightly towards the combustor inlet, but increasingly 
corrugates the flame front. Increasing the bulk velocity does not 
affect the mean flame font position, but due to higher 
turbulence intensities increasingly corrugates the flame front. 
The comparison of the PIV and the OH-PLIF results show that 
the flame stabilizes in a region where the turbulence intensity is 
maximal. 
Future studies will focus on the statistical flame front analysis 
to determine turbulent flame speeds. The influence of 
turbulence on the turbulent flame speed and flame front 
structure will be further investigated in the future work.
