Development of a Hydrogen Micro
Gas Turbine Combustor:
Atmospheric Pressure Testing
The vision of a carbon-neutral world implies the shift from fossil to clean fuels for
combustion-driven processes and machines like gas turbines. Green hydrogen isa promising
alternative to substitute natural gas and other fossil fuels. In the H2mGT project, funded by
the German BMWK, a microgas turbine (mGT) burner with 100% hydrogen ﬁring is
developed and validated. The project is collaboration between Technische Universit€at
Berlin (TUB) and the manufacturer Euro-K GmbH. The project consists of three phases: (1).
Atmospheric pressure tests with a fused silica combustion chamber; (2). Atmospheric
pressure tests with counterﬂow-cooled steel combustion chamber and secondary air
injection; (3). Validation of the burner in the micro gas turbine at elevated pressure levels.
This paper will present the results of Phase 1. The hydrogen burner is based on a swirl-
stabilized burner of TUB and was scaled to match the requirements of the mGT with its
130 kW thermal power. The burner design features multiple geometrical parameters to
enable the optimization of the ﬂame towards low NOx emissions. Therefore, a variable swirl
intensity, additional axial momentum of air in the mixing tube, a movable center-body and
different fuel injection locations are implemented. Phase 1 investigates the parameter space
in terms of ﬂame stability, operational range, and parameter impact on ﬂame shape and
emissions. Therefore, temperature, pressure, and emission measurements as well as OH*
imaging are carried out. It is found that the ﬂame can be operated over a large range of
equivalence ratios and preheating temperatures up to 500 8C for many parameter settings.
However, at some conﬁgurations, ﬂashback into the mixing tube is triggered. As expected,
the NOx emissions are mainly inﬂuenced by the equivalence ratio, the fuel distribution, and
the swirl intensity. Single-digit emissions are reached up to an equivalence ratio of 0.4 at
atmospheric pressure conditions. Furthermore, at low air mass ﬂow, the burner can be
operated at 100% natural gas or 100% hydrogen without any geometry changes. The fuel
switch, thereby, does not change the NOx emissions signiﬁcantly if reasonable normalization
is used. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4063708]
Keywords: micro gas turbine, combustion, hydrogen, emissions

Introduction
The current developments inthe global climate crisis make a rapid
shift away from fossil fuels inevitable. Nevertheless, combustion
engines—especially gas turbines—will continue to be an essential
part of our energy supply thanks to their ﬂexibility and reliability,
but fossil fuels need to be replaced. A CO2-free alternative is green
hydrogen produced from renewable energies. The joint H2mGT
project of the TUB and the micro gas turbine manufacturer Euro-K
GmbH aims to develop a hydrogen (H2) combustor for a micro gas
turbine.
The topic of H2 combustion has now also arrived in the gas turbine
industry. Almost all major manufacturers, such as Siemens [1–7],
GE [8–10], Mitsubishi Heavy Industries [11–13], Ansaldo Energia
[14–16], Solar Turbines [17] and PSM [18], are developing various
combustion systems for syngas and H2-enriched natural gas. In
general, increased NOx emissions and problems with ﬂame stability
are reported, so the H2 content in the fuel remains limited. Very few
gas turbines are suitable for 100% H2 so far. One was commissioned
in 2010 in Fusina, Italy (modiﬁed GE10.1 with 12 MW), another
with 1 MW was tested by Kawasaki Heavy Industries in 2018 in
Kobe, Japan [19]. A recent overview about the developments in the
industry is given by the ETN Global’s Hydrogen Gas Turbines
report [20].
On the lab-scale level, the ﬂame stability and the emissions of
mixtures with 45%vol H2 in natural gas have been investigated by
Schefer et al. [21,22]. H2 enrichment increases the risk of ﬂashback
but improves the lean blow-off limit, enhancing the ﬂammability
range. They found reductions in CO concentration with H2 addition,
without adversely affecting the NOx emissions. Hussain et al. [23]
measured a drastic increase in NOx levels for H2 addition of 30%vol
in methane and ethylene ﬂames. However, the H2 was injected
separately and further downstream than the main fuel, which leads to
a much higher stratiﬁcation. The ﬂashback propensity was studied
by Beerer et al. [24], Page et al. [25], and Syred et al. [26]. They also

A perfect platform to test H2 combustion on a machine level is the
mGT since the necessary investment, the operating pressure, and the
exhaust gas temperatures are much lower than for heavy-duty gas
turbines. In addition, mGTs usually offer good accessibility to the
combustion chamber. They provide an alternative to gas piston
engines in the small and medium combined heat and power segment.
They are very ﬂexible and emit signiﬁcantly less pollutants than
piston engines [30]. Modern mGT combustion chamber systems
achieve NOx emissions of 15 ppm, while current lean-burn engines
in the medium power class with oxidation catalyst emit NOx values
of 125–250 ppm. The German Aerospace Center (DLR) [30]
predicts a large potential for efﬁciency improvements and a rising
share on the combined heat and power market for mGTs. In fact,
there is a recent research interest in mGTs. However, most of the
studies are done numerically. A review of such studies with respect
to different mGT-based energy systems is given by Reale and
Sannino [31]. On the experimental side, mGT combustors have been
investigated for a variety of unconventional fuels thanks to their fuel
ﬂexibility. Liu et al. [32] studied the inﬂuence of different biofuels in
an mGT combustor and found that the NOx emissions increase with
the fuel’s Wobbe Index. Furthermore, the fuel injection method has
a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the pollutant emissions. Okafor et al. [33]
measured a strong rise of NOx emissions for ammonia-enriched
methane with single-stage combustion. In a small mGT (1–3 kWel),
du Toit et al. [34] measured emissions of H2/CH4/CO2 fuel blends.
Adding 15%vol H2 to CH4 showed no signiﬁcant effect on the NOx
emissions, while a 20=65=15%vol mix reduced it by almost 50%.
The study also reports the inﬂuence of the fuel switch on the mGT
operation and auxiliaries. On a larger mGT (Capstone C30,
30 kWel), de-Santoli et al. [35] tested H2 addition in natural gas up
to 10%vol. In this study, the H2 was used to compensate the engine
derating due to increased inlet temperature in the summertime. A
slight increase of the NOx is reported for the H2 enrichment. The
authors also discuss the problem of the correct normalization of the
emissions measured. As a result, they chose speciﬁc emissions
instead of volumetric ones to evaluate the environmental impact of
the mGT. In the current study, the normalization issue is discussed in
the last section. The UCI Combustion Laboratory retroﬁtted a
Capstone C60 mGT [36,37] for the operation with H2. Despite
adjustments to the fuel injection, the tests showed signiﬁcantly
increased NOx emissions. The integrated combustion chamber of
this machine allows only a few changes in this respect. In contrast, a
silo with a single can combustor is used in the current project, which
features better accessibility. Devriese et al. [38] also found high NOx
emissions for a mGT burner with H2 ﬁring in numerical studies.
Cappelletti et al. [39] numerically studied the burner of a T100
mGT (100 kWel) and found that the original design is not suitable for
100% H2, as the risk of ﬂame ﬂashbacks is too high. The issue of
ﬂashback was also addressed by Pappa et al. [40]. They showed by
LES that ﬂashback occurred already at 10%mass H2 enrichment in an
unmodiﬁed T100 mGT combustor. As a countermeasure, humidi-
ﬁed air (10%mass) was used to lower the reaction rate and, hence,
prevent ﬂashback. The concept of wet combustion was also
previously studied at a similar burner as in the current study. The
results show a strong reduction in NOx emissions even at large H2
contents and elevated pressure [41].
In summary, there are many challenges in the combustion of H2.
On the one hand, H2 has a lower volumetric energy density (Table 3),
which requires a higher fuel ﬂow. On the other hand, H2 has a high
gravimetric energy density and a signiﬁcantly higher reactivity
compared to natural gas, which might increase the ﬂame
temperature and speed. The former can have a negative impact on
NOx emissions, while the latter makes it difﬁcult to stabilize a
premixed ﬂame. In particular, the question of whether and to what
extent H2 increases NOx emissions seems to be contradictory in the
literature. These challenges are to be tackled in the present project
with a swirl-stabilized dry low NOx burner with axial air injection.
The TUB has investigated a similar burner in numerous projects
with H2 and enriched natural gas and was able to achieve very good
results regarding the prevention of ﬂame ﬂashbacks and the
minimization of NOx emissions [42–49]. The intended burner
design is presented in detail in the ﬁrst section.
The H2mGT project is divided into three phases. First, the base
combustor is scaled and redesigned according to the requirements of
the mGT. It is then tested in an atmospheric combustion test rig with
a fused silica combustion chamber using OH* ﬂame recording and
emission analysis. In phase two, the transparent chamber is replaced
by a steel ﬂame tube with counterﬂow cooling and secondary air
injection. Using this setup, the burner is optimized in the test rig
regarding emissions and stable operation. After this phase is
completed, the burner is adjusted for the integration in the mGT at
Euro-K’s test facility. Here, the pressure is increased and the new
burner is validated under nominal operating conditions. The current
paper presents the results of the project’s ﬁrst phase regarding
stability range, ﬂame shapes, and emissions. It additionally
discusses the aspects of natural gas/hydrogen mixtures and ﬂash-
back recovery.
2
Burner and Test Rig Design
The burner designed for this project is based on a generic swirl-
stabilized burner, which was already extensively studied at TUB
[42–49]. It was scaled to the thermal power of the micro gas turbine
by maintaining similar volumetric ﬂows in the burner. Geometrical
parameters in the burner design are supposed to allow for an
optimization of the operation range and NOx emissions.
Figure 1 showsa schematic of the burnerinPhase 1.The incoming
air is preheated up to 500 C and fed to the plenum via four inlets. In
the plenum, the air ﬂow splits between a tangential (via the swirler)
and an axial ﬂow to the mixing tube (left orange arrows in Fig. 1). As
they are fed by the same plenum, the splitting ratio is solely
determined by the pressure loss of each ﬂow path. Both ﬂows, the
axial and the swirled, merge again in the mixing tube (Dmt ¼ 31 mm)
and enter the combustion chamber (Dcc ¼ 100 mm, Lcc ¼ 180 mm)
further downstream. A third but much smaller ﬂow of air is added to
the mixing tube through a ring of holes to lean out the boundary
layer.
The fuel can be injected through three different injection points
(blue arrows in Fig. 1): (1). 16 nozzles (DH2 ¼ 1:4 mm) at the bottom
of the mixing tube, which is regarded as technically premixed, (2).
an axial nozzle (Dp ¼ 3 mm) fuel lance (Dcb ¼ 6 mm) in the mixing
tube as central injector, (3). eight pilot nozzles (Dp2 ¼ 1 mm) at the
burner front plate around the mixing tube exit. The latter are
supplied by a cavity behind the burner plate and can also be used
with air for active or passive cooling ofthe plate. The front plate pilot
is intended as emergency backup for ﬂashback recovery. The idea of
the central injection via the fuel lance is that the H2 fraction proﬁle in
the mixing tube can be altered.
The burner features four parameters to alter the ﬂow ﬁeld and fuel
distribution. These parameters are depicted in Fig. 1 and can be
adjusted manually from outside without disassembling the burner.
Hence, the design points in an optimization can be set during the
operation of the burner, which allows for the evaluation of much
more conﬁgurations. The parameters are

Measurement Settings
The micro gas turbine has a thermal power of 127 kW and a
nominal air mass ﬂow of 1280 kg/h. However, these values must be
scaled down since the tests are conducted at atmospheric pressure.
The thermal power was ﬁxed to 36 kW for most of the experiments.
In the mGT, the total air mass ﬂow at baseload conditions is ﬁxed by
the output of the compressor, but the air is split into primary and
secondary air. Only the primary air ﬂows through the burner, while
the secondary air is injected to the combustion chamber downstream
of the ﬂame. By changing the splitting ratio (e.g., changing the hole
pattern in the combustion chamber wall), the local equivalence ratio
of the ﬂame can be varied. As there is no secondary air injection in
the Phase 1 experiments, the total air ﬂow _mair runs through the
burner. Hence, _mair is varied to ﬁnd an optimal primary equivalence
ratio for the operation of the burner. The bounds are set to / ¼ 0:2
and 0.6 to avoid blow off and high NOx emissions, respectively. The
air is preheated to 500 C in the plenum to match the conditions of
the recuperated mGT. The fuel is supplied at room temperature.
However, the fuel heats up while ﬂowing through the hot plenum.

As the ﬂow in the mixing tube is swirling, it induces a velocity
deﬁcit (VD) at the centerline of the mixing tube as seen in Fig. 6,
which can increase the risk of ﬂashback. At the area jump from the
mixing tube to the combustion chamber, the swirled ﬂow undergoes
vortex breakdown and forms an inner recirculation zone (RZ) with
negative axial velocity. The RZ is indicated by the black stagnation
line in Figs. 5 and 7. Between the RZ and the outer recirculation
zone, there is a high velocity jet transporting the fresh air/fuel
mixture and generating strong shear layers (SL). These SL are
intended to stabilize the ﬂame.
The blockage of the swirler (S) increases the swirl intensity in the
mixing tube and, therefore, leads to the upstream shift of the RZ
shown in Fig. 7. Moreover, the VD is ampliﬁed by the enlarged swirl
intensity. The latter effect can be partly compensated by increasing
the axial air injection as shown by the ﬂow proﬁles in Fig. 6. Opening
the axial air injection channels (A) reduces the VD and leads to a
small downstream shift of the RZ’s stagnation point.
The fuel lance (L) has an additional effect on the VD due to its
wake. If it is placed far downstream in the mixing tube, its wake can
couple with the RZ such that the stagnation point shifts into the
mixing tube. The wake effect can be compensated by activating the
fuel ﬂow through the central injector (P).
In general, the P injection allows for a variation of the fuel
distribution at the exit of the mixing tube. Left side of Fig. 8 shows

Fig. 8 shows that the fuel concentration maximum shifts outside to

Flame Shape and Stability Range
To conduct the optimization process in Phase 2 of the project, a
map of stable operation points must be provided. Therefore, ﬂame
shapes have been recorded for a large number of parameter
variations.
In the case of a stable ﬂame, the technically premixed H2/air
mixture burns in V or M shape downstream of the mixing tube as
shown in Fig. 9 (l.). However, geometry changes can alter the ﬂow
ﬁeld in the aforementioned ways, which may trigger ﬂashback as
seen in the right image. Then, the ﬂame propagates upstream into the
mixing tube. The ﬂame stabilizes itself in the wake of the center
body and keeps burning as a jet ﬂame in the mixing tube and the
combustion chamber. In this case, the fuel valve is closed to avoid
damage to the burner.
Figure 10 shows the operational range of the ﬂame depending on
three of the parameters. The different markers symbolize increasing
levels of fuel share via the fuel lance (P). Hence, points with all ﬁve
markers feature stability for a variety of fuel distributions and are
very robust against ﬂashback. The main driver of instability is the
positioning of the fuel lance (L). For L <¼ 10 mm all ﬂames are
stable, while for higher L, the stability declines with increasing swirl
intensity as shown by the dashed line in Fig. 10. Next to the ﬂow ﬁeld
changes (VD and RZ), L varies the fuel distribution as seen in Fig. 8
and thus the local equivalence ratio and ﬂame speeds. However,
these changes in the concentration ﬁeld are smaller than for a
variation of P. Since the receptivity of ﬂashback is still higher for L
than for P, it can be concluded that the ﬂashback is dominantly
triggered by hydrodynamic effects.
Some points close to the stability line show an interesting
behavior. They are marginally stable for P ¼ 0% and P  40%. In
between these stable bounds, the ﬂame undergoes ﬂashback. On the
one side, shifting fuel to the central injection increases the local
equivalence ratio near the centerline in the mixing tube. Thus, the
ﬂame speed increases and the ﬂame propagates upstream along the
VD. On the other side, if the fuel ﬂow from the fuel lance is large
enough, the VD is reduced by the axial fuel momentum and the ﬂame
is pushed back into the combustion chamber.
By applying a very low swirl intensity (S¼ 5 mm), it is possible to
move the lance (P ¼ 0%) until the end of the mixing tube without
triggering ﬂashback. However, the ﬂames in these conﬁgurations are
not very robust and a variation of P has not been tested for L  50 mm.
Moreover, placing the fuel lance at the end increases its thermal load
because the ﬂame burns in the shear layers of the fuel lance (see
Fig. 11(d) r.). Thus, conﬁgurations with L> 70mm should be avoided.
The inﬂuence of the restriction of the axial air injection (A) is not
shown in Fig. 10. Closing channels generally decrease the stability
of the ﬂame because the VD is ampliﬁed. However, the actual point
of ﬂashback depends strongly on the swirl intensity and fuel lance
position. For example, six of the eight channels can be closed (A ¼ 6)
at S ¼ 10 mm and L ¼ 10 mm before ﬂashback appears.
The utilization of the burner in the micro gas turbine does not only
require static but also dynamic stability. During the experiments, the
sound pressure levels had been monitored by a microphone in the
exhaust tube. There are no indications of thermoacoustic insta-
bilities under normal conditions at any of the tested parameter
settings.

Parameter Study

the ﬂame. The optimization in the subsequent phase of the project
will be done with a constant splitting ratio between primary and
secondary air,e.g.,a nearly constantequivalence ratio. In order toset
the equivalence ratio properly for the optimization, its inﬂuence on

Furthermore, the ﬂame becomes more compact from / ¼ 0:5 to 0.7
due to a combination of the lower ﬂow velocities and the increased
ﬂame speed.
Figure 11(b) shows the inﬂuence of the swirl intensity on the
ﬂame shape at a constant equivalence ratio. For moderate swirl
intensities, the ﬂame stabilizes inside the combustion chamber.
Between S ¼ 5 mm and S ¼ 10 mm it, transitions from V to M shape
without changing the ﬂame root position. Further increasing the
swirl enhances the VD in the mixing tube and shifts the RZ upstream
(Fig. 7), which enables the ﬂame to propagate upstream as well. At
S ¼ 20 mm, the ﬂame root lies completely inside the mixing tube.
The ﬂame is still stable but has a low robustness against ﬂashback as
shown in Fig. 10.

the ﬂame topology. Hence, they qualify to be used as design
parameters in the planned optimization process. Moreover,
constraints for the parameter space can be derived from the recorded
ﬂame shapes and the stability map in Fig. 10. A second important
aspect is the inﬂuence of the parameters on the emissions, which will
be discussed in Sec. 7.
7
Emissions
The strongest impact on the NOx emissions has naturally the
variation of the equivalence ratio as depicted in Fig. 12. The
emissions rise for all curves due to the increasing ﬂame temperature.
For some design points, single digit NOx can be reached up to
/ ¼ 0:4.
Figure 12 presents also the inﬂuence of the swirl parameter S,
which reveals a monotonic behavior as increased swirl intensity
reduces the NOx emissions signiﬁcantly. On the one hand, the ﬂame
is very compact at low swirl, while it is stretched with increasing
swirl intensity (Fig. 11(b)), which improves heat release distribution
and could reduce NOx. On the other hand, the higher swirl intensity
increases the turbulence in the mixing tube and leads to a more
homogeneous air/fuel mixture in both the spatial and temporal
domain. This effect was measured for a similar burner by Reichel
et al. [45] and reduces local and temporary hot spots in the ﬂame.
Since the NOx emissions rise exponentially with the ﬂame
temperature, such hot spot related ﬂuctuations cause increased
NOx production. The combination of these effects halves the NOx
values from S ¼ 5 mm to S ¼ 19 mm, which is a remarkable
reduction. In the extreme case at S ¼ 19 mm, Fig. 11(b) shows
very high stretching of the ﬂame. The high strain rate could possibly
lead to local quenching, which would also reduce the NOx
emissions. However, the exhaust H2 concentrations and temper-
atures show no signs of incomplete combustion in this case.
The effect of the fuel distribution is further investigated by
shifting H2 to the fuel lance, i.e., increasing P, and, hence, artiﬁcially
impairing the fuel/air homogeneity. Figure 13 shows the inﬂuence of
a single parameter on the emissions while the other parameters are
held constant. Shifting the fuel to the central injection (P) increases
the local equivalence ratio in the center of the ﬂow. As expected, the
emissions increase accordingly. Interestingly, for P  20% the
curve starts to saturate. Even though, the fuel distribution (Fig. 8)
and ﬂame shape (Fig. 13) are still changing, the maximum ﬂame
temperature seems not to rise much further. Please note that the fuel
lance is placed at L ¼ 10 mm for this curve, which means that the
point of central injection is 20 mm downstream of the premixing
nozzles and 80 mm upstream of the combustion chamber. Hence, the
ﬂame can still be regarded as technically premixed rather than
diffusive.
The variation of the lance position (L) has no effect on the
emissions as long as P ¼ 0%. Restricting the axial air injection (A)
has also a low inﬂuence on the emissions up to the point close to
ﬂashback. At A ¼ 6 the NOx increase slightly. This is caused by a
strong narrowing and, hence, concentration of the ﬂame (Fig. 11(c)).
Even though both parameters show a low inﬂuence on the emissions
individually, they might combine with other parameters as they still
alter the ﬂame and should be included in the later optimization
nonetheless.
Please note that the absolute values of Figs. 12 and 13 cannot be
compared directly with each other because they were measured in
two campaigns with slightly different setups (e.g., passive/no
cooling). Nevertheless, both plots are consistent individually, which
justiﬁes the statements made.

burner because by integrating the combustor into the mGT, its
pressure loss is limited to the capabilities of compressor and turbine.

the ambient pressure for all emission measurement points. Figure 14

Fuel Mixtures
For the initial tests at machine level, it might be necessary to start
with natural gasand increase the H2content gradually, incase full H2
burning causes ﬂashbacks or other unexpected events. Therefore,
the burner is tested in the test rig with natural gas as well. The H2 and
natural gas (Table 2) are mixed in the supply line to the burner with
respect to a constant thermal power level as it is shown in Fig. 15.
Furthermore, the air mass ﬂow is ﬁxed for each measurement series,
which also means that the exhaust temperature stays approximately
constant. With this procedure, the burner allows switching the fuel
from 100% natural gas to 100% H2. However, since the burner was
designed for the properties of H2, the stability range of the natural
gas ﬂame is reduced signiﬁcantly from _mair,max > 200 kg/h to 
100 kg/h due to ﬂame blow off.
Figure 16 presents the OH* intensity of ﬂames featuring different
H2 contents in natural gas. The pure natural gas ﬂame reveals a very
long V-shape almost touching the combustion chamber walls. With
rising H2 content, the ﬂame becomes more compact due to the
increased reactivity. Finally, the ﬂame turns into M-shape as seen in
Sec. 6, Fig. 11.
The evolution of the NOx emissions during this transition is of
particular interest. As discussed in the introduction, the addition of
H2 togas turbines is often seen toincrease NOx emission. Please note
that the equivalence ratio of each series is not constant because the
stoichiometry of the fuel changes with the H2 mass fraction. Instead,
the air mass ﬂow and the exhaust temperature are kept constant. The
total mass ﬂow of the exhaust reduces only by 2  2:5% and the
volume ﬂow increases by 1  1:5% for H2 compared to natural gas.
Hence, the properties relevant for the turbine are all nearly constant
for this comparison.

content indeed. However, this rise cannot be seen in the raw data, but
is imposed by the standard normalization to dry exhaust gas with
15% oxygen content. This normalization is tailored to the regular
combustion of natural gas or methane and is intended to compensate
variations of the global equivalence ratio. Thereby it assumes an
otherwise relatively constant composition of the exhaust gas. This is
not the case for a fuel switch. The substitution of natural gas by H2

Flashback Recovery
The parameter study in Sec. 5 showed a large stable operation
window for the burner, which should allow safe operation in the
mGT. However, the ﬂame properties, e.g., the ﬂame speed, change
during the ramp-up of the static pressure (to 3.6 bara). The risk of
ﬂashbacks can be particularly high during certain transient
conditions such as startup or load changes.
The burner features eight additional fuel nozzles at the burner
front plate that can be used as an inherently stable pilot ﬂame. The
individual diffusion ﬂames from each nozzle can be seen clearly in
Fig. 18 (r.), which shows the OH* intensity of the ﬂame at 100%
pilot. As soon as ﬂashback is detected, the H2 can be shifted to the
pilot. The burner can be brought back to stable conditions before the
fuel is shifted to the premixed mode again. This procedure has been
tested successfully in the test rig by triggering ﬂashback via the fuel
lance position.
The front plate pilot should only be used shortly for ﬂashback
recovery as the NOxemissions are much higher than with premixing.
Figure 19 presents a comparison between the full (technically)
premixed mode, the fuel lance and the front plate pilot. As discussed
before in Fig. 13, the emission rise for shifting H2 to the fuel lance
saturates at P ¼ 40%. The emissions of the front plate pilot, on the
other hand, increase continuously. However, the NOx curve of this
ﬂame increases much slower than the one with the fuel lance until
70% pilot share. This is surprising since the injection via the front
plate is pure H2 that ignites immediately, while the injection via the
fuel lance is done approximately 80 mm upstream of the ﬂame.
Hence, the fuel homogeneity should be better using the fuel lance,
but the emissions are still higher for low and medium P.

10
Conclusion
The paper presents the results of the ﬁrst phase of the H2mGT
project, which aims to develop and validate a hydrogen burner for a
micro gas turbine. It is shown that the burner can be stably operated
for multiple parameter settings with 100% hydrogen fueling. The
inﬂuence of four geometric parameters on the ﬂame shape is
presented. At certain design points, ﬂashback is triggered by an
ampliﬁcation of the velocity deﬁcit on the centerline of the mixing
tube. This ampliﬁcation is caused by the position of the fuel lance
and its wake, the restriction of axial air injection, and increased swirl
intensity. At the same time, high swirl intensity lowers the emissions
and indeed single digit NOx can be reached (up to / ¼ 0:4 at
atmospheric pressure). Next to the equivalence ratio, the emissions
are mainly inﬂuenced by the fuel injection strategy and the swirl
intensity in the mixing tube. Switching the fuel from natural gas to
hydrogen at constant geometry alters the ﬂame shape but does not
lead to a signiﬁcant increase in emissions. However, the standard
volumetric normalization to 15% O2 artiﬁcially overemphasizes the
NOx by almost 40%. This normalization is often used in
governmental regulations, e.g., the German TA Luft, and penalizes
the usage of hydrogen. Hence, such regulations should be adjusted in
the future.
In the next phase of the project, the burner is equipped with a
counterﬂow cooled steel combustion chamber and secondary air
injection. Using this setup, an optimization toward low emissions
and high ﬂame stability is performed. The results from the ﬁrst phase
are used to deﬁne the parameter space of the optimization. The
variation of parameters showed a certain correlation between the
emissions and the burner pressure loss. As the possible pressure loss
of the burner in the mGT is limited by the compressor and turbine
capabilities, it needs to be constrained in the optimization.
