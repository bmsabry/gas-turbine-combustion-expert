A B S T R A C T
This study addresses the thermoacoustic oscillation issue in a radially staged swirl combustion chamber of an 
industrial gas turbine. Through a combination of high-temperature/high-pressure experiments and combustion 
large eddy simulation (LES), the influence of fuel staging ratio on combustion stability was systematically 
investigated. Experimental results captured oscillation frequencies of 263 Hz, 492 Hz, and 543 Hz during the fuel 
switching process. Acoustic modal analysis revealed that these frequencies correspond to the acoustic natural 
frequencies of the diffusion fuel, internal premixed fuel and external premixed fuel channel, respectively. When 
the combustion chamber thermal load was increased to 100 %, a thermoacoustic oscillation with a dominant 
frequency of 445 Hz and an amplitude exceeding 15 kPa emerged, accompanied by a sharp increase in the flame 
tube end face temperature, corresponding to the ignition phenomenon in the corner recirculation zone (CRZ). 
The mechanism of combustion oscillation generation was clarified through the large eddy simulation numerical 
method. When the external premixed fuel fraction exceeded 81.8 %, periodic vortex driven ignition in the CRZ 
led to in phase coupling of pressure and heat release fluctuations at 445 Hz, triggering strong thermoacoustic 
instability. Through fuel staging optimization, we demonstrated that limiting the outer premixed fuel to 
81.8–88.2 % while maintaining the internal premixed fraction below 12 % effectively suppresses CRZ driven 
oscillations. The key novelty of this work lies in identifying fuel system acoustics as a direct source of combustion 
instability and clarifying the intrinsic thermoacoustic mechanism behind CRZ flashback, providing physics based 
guidelines for the stable design of advanced staged combustors.
1. Introduction
Gas turbines, representing the pinnacle of national industrial capa­
bility [1], serve as core components of energy and power systems. In 
light of increasingly stringent international emission regulations [2], 
lean premixed staged combustion technology has emerged as the pre­
dominant solution due to its superior low-emission characteristics [3–5]. 
However, combustion instability remains a critical constraint in 
advancing lean premixed combustion [6–8]. Thermoacoustic insta­
bility—an instability in flow dynamics caused by the bidirectional 
coupling between acoustic waves and unsteady heat release rates [9] is 
intrinsically linked to unsteady combustion phenomena, and is thus 
frequently referred to as combustion instability [10]. This instability 
typically induces hazardous fluctuations in flow dynamics and heat 
release, potentially leading to structural damage, excessive vibrations, 
noise, flashback, and overall degradation of the combustion system [11].
The excitation mechanisms of thermoacoustic oscillations in com­
bustors exhibit high complexity [12,13]. Under swirl combustion con­
ditions, thermoacoustic instabilities originate from strong coupling of 
multiple physical processes [14], including: hydrodynamic instabilities 
(vortex precession [15], vortex shedding and breakdown [16]), acoustic 
modal characteristics dictated by combustor geometry [17], acoustic 
wave reflection at walls [18], entropy wave generation triggered by heat 
release fluctuations [19].

thermoacoustic oscillations in swirl-stabilized premixed flames [20–23], 
fuel staging ratios critically influence flame structures and consequently

combustion stability. Wang [24] experimentally identified four com­
bustion modes during fuel staging variation at atmospheric pressure. 
Their analysis under unstable conditions revealed that partial premixing 
in primary combustion zones intensifies thermoacoustic instability, 
inducing significant flame structural transitions. Fu [25] studied a triple- 
staged lean combustor with strongly coupled premixed flames. At high 
power, limit-cycle oscillations arose more readily, and increasing SR 
shifted flame structures from M-type to V-type. At low power, flame 
topology remained unchanged with SR, but higher SR raised heat release 
and induced thermoacoustic instability. Zhang [26] observed four stable 
flame configurations in a stratified premixed swirl burner. The study 
revealed that high swirl intensity, ASR, and SR promoted large- 
amplitude limit cycles, identifying them as key drivers of strong pres­
sure and heat release fluctuations.
Conversely, physical confinement by combustor walls significantly 
impacts flame structures and instability. Han [27] linked flame macro­
structure (S, V, L) directly to thermoacoustic behavior. While stratified 
flames showed superior stability from their heat release phase, V and 
lifted flames, driven by flame-wall impingement, underwent large- 
amplitude oscillations at certain SR. Durox [28] conducted funda­
mental experiments on flames impinging flat plates, revealing self- 
excited instabilities when premixed flames anchored at burner edges 
struck adjacent baffles. Sudden flame area reduction upon impingement 
generated intense acoustic sources, establishing feedback loops. Huang 
[29] showed that during power ramping, flames flashed back from the 
CRZ to form compact configurations. The resultant pulsations drove self- 
sustained oscillations, with minor inlet temperature shifts near stability 
boundaries triggering abrupt amplitude changes.
Regarding the thermoacoustic oscillation issue in staged combustion 
chambers, scholars generally adopt active control [30] and passive 
control methods [31]. Although active control offers broader adapt­
ability and greater flexibility in practical applications, its high efficiency 
in control performance often relies on complex and advanced control 
algorithms [32]. In contrast, passive control is simpler to implement, 
such as adjusting fuel supply parameters during combustion [33], 
applying external physical fields [34,35], and introducing jet interven­
tion [36], among other methods. Sun [37] demonstrated that transverse 
staging jets reduced thermoacoustic amplitudes by 58 % and NOx 
emissions by 48.8 % in unstable premixed combustors. Higher jet mo­
mentum flux and fluid density enhanced suppression effectiveness, with 
flame radical distributions confirming attenuated heat release intensity. 
Behzadi [38] demonstrated that larger bluff body diameters in a swirl 
premixed combustor enhance flashback resistance and broaden the lean 
blowout limit at low power, an effect that weakens as power increases. 
Song [39] revealed that ramp constraints suppress instability by inhib­
iting vortex shedding, in contrast to sudden expansions, where vortex 
roll-up and flow disparities amplify oscillations. Hu [40] showed that co- 
directional swirling jets, especially with argon, most effectively sup­
pressed thermoacoustic oscillations and reduced NOx. Argon’s 
superiority was linked to a flame transition from conical to flat, and 
higher jet flow rates progressively damped oscillations toward stable 
combustion.
Despite these advances research on thermoacoustic instability in 
staged swirl combustion remains predominantly confined to low- 
temperature/pressure laboratory-scale burners. Critical gaps persist 
regarding fuel distribution effects on flame structures and stability 
during load ramping operations and full-load conditions in practical gas 
turbine combustors. To bridge this knowledge gap, this study conducts 
full-load high-pressure/high-temperature combustion experiments on 
an industrial-scale single-can combustor component, integrated with 
large eddy simulation (LES). This study identifies the impact of fuel 
staging ratio variations on flame structure and clarifies the mechanism 
of combustion thermoacoustic oscillations caused by the interaction 
between the flame and the wall. Ultimately, it explores the operational 
safety boundary that balances combustion stability and emission per­
formance, providing engineering and theoretical technical support for 
the design of actual small and medium-sized gas turbine combustors.
2. Experimental and numerical simulation methods
2.1. Actual gas turbine combustion chamber experimental platform
Fig. 1 depicts the experimental platform, comprising integrated air 
supply, fuel delivery, exhaust heat recuperation, electric heating, water- 
cooling, and measurement/control systems. The air system employs 
twelve serially-connected compressors to achieve high-temperature/ 
high-pressure operational conditions, while natural gas is supplied 
from stabilized sources (>5 MPa). Combustion air undergoes dual-stage 
preheating via electric resistance heaters and exhaust gas recuperation 
prior to entering the test section. Pressure transducers, thermocouples, 
and flowmeters are instrumented across diffusion, inner-premixed, and 
outer-premixed fuel circuits, with supplementary pressure/temperature 
probes embedded within the combustor casing. Seventy K-type ther­
mocouples are uniformly distributed along axial/radial directions on the 
flame tube outer wall for wall temperature monitoring. A motor-driven 
swing-rake mechanism positioned at the combustor outlet incorporates 
five temperature measurement points and one multi-port gas sampling 
probe. Starting from the lateral outlet edge, the rake executes 1◦angular 
displacements with 10 s dwell intervals for exhaust temperature/emis­
sions sampling upon reaching steady-state, completing full cross-section 
profiling through 180◦sector scanning.
As illustrated in Fig. 2, the radial-staged combustor test section 
features counterflow cooling where air enters through the combustor 
inlet, traverses the casing annulus, and cools the flame tube in reverse 
flow direction. At the head-end, air self-distributes into diffusion, outer 
premixed, and inner premixed paths without flow partitioning devices. 
The flame tube employs film-cooling technology, while the transition

2.3. Mesh generation and its independence verification
The computational domain replicates the experimental system at 1:1 
scale (Fig. 1). To ensure the accuracy of the computational model and 
grid, two validation schemes were selected. The first scheme involves 
comparing the experimentally measured pressure fluctuation signals 
with the calculated pressure oscillation values at monitoring points 
under different grid densities. As shown in Fig. 4, the pressure fluctua­
tions at the LES monitoring points, located at the same positions as the 
pressure fluctuation sensors, were compared with the experimental 
measurements. The grid was refined in the flame region with refinement 
sizes of 1 mm, 0.8 mm, and 0.6 mm, respectively. It was found that the 
LES could accurately capture the frequency near 445 Hz. As the grid 
density increased, the oscillation frequency and amplitude became 
closer to the experimental values. Balancing computational accuracy 
and cost, a grid consisting of 45,213,032 cells was selected for this study.
Instantaneous kinetic energy spectra at shear-layer monitoring 
points (Fig. 5) reveal successive breakdown of large-scale vortices 
through an energy cascade process where inertial forces transfer energy 
to smaller eddies. However, this process does not continue indefinitely.

When the scale becomes sufficiently small, molecular diffusion becomes 
dominant, and non-viscous energy ceases to transfer. The kinetic energy 
is then dissipated into thermal energy. Kolmogorov’s turbulence distri­
bution theory is the classical theory that describes this phenomenon 
[42]. According to this theory, the energy spectrum follows E(n) ∝ n−5/3. 
E(n) represents the kinetic energy of a unit mass of matter within a unit 
wavenumber range. The total energy of a unit mass needs to be inte­
grated with respect to the wavenumber to obtain E(n). For a vortex with 
a characteristic scale of r, the wavenumber n is defined as n = 2Π/r. If 
the grid scale reaches the inertial subdomain scale, the energy spectrum 
distribution of the vortex should follow the Kolmogorov turbulent dis­
tribution theory. The kinetic energy spectrum in Fig. 5 indicates that the 
attenuation of turbulent kinetic energy conforms to the 5/3 law of the 
inertial subdomain.

2.5. Model validation
To validate numerical model accuracy, time-averaged outlet tem­
peratures from large eddy simulations at 100 % load are compared 
against experimental data acquired via a swing-rake thermocouple as­
sembly linearly distributing five measurement points. The rake performs 
sector scanning across an 18◦arc at the combustor outlet, yielding 90 
discrete temperature measurements that are spatially interpolated to 
reconstruct the experimental temperature contour map. In Fig. 6a, the 
measured outlet distribution exhibits a characteristic C-shaped profile 
where casing air injection forms peripheral cooling zones to satisfy 
downstream turbine thermal constraints, while the central region 
maintains a thermal core. The angular interval (1◦) of the oscillating 
mechanism and the number of radial measurement points jointly 
determine the spatial resolution of the experimental cloud image. Any 
flow structures below this resolution cannot be captured by the exper­
iment and are smoothed out during the interpolation process. In 
contrast, large eddy simulation can resolve all vortex structures larger 
than the grid scale, providing higher effective resolution. The results of 
large eddy simulation are not perfect either. Their smoothness partly 
stems from model simplification, as the subgrid models used inevitably 
introduce numerical dissipation, which suppresses small-scale fluctua­
tions, thereby making the simulated results slightly smoother than the 
actual physical field. As shown in Fig. 6b, the maximum RTDF occurs at 
the 0 mm position, corresponding to the central arc region of the outlet 
cross-section, indicating the highest temperature non-uniformity at this 
location. The −30 mm and 30 mm positions represent the average 
temperature at the combustion chamber outlet edges, which is closer to 
the overall outlet average temperature, resulting in the lowest RTDF 
values at these points. During the test, the variation process of OTDF at 
the exhaust section was recorded across power levels from 0 to 100. At 
the 30 % power level, the OTDF reached its peak, approaching 0.7, 
indicating the most uneven temperature distribution at the exhaust 
section under this power condition. Diffusion combustion is character­
ized by high local fuel concentration and uneven mixing between high- 
temperature combustion gases and surrounding air, resulting in higher 
temperatures at the center of the combustor exit compared to the pe­
ripheral regions. With further increases in power level, the proportion of 
fuel in the premix path continues to rise, leading to a more uniform 
outlet temperature. Numerical results demonstrate close agreement with 
experimental results.
Fig. 7(a) validates cold-flow LES predictions by comparing experi­
mental pressure oscillations at 1125 Hz during non-reacting operation, 
where numerical monitoring point P14 successfully captures this char­
acteristic frequency attributed to Kelvin-Helmholtz instability (detailed 
in subsequent sections). The cold-flow pressure spectrum exhibits low 
overall amplitudes without dominant patterns in low-frequency bands. 
Fig. 7(b) further contrasts experimental and LES-derived pressure 
spectra under intense thermoacoustic oscillation conditions, specifically 
when corner recirculation zone (CRZ) ignition occurs. Experimental 
measurements reveal prominent 450 Hz pressure pulsations during CRZ 
ignition events, which the LES model accurately reproduces this phe­
nomenon. In the experiment, the combustor walls are subject to complex 
heat transfer, including convective cooling and radiative heat loss. These 
effects lower the local gas temperature and density near the walls, 
altering the local speed of sound and creating acoustic boundary layers 
that dissipate wave energy. In contrast, the LES model employs an 
idealized adiabatic wall boundary condition, which neglects these 
damping effects, leading to less attenuation of the pressure waves and 
consequently a higher predicted amplitude. This confirms the compu­
tational model’s capability to faithfully replicate flow dynamics and 
combustion-induced pressure fluctuations observed experimentally.

Pressure oscillations in fuel supply systems critically affect combus­
tion stability, with gas-phase systems being more susceptible to such 
fluctuations than liquid-fueled systems. These oscillations modulate fuel 
injection at the nozzle exit, leading to localized heat release fluctuations 
that can phase-lock with acoustic waves and trigger thermoacoustic 
instability. As shown in Fig. 10, when internal premixed fuel was 
introduced, the oscillation frequency of its fuel path (417 Hz) was close 
to the second-mode frequency of the combustion chamber flow path 
(380 Hz), and a frequency of 398 Hz was measured during testing. The 
first-mode frequency of the external premixing path is 527 Hz, near the 
third acoustic mode of the chamber cavity (590 Hz), which resulted in a 
543 Hz oscillation under 40 % load conditions.The instability origi­
nating from the fuel supply path serves as the source of these charac­

the chamber, they produce distinct frequencies during load transitions. 
These circuit-specific frequencies appear transiently upon fuel circuit 
activation but diminish as mass flow increases. Initial fuel-filling- 
induced resonances in the plenum do not cause severe combustion 
instability, and subsequent flow pressurization via increased mass flux 
enhances hydrodynamic stability, effectively suppressing fuel-side 
oscillations.

3.2. Analysis of the mechanism of combustion-induced thermoacoustic 
oscillations
During the experiment, the emergence of thermoacoustic oscillations 
was accompanied by a rapid temperature increase at the flame tube 
front face, indicating ignition stabilization within the corner recircula­
tion zone (CRZ). As illustrated in Fig. 13, as the load increased from 80 % 
to 100 %, the flame tube wall temperature exhibited pronounced 
instability. Notably, at section A, temperature fluctuated significantly 
near the 90 % load condition, suggesting that the CRZ was approaching 
its lean flammability limit, where flame anchoring becomes transient 
and locally intermittent. A further load increase induced a sharp 
temperature rise at section A to 1200 K, accompanied by clear pressure 
oscillations, which decayed upon reduction of the outer premixed fuel 
fraction. This temperature surpasses the maximum continuous service 
temperature for the flame tube’s nickel-based superalloy material 
(typically 1150 K for long-term application). Sustained operation at 
1200 K drastically accelerates creep deformation and oxidation/thermal 
fatigue damage, leading to a severe reduction in component lifespan. 
Time–frequency analysis of dynamic pressure during outer premixed 
fuel modulation (Fig. 14) reveals that oscillation frequency is repre­
sented on the ordinate, with colormap intensity reflecting pulsation 
amplitude. No significant oscillations occurred when the inner premixed 
fuel fraction remained below 86 %. Beyond this threshold, however, 
self-excited oscillations emerged and stabilized at 445 Hz, indicating 
coupling between heat release and a dominant acoustic mode. A hys­
teresis behavior was observed: once oscillations were triggered, 
decreasing the outer premixed fuel fraction to 85 % failed to suppress 
them; instead, a reduction below 83 % was required to fully quench the 
instability, suggesting a nonlinear transition between stable and unsta­
ble combustion states [10].
Under 100 % load conditions, large-eddy simulation results suc­
cessfully reproduced the ignition phenomenon observed in the corner 
recirculation zone during experiments, with physical signals monitored 
at 18 locations within the combustion region. The pressure signals at 
these locations exhibited periodic oscillations, with the 445 Hz oscilla­
tion signal being the most prominent, closely matching the experimental 
test signals. However, this frequency differed from the inherent acoustic 
frequency of the combustion chamber, indicating that thermoacoustic 
oscillations were triggered by combustion instability under the given 
combustion distribution and aerodynamic parameters. As shown in 
Fig. 15(a), although pressure and velocity fluctuations at the central air 
path outlet exhibited the dominant 445 Hz component, neither vorticity 
nor heat release rate varied significantly at this frequency. Moreover, the 
absence of phase coherence between pressure and heat release rate 
fluctuations suggests that this region was not a driving source of the 
thermoacoustic instability.

3.3. Methods for expanding the stable operation boundary
The mechanism of thermoacoustic oscillations, as analyzed previ­
ously, is attributed to flashback in the corner recirculation zone, and the 
proposed solution involves suppressing fixed-frequency heat release in 
this zone. Without modifying the combustor structure, optimizing the 
fuel staging ratio to prevent flashback in the corner recirculation zone is 
identified as the most effective approach. The ignition in the corner 
recirculation zone results from an excessively high methane concen­
tration, coupled with elevated temperatures and high methane content 
within the flashback vortex group. The methane concentration in the 
outer premixed path directly influences these factors, suggesting that 
reducing the fuel supply in the outer premixed path could effectively 
mitigate flashback in the corner recirculation zone. To investigate the 
flashback boundary in this zone, experiments were conducted 
comparing four operating conditions with outer premixed fuel ratios of 
91 %, 88.6 %, 86 %, and 82 %, with the fuel distribution for each 
condition detailed in the accompanying table. As shown in Table 2, cases 
1–3 involve progressively increasing the inner premixed fuel quantity, 
while Case 4 incorporates an increased proportion of diffusion fuel.
As shown in Fig. 19(a), the temperature variation at monitoring 
point P16 in the corner recirculation zone is presented, with an inlet air 
temperature of 734 K. Under normal operating conditions, the temper­
ature in the corner recirculation zone must not exceed 1000 K. During 
the process of gradually reducing the outer premixed fuel ratio, it was 
observed that the temperature in the corner recirculation zone signifi­
cantly decreases only when the outer premixed fuel proportion falls 
below 86 %, achieving a stable low temperature at 82 %. Fig. 19(b) 
depicts the temperature distribution at axial positions of 270 mm and 
300 mm, where the outer premixed path has a relatively high air pro­
portion. Consequently, reducing the outer premixed fuel quantity while 
increasing the diffusion and inner premixed fuel quantities leads to an 
increase in the peak combustion temperature. At an outer premixed fuel 
ratio of 91 %, the peak combustion temperature reaches 1600 K, 
resulting in low NOx emissions, though attention must be paid to 
flashback-induced oscillations in the corner recirculation zone. The axial 
regions between 400 mm–450 mm and 550 mm–600 mm correspond to 
the corner recirculation zone, where an outer premixed fuel ratio of 82 
% ensures no ignition occurs in this zone. At this condition, the peak 
combustion temperature, located at the center of the flame tube, reaches 
1900 K, with no occurrence of combustion-induced thermoacoustic 
oscillations.
As shown in Fig. 20, the temporal variation of OH concentration 
under different fuel staging ratios is presented. In Case 1, a pronounced 
flashback phenomenon is observed in the corner recirculation zone, with 
the flashback frequency aligning with that of the thermoacoustic oscil­
lations. Additionally, due to the relatively low inner premixed fuel 
quantity, the flame root is positioned at a certain distance from the 
central bluff body, making it susceptible to disturbances from pressure 
fluctuations within the combustor. In Case 2, the flame root exhibits 
relative stability, yet flashback persists in the corner recirculation zone. 
In Case 3, the inner premixed fuel quantity is increased to the safety 
boundary, resulting in flame attachment to the bluff body, and experi­
mental observations indicate that when the inner premixed fuel exceeds 
14 %, ablation occurs on the end face of the central bluff body. In Case 4, 
an increased diffusion fuel quantity leads to elevated OH concentrations 
near the diffusion path outlet, with the intense combustion region being 
more concentrated compared to other conditions, and no flashback is 
observed in the corner recirculation zone. Balancing the flashback in the 
corner recirculation zone and the ablation of the central bluff body, Case 
4 demonstrates the potential to be the optimal operating condition.

under high-temperature and high-pressure conditions, the fraction of

the emission criteria were met. Further increasing the outer premixed 
fuel ratio resulted in additional reduction of NOx, but the overall tem­

The stability limit was defined based on a pressure fluctuation amplitude 
threshold set at 0.5 % of the operating pressure; exceeding this value was 
considered indicative of unstable combustion. When the outer premixed 
fuel fraction reached 88.2 %, the pressure oscillations exceeded the limit 
before CO emissions became unacceptable. Thus, for this radially staged 
combustor, the inner premixed fuel should be limited to 12 %, and the 
outer premixed fuel ratio should be maintained within 81.8–88.2 % to 
ensure both pollutant emissions and pressure oscillations remain within 
acceptable ranges.
4. Conclusion
This study investigates the combustion stability of a radially staged 
single-can combustor under variable operating conditions through high- 
temperature and high-pressure experiments. Acoustic modal analysis 
and large eddy simulation (LES) were employed to identify the origins of 
pressure oscillations across different frequency bands and to clarify the 
mechanism by which flashback in the corner recirculation zone (CRZ) 
triggers thermoacoustic instability. Ultimately, a strategy for extending 
the stable operational range is proposed. The key findings are summa­
rized as follows:

492 Hz, and 543 Hz were captured. Acoustic modal analysis revealed 
that these frequencies correspond to the natural acoustic modes of
