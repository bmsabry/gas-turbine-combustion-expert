Gasification
Different processes produce gases of differing calorific values
 Medium CV
15 - 37 MJ/Nm3
(pyrolysis and indirectly 
heated gasifiers)
 Low CV
7 - 15 MJ/Nm3
(oxygen blown and
indirectly heated gasifiers)
 Very low CV
3 - 7 MJ/Nm3
(air blown gasification)
This affects turbine combustor configuration, turbine performance 
and overall plant efficiencies (and unit availability)
Choice of gasification system depends on feedstock and application
Choice between atmospheric and pressurized systems can affect 
plant NOx emissions

Gasification - Medium CV Atmospheric Processes
Typically :
 Indirectly Heated Gasifier
(e.g. FERCO)
 Pyrolysis Kiln
(e.g. Techtrade, JND, GEM)
Advantages
 Simplicity of concept
 Removal of ammonia etc. pre-turbine, so low NOx 
and ‘clean’ gas to GT
 Reduced gas compression power compared to low CV processes
Disadvantages
 High tars from pyrolysis
 Lower tars from indirect gasification, but still requirement for cracking
 Complex gas cleaning systems, waste water disposal 
 Parasitic load of gas compressor, slightly reduced GT output & 
efficiency

Fuel Quality
 Fuel quality must be recognised when sourcing fuels
 Equally applicable to pipeline fuels as well as to these alternative fuels
 The following notes provide some of the issues and concerns associated with 
various aspects of fuel quality.  This subject is a major one in its own right and 
should be treated as such when reviewing alternative fuels
 Fuel is not the only source of contamination, and all fluids entering the gas 
turbine must be equally assessed.  Aspects of air, fuel, lube oil, water … must 
be equally be considered.

Gas Fuels
 The quality and composition of gaseous fuels can impact gas turbine operation, 
including combustor and hot gas path life.
 Changes in quality of gas fuels can lead to operational difficulties such as 
stability or combustion dynamics under both steady state and transient conditions.
 For example a moving from pipeline quality gas fuels to these ‘alternative’ may 
require additional processing and pre-treatment to make them suitable for use in a 
gas turbine application.  Also, combustion control parameter settings  will have to 
change along with changes to the combustion hardware.
 Recognition of fuel species is necessary in terms of additional monitoring 
equipment, such as that associated with Hydrogen fuels.

Gas Fuels 
Contamination Issues
Operational concern
Effect
Solids in gas fuel
¾ Scale, rust, sand, dirt, weld splatter, grit blast
¾ From old poorly maintained pipe system
¾ From new or modified fuel system
¾ Wear of fuel system component
¾ Valve failure to seat increased leakage
¾ Corrosion and wear of fuel injector
¾ Erosion of fuel/combustion components
¾ Build up of debris in gas passageways - impaired operation
Heavy Hydrocarbons as liquids
¾ Incorrect process control
¾ Not present in pipeline quality gases
¾ Incorrect temperature for fuel dew point 
¾ Over fuelling (uncontrolled)
¾ Can drop out in fuel system, resulting in poor fuel control
¾ Carried in combustion resulting in uncontrolled combustion - 
explosions, flashback
¾ Abnormal distribution and localised hot gas path component 
damage
¾ Coking of fuel burner passages and mal distribution
¾ Abnormal temperature spread, as seen in exhaust / interduct 
thermocouples
¾ Adverse impact on performance and emission targets
Water in gas fuel
¾ Affinity of other contaminant - eg sodium, calcium etc
¾ Acid formation
¾ Formation of Hydrates
¾ At low temperature can freeze resulting in pipe 
blockage and reduced gas flow
¾ ingestion of liquids into combustion with 
consequential damage
¾ Ice & Hydrates can cause valve failure 
¾ Corrosion of pipe system and valve
¾ Corrosion of hot turbine components
¾ Poor combustion operation, including loss of flame
¾ Unstable operation

Gas Fuels 
Contamination Issues
Operational concern
Effect
Gas Fuels containing Hydrogen Sulphide H2S
¾ Poisonous even in small quantities
¾ Flammable
¾ Acidic when water present
¾ Corrosive
¾ Harmful to personnel
¾ Can result in hot gas component erosion
¾ Sulphidation attack on some materials
¾ Increased component attack in the presence of other 
contaminants, such as Sodium
Gas Fuels containing Carbon Dioxide, CO2
¾ Acidic when water present
¾ Lowers effective heating value of fuel
¾ Reduced output for same volume input (lower heating value)
¾ Increased supply pressure
¾ Combustor passage size increase
Gas fuels containing Hydrogen, H2
¾ Increased flammability
¾ Explosive
¾ Leakage of pipe work - consider regulations - eg Group 2C 
approval
¾ Explosive
¾ System design - flange joints and seals - embrittlement
Gas fuels containing Carbon Monoxide, CO
¾ Poisonous
¾ Exacerbates flame velocity, especially if H2 present
¾ Flash back
¾ Harmful to personnel
¾ Flashback results in damage to combustion components

Summary
Siemens (and as part of Alstom beforehand) have developed alternative fuels 
capabilities.
Access made to UK government funding, and with several external 
bodies/agencies in collaborative projects
Many of the technical challenges have been met and overcome, however, there 
are still many more to be met. (Some associated with clean-up to achieve GT 
specifications for fuel quality have yet to be met)
One fundamental problem still exists and that is market acceptance for a GT 
based BIGCC solution.  Market study completed serves to demonstrate this.
This leaves the other types of opportunities to be considered, such as using waste 
gases – high inert content and process gases such as COG and H2 .  Many such 
projects have shown to offer both environmental as well as economic benefits.
New opportunities / projects using high inert containing methane fuels such as 
Landfill gas or gas derived from anaerobic digestion seem to be a growing trend.

Questions to consider - SynGas
Does the audience think the generation of syngas through gasification or pyrolysis 
processes is sufficiently mature and free of operational problems to make a 
BIGCC solution a viable or tenable application.
Should such projects be aimed at the gas turbine market, or should such 
processes as “fast pyrolisis” be used to provide liquid fuels suitable for 
transportation use
Should the uses of gases such as landfill gas be classed as renewable fuels, or 
should more effort be made in re-cycling thus minimising the amount of rubbish 
sent to landfill.  As a supplementary question if recycling is maximised and there 
is still waste left should this go to landfill or should it be offered as a feedstock for 
incineration (either simple burning via Fluidised Bed process or via pyrolysis)
