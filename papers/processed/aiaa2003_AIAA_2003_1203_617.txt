ABSTRACT
CFD 
simulations 
of 
gas 
turbine 
combustors, 
typically, do not include the inner and outer passages 
of the diffuser in the models for design analyses.  
This necessitates the specification of boundary 
conditions at the exit of the dilution holes into the 
combustor domain.  This paper explores an approach 
for specifying this boundary condition that is 
applicable for combustor operating conditions at 
different power levels.  First, the predictions for 
discharge coefficient are validated against the NACA 
benchmark data for different circular hole sizes and 
wall thickness.  The agreement with data is within 
5%.  For this configuration, simulations are also 
performed to understand the effects of cross-flow on 
discharge coefficient and jet angle.  Results indicate 
that these parameters are not very sensitive to the 
crossflow momentum ratio.  Simulations are also 
performed for a CFM-56 swirl cup package in a 3-
inch square flame tube.  To understand the flow 
characteristics at dilution hole, the configuration 
included an outer flow passage.  Results for this case 
indicate that distributions of flow variables at the 
dilution holes are two-dimensional and complicated 
due to existence of reverse flow regions.  The 
swirling flow in the flame tube also has an impact on 
the dilution jet as the flow is subsonic.  Since the 
flow variables distributions do not follow a pattern 
amenable for developing scaling rules, accurate 
modeling of dilution flow requires integrated 
diffuser-combustor analysis.  
INTRODUCTION
Gas 
turbine 
combustion 
systems 
are 
being 
designed/optimized through the aid of CFD tools1-3.  
Though modern computers afford higher fidelity in 
CFD models in terms of resolving finer geometric 
and flow features, integrated modeling of diffuser-
combustor system is still expensive for design 
analyses.  Combustor modeling, then, requires 
specification of inlet conditions for swirlers, dilution 
and cooling hole boundaries.  In rich-dome 
combustors, the penetration and mixing of primary 
and secondary dilution flows are critical to the 
predictions of emissions and pattern factors.  The 
flow around the dilution jet is quite complex as it 
interacts with the dome swirling jet and the central 
recirculation bubble.  The heat release often reaches a 
maximum just upstream of the primary dilution jets, 
resulting in high density gradients around the jets.  
Several studies on mixing of dilution jets have been 
reported4-6 due to its importance for RQL concept to 
achieve 
low 
NOx 
emissions 
in 
rich-dome 
combustors.  The equivalence ratio of primary 
reaction zone including the primary dilution air is 
adjusted to achieve complete combustion of fuel/air 
mixture.  The dilution flow rates are also controlled 
to achieve a desired exit temperature profile.  
For a typical combustor mesh with less than half a 
million nodes, the dilution holes are meshed with 
roughly 4X4 cells.  The current practice is to assume 
a plug flow boundary condition over the effective 
diameter of the hole and the jet angle interpolated 
from data10.  For this, the discharge coefficient is 
needed 
apriori 
and 
it 
is 
interpolated 
from 
experimental data.  Since the dilution holes are on a 
curved surface and the dilution jets enter the 
combustor domain at an angle, calculation of 
boundary conditions becomes complex even for the 
plug flow assumption.  For specifying a profile 
condition, at least a 10X10 mesh would be needed.  
This could be achieved by refining the coarse mesh 
locally in the dilution hole region.  As will be shown 
later, the flow through the dilution hole is not 
symmetric about the center of the hole.  Hence the 
boundary condition will not be a radial profile, but a 
two-dimensional mapping on a curved surface.  This 
paper explores a methodology for specifying the 
dilution hole inlet conditions that is better than the 
current plug flow assumption.  This problem has not 
been addressed in the literature. 
The critical parameters in the flow through dilution 
hole problem are: pressure loss parameter (K) 
through the hole, hole size, wall thickness, boundary 
layer thickness, and cross-flow momentum ratio.  The 
pressure loss parameter is defined as

APPROACH
Fluent CFD code9 is used to perform all the 
simulations presented in this paper.  A sequential 
pressure-based algorithm is selected to solve the 
Favre averaged Navier-Stokes equations.  Since the 
jet velocities would be high for high pressure ratio 
parameter cases, the compressibility effects are 
included in the model.  Though Fluent offers several 
turbulence models, earlier studies have indicated that 
standard and realizable k-ε models provided the best 
results for the swirling and mixing flows.  Compared 
to the standard k-ε model, the realizable k-ε model 
converges faster and hence it is selected for all the 
calculations. 
VALIDATION
In order to validate the dilution hole effective areas, 
the 
Fluent 
predictions 
are 
validated 
against 
benchmark data of Dittrich and Graves7.  In this 
NACA experimental study, the discharge coefficients 
for circular holes were measured for various flow 
parameters.  The parameters considered were hole 
diameter, wall thickness at the hole, boundary layer 
thickness, and pressure ratio across the hole.  The 
measurements showed that the corrected discharge 
coefficient correlated well with the pressure ratio 
parameter.  The effects of boundary layer thickness 
and wall thickness were found to be small.
The computational domain for this case extended 12 
diameters upstream of the hole and 5 diameters 
downstream of the hole.  The height of the duct is 
taken to be 5 diameters to minimize any effect of the 
boundary layer on the duct wall above the hole.  The 
plenum below the hole is made very large in order to 
prevent any influence of boundary condition on the 
flow near the hole.  A total pressure boundary 
condition is applied at duct inlet.  The total pressure 
can be calculated for a given value of pressure 
parameter (K).  A static pressure condition is 
imposed at the free boundaries of the plenum below 
the hole as well as at the exit of the duct.      
Grid Independence Study
First a grid sensitivity study has been carried out to 
select the optimum mesh size for parametric 
calculations.  A hexahedral baseline mesh with about 
600,000 cells was generated.  The computational 
cells are clustered near the walls and inside the hole.  
Four cells were used to resolve the wall thickness of 
the hole.  Two levels of adaptive mesh refinements 
were performed to generate additional meshes with 
velocity gradient as the adaptation criteria.  In this 
study the value of K was taken as 10, the liner wall 
thickness and dilution hole diameter were taken as 
0.04 and 0.75 inches, respectively.  The computed 
mass flow rates through the dilution hole using the 
three levels of grids are shown in Table 1.  The 
variation in mass flow rates is within 1%.  For further 
study, depending upon the hole diameter, a typical 
baseline mesh size of 600,000 to 750,000 cells was 
used.

Effect of Crossflow
In order to study the effect of cross flow on jet exit 
angle and discharge coefficient, for a constant 
pressure ratio parameter (K = 20), three different 
values of cross flow momentum ratios (defined as 
ratio of jet momentum to crossflow momentum) are 
taken i.e. 0.1, 0.2 and 0.3.  For these cases, dilution 
hole diameter is taken as 0.125 inches. The wall 
thickness has also been varied between 0.04 inch and 
0.1 inch.  In table 4 the first row corresponds to a 
case with no cross flow, and it has been included for 
the sake of comparison.  It is noted that the discharge 
coefficient is almost independent of cross flow.  The 
jet angle, however, decreases for low momentum 
ratios and remains constant for higher momentum 
ratios.  The crossflow sets up a streamwise pressure 
gradient as it flows around the dilution jet.  This 
causes additional deflection of the jet.  It is clear that 
the effect of cross flow on jet angle is less prominent 
for the case of 0.1 inch wall thickness.

Effect of wall thickness and hole diameter
Present work has been extended to study the effect of 
liner wall thickness on discharge coefficient and jet 
angle.  Two different values of wall thickness were 
taken i.e. 0.04 and 0.1 inch.  For each wall thickness 
the hole diameter has been varied: 0.125, 0.25, 0.75 
and 1.5inch.  During this study the value of K was 
kept constant at 20 and the results are summarized in 
Table 5.  It can be seen from this table that as wall 
thickness increases, discharge coefficient as well as 
jet angle increases.  This is because increasing the 
wall thickness enables more pressure recovery after 
the flow makes the turn.  It can also be noted that the 
increment in the discharge coefficient as well as jet 
angle decreases with respect to the hole diameter.  
Therefore, there should be an optimum wall thickness 
to diameter ratio for which maximum discharge 
coefficient and jet angle can be obtained.

Flow Distribution at Dilution Hole Exit 
In order to explore the possibility of scaling the 
boundary conditions, the flow through the dilution 
hole has been analyzed.  The distributions of 
pressure, velocity magnitude and turbulent kinetic 
energy are shown in figure 1 for the cases of J = 0.0 
and J = 0.3.  Figure 2 shows the velocity distribution 
on a cross-sectional plane.  As can be seen, flow is 
drawn from downstream of the dilution hole causing 
a reverse flow in the duct.  This reverse flow 
becomes stronger as the pressure drop through the 
hole increases.  Because of the high shear caused by 
this reverse flow, the turbulent kinetic energy is high 
in that region.  Since the flow is unable to make a 
sharp 90-degree turn, there is also a reverse flow 
inside the hole for high pressure drops.  The velocity 
magnitude peak on the exit plane occurs downstream 
of the geometric center of the hole.  As shown in 
figure 3, the offset between the location of peak

Based on figures 3 and 4, several attempts were made 
to develop an equation for flow distribution at 
dilution hole exit.  The independent variables in this

DILUTION JET MIXING
Holdeman presented a review5 of dilution jet mixing 
studies under subsonic cold and hot crossflow 
conditions.  These studies have indicated that jet 
penetration and mixing were mainly influenced by 
momentum flux ratio, hole size, and spacing.  To 
understand the mixing characteristics of the CFM-56 
combustor 
dilution 
jets, 
a 
nonreacting 
flow 
simulation was performed for a simpler configuration 
of a CFM-56 swirl cup in a 3-inch square flame tube.  
This configuration corresponds to an experimental 
setup in which cold and reacting flow measurements 
were obtained11,12.  Outer diffuser passage is modeled 
as a square duct around the flame tube.  The 
computational domain for this case is shown in figure 
6.  A tetrahedral mesh with about 400,000 cells was 
generated for this model.  The operating conditions 
for this study correspond to the maximum power 
condition of the CFM-56 combustor.  The primary 
jets and secondary swirler inlet conditions were 
obtained from through-the-vane (TTV) calculations13.  
A uniform velocity condition was specified at the 
duct inlet.  A fixed pressure condition was imposed at 
both the flame tube and duct outlets.  The duct outlet 
static pressure was adjusted to get the correct amount 
of air flow through the dilution hole.

confined between the dilution and secondary swirler

is asymmetric about the duct streamwise direction

To understand the extent of jet mixing, a scalar 
transport equation was solved.  An additional 
equation for scalar fluctuations due to turbulence was 
also solved.  The value of the scalar was set to unity 
at the duct inlet and set to zero at all other inlets.  
Earlier studies2,14 have indicated that k-ε turbulence 
models underpredict scalar mixing.  Reacting flow 
simulations performed for different combustors 
showed that combustor exit temperature profile 
predictions were in agreement with data when 
Schmidt number for scalar equation was set to 0.3.  
Hence this value is used in the results shown here.  
Figure 9 shows the scalar distribution indicating the 
penetration and mixing of the dilution jet.  The 
maximum value of scalar in the bubble is about 0.5.  
The dilution air is observed to reach up to the venturi 
exit plane upstream of the jet.  The scalar variance 
shown in figure 10 indicates large fluctuations in 
scalar concentration on the upstream side of the jet.  
The air flow from the primary jets and secondary 
swirler is squeezed between the dilution jet and the 
central recirculation bubble, creating high shear and 
turbulence kinetic energy (see figure 11) around the 
jet.  This enhances mixing of dilution air into the 
bubble.  The turbulent kinetic energy is observed to 
be maximum at the eye of the recirculating bubble.

CONCLUSIONS
In this paper, an attempt was made to develop a 
methodology for specifying the dilution hole inlet 
conditions that would be applicable for a wide range 
of combustor operating conditions.  As a first step, 
the discharge coefficient predictions for various 
pressure parameters were validated with benchmark 
NACA data.  The flow distribution for circular 
dilution holes was found to be complex due to 
existence of two recirculation regions in the 
proximity of the dilution hole.  At high pressure ratio 
parameters, flow was drawn from downstream of 
hole in the duct creating high shear regions.  For 
noncircular dilution holes, the flow pattern was even 
more complex.  Since the jet Mach number is 
subsonic, the swirling flow within the combustor also 
has an impact on the dilution hole flow distribution.  
Attempts to develop a two-parameter mapping profile 
have failed.  It is concluded that the only way to 
capture the details of the flow through the dilution 
holes is to perform integrated diffuser-combustor 
analyses.  Such analyses have already been 
demonstrated2,3,14.  The integrated analysis, however,

The dilution jet mixing characteristics has been 
studied for the simpler configuration of a square 
flame tube.  The curvature effect of annular 
combustors, hence, has not been modeled.  Also, the 
effect of multiple holes has not been included in the 
present analysis.  The results presented here indicate 
a significant impact of the crossflow that is swirling.  
In a reacting flow, the density gradients may also 
have an impact on the mixing patterns.  Due to these 
reasons, the dilution jet mixing studies should be 
carried out for the annular combustor configurations 
with combustion model turned on.  These studies are 
currently being pursued and the results will be 
published in the near future.
