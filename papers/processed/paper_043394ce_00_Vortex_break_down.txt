1. Introduction
1.1. General introduction
Swirling ¯ows are observed in natural ¯ows, such as
tornadoes and typhoons, and have been widely used, for
many years, in technical applications, such as aeronautics,
heat exchange, spray drying, separation, combustion, etc.
Their
importance
and
complexity
have
preoccupied
research investigations for decades.
In combustion systems, such as in gas turbine engines,
diesel engines, industrial burners, and boilers, swirling ¯ows
were originally used to improve and control the mixing rate
between fuel and oxidant streams in order to achieve ¯ame
geometries and heat release rates appropriate to the particu-
lar process application [1]. At suf®cient degree of swirl, an
internal recirculation zone is generated, which allows high
rate of heat release, as products of combustion are recircu-
lated and ignite the incoming fuel/oxidant streams. This
provides a stable, compact ¯ame [2], with a good per-
formance for dif®cult carboneous materials and poor quality
gases [3].
In aeronautics, leading-edge vortices shed from a delta
wing induce a velocity ®eld that results in increased lift and
stability of the wing. However, under certain conditions
related to the angle of attack of the wing, these vortices
can undergo a sudden and drastic change in structure,
which can adversely alter the aerodynamic characteristics
of the wing. A similar bursting phenomena has been
observed for trailing wing-tip vortices, which is, this time,
desirable as these vortices represent a hazard to smaller
aircraft in areas of dense air traf®c [4]. The ability to control
these vortical structures is an important and active area of
research.
One important feature of swirling ¯ows was put forward
by Ranque [5] who discovered the ability of vortex tubes to
separate compressed air into hot and cold air streams,
located near the periphery and the tube centreline, re-
spectively. This has resulted in a large number of engineering
applications utilising this ¯ow behaviour, such as separation
of gases of different molecular weight, Ranque±Hilsch
vortex tube for a refrigeration scheme, cyclone separation,
gas turbine cyclone, combustion chamber, and spray dryers.
Despite its simplicity, the mechanism of the Ranque±Hilsh
effect has been a matter of long-standing dispute.
Swirl, de®ned as the ratio of the axial ¯ux of momentum
to the axial ¯ux of axial momentum times the equivalent exit
radius, has been shown to have large-scale effects on ¯ow
®elds [6]. Introducing swirl in turbulent jets causes an
increase in jet growth, rate of entrainment and rate of
decay of the jet. These effects increase with increasing
level of swirl.

A number of workers, such as Harvey [8] and Sarpkaya
[10], have shown that the simplest ®rst breakdown of a
vortex takes the form of an axisymmetric bubble of circulat-
ing ¯uid. Initially this occurs several exit diameters down-
stream of the ¯ow exit. The ¯ow then reestablishes itself
before ®nally breaking down into a spiral form, which is
again symmetrical about the central axis. Sarpkaya [10] also
showed that this ®rst stage of breakdown of a vortex may be
helical in nature, forming a double helix. Further work by
Faler and Leibovich [17] showed that the vortex breakdown
can in fact occur and exhibit a total of six forms, and was
primarily dependent on the Reynolds number. In nearly all
cases these forms were considered to be of either a bubble
form or spiral form. The recirulation zone is therefore
analogous to the bubble form.
1.3. Precessing vortex core
There is evidence to show that the vortex breakdown can
often be not only asymmetric, but also highly time depen-
dent [18±22]. This is the result of the forced vortex region of
the ¯ow becoming unstable and starting to precess about the
axis of symmetry thus forming the so called precessing
vortex core or PVC [20,21]. Syred and BeeÂr [20,21] found
that the PVC lies on the boundary of the reverse ¯ow zone
between the zero velocity and zero streamline. Subsequent
work has shown that the presence of a PVC distorts the
reverse ¯ow zone into a `kidney shape' which can lag
behind the precessional centre of the vortex by upto 1808
[23]. Lucca-Negro [24] showed that the reverse ¯ow zone
®nally becomes shaped by the PVC forming a helical
bubble.
The formation of a vortex breakdown may excite and/or
couple with acoustic modes of a system creating pressure
oscillations which may alter the ¯uid dynamic behaviour of
the system [25,26]. Syred and BeeÂr [21] highlighted, and
Kurosaka [27] con®rmed, the presence of a precessing
vortex core (PVC) in a Ranque±Hilsh tube and concluded
that this phenomenon must contribute substantially to the
energy separation process. Similarly, the existence of PVC
has also been observed within combustors and dust
separation/classi®cation systems. While studies indicate
that there maybe a potential bene®t in its presence in
combustion systems, this has been found to be detrimental
in cyclone dust separators [22].
Vonnegut [18] was probably the ®rst to report, without
any attempt to explain it, on a time-dependent periodic
motion appearing in a vortex whistle: he detected a tone
whose frequency was almost directly proportional to the
¯ow rate, for both air and water. In 1957, Peckham and
Atkinson [111] observed the breakdown of leading-edge
vortices above a delta wing. Lambourne and Bryer [28]
also observed a spiral structure on a delta wing and also
found that the frequency increased with increasing ¯ow
rate. These ®rst observations launched many investigations
and re¯ections.
Despite the extensive experimental, numerical and
theoretical research undertaken over several decades, the
phenomenon of vortex breakdown remains controversial
and no generally accepted explanation has emerged.
Comprehensive reviews covering the experimental and
theoretical aspects and presenting the progress made in
understanding the phenomenon and predicting its occur-
rence have already been given by Hall [29], Leibovich
[7,15], Stuart [30], Escudier [16] and Althaus et al. [31].
1.4. Review aims
The aims of this review are to present a recent `guide' to
the literature, and to emphasise and summarise the signi®-
cant features of vortex breakdown, the different explana-
tions and also the criticisms or remarks emerging. With
the reviews mentioned above as background, it is not
necessary to elaborate in great depth on all the contributions,
except maybe for the more recent ones, but rather to
summarise the information and highlight the salient
features.
Also, due to the great number of publications, an exten-
sive review covering all aspects of vortex breakdown cannot
be expected. This work mainly addresses vortex breakdown
in tubes; other facets, like the breakdown phenomenon over
delta wings and in closed containers with a rotating endwall,
are mentioned. More detail and wider information can be
found in the literature [16,28,31±33].
This paper considers the studies undertaken into vortex
breakdown under three main sections, each of them consid-
ering one aspect of the phenomenon: experimental, numer-
ical and ®nally theoretical. This layout is an attempt to
structure, as much as possible, all the information contained
in the numerous publications on the subject. Obviously,
there are many connections between the different parts
and an effort has been made to highlight these as often as
possible.

2.7. Overview of the experimental investigations
Different types of vortex breakdown have been observed
for different combinations of Reynolds number Retube and
circulation number (e.g. [10,17]). It has to be remembered
that the dependence on Retube has to be regarded as a vor-
ticity effect, not a viscosity effect.
All breakdown forms, including the bubble breakdown,
are periodic and asymmetric in azimuth. The approach ¯ow
usually presents a jet-like axial velocity pro®le, whereas the
¯ow downstream of the breakdown is wake-like and (more)
turbulent with strong periodic oscillations. This is remi-
niscent of the wake of a solid body revolution.
At high Retube, the only forms observed are the bubble and
the spiral. The bubble mode of disruption of the vortex core
is characterised by a stagnation point on the swirl axis,
followed by a bubble of recirculating ¯uid. The spiral break-
down is also characterised by a rapid deceleration of the
¯ow causing stagnation and, in addition, by an abrupt kink
followed by a spiral twisting of the ¯ow. The stagnation
point appears to be off-centred. The winding of the spiral
has been observed to occur in the sense of rotation of the
outer ¯ow (e.g. [10,11,17]) or opposite to it [13,38,39]. No
de®nite explanation has been provided so far to explain this
discrepancy. The spiral rotates as a structure in the sense of
the base ¯ow. Another discrepancy between experiments
concerns the core size downstream of breakdown: the vortex
core either appears expanded and weaker, the expansion
being signi®cantly larger for the bubble form than for the
spiral [15,37] or the core radius is reduced and the axial
vorticity intensi®ed [59].
In the progression of forms as the swirl is increased, spiral
breakdown always occurs before bubble breakdown. The
spiral form also appears to be located further downstream
than that of the bubble form. Depending on the combination
of Retube and circulation, the bubble may evolve from a
spiral, or directly from an axisymmetric swelling of the
vortex core. There are ranges of ¯ow settings for which
spiral and bubble can exist and transform spontaneously
into each other.
From Refs. [10,11,29,46], it appears that the occurrence
of the breakdown and its location when it does occur
depends, among other things, on the magnitude of the
¯ow and the swirl, the external pressure gradient, the degree
of divergence of the ¯ow, and the initial upstream con-
ditions. It is also strongly dependent upon the con®nements
placed upon it.
The frequency and the amplitude of the unsteady wall
pressure have been shown to increase rapidly with increasing
swirl.
Quantitative measurements described the interior of the
bubble breakdown as a two-celled structure with positive
axial velocity near the centreline or as a single tilted ring-
like structure with an overall ¯ow reversal near the centre-
line. The bubble seems to exchange ¯uid continuously with
the outer ¯ow through its downstream end.
Ideas, which are discussed in more detail later, also diverge
regarding the fact that bubble and spiral forms of breakdown
are different aspects of a same phenomenon or are two
distinct phenomena.

3.2. Quasi-cylindrical approximation
Upstream of the breakdown, the axial gradients are small
compared with the radial gradients, and the stream surfaces
are approximately cylindrical if the vortex is assumed to be
slender, i.e. if the ratio of the core radius to the breakdown
length is much smaller than unity. Quasi-cylindrical equa-
tions, discussed in Ref. [43], can then be used to calculate
the development of the ¯ow: if the velocity distributions are
given at some initial upstream location, the solution can be
computed by proceeding step by step axially downstream,
similarly the calculation of two-dimensional boundary
layers. The idea is that the development of large axial gradi-
ents associated with vortex breakdown violates the assump-
tions of the quasi-cylindrical approximation (QCA), in the
same way the approach to separation violates the boundary-
layer approximation, so that the failure of the QCA corre-
sponds to vortex breakdown. As Reynolds numbers in most
experiments are suf®ciently large to insure that viscous
forces are negligible, viscosity is unlikely to cause the
approximation to fail.
Different versions have been proposed, applying different
assumptions, mainly on velocity pro®les of the approaching
¯ow: [42,60±62]. Usually, the ¯ow has been assumed to be
steady, laminar, incompressible, and axisymmetric.

Hall [42], using the QCA, provided an analogy between the
initiation of vortex breakdown with that of the development
of a separated region for a boundary layer subjected to an
adverse pressure gradient. Hall backed this analogy by

of divergence of the tube (or positive pressure gradient) in
just the same way as vortex breakdown does.
Krause [44] also demonstrated the crucial in¯uence of the
pressure ®eld on the breakdown length of slender vortices.
He also got some insight into the process of vortex break-
down as he compared computational results for inviscid and
viscous ¯ows. With inviscid ¯ows, an externally imposed
adverse pressure gradient was necessary for initiating break-

any for viscous ¯ows; however, if an external pressure

length was largely reduced. From these numerical exper-
iments, it appears that the pressure ®eld indeed dominates
the breakdown mechanism of slender vortices, but the
viscous forces also play a role in initiating the widening
of the core.
While this numerical method seems to be able to predict
the occurrence and position of breakdown, it is, however,
limited as it cannot account for upstream in¯uences and
cannot describe the internal structure of the breakdown
nor the ¯ow downstream of it. A more complete simulation
of vortex breakdown requires the full Navier±Stokes
equations.

4. Theories and explanations for vortex breakdown
4.1. Introduction
Different theoretical goals have been pursued: earlier
analyses tried to characterise the conditions under which
breakdown is likely to occur, predict its location and
establish criteria, while most of the recent work is
aimed at describing physically the whole process and
predicting the internal structure. Several theories have
been proposed for vortex breakdown, which can roughly
be categorised according to the following underlying
ideas [16,29,30]:
1. The phenomenon is associated with the concept of
critical state or, more generally, with wave phenomena
[51,61,72,83,84].
2. The phenomenon is analogous to boundary layer
separation or ¯ow stagnation [42,44,60].
3. The phenomenon is a consequence of hydrodynamic
instability [85,86] (cited in Refs. [16,87±89]).
Some other approaches combine several concepts and
extend the previous ideas [30,55,90±93,112] or develop
new arguments [32].
4.2. Wave phenomena
Independently, Squire [72] and Benjamin [51,83,84]
developed theories for an axisymmetric, inviscid, and steady
vortex. They both suggested the existence of a `critical state'
which separates the sub from the supercritical ¯ow state.
In a subcritical ¯ow, disturbances can propagate upstream
and downstream and standing waves are supported, whereas
in a supercritical ¯ow, only downstream propagation is
possible.
Although Squire and Benjamin shared the same de®nition
for the critical state, their interpretations, as pointed out by
Hall [29], were different: Squire understood breakdown as
an accumulation of upstream travelling disturbances at the
locus of the critical state (like a shock wave), whereas
Benjamin thought of breakdown as an abrupt change, a
transition with ®nite amplitude, between two conjugate
¯ow states (like a hydraulic jump in an open-channel
¯ow). Stuart [30] highlighted the analogy between the char-
acteristic parameters of these two different phenomena: both

4.3. Flow stagnation
One feature, generally accepted as an essential aspect of
vortex breakdown, is the stagnation of the ¯ow at or near the
vortex axis. On the basis of this characteristic, the position
and occurrence of vortex breakdown could be estimated by
the quasi-cylindrical approximation (QCA). This method
has already been reviewed earlier. However, it is interesting
to note that [95] demonstrated that failure of the QCA and
critical state are related. They showed that the critical state
corresponds to a singularity. Under the QCA, if the axial and
azimuthal velocity pro®les are given, then the radial velo-
city pro®le satis®es a linear ordinary differential equation of
second order. As a ¯ow shifts from a supercritical state to a
subcritical one by increasing the swirl level continuously,
the radial velocity pro®les separately tend to positive and
negative in®nity. Calculations with the QCA must then
necessarily fail at this point. It has to be underlined that
the equation giving the radial velocity pro®le is merely
one term more than the test equation of the critical classi®-
cation [51]. This is a nonhomogeneous term, related to vis-
cosity, which causes the point of failure to precede the
critical point (Fig. 39).
Under the assumption of inviscid ¯ow, Ludwieg [97] and
Hall [29] both veri®ed that the point where calculation using
the QCA fails corresponds exactly to the critical state. These
inviscid calculations were criticised and judged meaningless
by Shi and Shan [95] as the only solution is the trivial one.
Krause [44], however, got some insight into the phenom-
enon (initiation of the process and effect of imposed pressure
gradient) by comparing inviscid and viscous solutions.
For Gartshore [60], vortex breakdown was due to the
diffusion of vorticity accompanying the axial ¯ow reversal,
rather than to disturbance propagation. Nevertheless, he
added that the ¯ow downstream of this reversal might be
unstable. He proposed that the observed spiral ¯ow is
evidence of this instability. Grabowski and Berger [64]
also stated that in the absence of an externally imposed
pressure gradient or ¯ow divergence, breakdown results
from the diffusion and convection of vorticity away from
the vortex core.
While this method seems to be able to predict the occur-
rence and position of breakdown, it is, however, limited as it
cannot account for upstream in¯uences and cannot predict
the behaviour of the ¯ow beyond the stagnation point.

Furthermore, the ¯ow observed by Harvey [8] retained a

Other arguments were advanced by Escudier [16]. First,
the phenomenon of breakdown appears, experimentally, as a

explanation to vortex breakdown.
4.5. Combinations of different theories
As the different explanations, independently, cannot
completely and accurately describe all the features of the
phenomenon, and do present shortcomings, combinations
have been proposed.
For example, vortex breakdown has been postulated to be
the result of a process combining wave propagation and
hydrodynamic instability phenomena in an intimate way.
Randall and Leibovich [90] extended the concept of
Benjamin and modelled the bubble form of vortex break-
down within slowly diverging tubes, as a standing, axisym-
metric trapped wave. As deceleration of the basic ¯ow
causes energy transfer from the ¯ow to the wave, the slight
divergence of the tube walls can cause the ampli®cation of
the waves travelling upstream. Wave propagation is
however limited if dissipation is present and an equilibrium,
resulting from the competition between ampli®cation and
dissipative effects, can be reached: the stationary wave is
`trapped'. Wave trapping has been found to be possible only
if the wave had reached a particular amplitude, which also
appeared to be suf®cient to lead to stagnation. Their model
was based on geometrical parameters and experimental data
from Ref. [10]. Many of the experimentally observed
features of vortex breakdown were reproduced, including
the in¯uence of an adverse pressure gradient, a well-de®ned
closed axisymmetric cell of recirculating ¯uid approxi-
mately the same size and aspect ratio as that observed by
Sarpkaya [10], and a wall pressure behaviour consistent
with Sarpkaya's [10] and Kirkpatrick's [48] observations.
Furthermore, the ¯ow was found supercritical upstream of
breakdown and subcritical downstream and the calculated
position of the breakdown as a function of Reynolds number
agreed well with that of Sarpkaya's [10]. One criticism
against this proposal was that the model was centred on a
theory of long weakly nonlinear waves, and could not there-
fore represent a logical explanation to a strongly nonlinear
phenomenon [29]. Furthermore, as wave trapping only
occurs at large amplitudes, the theory had to be extended
beyond its limits of validity to capture the phenomenon.
Unreported work by Leibovich suggests, however, that
wave motions of the form proposed here but of arbitrary
amplitude may be possible [94]. Leibovich [7] also high-
lighted a shortcoming of the model: reversed swirl velocities
were predicted inside the recirculating region, which was
physically impossible for a steady axisymmetric ¯ow.
Moreover, Randall and Leibovich [90] stated that reversal
of tangential velocities had not been reported experimen-
tally. The model was thus judged inadequate in describing
the ¯ow inside the breakdown. The prediction of reversed
swirl velocity was thought to be due to the inviscid assump-
tion, as in Ref. [61], and Leibovich concluded that the ¯ow
in the interior of a breakdown was then controlled by visc-
osity if laminar or by turbulent Reynolds's stresses if turbu-
lent. However, reversed swirl velocities have been detected
experimentally in bubble breakdown by BruÈcker and
Althaus [59]. Another weakness of this theory concerned
the dissipation of wave energy. Experimental evidence indi-
cated that viscosity did not greatly affect vortex breakdown.
Furthermore, it would not be suf®cient. Additional dissipa-
tion through turbulence could not be invoked either since
breakdown also occurs in a laminar vortex. Huang [102],
one of Leibovich's co-workers, suggested that another

Escudier et al. [91] presented evidence to suggest a

and stability of the vortex core upon which it occurs.
Their results showed that the stability criterion [88] does
not predict the location of the breakdown even though

difference in the growth rate of instability waves on different
types of axial velocity pro®les (slow on a high-speed axial
¯ow and rapid on a low-velocity wake-like pro®le). So, it is

that a consequence of breakdown is a wake-like velocity

of spiral disturbances, and it is these disturbances that are
responsible for the wide range of observed breakdown types.
This approach could explain many of the observed features.
Indeed, starting from a low swirl, the core is unstable to
spiral disturbances (Lessen et al.'s [88] stability criterion).
In the region of jet-like axial velocity pro®les, the growth

increased (or the Reynolds number, or the adverse pressure
gradient, or the divergence), the back-¯ow becomes larger
and the axial velocity lower, so that the growth of the
instability takes place over a shorter distance. The break-
down region also moves upstream. This could justify the
sense of winding of the spiral, the progression of forms
from a mild disturbance of the vortex core to a spiral,
more and more tight. At a higher given swirl, the stability
criterion is reached and the vortex core is not unstable
anymore: only the bubble is observed. Escudier et al. [91]
noted that in many cases the spatial growth of the instability
is so rapid that a distinct axisymmetric breakdown does not
have time to form before the waves dominate the observa-
tions. They were of the opinion that this is one of the
phenomena that has confused efforts to separate the criti-
cality and instability criteria.

4.7. Bubble and spiral breakdown: same or different
phenomena?
Leibovich [15] stated that at least two pieces of evidence
exist to show that the two forms are in fact distinct ¯ow
disturbances. The ®rst one is the different core expansion.
The diameter of the core downstream is larger than that
upstream of breakdown for all forms of breakdown, but is
larger for the bubble form than it is for the spiral form.
Apparently, the bubble form of breakdown creates a wake
that is more unstable, with greater mixing resulting in a
larger core. Secondly, the transition from one form to
another is associated with a discontinuity in the mean pos-
ition (cf. Fig. 10 and this discontinuity is the clearest
evidence that the two disturbance forms are truly distinct.
On the other hand, Lambourne [38] argued that the break-
down is initially axisymmetric but changes into the spiral
form due to instabilities. Similarly, Escudier and Keller
[55,92] considered the two forms as two different aspects
of one single phenomenon which is inherently axisym-
metric. They suggested that departures from axial symmetry
resulted from azimuthal instabilities not directly associated
with the breakdown process and leading to roll up and
detachment of the shear layer surrounding a bubble. This
detachment would then result in a precessive motion of the
near-stagnant interior ¯uid and in a spiral appearance. The
experimental results of Escudier and Zehnder [13] tend to
support this idea of a unique phenomenon as the data corre-
lation established is independent of the breakdown's appear-
ance. A further point put forward by Escudier and Keller
[92] was that if the spiral form was inherently nonaxisym-
metric, then the structure would have to remain stationary
instead of rotating with the ¯uid, as it was observed for the
nonaxisymmetric standing-wave patterns in hollow-core
vortices [110].

phenomenon, attendant to leading-edge vortices, always
commences as a spiralling of the axial ®lament while the

[11] suggested that the spiral is the result of an instability in
¯ows with moderate swirl. Hall [29] suggested that these
instabilities might not occur in ¯ows with high swirl,

4.8. Overview of the theoretical investigations
A chronological table listing the publications used in this
study and related to theories and explanations of vortex
breakdown is presented in Table 3.
Many interesting interpretations have been brought up by
different investigators. However, while an explanation
appears to agree very well with one (or several) character-
istic(s) of the phenomenon, it usually cannot satisfy all the
observed features. Many explanations also complement
each other. Criticality seems to be an essential part of the
vortex breakdown process. Indeed, downstream conditions
do appear to be crucial in vortex breakdown, and all the
¯ows were observed to be supercritical upstream of break-
down and subcritical downstream. Stagnation is commonly
accepted as a characteristic of the phenomenon. Instability
also exists. In several studies, these different notions have
been intimately linked together. Shi and Shan [95] showed
that stagnation and critical state are related. Randall and
Leibovich [90] presented the concept of wave trapping,
combining wave propagation and hydrodynamic instability.
Similarly, Escudier et al. [91] and Maxworthy et al.
[106,107] suggested a connection between vortex break-
down and criticality and stability of the vortex core upon
which it occurs, stability being a superimposed phenom-
enon. Brown and Lopez [32] also considered stability as
secondary and disregarded the role of criticality. Instead,
they proposed a mechanism, based on the production of a
negative azimuthal component of vorticity. This study was
limited to the breakdown bubble. BruÈcker's [39] proposition
(different forms determined by the slope, winding and
diameter of the spiral vortex core) brings an interesting
view on the phenomenon.
Considering all these studies, it appears that a complete
scenario is still to be proposed and approved. However, a
clearer structure of the ¯ow pattern has emerged.
