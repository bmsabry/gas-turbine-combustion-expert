ABSTRACT
Aero gas turbine engine combustors are transitioning from traditional Rich-Quench-Lean (RQL) stag-
ing to Lean Premixed Prevaporized (LPP) operation to reduce harmful NOx emissions and improve
combustion efficiency. This operational shift, however, introduces the risk of thermoacoustic insta-
bilities, a significant concern for reliable operation and integrity of the jet fuel fired aero gas turbines.
Moreover, the complex geometries of aero engine combustors and the challenges of partially pre-
mixed conditions further complicate stability analysis and demand advanced diagnostic techniques.
To that end, this work aims to analyse thermoacoustic and swirl burner instabilities of our in-house
designed Magister burner under engine representative lean conditions using Detached Eddy Sim-
ulation. We extended our prior analysis by applying Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) in a
distributed computing environment, leveraging our PARAMOUNT modal analysis package, to cap-
ture the burner’s critical acoustic and transient dynamics. The proposed novel DMD combined
spectrum effectively identified the mechanism driving vortex core rotation, capturing its frequency
to within 1% of the measured value. It was demonstrated that using Proper Orthogonal Decomposi-
tion (POD) modes directly is more effective than an exact DMD formulation, yielding a 44% reduction
in the Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of DMD predicted temperature values. Furthermore, the applica-
tion of variable stacking reduced the MAE of the short-term DMD temperature predictions by 40%.
Finally, an MRDMD adjustment is proposed that leverages a critical Strouhal number (St = 0.6) to
significantly enhance ROM by ensuring essential flow dynamics are retained. This novel analysis
framework demonstrates a powerful capability to assess thermoacoustic stability, extracting criti-
cal physical insights from complex simulations that produce large datasets and thereby accelerating
the design cycle for aero-engine combustors.

1. Introduction
The demand for improved and cleaner combustion tech-
nologies in aviation has become increasingly urgent as
concerns of global climate change and stringent emis-
sions regulations for NOx and particulates intensify. The
widely adopted Rich Burn-Quick Mix-Lean Burn (RQL)
combustion strategy has long been regarded as a reli-
able approach. This is because it leverages the inher-
ent stability of rich combustion in the primary zone
and the low-emission benefits of lean combustion down-
stream (Liu et al., 2017). However, the overall environ-
mental performance of RQL has become increasingly
unsatisfactory, with significant contributions from aero
engines to greenhouse gas emissions and pollutants like
NOx, UHC and soot (El Helou et al., 2021; Kyprianidis
et al., 2015). These emissions pose severe environmental
and health risks, prompting regulatory bodies to impose
stricter standards and drive the development of cleaner,
more sustainable energy practices (Špelić & Mihelić-
Bogdanić, 2024).
Among the emerging solutions, LPP combustion
schemes for aero engine gas turbine combustors have
gained prominence. LPP involves the mixing of fuel
vapor with an excess of air before ignition, thereby low-
ering flame temperatures and reducing the formation of
NOx. This method is highly effective in curbing emis-
sions and improving combustion efficiency. Despite their
promise, however, LPP schemes are notoriously suscepti-
ble to thermoacoustic instabilities (Dhanuka et al., 2011;
Lohrmann & Büchner, 2005; Mularz, 1979). These insta-
bilities arise from the dynamic interaction between Heat
Release Rate (HRR) and acoustic waves within the
combustion chamber. This interaction can potentially
lead to limit cycle oscillations that can damage turbine
components , degrade performance and compromise the
structural integrity of the engine and flight safety (Ghani
et al., 2019; Lieuwen, 2012; Rayleigh, 1878).
Thermoacoustic instability in aero engines depends
on multiple factors, including the operating pressure,
temperature, combustion chamber geometry, burner
design and fuel type. Addressing these potential instabili-
ties demands robust design methodologies at lower TRLs
as well as extensive experimental testing of the engines
under flight conditions that may be prohibitively expen-
sive to conduct (Poinsot, 2017). Various solutions have
been proposed to mitigate these instabilities, including
active and passive control schemes as well as redesign-
ing aeroengine combustors to be inherently stable in their
operation. Moreover, research into thermoacoustic insta-
bilities has predominantly focussed on simplified geome-
tries and premixed combustion at atmospheric condi-
tions. This has left critical gaps in our understanding
of combustion systems that involve complex geometries,
challenging operating conditions and liquid fuel combus-
tion in non-premixed or partially premixed scenarios.
The novelty of this paper lies in the application of robust
analysis frameworks to deepen our understanding of the
complexities associated with combustion dynamics in
real aero engines.
In the pursuit of cleaner combustion technologies
for aero engines, CFD analysis and advanced post-
processing tools have become indispensable for reduc-
ing design-phase costs and improving performance. Tra-
ditional methods often rely on extensive experimental
testing or pre-existing knowledge of potential resonance
frequencies. In contrast, data-driven approaches allow
for the investigation of system dynamics without a pri-
ori assumptions. One of the prominent analysis frame-
works in this space is Proper Orthogonal Decomposition
(POD), which is widely used due to its data-driven nature
and the inherent sorting of the decomposed modes,
which is well-suited to combustion dynamics analysis
(Holmes et al., 2012).
Efforts have been made to address some of the short-
comings of POD when applied to combustion dynamics,
including spectral POD methods (Sun et al., 2024) and
multiresolution POD analysis (Grader et al., 2021; Pro-
cacci et al., 2023; Yin & Stöhr, 2020). However, similar
to the FFT, POD provides limited insight into the tran-
sient nature of the combustion process and the time
dynamics of the acoustics response. Because thermoa-
coustic instabilities evolve rapidly and involve complex
interactions over time, the use of analysis methods that
capture these transient dynamics is essential (Schmidt
& Schmid, 2019).
DMD offers a unique advantage in analysing both
spatial and temporal characteristics simultaneously,
thereby providing deeper insights into the evolution of

DMD is highly effective in the context of combustion

used standard DMD modes to create a local Flame Trans-
fer Function (FTF) based on Large Eddy Simulation
(LES) of an atmospheric swirling spray flame.
This study builds on these developments by applying
DMD to investigate the thermoacoustic behaviour of the
Magister burner, a laboratory-scale aero engine repre-
sentative burner designed at the University of Twente.
Featuring co-swirling flow and an airblast pre-filming
hybrid atomisation scheme for burning liquid jet fuel
under pressurised conditions, the burner serves as a
critical platform for understanding the sensitivities of
non-premixed combustion to thermoacoustic instabili-
ties. The complexities of this setup, characterised by tur-
bulent flows, droplet breakup, hybrid atomisation and
liquid fuel combustion, necessitate high-fidelity LES.
These simulations are computationally expensive and
result in large datasets that challenge conventional post-
processing methods.
To address these challenges, we leverage PARA
MOUNT, our in-house software package developed
for modal analysis. The parallel distributed nature of
this package is well-suited to large datasets such as
LES results. Building on our previous work with POD
(Ghasemi et al., 2023), a comprehensive framework is
introduced for the analysis of non-premixed combustion
data using a DMD combined spectrum. Different formu-
lations are compared by examining the impact of project-
ing DMD modes onto the SVD basis versus using unpro-
jected modes. Additionally, an error metric is defined
to quantify discrepancies between simulation results and
DMD reconstructions as well as predictions. The anal-
ysis identifies critical dominant modes in the flow and
correlates their resonance frequencies with associated
time dynamics. This information serves as a method to
identify the dominant vortex core of the swirler. More-
over, multiple variable stacking scenarios are compared,
and it is shown that careful selection of the variables
for the stacking procedure is crucial in improving short-
term prediction accuracy of the applied DMD algorithm.
Finally, Multiresolution DMD (MRDMD) is explored to
develop a ROM that captures the appropriate level of
detail for swirl burners. This is calibrated to a characteris-
tic Strouhal number that describes the unsteady swirling
motion. These perspectives are aimed at tailoring the
DMD framework to the sensitivities of the combustion
dynamics and to help facilitate and improve the analy-
sis of the acoustics response of aero engine representative
swirl burners.
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows:
A CFD Simulation Setup section that details the simula-
tion setup, including the geometry of the Magister burner
and the CFD configuration. Subsequently, a Transient
CFD Simulation Results section presents the CFD results,
highlighting the non-premixed flame position and flow
patterns. Next, the DMD Formulation in PARAMOUNT

application to predicting future system states. Finally,
the DMD Analysis of Simulation Results and Discussion
section provides an in-depth analysis of the extracted
modes and discussions on the impact of variable stack-
ing on the short-term validity of DMD predictions and
multiresolution techniques.
2. CFD simulation
The Magister burner features a three-part design that
employs two layers of angled air entry ducts (A and B
ducts in Figure 1) that split the airflow into two co-
swirling streams. The two streams merge just upstream
of the dump plane with significant axial and tangential
velocity differentials, resulting in a region of high shear
force. The liquid fuel is injected as a spray of fairly large
droplets at the base of the burner onto the inner walls
of the swirler. This produces a thin fuel film that subse-
quently enters the shear region, which accomplishes the
hybrid atomisation of fuel ligaments into fine droplets.
The overall schematic of the burner is displayed in Figure
1(a). The burner operates under a nominal pressure of
3 bar with a global equivalence ratio of 0.51 and imposes
a relatively low 3% air pressure drop across the burner.
Liquid droplet injection of Diesel at a mass flow rate of
74.5 g/s results in a thermal power delivery of around
82 kW per bar.

2.2. Transient CFD simulation results
In order to maintain a CFL number below unity within
the critical LES shear region of the burner, a time step
of t = 2 × 10−5 s was used for the transient simula-
tion, which was carried out for a duration of 2.78 ×
108 CPU−s. Figure 2 shows the time-averaged contour
plots for the axial and rotational velocity. This provides
a clear depiction of the dominant flow structures inside
and outside of the burner. The axial flow exhibits a dis-
tinct reversal of the flow in the vortex core breakdown
region that reaches all the way to the base of the burner.
The shear region is observed just upstream of the dump
plane. The rotational (tangential) velocity displays the
presence of a co-swirl in the air entry ducts, with the swirl
extending into the combustion chamber.
The flow reversal pattern signals the presence of a neg-
ative gauge pressure region at the core of the burner.
This can be seen in the time-averaged contour plots dis-
played in Figure 2. Here, the OH radicals mass fraction is
chosen as the main indicator of the regions where com-
bustion is taking place and thus acting as a surrogate
for HRR. It can be observed that the droplets start the
evaporation and combustion process soon after they are
introduced into the domain, with combustion reaching
its peak intensity just downstream of the dump plane. The
resulting high-temperature combustion products form
an M-shaped flame, with the recirculation zone stretch-
ing back into the burner and providing pre-heating for
the upcoming fuel droplets.
3. DMD formulation in PARAMOUNT
Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) is a data-driven
technique that offers a powerful framework for analysing
spatiotemporal characteristics of a dynamical system
with its roots in fluid flow analysis (Schmid, 2010). The
analysis can distinguish various flow patterns along-
side their associated distinct frequencies and growth
rates. Unlike traditional spectral analysis methods such
as Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), which provide purely
frequency-based insight, or POD, which identifies
energy-dominant spatial structures without time dynam-
ics, DMD combines both into one analytical framework.
By extracting modes that describe the system’s inher-
ent dynamics, DMD provides a reduced-order represen-
tation that retains the essential physics of the system.
Its non-intrusive nature enables the analysis of high-
dimensional datasets without requiring governing equa-
tions, allowing researchers to identify dominant flow
structures and even predict future system states (Li
et al., 2023; Misar et al., 2023). DMD, in essence, produces
the best linear time operator that can evolve the dynami-
cal system in time starting from a given initial condition
such that it closely matches the underlying dataset.

3.2. Multiresolution DMD algorithm
Modeling multiscale systems in both space and time
is a central challenge in modern engineering, biologi-
cal, and physical sciences, as these systems often exhibit
interactions between microscale and macroscale phe-
nomena separated by orders of magnitude in spatial
and temporal scales (Horstemeyer, 2009). Combustion
processes are inherently multiscale, involving complex
interactions between turbulence, chemical reactions, and
heat release across a wide range of temporal and spatial
scales (Peters, 2009). A common approach for the numer-
ical calculation of species concentrations is to separate
the species into fast and slow categories based on their
characteristic reaction time scales. Slow species influ-
ence the overall dynamics of combustion over longer
timescales, while fast species reach quasi-steady state
rapidly (Law et al., 2003). Similarly, the flow patterns
exhibit modes with slower growth rates that deter-
mine the overall dynamics of the flow, while faster-
growing patterns emerge as undulations that result in
faster-evolving and more local flow structures. Tradi-
tional approaches, such as wavelet-based methods or
windowed Fourier transforms, are well-suited for mul-
tiresolution analyses by recursively refining data sam-
pling to isolate features at different scales (Ghasemi
& Kok, 2022; Zheng et al., 2022). However, these tech-
niques typically focus on either spatial or temporal scales
independently, rather than addressing both simultane-
ously. This limitation highlights the need for a robust
method capable of simultaneously capturing multiscale
spatiotemporal dynamics. In this context, MRDMD is
utilised.

3.3. Future system state prediction
Equation (13) implies that the DMD algorithm can pre-
dict the future states of a dynamical system based on its
DMD modes, the time dynamics and the provided first
instance to be used as an initial state. Considering that
the DES is initialised using an unsteady RANS simula-
tion, the initial state x1 is taken as the snapshot where the
LES fields are sufficiently well-developed. In our earlier
investigation (Ghasemi et al., 2023), a precessing vortex
core was identified at the base of the burner, exhibiting
the same frequency as observed during our experimental
measurements. This precessing vortex core has an associ-
ated frequency of 879 Hz. In this study, we limit the num-
ber of snapshots n to 3000, which allows for upwards of
100 precession cycles of the vortex core of the burner. The
averaged flow fields in Figures 2 and 3 are based on the
same time horizon and show a relatively smooth contour
field that supports a well-developed periodic dynamic
footprint of the burner within the considered number
of snapshots. The number of spatial points to consider
was limited to m = 105 positions both in a 2D cross-
section of the burner as well as a 3D point cloud randomly
selected from the computational domain. Looking at the
voxel mesh in Figure 1, a concentration of data points
near the region of interest is evident. This region includes
the vortex breakdown region and the axial flow rever-
sal zones, creating a desired bias for the point cloud to
include more of the relevant system dynamics.
Additionally, the calculation of subsequent snapshots
can be continued from x1 to xn and even further into
xn+k beyond the final snapshot. Based on the DMD
framework, this extrapolation is only sound if the system
exhibits strictly periodic dynamics and sufficient snap-
shots have been observed to develop adequate DMD
modes. Otherwise, the predicted values lack a physical
basis and tend to diverge from the actual system dynam-
ics. Here, to concretely discuss the validity of the future
state predictions, the DMD system is limited to 80% of
the snapshots and the remaining 20% of the calculated
snapshots are used as a test set for validating the DMD
predicted data.

5. Conclusions
In this study, various implementations of the Dynamic
Mode Decomposition (DMD) algorithm were employed
to analyse the combustion dynamics of a non-premixed
swirl-stabilised airblast burner operating under engine-
representative conditions. Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
was used to quantify the validity of DMD reconstruc-
tion and prediction results. The extracted DMD modes
provided valuable insights into the underlying flow struc-
tures, and the proposed DMD combined spectrum was
demonstrated to be effective in identifying the mecha-
nisms driving vortex core rotation frequency within 1%
of the measured value. It was shown that the localised
combustion region is best captured by directly using the
modes obtained by Proper Orthogonal Decomposition
(POD) instead of an exact formulation; with Tempera-
ture values showing a 44% reduction in their predicted
values.
Variable stacking demonstrated a significant effect on
the validity of DMD predictions for CFD data. By com-
bining the variables that incorporate essential dynam-
ics of the system, such as temperature in a combustion
simulation using a weakly compressible solver, the short-
window prediction MAE of the DMD results was reduced
by 40%. This finding, together with the MAE metric, pro-
vides an important means of assessing whether the com-
puted snapshots adequately capture the system dynamics
and offers a measure of confidence in the sufficiency of
the total calculated timesteps and computational effort.
The high Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of the DMD-
predicted values signified that the linear Koopman oper-
ator has effectively identified a non-oscillatory behaviour
in the considered simulated data. This hints at a stable
operation of the burner without thermoacoustic oscilla-
tory instabilities. This result is reinforced by our other
published study (Navarro-Arredondo & Kok, 2024),
where the thermoacoustic stability of the burner was
investigated experimentally using non-linear chaos the-
ory. The results demonstrated a stable operation without
limit-cycle oscillations that resulted in an unorganised
phase portrait without attractors, consistent with the con-
clusions derived from the current DMD analysis.
Finally, the Multiresolution DMD (MRDMD) frame-
work was demonstrated to effectively capture the
dynamics of non-premixed combustion within the swirl-
stabilised burner. The proposed threshold for identifying
slower-growing modes, based on the Strouhal number
(St = 0.6), proved particularly effective. This threshold,
which aligns with the dominant vortex core frequency,
successfully isolated flow structures critical to the com-
bustion process, such as vortex breakdown and rota-
tional patterns, while filtering out less significant high-
frequency components.
Collectively, the proposed analysis framework offers
a powerful capability to assess thermoacoustic stability,
extract critical physical insights from complex simula-
tions and thereby accelerate the design cycle for aero-
engine combustors.
