Flame Response Mechanisms
Due to Velocity Perturbations in a
Lean Premixed Gas Turbine
Combustor
The response of turbulent premixed ﬂames to inlet velocity ﬂuctuations is studied experi-
mentally in a lean premixed, swirl-stabilized, gas turbine combustor. Overall chemilumi-
nescence intensity is used as a measure of the ﬂuctuations in the ﬂame’s global heat
release rate, and hot wire anemometry is used to measure the inlet velocity ﬂuctuations.
Tests are conducted over a range of mean inlet velocities, equivalence ratios, and velocity
ﬂuctuation frequencies, while the normalized inlet velocity ﬂuctuation V/Vmean is ﬁxed
at 5% to ensure linear ﬂame response over the employed modulation frequency range.
The measurements are used to calculate a ﬂame transfer function relating the velocity
ﬂuctuation to the heat release ﬂuctuation as a function of the velocity ﬂuctuation fre-
quency. At low frequency, the gain of the ﬂame transfer function increases with increasing
frequency to a peak value greater than 1. As the frequency is further increased, the gain
decreases to a minimum value, followed by a second smaller peak. The frequencies at
which the gain is minimum and achieves its second peak are found to depend on the
convection time scale and the ﬂame’s characteristic length scale. Phase-synchronized
CH chemiluminescence imaging is used to characterize the ﬂame’s response to inlet
velocity ﬂuctuations. The observed ﬂame response can be explained in terms of the
interaction of two ﬂame perturbation mechanisms, one originating at ﬂame-anchoring
point and propagating along the ﬂame front and the other from vorticity ﬁeld generated
in the outer shear layer in the annular mixing section. An analysis of the phase-
synchronized ﬂame images show that when both perturbations arrive at the ﬂame at the
same time (or phase), they constructively interfere, producing the second peak observed
in the gain curves. When the perturbations arrive at the ﬂame 180 degrees out-of-phase,
they destructively interfere, producing the observed minimum in the gain curve.

Introduction
Modern low-emission gas turbines employ lean premixed com-
bustion to minimize NOx emissions. This approach, however, is
known to increase a combustion system’s susceptibility to self-
sustained combustion instabilities 1,2. These combustion insta-
bilities occur as a result of the coupling between system acoustics
and unsteady heat release from the ﬂame. The sustained high am-
plitude pressure ﬂuctuations decrease system efﬁciency and com-
promise structural integrity. To facilitate the prediction of combus-
tion instabilities in the combustor design stage, a thorough
understanding of the ﬂame’s response to periodic ﬂow distur-
bances is necessary and has been an active subject of both ana-
lytical 3–8 and experimental 9–19 studies. Experimental ﬂame
response measurements can be used to guide the formulation of
and validate the predictions of analytical ﬂame response models.
Once known, the ﬂame transfer function FTF can be incorpo-
rated as a source term in a thermoacoustic network model to pre-
dict combustion instabilities 20,21.
In order to quantitatively describe a premixed ﬂame’s response
to upstream velocity perturbations, a FTF describing the ratio of
normalized heat release and inlet velocity ﬂuctuations is deﬁned
as

Flame transfer functions for laminar premixed ﬂames have been
studied by a number of researchers 4,7,22. Fleiﬁl et al. 4 ana-
lytically described the response of a laminar premixed ﬂame to

and the ﬂame shape i.e., conical, inverted wedge, etc.. Similar to
results from analytic and experimental investigations of laminar
ﬂame transfer functions, Kim et al. 19 experimentally deter-
mined that the response of turbulent premixed ﬂames is governed
by three parameters: the Strouhal number, the ﬂame length, and
the ﬂame angle. Therefore, the dynamics of turbulent and laminar
ﬂames are qualitatively similar 23.

In general, the ﬂame behaves as a low pass ﬁlter; i.e., the gain

Two ﬂame response mechanisms govern ﬂame dynamics for
premixed ﬂames subjected to upstream velocity oscillations: ﬂame
perturbations originating from the ﬂame-anchoring point, as re-
ported in Balachandran et al. 11, or perturbations resulting from
ﬂow nonuniformities along the ﬂame, described for laminar
ﬂames in Durox et al. 10. Chaudhuri and Cetegen 17 described

ﬂames. There was, however, no mention of these mechanisms
interacting destructively and resulting in a signiﬁcantly damped
global ﬂame response. Preetham and Lieuwen 16 analytically
described destructive interactions of two ﬂame perturbation
waves, originating from the two ﬂame response mechanisms,
which could result in a signiﬁcantly damped global ﬂame re-
sponse for both conical ﬂames and wedge-shaped ﬂames.
The objectives of this paper are to experimentally determine the
ﬂame transfer function, to identify and quantitatively characterize
the mechanisms affecting the ﬂame’s response, and to generalize
the ﬂame transfer function over a range of inlet velocity and
equivalence ratio.
2
Experimental Methods
The experimental measurements are performed in a lean pre-
mixed, swirl-stabilized gas turbine combustor facility, utilizing the
same combustor and nozzle assembly as Kim et al. 18. The
facility consists of an inlet section, siren modulation device, an-
nular nozzle assembly, and optically accessible quartz combustor
section leading to an open exhaust. To eliminate the possibility of
equivalence ratio perturbations, air and natural gas are completely
premixed before a choked inlet. A siren-based modulation device
is used to impose inlet velocity perturbations. Driven by a
variable-speed dc motor, the siren is capable of forcing the pre-
mixed fuel-air mixture at frequencies ranging from 30 Hz to 500
Hz. Two metering valves control the ﬂow rate of the fuel-air mix-
ture entering the siren device and the siren bypass line. The inlet
velocity ﬂuctuation amplitude is varied by changing the fraction
of the fuel-air mixture that ﬂows through the bypass line. The
annular mixing section consists of a 60 mm inner diameter i.d.
mixing tube and a 40 mm outer diameter o.d. centerbody, as
shown in Fig. 1. The centerbody is recessed 20 mm from the
dump plane. Within the mixing section, 45 deg swirl vanes are
mounted 120 mm upstream of the combustor dump plane. The
optically accessible combustor is made of a fully open quartz tube
150 mm in diameter and 300 mm in length.
Measurement instruments include a hot wire anemometer, pho-
tomultiplier tubes PMTs, and an intensiﬁed charge coupled de-
vice ICCD camera. The hot wire anemometer HWA, TSI model
1210 is used to measure velocity ﬂuctuations in the annular mix-
ing section at 60 mm upstream of the dump plane. Three PMTs
Hamamatsu model H7732-10 are used to measure global chemi-
luminescence emission intensities from the entire ﬂame. Narrow
bandpass ﬁlters are mounted on each PMT to allow a separate
detection of ﬂame chemiluminescence from CH 4325 nm,
CO2
 3655 nm, or OH 3075 nm. Under the premixed
conditions of this study, the chemiluminescence intensities pro-
vide a measure of the heat release response to inlet velocity ﬂuc-
tuations 25. To acquire hot wire anemometry and PMT signal
data, a digital data acquisition system is used to record 32,768
data points at a sampling rate of 8192 Hz for each test. An ICCD
camera Princeton Instruments model 576 G equipped with a
CH bandpass ﬁlter 4305 nm is used to record ﬂame images.
Line-of-sight time-averaged images and phase-synchronized im-
ages are taken for a number of experimental conditions. For
phase-synchronized images, 40 images are taken per phase angle
and 12 phase-averaged images are separated in 30 degree incre-
ments of the forcing period with the hot wire signal serving as a
reference. To extract a two-dimensional ﬂame structure, a three
point Abel deconvolution scheme is applied to the phase- or time-
averaged CH chemiluminescence line-of-sight images 26.
Experiments are performed at atmospheric pressure with a con-
stant inlet temperature of 23°C. Mean velocities in the nozzle are
20 m/s, 25 m/s, and 30 m/s with mean equivalence ratios 
ranging from 0.65 to 0.80. The corresponding Reynolds number
ranges from 23,700 to 35,600. Normalized ﬂow velocity harmonic
forcing amplitudes V/Vmean up to 35% can be achieved de-
pending on the forcing frequency. For this study, a ﬁxed forcing
amplitude of 5% is used over a frequency range from 80 Hz to
340 Hz.

Results and Discussion
3.1
Characterization of Stable Flame Structure. Previous
results show that the gain and phase of the ﬂame transfer function
depend on the ﬂame structure ﬂame shape and characteristic
ﬂame length as well as the frequency and magnitude of inlet
velocity modulation 6,7,18,19. Also, ﬂames with the same char-
acteristic ﬂame length exhibit similar ﬂame responses regardless
of their inlet ﬂow conditions 19. The ﬂame length scale is found
to be an important parameter determining ﬂame response to inlet
velocity perturbations. Time-averaged CH chemiluminescence
images provide insight into the overall ﬂame structure and the
spatial distribution of the ﬂame’s heat release. Two-dimensional
chemiluminescence ﬂame images are shown in Fig. 2 for a range
of mean inlet velocities and equivalence ratios. The ﬂow direction
is from left to right, and as mentioned previously, the ﬂame im-
ages are deconvoluted, and only the upper halves of the deconvo-
luted images are shown because the images are axisymmetric. The
intensity of chemiluminescence is displayed in pseudocolor, with
black being the lowest and white the highest intensity. As the
mean velocity and/or equivalence ratio change/s, the ﬂame’s
shape and length change. Two general ﬂame shapes are observed:
typical “V” ﬂames anchored on the centerbody and “V” ﬂames
with an extension into the outer corner recirculation zone CRZ.

3.2
Characterization of Average Flame Structure Over a
Period of Inlet Velocity Fluctuation. The effect of velocity forc-
ing frequency on the averaged characteristic ﬂame length is inves-
tigated. An inlet velocity perturbation, which when normalized by
the mean velocity V/Vmean has an amplitude of approximately
5%, is imposed upon the premixed mixture. This level of har-
monic forcing amplitude is chosen because it is small enough to
yield a ﬂame response in the linear regime and large enough to
provide satisfactory signal coherence between the inlet velocity
ﬂuctuation and the chemiluminescence intensity ﬂuctuation.
Flame transfer function measurements presented in the next sec-
tion are determined with the same level of forcing amplitude of
5% of the mean velocity.
As shown in Fig. 4, ﬂame images averaged over each forcing
period show that LCOM remains relatively constant with respect to
forcing frequency. For all inlet ﬂow conditions, unforced ﬂames,
which are shown in Fig. 4, at 0 Hz are found to have similar
length scales to those of forced conditions. Therefore, the ﬂame
transfer function measurements presented in the next section have
nearly constant ﬂame length scales with respect to frequency.
However, as the ﬂame is subject to greater inlet velocity modula-
tion and responds nonlinearly, LCOM is found to decrease with
increasing forcing frequency 18.
3.3
Flame Transfer Function Measurement. The ﬂame
transfer function is determined for a range of operating conditions
with a ﬁxed inlet velocity harmonic forcing amplitude of
V/Vmean=5%. Typical ﬂame transfer function results gain and
phase are shown in Fig. 5 for a mean inlet velocity of 25 m/s and
equivalence ratios from 0.65 to 0.8 and in Fig. 6 for an equiva-
lence ratio of 0.7 and mean inlet velocities from 20 m/s to 30 m/s.
For a given operating condition, the gain is greater than 1 at low
forcing frequencies, indicating that the ﬂame acts as an ampliﬁer
at low frequencies. This has been observed in other studies of both
laminar 7,16 and turbulent 18,19 V-ﬂames. As the forcing fre-
quency increases, the gain reaches a maximum and then starts to
decrease, acting as a low pass ﬁlter. As the forcing frequency
increases further, the gain reaches a minimum and then starts to
increase again until reaching a second peak. Recalling that the
characteristic ﬂame length increases as the equivalence ratio de-
creases for a given inlet velocity or as the inlet velocity increases
for a given equivalence ratio see Fig. 3, the ﬂame transfer func-
tion results indicate that the magnitude of the ﬁrst peak in the gain
plot increases as the characteristic ﬂame length LCOM increases.
A similar behavior has been reported by Kim et al. 19. The
opposite behavior is observed for the second peak where the mag-
nitude of the second peak decreases as LCOM increases. Also, the
frequency at which the ﬂame response becomes minimum fmin
increases as LCOM increases. These results clearly demonstrate
that ﬂame length LCOM affects the ﬂame response to inlet veloc-
ity ﬂuctuations.

phase increases linearly until the modulation frequency ap-

around 180 deg, as predicted by Preetham and Lieuwen 16, after
which the phase continues to increase linearly, although at a dif-
ferent rate. The slope of the phase versus frequency curve is di-

3.4
Mechanism of Flame Response. Figures 7a and 8a
show a sequence of phase-averaged ﬂame images recorded in 30
degree phase angle increments at velocity modulation frequencies
of 100 Hz and 180 Hz, respectively. In both cases, the mean inlet

100 Hz case corresponds to a condition where the gain of the
ﬂame transfer function is greater than 1 refer to Fig. 5, and the
180 Hz case corresponds to a condition where the gain is at its
minimum. In each image, the ﬂow direction is from left to right,
and the phase in degrees is referenced to the time when the inlet
velocity ﬂuctuation is at its minimum. In both cases, it can be seen
that the intensity in the central part of the ﬂame and the overall
ﬂame length change during the modulation cycle. Figures 7b and
8b show plots of the mean ﬂame surface area, center-of-mass
location, overall CH intensity from the whole ﬂame, and the sum

and the sum of the CH intensity over the COM region are calcu-

Using a kinematic model of ﬂame front evolution, Preetham
and Lieuwen 16 studied the dynamics of ﬂames submitted to
inlet velocity modulation in terms of the superposition of different
perturbations propagating at different velocities: those originating
at the ﬂame-anchoring point such as vortical disturbances and
those due to ﬂow nonuniformities along the ﬂame acoustic dis-
turbances. The kinematic model showed that the perturbations
can either constructively or destructively interact, resulting in an
increase or a decrease in the ﬂame’s response, respectively. The
results presented in this paper provide an opportunity to experi-
mentally study and validate this behavior.
As discussed in the previous section, the overall rate of heat
release of a premixed turbulent ﬂame is directly related to the
overall ﬂame area, and changes in the overall ﬂame area can be
expressed in terms of changes in the mean ﬂame area or ﬂame
length and changes in the local wrinkling of the ﬂame surface.
Similarly, the effect of inlet velocity ﬂuctuations can be repre-
sented by two separate phenomena. One is the effect of the veloc-
ity ﬂuctuation on the mean ﬂame area or mean ﬂame length, while
the second is the effect of the vorticity ﬂuctuations generated at
the exit of the injector by the inlet velocity ﬂuctuations on local
ﬂame wrinkling 10,11. Because these perturbations travel from
the combustor inlet at different speeds, i.e., the acoustic velocity
ﬂuctuation propagates at the speed of sound and the vorticity ﬂuc-
tuation propagates at the mean ﬂow velocity, their combined ef-

4
Conclusion
An experimental study is conducted to understand the mecha-
nisms responsible for the linear response of a premixed turbulent
ﬂame to inlet velocity perturbations.
The gain of the ﬂame transfer function exhibits the overall low
pass ﬁlter behavior. However, the gain is ampliﬁed at low fre-
quency and becomes a minimum and increases to the second peak
as the perturbation frequency increases further. The frequencies at
which the gain becomes minimal fmin and the second peak oc-
curs depend on the convection time scale as well as on the ﬂame’s
characteristic length scale: The former increases with respect to
the ﬂame’s characteristic length scale, and the latter increases with
respect to fmin.
The mechanism resulting in the measured ﬂame response is
explained in terms of the interaction between two perturbations
one originating at the ﬂame-anchoring point and propagating
along the ﬂame front mean ﬂame surface area and the other from
vorticity ﬁeld generated in the outer shear layer in the annular
mixing section. A detailed image analysis shows that when the
ﬂame response is ampliﬁed the heat release ﬂuctuation caused by
the two disturbances is almost in phase, and they result in a larger
10% of mean amount of overall heat release compared to that of
inlet velocity ﬂuctuation 5% of mean. However, when the ﬂame
response is minimal, the heat release ﬂuctuation caused by the two
disturbances is almost out-of-phase, and this destructive interac-
tion leads to relatively low overall heat release ﬂuctuation even
though each perturbation causes a rather signiﬁcant amount of
heat release ﬂuctuation. This observation indicates that the local
ﬂame responses can interact with each other, and a detailed nature
of the interaction determines the global response.
The gain of ﬂame response function is generalized over the
frequency range of f / fmin1 in terms of normalized frequency by
the frequency at which the gain becomes minimal, suggesting that
the gain of ﬂame response is governed by the interaction between
the aforementioned perturbations. The phase is also generalized
over the same frequency range in terms of the ﬂame’s character-
istic length scale and mean inlet velocity.
