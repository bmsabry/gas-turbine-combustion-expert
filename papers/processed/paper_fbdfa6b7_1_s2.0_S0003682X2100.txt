Two modiﬁed Helmholtz dampers with multi-adjustable frequency bands are proposed to overcome the
drawback of Helmholtz dampers and Quarter-lambda tubes by which multi-irregular eigenfrequencies in
gas turbine combustors cannot be simultaneously treated via one interface. Theoretical impedance mod-
els of these dampers are established and experimentally validated in an impedance tube. Enhanced ther-
moacoustic instability attenuation by modiﬁed dampers is validated in a Rijke tube. A Helmholtz-based
method is combined with damper impedance models to predict the limit cycle behavior of a Rijke tube
equipped with dampers. Results indicate that the impedance models can effectively calculate reﬂection
coefﬁcients of modiﬁed and traditional dampers with cooling purge air. The amount of the secondary
tube inﬂuences the reﬂection coefﬁcients of modiﬁed dampers. Experimental results indicate that ampli-
tudes of the overall pressure ﬂuctuation and each order ﬂuctuation at the limit cycle are further attenu-
ated by modiﬁed dampers compared with those by traditional ones. Larger purge ﬂow rates for dampers
lead to more pulsation attenuation caused by vortex shedding dissipation. The numerical method can
predict the eigenfrequencies, velocity ﬂuctuation ratios, and mode shapes at the limit cycle after using
different types of dampers. When activating modiﬁed dampers, weaker ﬂuctuations are successfully cap-
tured by this numerical approach.

1. Introduction
Thermoacoustic instability is considered one of the major issues
in developing modern low-emission gas turbine combustors. This
phenomenon is mainly due to interactions between pressure ﬂuc-
tuations and heat release pulsations [1]. Numerous thermoacoustic
suppression strategies have been proposed, including active and
passive control methods [2]. The active control working in open-
loop or closed-loop conditions uses mechanical or acoustic actua-
tors to disrupt thermoacoustic feedback networks [3,4]. Passive
controls use cavities [5–7] or perforated plates [8] to dissipate
the ﬂuctuation of energy, with lower costs and more straightfor-
ward structures. Typical passive damping devices for land-based
gas turbines are Helmholtz dampers (HD) [9–11] and Quarter-
lambda (1/4-k) tubes [12,13], whose applications have been well
reported in many references.
Helmholtz dampers only work well in a narrow frequency band,
while 1/4-k tubes have periodic frequency bands, whose nth order
eigenfrequency is 2n – 1 times the ﬁrst one, indicating the fre-
quency interval is ﬁxed. Current dampers have some weaknesses
and cannot handle varying work-load conditions for actual ﬂuctu-
ations with multiple and irregular eigenfrequencies in modern gas
turbines. Additionally, longitudinal and azimuthal instabilities
which possess different frequencies may coexist in annular com-
bustors [14]. More ports need to be reserved for installing conven-
tional dampers to alleviate longitudinal and azimuthal modes. The
Helmholtz damper inserted by two perforated plates has been pro-
posed to broaden the frequency band and validated in a full-scale
annular combustor by Alstom [15,16]. The acoustic model of this
damper is established by ﬁtting experimental results in the previ-
ous work, but this method is not very easy to be utilized in the
design stage. The analytic model and sound absorptive properties
of pure dual perforated plates under bias ﬂow have been detailedly
investigated in the literature [17]. Recently, it has been reported
that combustion instabilities in the frequency range 100–800 Hz
can be stabilized by adding a damper with a reﬂection coefﬁcient
of less than 0.1 when a ‘‘diode” loudspeaker box is inserted into

the plenum [18]. This inserted object may make the interior struc-
ture of combustors more sophisticated. The resonator consisting of
two Helmholtz resonators connected in series, which has two sep-
arated eigenfrequencies, has been reported in the mufﬂe area
[19,20]. Analytical models for both the resonance frequencies and
the transmission loss are based on a piston-driven method.
In contrast, their work mainly focuses on acoustic transmission
loss, which is different from the impedance and absorptive charac-
teristics in the area of passive control for combustion instabilities
in combustors. Meanwhile, dampers for combustors need to be
cooled by purge air from the back panel, which is different from
static analysis in mufﬂes. Dissipations caused by cooling air may
be considerable, while those properties are not necessary for muf-
ﬂe models.
When combustors are equipped with dampers, numerically
determining suitable geometric sizes of dampers and optimizing
positions for installing them is another critical question. A low-
order network model based on homogeneous acoustic elements
can be combined with the damper model to investigate the optimal
design of dampers for combustors [21,22]. Other network codes,
such as Ta3 [15,23,24], OSCILOS [25,26], etc. are well reported for
predicting thermoacoustic instabilities in combustors with and
without dampers. Geometric details, aerodynamic structures, and
non-uniform distributions of physical parameters in real combus-
tors are ignored in those codes.
The Helmholtz method has been developed for remedying the
above limitations. Eigenfrequencies, growth rates and eigenmodes
of a speciﬁc combustor can be solved, and corresponding combus-
tion stabilities can be estimated [27]. Generally, the ﬂame transfer
function (FTF) representing ﬂame response dynamics needs to be
embedded into the Helmholtz method. The FTF can be extracted
from experimental data [28] or numerical results by the system
identiﬁcation technique [29,30]. Campa et al. [31,32] performed
the Helmholtz method for an annular combustor. When the ﬂame
transfer function is extended to a nonlinear ﬂame describing func-
tion (FDF), the limit cycle of thermoacoustic instabilities in com-
bustors can be predicted [33–35]. It is based on the assumption
that when the growth rate is equal to the inherent dissipation of
the system, pressure oscillation amplitudes reach the limit cycle
state. This dissipation coefﬁcient can be measured by the fre-
quency response method [36]. Recently, the Helmholtz solver with
an experimentally measured ﬂame describing function has been
employed to numerically investigate the attenuation characteris-
tics of perforated plates on the combustion instability in a pre-
mixed combustor [37,38]. Nevertheless, it is scarcely reported
how to simplify the Helmholtz method to numerically evaluate
the absorptive properties of passive dampers on thermoacoustic
instabilities. Experimental and numerical comparisons between
traditional and modiﬁed dampers are still limited in previous
studies.
In this work, two modiﬁed Helmholtz dampers with multi-
adjustable frequency bands are proposed to overcome the weak-
ness of traditional dampers. These dampers can simultaneously
attenuate pressure ﬂuctuations with multiple frequencies through
on interface, beneﬁcial for combustor reliability and lower costs.
Purge air is employed for dampers to simulate the cooling condi-
tion. Theoretical impedance models of modiﬁed dampers are
established, and validated by the impedance tube apparatus. Dissi-
pation coefﬁcients caused by cooling air are introduced to these
impedance models. The inﬂuence of the amount of the secondary
tube on impedance characteristics is also investigated.
Moreover, experimental comparisons between modiﬁed and
traditional dampers on pressure ﬂuctuation attenuations are car-
ried out in a Rijke tube, generating one or two eigenfrequencies
at the limit cycle depending on the heater location. With the com-
bination of corresponding damper impedance models and mea-
sured damping rates, the Helmholtz method predicts the limit
cycle properties of a Rijke tube equipped with modiﬁed dampers.
This numerical approach also evaluates the improved acoustic per-
formance of modiﬁed dampers.
2. Experimental
2.1. Reﬂection coefﬁcient tests
The transfer function method based on two microphones is
used to determine the sound reﬂection coefﬁcient of the damper
in an impedance tube. This impedance tube is designed according
to the international standard (ISO 10534-2-1998) [39]. The lower
and upper frequency limits mainly depend on the spacing of
microphones and the diameter of the tube, respectively.
The impedance tube shown in Fig. 1 is made of Plexiglas with an
internal diameter d of 100 mm and a length of 900 mm. A full-
frequency loudspeaker (Peerless 75F25AL02-08) is ﬁxed at the
tube inlet. The sweep sine signal generated by a function generator
is ampliﬁed to drive the loudspeaker. Two 1/4-inch pressure-ﬁeld
microphones (G.R.A.S. 46BL) are mounted on the side wall with
an interval x1 – x2 of 0.18 m. The distance x2 between the second
microphone and the tube outlet is 0.08 m. The test frequency range
is around 95–858 Hz, according to the ISO standard. All the pres-
sure signals are simultaneously collected by an acquisition card
(NI-9230, maximum sampling frequency 12.8 kHz). An absorptive
enclosure is installed near the loudspeaker to avoid air column res-
onance. Purge air ﬂows into the back cavity of the damper through
a small hole to simulate the cooling condition.
The period of the excitation signal is 1 s, and its frequency is in
the range 60–1700 Hz. Pressure signals with a duration of 2 s are
used for the fast Fourier transform (FFT) with a Hanning window,
and the corresponding frequency resolution is 0.5 Hz. The normal
reﬂection coefﬁcient R neglecting the mean ﬂow can be calculated

2.2. Rijke tube apparatus
Passive control tests for dampers are performed in a vertical
steel Rijke tube (50 mm i.d., 0.9 m length), as shown in Fig. 2. This
Rijke tube has a much less cross-section diameter than the mini-
mum wavelength, thus only longitudinal modes are considered.
Nichrome wires with a length of 2.02 m and a resistance of 8.9 X
are ﬁxed on a mica plate as the heat source. The diameter of wires
is 0.6 mm and the thickness of the heat region is 15 mm. The AC
regulator is supplied to drive wires. Pressure signals are collected
by two microphones (G.R.A.S 46BL) through the waveguides with
a semi-inﬁnite technique. Cooling jackets are employed to cool
the hot stream. The positions of microphones need to be far away
from the pressure nodes of each mode. The distances from two
microphones to the tube inlet are 0.75 m and 0.67 m, respectively.
The K-type thermocouple is ﬁxed at the axial location of 0.49 m.
The ﬂuctuation will disappear even if a tiny damper is installed
near the pressure antinode in the Rijke tube, which makes the
absorptive properties of different dampers hard to be compared.
Therefore, dampers are mounted at the axial location of 0.78 m.
All dampers are installed through one interface to alleviate the
impact of the port quantity.
3. Numerical methods
3.1. Damper impedance model
This work has designed and compared four kinds of dampers,
including a Helmholtz damper (HD), Quarter-lambda tube, and
two modiﬁed dampers (MHD-1 and MHD-2). The sketches of these
dampers are presented in Fig. 3. Purge air enters the cavity of dam-
pers through a tubule whose end is equipped with a check valve.
MHD-1 consists of two Helmholtz dampers connected in series
and MHD-2 is similar to the combination of a Helmholtz damper
and a wavelength tube. Multiple ﬁlter bandwidths can be simulta-
neously generated by two parts of the damper. By tuning the geo-
metric sizes of each component, any given two eigenfrequencies
can be obtained. L, l2 and L2 are selected as the variables and quan-
titatively determined to satisfy the design frequencies by a search
algorithm.

Rijke tube. Comparing the growth rate with the damping rate,
the stability of the system can be determined. The limit cycle con-
dition is reached when the growth rate equals the damping rate.
The damping rate can be estimated from the frequency response
test by imposing an external excitation with a loudspeaker
[33,36]. The frequency bandwidth Df is deﬁned by the frequency

4. Results and discussion
4.1. Acoustic characteristic of dampers
4.1.1. Geometric parameter
Four types of dampers with different neck sizes are designed
and compared in this work, including Helmholtz dampers, 1/4-k
tubes and modiﬁed dampers. The 1st and the 2nd resonance fre-
quencies of modiﬁed dampers are designed at around 214 Hz
and 428 Hz, the same as eigenfrequencies of the Rijke tube with
85 V heating voltage at the heater location of 0.17 m. The reso-
nance frequency of traditional dampers is around 214 Hz. The geo-
metric parameters of the above four kinds of dampers used in this
work are listed in Table 1. For modiﬁed dampers, there are two
tubes named the neck and the secondary tube. How to choose
the size of the secondary tube is based on the consideration of
maintaining the reﬂection coefﬁcient amplitude at the 1st eigen-
frequency and minimizing the value at the 2nd eigenfrequency.
When the neck size is constant, there are several conﬁgurations
to achieve two requested eigenfrequencies. The geometries of
these conﬁgurations are also shown in this table for the latter tests.
4.1.2. Helmholtz damper and Quarter-lambda tube
Initially, the reﬂection coefﬁcients R of Helmholtz dampers and
1/4-k tubes were measured in the impedance tube. The modeled
impedance ratio needs to be multiplied by the area ratio r (r = Stube/
Sneck) to be quantitatively compared with experimental results. It is
found that the wall viscous dissipative resistance determines the
amplitude of the reﬂection and the end correction determines
the eigenfrequency according to the impedance model. Therefore,
end correction factors, wall viscous dissipation coefﬁcients, and
vortex shedding coefﬁcients of analytic impedance models can be
slightly tuned to ﬁt the experimental curves.
Fig. 8 compares the measured and modeled reﬂection coefﬁ-
cients for Helmholtz dampers and Quarter-lambda tubes with dif-
ferent neck sizes under a purge ﬂow of 0 L/min. The end correction
factors for Helmholtz dampers with neck sizes r of 6, 2, and 1.5 mm
are 0.75, 0.3, and 0.4, respectively. Corresponding wall viscous dis-
sipation coefﬁcients are 1.85, 1.10, and 0.94. The wall viscous dis-
sipation coefﬁcients for 1/4-k tubes with neck radiuses r of 5, 3.5,
and 2 mm are 1.65(1.15), 1.2(1.0), and 1(0.75), respectively. The
values in brackets are applied to the second eigenfrequency. The
corresponding end correction factors are all equal to 1. Overall,
the curves calculated by analytic models can well track experimen-
tal ones. It can be found that the reﬂection coefﬁcient amplitude
decreases, and the phase shifts from negative to a positive value
near the eigenfrequency band. Thus, the pressure ﬂuctuation is
selectively ﬁltered by this acoustic feature. Compared with Helm-
holtz dampers, 1/4-k tubes have two eigenfrequencies below
800 Hz and the second frequency is three times as large as the ﬁrst
one. The reﬂection coefﬁcient amplitude curves of 1/4-k tubes are
shaper, and the minimal amplitudes are more signiﬁcant than

4.1.3. Modiﬁed dampers
Fig. 9 depicts the reﬂection coefﬁcients of MHD-1 with different

Theoretical impedance models of modiﬁed dampers proposed
in this work can track well the experimental curves. Thus those
validated impedance models can be applied for subsequent numer-
ical simulations to test the damper passive control performances
for thermoacoustic instabilities in a Rijke tube.
The effect of the amount of the secondary tube in modiﬁed
dampers on reﬂection coefﬁcients is further explored based on
the validated theoretical impedance models, as presented in
Fig. 11. The parallel secondary tubes have the same neck size. For
MHD-1 and MHD-2, the frequency interval between two resonance
frequencies gradually increases with the increase of the quantity of
the secondary tube. The absorptive ability of MHD-1 for alleviating
pulsations is accumulated to the ﬁrst resonance frequency with the
increasing quantity of the secondary tube. At the same time, that of
MHD-2 slightly increases at both resonance frequencies, which
indicates that the performance of MHD-2 can be enhanced by add-
ing more secondary tubes.

5. Conclusion
Two modiﬁed Helmholtz dampers with dual-adjustable fre-
quency bands are proposed in this work. Enhanced thermoacoustic
instability attenuation by modiﬁed dampers via one installing
interface is experimentally and numerically validated. The theoret-
ical impedance models can effectively calculate the reﬂection coef-
ﬁcients of the Helmholtz damper, the 1/4-k tube, and modiﬁed
dampers with purge air. However, according to impedance tube
tests, end corrections, viscous wall dissipations, and vortex shed-
ding coefﬁcients need to be slightly tuned in advance. Here, the
vortex shedding factor is used to establish the relationship
between the impedance and the purge volume ﬂow. A more signif-
icant purge ﬂow rate leads to a larger reﬂection coefﬁcient ampli-
tude
due
to
the
intensiﬁed
vortex
shedding.
Geometric
conﬁgurations of modiﬁed dampers designed at any given reso-
nance frequencies can be determined by performing a searching
code on damper impedance models. Both resonance frequencies
and reﬂection coefﬁcient amplitudes of modiﬁed dampers are
inﬂuenced by the amount of the secondary tube.
Experimental results from Rijke tube tests indicate that the
amplitudes of the overall pressure ﬂuctuation and each order ﬂuc-
tuation at the limit cycle decrease after being equipped with the
Helmholtz damper and the 1/4-k tube, and further decline after
using modiﬁed dampers possessing speciﬁc dual frequency bands.
The ﬁrst and second-order velocity/pressure mode shapes recon-
structed from experimental data at the limit cycle maintain lower
values employing modiﬁed dampers than traditional ones.

Eigenfrequencies, velocity ﬂuctuation ratios, and mode shapes
after using conventional and modiﬁed dampers can be predicted
by the numerical method, where damper impedance models are
embedded into the Helmholtz solver. Pulsation reductions for the
1st and the 2nd modes by modiﬁed dampers are underestimated
and overestimated by the numerical method, respectively. This
may result from the weak nonlinearity of the simpliﬁed heat
release model. Nevertheless, this Helmholtz approach can efﬁ-
ciently capture the enhanced suppression of thermoacoustic insta-
bilities by modiﬁed dampers. The accuracy of predicted results is
affected by the vortex shedding caused by the purge ﬂow, resulting
from the fact that the Helmholtz method is based on the frozen
ﬂow hypothesis. Considering the tolerable accuracy and low costs,
this method can still be available for damper design.
