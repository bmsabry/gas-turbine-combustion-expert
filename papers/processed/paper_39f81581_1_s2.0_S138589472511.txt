and high pressures

School of Energy and Power Engineering, Jiangsu University, Zhenjiang, 212013, China

A B S T R A C T
Accurate predictions of ignition and flame propagation performances of ammonia/hydrogen combustion under 
intermediate-to-high temperatures and high-pressure conditions are significant in the design and development of 
gas turbines and internal combustion engines. This work tailors a reduced mechanism for NH3/H2 combustion 
featuring 19 species and 83 elementary reactions. It is found that removing N2Hx (x = 2, 3, and 4) chemistry 
produces better or comparably satisfactory results over experimental data relative to the remaining models. The 
inclusion of NNH chemistry slows down auto-ignition processes under intermediate temperature conditions but 
plays a minimal role under high-temperature conditions. However, the ignition difference between Mech18 and 
19 is negligible if the blending ratio exceeds 30 %, indicating the gradually weakened impact of NNH-related 
chemistry on auto-ignition processes. Further evaluation in terms of laminar burning velocities indicates that 
Mech19 generally exhibits a better predictive ability at elevated inlet temperatures and pressures. By imple­
menting sensitivity and pathway flux analyses, the NNH-related reaction of NH2 + NO=NNH + OH has been 
identified to be critical in determining flame propagation characteristics of ammonia/hydrogen blends. This 
reduced mechanism is then applied to evaluate the dependence of NOx emissions on inlet operational parame­
ters. Increasing inlet pressure facilitates reducing pollutant formation, which is much more remarkable under 
fuel-rich conditions, whereas a higher inlet temperature is not favored. Finally, the non-monotonic response 
characteristics of pollutant generation to the blending ratio are also pinpointed. In general, the simplified 
mechanism can be applied to shed light on the fundamental physics/chemistry of combustion phenomena under 
gas turbine-relevant conditions.
1. Introduction
There is increasing demand for searching for alternative zero/low- 
carbon fuels, aiming to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions in the 
power generation and propulsion sector and extend energy availability 
by minimizing fossil fuel dependence [1–4]. Ammonia, as a carbon-free 
and hydrogen carrier, has recently emerged as a promising solution to 
these issues due to the mature production technology, well-established 
storage, and transportation methods. This is further pushed by the 
renewable strategies to produce green ammonia via wind and solar 
energy or electrochemical nitrogen reduction reaction [5,6]. Neverthe­
less, pure ammonia/air combustion features quite low flame speed and 
slow ignition, presenting a barrier to practical operation [7–9]. Co-firing 
ammonia with active species is quite attractive in terms of enhancing the 
combustion reactivity and reducing carbon footprint [10–12].
In the past decade, extensive research interest has been paid to 
applying renewable ammonia to gas turbines [13,14], compression en­
gines [15–17], and spark ignition engines [18,19]. In this context, the 
efficient prediction of the ignition characteristics of ammonia binary 
mixtures under internal engines and/or gas turbines-relevant operating 
environment is of great importance in the design and development of 
power and propulsion systems [20–22], which requires the determina­
tion of accurate kinetic mechanisms. Earlier work on developing 
ammonia/hydrogen reaction mechanisms was implemented by Mathieu 
and Petersen [23] by using a shock tube. The high-temperature ignition 
data from 1560 to 2455 K were obtained and then adopted to verify the 
accuracy of the proposed chemical mechanism. By constructing a 
computational model containing 176 species and 1418 elementary

detailed mechanism, a simplification work was carried out by Guan et al. 
[25], who proposed a reduced mechanism comprising 48 species and

As another critical fundamental parameter, laminar burning veloc­
ities have also been commonly selected to validate the applicability of 
ammonia combustion [27,28]. Nakamura et al. [29] developed an 
ammonia/air reaction mechanism featuring 33 species and 232 
elementary species, which was shown to reasonably capture weak flame 
characteristics within a cylindrical micro-tube equipped with external 
heating. A detailed chemical mechanism for ammonia/methane com­
bustion containing 59 species and 356 steps was proposed by Okafor 
et al. [30], who identified the ammonia oxidation path as the predom­
inant rate-limiting reaction. In a follow-up work [31], they proposed a 
reduced mechanism that was able to predict the laminar burning ve­
locities of ammonia/methane combustion at elevated pressure condi­
tions and found that the reaction of NH + H2O = HNO + H2 was critical 
to NO formation. Furthermore, by extracting thermophysical data from 
San Diego [32] and Konnov mechanisms [33], Wang et al. [34] deter­
mined a new mechanism for ammonia/methane mixtures and demon­
strated the viability of predicting flame speed, while Arunthanayothin 
et al. [35] revealed different enhancement mechanisms of ammonia- 
methane combustion with elevating operating temperature ranges. Co- 
firing ammonia with alcohol is a promising way to enhance reactivity 
chemistry and reduce carbon footprint [36]. By conducting a systematic 
verification, Han et al. [37] proposed a detailed reaction mechanism 
suitable for the analysis of NH3/CO and NH3/syngas.
Developing ammonia/hydrogen co-firing technology has aroused 
wide research interest since the combustion of both fuels does not pro­
duce any carbon-based emissions [38]. Shu et al. [39] extensively 
evaluated the ignition process of ammonia/hydrogen combustion under 
low to intermediate temperature conditions and identified the different 
response characteristics. It was found that the auto-ignition was highly 
dependent on the elementary reaction of NH2 + NO=NNH + OH as the 
operating temperature ranges from 1100 to 1600 K, whereas under low- 
temperature conditions of 950–1150 K, the mechanism’s predictive 
ability was greatly affected by the reactions of NH2 with NO and H2NO 
+ O2 = HNO + HO2. Pochet et al. [40] also analyzed the low- 
temperature ignition characteristics of ultra-lean ammonia/hydrogen 
combustion experimentally and numerically under high pressures (43 
and 65 bar) and low temperatures (1000−1100K) conditions. H2NO- 
involved reactions were shown to be extremely significant to pure 
ammonia combustion, while N2Hx-related steps were responsible for the 
over-reaction of ammonia/hydrogen combustion. By comparing the 
predictive performances of ammonia and ammonia/hydrogen combus­
tion using ten different chemical mechanisms, da Rocha et al. [41] 
highlighted the significance of NNH and HNO sub-mechanisms on the 
laminar burning velocity. Zhao et al. [42] developed a reduced mech­
anism for 50 %NH3/50 %H2 combustion under atmospheric operating 
conditions and applied it to evaluate the emission and thermal perfor­
mances of micro-power systems. Recently, Wang et al. [43] numerically 
identified two different ignition modes of hydrogen-enriched ammonia 
combustion under low and high pressure conditions in the presence of a 
localized energy deposition. The autoignition enhancement arising from 
such a strategy was mainly due to ignition kernels preheating and local 
temperature elevation.
Furthermore, species profiles with advanced measurement apparatus 
are also reliable for developing a suitable chemical mechanism and 
validating the feasibility [44,45]. Zou et al. [46] explored the interac­
tion chemistry of ammonia and nitrogen oxides using laser-based multi- 
species measurements in a shock tube. It was demonstrated that for NH3- 
NO systems, the reactions NH2 + NO=N2 + H2O and NH2 + NO=NNH 
+ OH played dominating roles in NH3 consumption and NO reduction, 
while NH + NO=N2O + H governed N2O formation. By performing 
experimental and kinetic analysis of NH3/CH3OH combustion, Welp 
et al. [47] pointed out that under plug-flow conditions, CH3OH + HO2 =
CH2OH + H2O2 was one of the most sensitive reactions enhancing the 
system’s reactivity. Li et al. [48] developed two-step reaction kinetics 
for ammonia/hydrogen, including 2H2 + O2 = 2H2O and 4NH3 + 3O2 =
2 N2 + 6H2O, which can be further used to predict deflagrations with the 
aid of high-accuracy simulation tools.
Previous studies indicated that most mechanism simplification for 
ammonia/hydrogen combustion was conducted under low and inter­
mediate operating conditions. Little attention has been paid to the 
combustion characteristics, such as autoignition properties and NOx 
emissions under intermediate-to-high temperatures and high-pressure 
conditions, which are significant to the design and development of gas 
turbines and internal combustion engines. Furthermore, there is a need 
to establish a reduced mechanism with a smaller number of species and 
elementary reaction steps to control computational cost while main­
taining sufficiently accurate predictions of combustion processes. This 
partially motivates the present work. Considering the scarcity of 
ammonia/hydrogen reduced mechanisms under intermediate-to-high 
temperatures and high-pressure conditions, this work presents a 
simplification investigation towards the existing detailed mechanism. It 
aims to examine the feasibility of the reduced mechanisms in capturing 
the critical features, such as ignition delay time and laminar burning 
velocity, identify the reaction pathway change via the key radical gen­
eration, and discuss the dependence of NOx formation on the thermo­
dynamic operational parameters.
2. Numerical methodologies
Starting from the detailed mechanism involving C-H-O-N in­
teractions developed by Okafor et al. [30], an effort to achieve reduced 
but accurate chemical kinetics is made in this work. This detailed 
mechanism has been extensively used to explore the combustion fun­
damentals of NH3/CH4/H2 mixtures and is shown to be extremely 
effective in capturing the laminar flame speed and auto-ignition prop­
erties. By removing all C-related and less important N-containing re­
actions, two reduced mechanisms (Mech18 and Mech19) for NH3/H2 
mixtures are proposed with the aid of a direct relation graph with error 
propagation (DRGEP) method in Chemkin-Pro 2020. Table 1 presents a 
summary of the number of species and elementary reactions for the 
detailed and simplified chemical-kinetic mechanisms in this work. 
Depending on the simplification degree, different numbers of N- 
involved species are removed in the reduced mechanisms, along with 
the corresponding reaction steps. Among them, Mech18 is the most 
simplified one, with seven N-involved species excluded in comparison to 
the original mechanism.
The fidelity of the proposed mechanisms in the prediction of the 
ignition delay time under intermediate-to-high temperatures and high 
pressures is validated against the experimental measurements in the 
previous work. Regarding the numerical determination of ignition delay 
time, a zero-dimensional constant-volume reactor is applied by solving 
the enthalpy equation. By identifying the time required when the 
maximum temperature inflection occurs, the ignition delay time is 
determined. Meanwhile, the auto-ignition sensitivity is also calculated 
to identify dominant elementary reactions, where the positive sensitivity 
coefficient signifies the ignition enhancement and the negative value 
denotes the ignition suppression. Furthermore, a mechanism applica­
bility analysis in the laminar burning velocity estimation is also con­
ducted using a one-dimensional freely flame propagation model. The 
computational domain is 10 cm, with the solution gradient and curva­
ture of 0.02 and 0.1, respectively. The mixture-averaged transport 
model is applied to determine the species diffusion ordinary coefficients 
and fluxes.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Autoignition behavior prediction
As a starting point for validating the applicability of the reduced 
mechanisms described, the auto-ignition behaviors of pure ammonia 
combustion are explored and compared with the experimental data 
marked in symbols from Ref. [49] under intermediate-to-high temper­
atures, where the oxidizer is composed of oxygen and argon instead of 
nitrogen. Fig. 1 depicts the variations of experimental and numerical 
ignition delay times at various inlet pressures. Most reaction mecha­
nisms overestimate the ignition delay time, especially under atmo­
spheric pressure, as presented in Fig. 1(a). Of them, Mech18 produces 
the largest overestimation by a factor of 1.47 against the experimental 
data. Nevertheless, as the inlet pressure is elevated to 10 atm, the pre­
dictive ability of these reaction mechanisms is enhanced, especially for 
Mech 19, where the experimental and numerical results almost overlap. 
This may imply that radical formation and elementary reaction rates in 
Mech 19 have a stronger pressure dependence.
From Fig. 1, it can also be observed that an increase in the inlet 
pressure gives rise to a lower ignition delay time, which is a commonly 
applied approach to promote ignition in practical applications such as 
internal combustion engines and gas turbine engines. Regardless of the 
inlet pressure, predictions from Mech18 are significantly higher 
compared to those of Mech19. This phenomenon is much more signifi­
cant at P = 10 atm (see Fig. 1(b)), indicating that NNH-related reactions 
tend to play a stronger role in the reaction process at high pressures, and 
removing them leads to a longer ignition time. To conclude, estimations 
from Mech19 are generally better, or comparable to those computed by 
the remaining mechanisms, with the maximum discrepancy only being a 
factor of 0.6, and thus can be regarded as the most appropriate mech­
anism for pure ammonia combustion.
In consideration of the merits of hydrogen combustion, including 
large laminar flame speed and wide flammability limit, blending it with 
ammonia is a promising and effective way to enhance the weak reac­
tivity of ammonia combustion. Before conducting a relevant evaluation, 
it is imperative to define the ammonia/hydrogen blending ratio (R) 
signifying the percentage of hydrogen to the total fuel on a molar basis. 
Fig. 2 illustrates the variation of the ignition delay time of ammonia/ 
hydrogen mixtures at R = 5 %. The agreement between experimental 
data and numerical results calculated with the adopted chemical reac­
tion mechanisms is satisfactory, irrespective of the inlet temperature and 
pressure. Meanwhile, it is seen that Mech19 tends to have a quick 
response to the inlet temperature variation compared to the remaining 
mechanisms, as evidenced by the relatively small ignition time. 
Furthermore, by comparing Figs. 1 and 2, it is revealed that the ignition 
delay time can be significantly shortened even though the hydrogen 
content added is as low as 5 %. Furthermore, the dependence of the 
ammonia autoignition on the pressure is much more remarkable in the 
presence of hydrogen. For instance, the response of the autoignition 
behavior to inlet pressure variation in pure ammonia combustion is 
much weaker than the ammonia/hydrogen mixture. For example, at the 
inlet temperature of approximately 1666.7 K, increasing the inlet pres­
sure from 1.2 to 10 atm leads to the ignition delay time being decreased 
by a factor of 4.5 for pure ammonia combustion, whereas the factor is 
about 6.64 for ammonia/hydrogen combustion at R = 5 %.
Fig. 3 shows the profiles of computed and measured ignition delay 
times as the blending ratio is set to 30 %. Regardless of the initial inlet 
pressure, the agreement between experiments and simulation predicted 
by all chemical mechanisms becomes much more satisfactory in com­
parison to the case of a lower blending ratio, as presented in Figs. 1 and 
2. Further, the difference in the predicted ignition delay time between 
Mech18 and Mech19 is shown to gradually reduce with decreasing 
ammonia concentration. This is acceptable since, as the blending ratio is 
elevated, hydrogen combustion chemistry starts to play a greater role 
and thus minimizes the impact of the NNH reaction. It is also interesting 
to note that the NNH chemistry on the oxidation process under

In general, the N2Hx (x = 2, 3, and 4) chemistry is extremely 
important for the prediction of ignition performances in ammonia 
combustion, but removing them produces better results against 
experimental data available in the literature in comparison to the full 
Okafor mechanism. This phenomenon has also been demonstrated by 
Mathieu and Petersen [23] and Zhang et al. [51]. A comparison of 
Figs. 1–5 reveals that the inclusion of N2H2-related typically leads to a 
slow auto-ignition since it is a quickly turned-over species, which was 
also confirmed in the previous work [52]. In contrast, the NNH-involved 
chemistry impact on ignition processes is highly dependent on the fuel 
composition, operational temperature, and pressure. Its removal yields 
significant overestimation when ammonia concentrations are relatively 
high, but leads to negligible difference at low concentration conditions, 
which has also been reported by Monge-Palacios et al. [53]. To 
conclude, in consideration of the model accuracy and mechanism 
simplification, Mech19 can be reasonably regarded as the most suitable 
one for the ignition prediction of stoichiometric ammonia combustion.

most important reaction steps on which the laminar burning speed of a 
reacting mixture is dependent. In this work, the 10 most significant steps 
for flame propagation calculated with different chemical mechanisms 
are considered and compared. Fig. 12 shows the sensitivity coefficients 
of the laminar burning speed with Mech18 and Mech 19 under fuel-lean 
and rich conditions (ϕ = 0.9 and 1.2) at T = 298 K and P = 3 bar. It is 
apparent that the top 2 important steps remain unchanged even if the 
chemical mechanism or equivalence ratio is varied. Certainly, it is worth 
mentioning that there are some differences in the 10 most important 
reaction steps that the laminar burning velocity is sensitive to. For 
example, when applying different chemical mechanisms, the third 
important step contributing to the laminar burning velocity is N2O(+M) 
= N2 + O(+M) (R37), which becomes NH2 + NO=NNH + OH (R64) in 
Mech19. These results further confirm that removing the NNH reaction 
step can affect the contribution rates of elementary steps, thus changing 
the flame propagation behavior. Further, a closer examination of this 
figure shows that for a fixed chemical mechanism, varying the equiva­
lence ratio leads to the terminating reaction steps being changed as 
marked in red, which then explains the different predictive ability under 
fuel-rich and lean conditions.
To further identify how NNH-related reaction affects the flame speed 
prediction performance, a rate of production analysis is implemented. 
Fig. 13 compares the rate of production for NNH species of ammonia/ 
hydrogen combustion calculated with Mech19 at R = 10 % under fuel- 
lean and rich conditions. Notably, the 10 most significant reactions 
responsible for intermediate NNH species remain unchanged, regardless 
of the equivalence ratio. Additionally, it can be noted that the reaction 
rates of the most terminating/promoting steps for NNH destruction/ 
formation at ϕ = 0.9 are much higher than those of ϕ = 1.2. This is 
therefore beneficial to the generation of active OH and H radicals, 
enhancing the reactivity of the mixture, which is the reasoning for larger 
laminar burning velocity predictions under fuel-lean conditions.
As far as ammonia combustion is concerned, burning it under fuel- 
rich conditions is advantageous in terms of minimizing hazardous NOx 
emissions in practice. Further evaluation of the model is conducted for 
ammonia/hydrogen blends at the equivalence ratio and inlet tempera­
ture of 1.1 and 473 K, as the inlet pressure varies from 1 to 7 bar, as 
presented in Fig. 14. At atmospheric pressure, predictions of laminar 
burning velocities by Mech19 and Okafor59 are almost overlapped, 
which are lower than experimental measurements at R > 5 %. On the 
contrary, these two reaction mechanisms produce a larger estimation, 
indicating the weak pressure dependence of reaction rates of these 
chemical mechanisms and better prediction performance. Furthermore, 
a closer examination of this figure shows that neglecting NNH-involved 
reactions leads to an overestimation of the laminar flame speed, which is 
much more significant at an increasing inlet pressure. Certainly, further 
kinetic parameters optimization over flame burning velocities of 
ammonia/hydrogen combustion at elevated temperature conditions is 
still required, especially for relatively higher blending ratio conditions.
To distinguish the different response mechanisms of Mech18 and 
Mech19 in predicting the flame propagation at elevated inlet tempera­
ture conditions, a sensitivity analysis on OH radical generation/ 
destruction is conducted by examining the 10 most important reaction 
steps, as illustrated in Fig. 15. Regardless of which chemical mechanism 
is applied, the sensitivities of the flame speed to the chain branching and 
terminating reactions change in the opposite way, which are progres­
sively weakened and enhanced respectively with an increase in the 
initial pressure. This synergetic effect leads to a reduction in the com­
bustion burning rate and thus flame speed. By comparing Fig. 15(a) and 
(b), it is seen that the most important reaction steps are less sensitive to 
the chemical mechanism, but the sensitivity degree of each reaction 
varies, especially for major termination and chain branching steps. 
Taking the main branching reaction H + O2 = OH + O(R11) for 
example, the sensitivity coefficient of Mech18 at P = 1 bar is 0.5053, and 
it becomes 0.5158, accounting for a 2.1 % increment. This is also 
accompanied by the sensitivity variation in the termination reactions, 
further highlighting the significance of NNH-related reactions in the 
determination of the flame speed at elevated pressures and 
temperatures.

Fig. 16 depicts the NO mole fractions of ammonia/hydrogen mixtures at

increasing the equivalence ratio gives rise to a reduction in the NO

interesting to point out that the sensitivity of NO formation to the inlet

These results suggest that properly designing inlet thermodynamic pa­
rameters can be an effective means to control NO emissions.
In consideration of the significant drop in the NO emission as the 
inlet pressure is elevated from 1 to 5 atm, a rate of production analysis 
concerning NO formation is implemented to shed light upon the un­
derlying mechanism, as illustrated in Fig. 17, where the equivalence 
ratio is set to 1.0. Regardless of the initial inlet pressure, the most 
important contributing/terminating reactions remain unchanged, which 
are HNO + H=H2 + NO (R78) and NH + NO=N2O + H (R50), respec­
tively. By comparing Fig. 17(a) and (b), it is seen that increasing the inlet 
pressure enables the NO formation pathway to be changed, as evidenced 
by the emergence of H + NO+M = HNO + M (R76) and HNO +
OH=NO+H2O (see black lines). Furthermore, it can be observed that 
reaction rates of all steps have been increased with the inlet pressure 
elevated from 1 to 5 atm. Nevertheless, it is worth noting that the 
relative importance of the NO termination step (R50) to the promotion 
step (R78) is enhanced at a higher inlet pressure, which thus causes low 
NO emissions.
In practice, inlet preheating is a viable way to increase the mixture’s 
laminar burning velocity and thus enhance the combustion process. 
Thus, it is also meaningful to explore how pollutant generation varies 
with the inlet temperature, as illustrated in Fig. 18, where the equiva­
lence ratio and inlet pressure are fixed to be 1.0 and 1 atm, respectively. 
Notably, elevating the inlet temperature results in a higher NO mole 
fraction, which should be taken into consideration in practical appli­
cations. Meanwhile, the most striking observation to emerge from this 
figure is that the response characteristics of NO formation to the inlet 
temperature are gradually weakened. For instance, elevating the inlet 
temperature from 300 to 400 K at R = 25 % leads to NO emission being 
increased by 6 %. Nevertheless, such a percentage increment is only 1.6 
% with T varying from 700 to 800 K. Furthermore, it is worth pointing 
out that for any specified inlet temperatures, the NO mole fraction shows 
a non-monotonic changing trend with the blending ratio, with the peak 
pollutant emission taking place at R = 25 %. Such a phenomenon has 
also been observed in previous studies [58–60], most attributed to the 
enhanced thermal-NO pathway. However, this is quite different from the 
ammonia/hydrogen stratified combustion [61], where a low hydrogen 
blending ratio facilitates lower nitrogen oxides emissions via the DeNOx 
process. Thus, it can be concluded that optimizing the ammonia/ 
hydrogen blending ratio is beneficial to suppress NO emissions under 
elevated inlet temperatures.
5. Conclusions
Insights into the ignition and flame propagation characteristics of 
ammonia/hydrogen combustion under intermediate-to-high tempera­
tures and high-pressure conditions enable its application to gas turbines 
and internal combustion engines. For this, this work developed a 
reduced chemical mechanism for ammonia/hydrogen combustion, 
whose applicability is widely validated over different combustion re­
gimes such as laminar burning velocity and ignition delay time. The 
different response laws of various chemical mechanisms in the pre­

dictions of the results are underpinned by adopting reaction pathways

applied to explore the NOx emission performance over elevated inlet 
temperatures and pressures. The key findings achieved through this 
work are as follows: 
(1) The exclusion of N2Hx (x = 2, 3, and 4) chemistry produces better 
or comparably satisfactory ignition results over experimental 
data compared to the remaining models under intermediate-to- 
high temperatures and high pressures. The reaction step of 
NH2 + NO=NNH + OH is identified as the most important 
pathway to affect the ignition process between Mech18 and 
Mech19.
(2) Removing NNH-related chemistry is shown to extend the ignition 
delay time. Such reaction steps are more likely to play a greater 
role in the ignition process due to the changed reaction pathways 
and radical formation, but their impact can be negligible when 
the blending ratio is up to 70 %. Further, the effect of these re­
actions on ignition is much weakened by elevating the inlet 
pressure and temperature.
(3) The response of the autoignition behaviors to inlet pressure 
variation in pure ammonia combustion is much weaker than that 
of the ammonia/hydrogen mixture. For example, at the inlet 
temperature of approximately 1666.7 K, increasing the inlet 
pressure from 1.2 to 10 atm leads to the ignition delay being 
decreased by a factor of 4.5 for pure ammonia combustion, 
whereas the factor is about 6.64 for ammonia/hydrogen com­
bustion at R = 5 %.
(4) The reduced mechanisms are shown to reveal the flame propa­
gation behaviors at an inlet temperature of 473 K, but can result 
in over-estimations when the inlet pressure is much larger due to 
the large sensitivity of H + O2 = OH + O (R11). For all operating 
conditions considered, laminar burning velocities calculated with 
Mech19 are larger than those of Mech18, where NH2 +
NO=NNH + OH (R64) has been identified as the key reaction step 
for flame propagation.
(5) An increase in the inlet pressure and equivalence ratio is highly 
desirable in terms of minimizing NO emissions, while elevating 
inlet temperatures results in more pollutants being generated. 
The sensitivity of NO formation to the inlet pressure is stronger 
under fuel-rich conditions. Specifically, elevating inlet pressure 
from 1 to 50 atm enables the NO emission at ϕ = 1.1 to be 
decreased by a factor of 0.993, but this value is only 0.759 at ϕ =
0.9. For any specified inlet temperatures, the NO emission shows 
a non-monotonic changing trend with the blending ratio, with the 
peak pollutant emission taking place at R = 25 %.
In general, this work has identified the key radical formation and its 
reaction pathway in affecting the ignition and flame propagation per­
formances of ammonia/hydrogen combustion under gas turbine- 
relevant conditions. The insights gained from this work are beneficial 
to developing more simplified chemical mechanisms of ammonia com­
bustion and can also be applied to explain combustion phenomena. 
Meanwhile, the response of NO formation to high inlet temperatures and 
pressures is also helpful for designing the low-emission ammonia-pow­
ered practical applications.
