A B S T R A C T
This work experimentally evaluates how the velocity ratio (Vr = pilot-stream velocity/main-stream velocity), 
hydrogen enrichment, and oxygen concentration affect lean blowout limits, flame structure, temperature, and 
emissions in a dual annular counter-rotating swirl (DACRS) combustor. Three velocity ratios (2.27, 3.00, 3.87, 
with pilot velocity fixed at 6 m/s) were tested alongside hydrogen fuel fractions (HF) of 0–15 % and O2/CO2 
main-stream oxidizer oxygen fractions (OFm) of 20–34 %. The results show that a lower Vr (stronger main swirl) 
and higher hydrogen/oxygen enrichments dramatically extend the lean blowout limit, enabling stable com­
bustion at ultra-lean overall equivalence ratios (ϕm ≈0.35), while also altering the flame shape. With Vr = 2.27, 
15 % HF, and 34 % OFm, the flame remained compact and attached even at very lean mixtures, and carbon 
monoxide emissions fell below 5 ppm, indicating near-complete combustion. In contrast, a high Vr and lower 
enrichments produced an elongated, lift-prone flame and incomplete combustion (CO up to 50–65 ppm). These 
findings underscore the significance of optimizing swirl intensity and fuel/oxidizer composition in oxy-fuel 
combustors to achieve enhanced flame stability, ultra-lean operability, and minimal emissions.

1. Introduction
Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, primarily carbon and nitrogen 
oxides, play a pivotal role in global warming and climate change [1,2]. 
These gases trap heat in the Earth’s atmosphere, leading to a rise in 
average temperatures, which leads to the greenhouse effect. Rising sea 
levels, melting polar ice caps, more frequent and severe weather events, 
and disturbances to ecosystems and biodiversity are all consequences of 
increased greenhouse gas emissions [3]. These environmental changes 
pose significant risks to human health, food security, water resources, 
and economic stability. In response to the escalating climate crisis, the 
global community has set ambitious targets to reduce emissions. The 
2015 Paris Agreement is a major endeavour, pledging governments to 
limit global temperature rise to far below 2 ◦C above pre-industrial 
levels, with efforts to reduce it to 1.5 ◦C [4]. Achieving these goals re­
quires a collective push toward decarbonization across sectors, 
including energy, transportation, and industry. Measures such as tran­
sitioning to renewable energy sources, improving energy efficiency, and 
implementing carbon capture technologies are crucial. Reducing emis­
sions quickly and sustainably is the goal to lessen the effects of climate 
change and provide a safer, more resilient planet for coming generations 
[5,6].
Oxy combustion is a process that uses pure O2 instead of air to burn 
fuel, leading to cleaner combustion. This method helps decarbonize 
power production, particularly in gas turbines, by facilitating cost- 
effective carbon capture and minimizing nitrogen oxides (NOx) forma­
tion [7]. The achieved high temperatures promote more complete fuel 
use, supporting the energy sector’s shift toward lower CO2 emissions and 
sustainable power generation. One of the key advantages of oxy-fuel 
combustion technology lies in its use of CO2 as a diluent gas. Unlike 
nitrogen in conventional air combustion, CO2 has a high heat capacity, 
which helps moderate the flame temperature and improve stability 
under lean conditions. More importantly, because the exhaust stream in 
oxy-fuel systems is composed mainly of CO2 and H2O, the CO2 can be 
readily separated, captured, and recycled back into the combustor. This 
recycling process not only reduces the cost and complexity of 
post-combustion carbon capture but also turns CO2 into a useful 
component of the combustion cycle. In this way, oxy-fuel combustion 
provides an integrated pathway for lowering net greenhouse gas emis­
sions while maintaining stable and efficient flame operation. Numerous 
studies have been conducted to assess and compare the stability and 
combustion properties of oxy-fuel combustion versus conventional air 
combustion [8,9]. The findings indicate that maintaining a stable flame 
in an O2/CO2 environment necessitates a significantly higher oxygen 
concentration than is needed for stability in traditional air combustion. 
These results underscore the unique oxygen requirements for ensuring 
stability in oxy-fuel combustion. Several numerical research on com­
bustion frequently utilizes computational fluid dynamics methods to 
simulate and analyze combustion processes, including oxy combustion 
[10–12]. These CFD models help predict various performance parame­
ters, such as temperature distribution and emissions profiles, providing 
detailed insights into the complex interactions within the combustion 
environment. Beyond natural-gas blends, oxy-fuel combustion has 
shown

CO2/O2 largely suppresses thermal NOx formation and concentrates CO2 
in the flue stream for efficient capture, while enabling low-CO operation 
at lean conditions [13].
Gas turbines for aero and stationary applications commonly use 
diffusion flame combustors, known for their safety and stability. 
However, these systems produce high NOx emissions due to the high 
temperatures in near stoichiometric combustion. Strict emission regu­
lations are prompting scientists to seek new combustion methods [14,
15]. Advanced technologies like lean-premixed (LPM) and catalytic 
combustion are emerging as potential alternatives that could lower 
emissions compared to traditional approaches [16,17]. A widely used 
method for reducing both NOx and CO2 emissions involves running 
combustors under lean premixed or partially premixed conditions with 
low-carbon fuels [18–20]. This strategy adjusts combustion settings to 
achieve a lean or partially premixed state, optimizing the process to 
lower emissions. By using low carbon fuels, the approach effectively 
minimizes both NOx and CO2, contributing to improved environmental 
sustainability. Conversely, combustion instability is a major challenge 
that limits the development of LPM combustors [21]. This instability can 
cause fluctuations in combustion performance, leading to increased 
emissions and reduced efficiency. Effectively addressing combustion 
instability is vital for optimizing LPM combustors and ensuring their 
reliability and effectiveness in achieving low emissions and high thermal 
efficiency.
Hydrogen possesses several advantageous properties that make it an 
ideal fuel for various energy applications, including transportation [22], 
aviation [23–26] and industrial sectors [27,28]. As a component of the 
clean energy industry, hydrogen gas turbines provide substantial 
financial and environmental benefits that aid in the energy transition. 
Many experts concur that using hydrogen-enriched natural gas and 
attempting to modify new technology to enable hydrogen-based power 
production in gas turbines are the main routes toward low carbon energy 
[29–33]. However, employing rich hydrogen fuel can lead to significant 
combustion thermal shocks and cause the turbine inlet flow rate to 
exceed the flow matching limit [27]. This can negatively impact or 
restrict the operational performance of gas turbines. Currently, there is 
no fully reliable data on the use of 100 % hydrogen as a fuel within the 
existing high-power gas turbine industry [34]. Hydrogen fuel is often 
portrayed as an environmentally clean energy source due to its ability to 
produce energy with water as the only byproduct, emitting no direct 
greenhouse gases. Unlike fossil fuels that release CO2 and pollutants, 
hydrogen can contribute to cleaner air and reduced environmental 
impact [35]. Modifying the design of the burner can further support 
emission reduction. By altering the burner’s structure, fluid flow pat­
terns, fuel injection methods, and flame stabilization features, it is 
possible to enhance the combustion process and achieve more efficient 
mixing of oxidizer and fuel [36]. These improvements can result in more 
uniform combustion, reducing peak temperatures that typically lead to 
higher NOx formation. Low swirl combustion, first introduced by Cheng 
[37] for basic research, has gained prominence as an effective method 
for the lean premixed combustion. This technique, based on the prin­
ciples of premixed flame wave propagation, is noted for its ability to 
produce ultralow NOx emissions. In Low swirl combustion, a unique 
divergent flow pattern is created downstream of the low swirl injector.
The dual annular counter rotating swirl (DACRS) burner is a prom­
ising technology that can extend the lean blowout limits of premixed 
flames [38]. The interactions and transfer of radicals between the two 
flames helps for better flame stability. This enhancement not only boosts 
operational capacity but also reduces emissions [39]. El-Adawy et al. 
[40] experimentally investigated the flow and flame interactions, sta­
bility, combustion characteristics, and emissions of oxy-methane 
(CH4/O2/CO2) stratified flames stabilized by DACRS burner for gas 
turbine applications. The effects of equivalence ratios and the velocity 
ratio (Vr) were examined at a fixed oxygen fraction of 34 %. Results 
showed no flame flashback up to φm = 1.0. Near φp = 1.0, the main 
flame could maintain very lean conditions (φm = 0.45 at Vr = 3), 
extending blowout limits. Also, they found that increasing φp from 0.4 to 
1.0 reduced φm and φg at blowout, while lower φp values led to earlier 
flame lift-off and extinguishment, highlighting the importance of φp on 
flame structure. Also, Hamdy et al. [41] conducted an experimental 
study on stratified oxy-methane flames using the same burner (DACRS).

The experiments featured a fixed velocity ratio and a pilot-stream ve­
locity of 5 m/s, with a pilot-stream equivalence ratio of 0.9, while 
varying the oxygen fractions and main-stream equivalence ratios. The 
findings indicated no flame flashback within the tested operational 
ranges, including up to φm = 1.0. The pilot-stream conditions allowed 
the main flame to maintain very lean conditions, extending blowout 
limits. Increasing the oxygen fraction reduced the main and global 
equivalence ratios at blowout, while lower oxygen fractions resulted in 
earlier flame lift-off, demonstrating the significant impact of oxygen 
fraction on flame behavior. Additionally, extending the blowout limit 
toward leaner mixtures is achieved by enhanced combustion efficiency, 
reduced fuel consumption, and lower emissions. Stratified oxy-methane 
flames stabilized by DACRS burners effectively operate under lean 
conditions without flame flashback [40,41]. Oxygen fractions, equiva­
lence ratios, and fuel composition significantly influence their blowout 
limits. Increasing the pilot-stream’s oxygen fraction and equivalence 
ratio extends operational stability limits, changing the flame structure 
from V-shaped at low oxygen fractions to conical at higher fractions 
[42]. Hydrogen enrichment notably improves flame stability and ex­
tends lean operability due to enhanced chemical reactivity and heat 
recirculation, though careful management is necessary to avoid elevated 
NOx emissions associated with higher flame temperatures [43,44].
DACRS burners have yet to be experimentally tested with hydrogen- 
enriched fuels, which represents a critical gap in literature. Prior studies 
on stratified oxy-methane DACRS flames [40,41] were limited to 
methane fuel only, without the added complexities and benefits of 
hydrogen. In the present work, we introduce hydrogen into a DACRS 
oxy-fuel flame for the first time and systematically explore the combined 
effects of swirl intensity (velocity ratio), hydrogen blending, and oxygen 
fraction on flame behavior. This approach provides new insight beyond 
the scope of earlier studies revealing how hydrogen’s fast kinetics and 
enhanced reactivity interact with dual swirl flows to extend lean sta­
bility limits far into ultra-lean regimes. By measuring flame stability, 
temperature profiles, and emissions under various hydrogen and oxygen 
levels, this study delivers novel experimental data that fills the knowl­
edge gap on hydrogen-enriched oxy-combustion. The findings demon­
strate innovations in flame stabilization strategies (through H2 addition 
and optimized swirl) that were not achievable in methane-only flames. 
Overall, this work offers a distinct contribution over prior research by 
showing how hydrogen enrichment in an oxy-fuel DACRS combustor 
can push stability and efficiency boundaries, guiding the development of 
next-generation low-carbon gas turbine combustors.
2. Experimental setup
This study conducted combustion experiments using a Dual-Annular 
Counter-Rotating Swirl (DACRS) burner, as depicted in Fig. 1. The 
burner consists of two concentric tubes, which form separate plenums 
for the pilot and main gas streams. The inner tube carries a mixture of H2 
and CO2, serving as the pilot stream, while the annular space between 
the inner and outer tubes delivers the main gas stream, composed of a 
mixture of CH4,O2,and CO2. The inclusion of CO2 in both streams helps 
regulate flame stability by minimizing its destabilizing effect on the 
main oxy-methane flame. The destabilizing effect of CO2 on lean pre­
mixed flames reduces the laminar flame speed and radical concentra­
tions, makes ignition more difficult and the flame more prone to 
extinction. In the present DACRS design, however, we intentionally 
introduce CO2 into both the central pilot and annular main-streams. This 
mitigates sharp compositional and thermal gradients in the shear layer 
and preserves stabilizing recirculation zones. These combined effects 
prevent localized quenching and enhance overall flame anchoring and 
stability, even under significant dilution. Each tube is approximately 1 m 
long, ensuring proper mixing of the gases before combustion. To 
enhance flame stabilization, counter-rotating swirlers are installed at 
the tube outlets, with the inner swirler featuring eight vanes and the 
outer swirler having twelve. This swirling motion creates well-mixed,

A quartz tube encloses the burner’s combustion zone, acting as a 
shield against ambient air while allowing optical access for observation. 
Precise thermal mass flow controllers (Aalborg, ±0.5 % accuracy) 
regulate the gas flow rates, ensuring consistency in experimental con­
ditions. A CANON EOS R5 Digital SLR camera with a 35 mm prime lens 
was used to capture flame behavior, achieving an image resolution of 
approximately 30 pixels/mm within the combustion zone. Flue gas 
emissions were analyzed using a Sauermann E8500 industrial gas 
analyzer, positioned at the centerline of the quartz tube at 1 inch below 
its open end for accurate sampling. The analyzer is equipped with 
electrochemical sensors (O2, CO, NO/NO2, SO2 as available) and an 
NDIR CO2 channel. The analyzer was operated and maintained ac­
cording to the manufacturer’s guidance: on each power-up an auto-zero 
of 180 s was executed in clean, dry air with the sampling hoses 
disconnected before reconnecting and beginning measurements; an 
auto-zero was repeated every 45–60 min during testing to control 
baseline drift; the automatic condensate drain was left at the default 25 
min interval (shortened to ∼10 min for high-moisture points); and, at 
shutdown, the analyzer was purged in clean air for several minutes with 
the probe connected and then for ≥10 min with hoses disconnected to 
protect the sensors. Internal line and condensation filters were inspected 
periodically and replaced as needed, and the instrument was factory- 
recalibrated annually (certificate archived with the dataset). These 
procedures follow the E8500 quick-reference/operating guidance (auto- 
zero cycle and frequency, condensate drain, purge, filter maintenance, 
annual calibration). According to the manufacturer’s specifications, the 
accuracy of the CO sensor is approximately ±8 ppm for concentrations 
below 300 ppm, which is well within the range of the values reported in 
this study. Multiple sampling intervals were recorded for each operating 
point to ensure stable and repeatable reading.

The experimental procedure for evaluating combustion stability, 
temperature profiles, and emissions in the DACRS combustor involved a 
structured sequence tailored individually for each velocity ratio.

• Initially, the oxidizer streams comprising specified oxygen and car­

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Combustor stability limits
The lean blowout stability limit, defined as the minimum equiva­
lence ratio necessary for stable combustion, is crucial for gas turbine 
performance and operability. Figs. 2 and 3 present the blowout limits for 
the DACRS combustor using two different definitions of mixture frac­
tion: main-stream basis (Fig. 2) with main-stream oxygen fraction (OFm) 
and main-stream equivalence ratio (ϕm), versus global basis (Fig. 3) with 
overall oxygen fraction (OFg) and global equivalence ratio (ϕg). In both 
representations, similar trends are evident: lower velocity ratio, higher 
oxygen fraction, and higher hydrogen fraction each dramatically extend 
the lean blowout limit (allowing stable combustion at leaner mixtures).
Fig. 2 presents the blowout limits for different HFs as a function of 
the OFm at varying velocity ratios (Vr = 2.27, 3, and 3.87). Each subplot 
illustrates how the main-stream equivalence ratio (ϕm) changes with 
increasing OFm for specific HF levels of 0 %, 5 %, 10 %, and 15 %. As 
indicated in Fig. 2, the velocity ratio Vr strongly affects flame stability by 
altering swirl strength and recirculation. A lower Vr (i.e. relatively 
higher main-stream velocity) intensifies the swirl of the main oxy- 
methane flame, creating stronger recirculation zones that help anchor 
the flame. This enhanced internal recirculation provides a stable heat 
source in the flame’s core, increasing resistance to blowout under lean 
conditions [40]. Consequently, flames with low Vr can sustain com­
bustion at much leaner mixtures compared to those with higher Vr. In 
Fig. 2, the lowest Vr = 2.27 consistently achieves the lowest blowout 
equivalence ratios (e.g. ϕm = 0.35 0 at 34 % OFm and HF 15 %) whereas 
the highest Vr = 3.87 requires richer mixtures (ϕm = 0.41) to avoid 
blowout. In other words, decreasing Vr widens the lean blowout limit, 
improving operability in ultra-lean regimes.
Increasing the oxygen fraction significantly extends the lean blowout 
limits. A higher oxygen concentration intensifies the flame by raising the 
flame temperature and burning velocity, allowing stable combustion at 
lower fuel equivalence ratios. Experimentally, raising the OF from 25 % 
to 39 % was shown to lower the global equivalence ratio at blowout from 
roughly ϕg ≈0.55 down to 0.47 as reported in Ref. [52]. In other words, 
with more oxygen available, the flame can be sustained at about 15 % 
leaner overall fuel mixtures before extinguishing. This trend is evident in 
Fig. 2: as OFm increases along the x-axis (toward 30–34 % OFm), the 
blowout equivalence ratio drops markedly for all cases. The pilot flame 
in the dual-swirl configuration plays a key role here, supplying extra 
radicals and heat to stabilize the lean main flame when oxygen is 
abundant. At the highest tested OFm level (34 %), the combined 
pilot-main flame could remain stable at ϕm = 0.4 −0.5 (depending on 
Vr and HF), highlighting how oxygen enrichment enables ultra-lean 
operability.
Enriching the fuel with hydrogen profoundly influences flame sta­
bility and combustion intensity. Hydrogen has a higher flame speed and 
a wider flammability range than methane, so even modest HF enhances 
the reaction kinetics and helps sustain flames at lean conditions. As a 
result, adding hydrogen extends the lean operability of the combustor. 
For instance, in Fig. 2 increasing the HF from 0 % to 15 % (Fig. 2a–d) 
shifts the blowout curves downward: at a given oxygen level, the flame 
can survive at a lower ϕm when more H2 is present. With 15 % HF, the 
blowout equivalence ratio at 34 % OFm drops to 0.35 (vs. ~0.50 with 0 
% HF) for Vr = 2.27, indicating a noticeably leaner blowout limit. This 
agrees with experimental findings that hydrogen addition can widen the 
lean flammability limits and bolster flame stability [53]. Physically, 
hydrogen enhances radical production and burning velocity in the flame 
front, allowing it to remain anchored and stable even as the methane 
fraction (and overall fuel energy content) is reduced. Researchers 
observed that stable, well-anchored flames were achieved across a range 
of H2/CH4 mixtures, underscoring hydrogen’s role in widening 
combustor operability under lean, stratified conditions [38]. In other 
words, hydrogen blending helps delay the onset of blowout when the 
flame is very lean. At the same time, a higher HF increases the flame’s 
combustion intensity and heat release rate. Hydrogen-enriched flames 
burn more vigorously.
Comparing Figs. 2 and 3, the global blowout curves in Fig. 3 are all 
shifted to the left relative to the main-stream curves in Fig. 2 due to the 
influence of pilot stream. Because this stream contains no oxygen (just 
H2 and CO2), the overall combustor oxygen fraction, OFg, is always 
lower than its corresponding main-stream value, OFm, for any given 
condition. Conversely, the global equivalence ratio, ϕg, is higher than its 
main-stream counterpart, ϕm, because the former accounts for the pilot 
hydrogen as additional fuel. In other words, a condition appearing lean 
on a main-stream basis (low ϕm) registers a higher ϕg once the pilot fuel 
is accounted for. The difference between ϕg and ϕm is zero at HF = 0 % 
and amplifies as the HF is increased from Fig. 3a to d. This explains the 
crossover in the curves at higher oxygen fractions in Fig. 3c and d (i.e., at 
higher HF). It should be noted, however, that the qualitative trends 
remain the same in all the plots of Figs. 2 and 3, i.e., they show that 
enriching the oxidizer with oxygen yields leaner blowout limits. For 
instance, increasing OFm (or OFg) from the lowest tested values (~20 %

blowout boundary shifts to leaner ϕm as Rem increases. The trend is 
consistent with the expected physics: higher Rem strengthens turbulent 
transport and entrainment into the internal recirculation zone, 
increasing the convective return of heat and radicals to the flame base 
and improving local burning rates in the shear layer. In practical terms, 
the cases with low Vr/high Rem maintain attached, self-sustained reac­

the flame loses anchoring and the core cools. Within the present range 
the effect is monotonic; any potential high-strain quenching at very 
large Re was not encountered here.

specific combustor configuration and operating conditions. The axial

beginning 10 inches downstream of the burner faceplate (equivalent to 2 
inches below the quartz tube exit) and progressing upstream in 1-inch 
increments until reaching 1 inch above the burner faceplate, a

flame temperature, stability, and emissions. Hydrogen has a high flame 
speed and zero-carbon content, so even a modest HF addition can 
accelerate the combustion and raise flame temperatures. Under identical 
conditions, adding hydrogen increases the flame temperature (for

more compact and intense as HF increases. Numerical simulations in a 
DACRS burner showed that with increasing hydrogen fraction, the flame 
front moves closer to the burner, and the inner recirculation zone (IRZ) 
shrinks. At 100 % HF, the inner recirculation zone can disappear entirely 
(only an outer recirculation zone remains), and the flame’s maximum 
temperature can reach roughly 2350 K (significantly higher than typical 
methane flames) [38]. Such hydrogen-rich flames are strongly anchored 
and compact, indicating that most heat release occurs in a tight region 
near the burner. The axial temperature profile for high-HF cases thus 
shows an earlier and higher peak, as well as a steeper gradient, since the 
flame completes combustion faster and closer to the inlet.

Hydrogen and oxygen fractions significantly influence flame emis­

efficiency. Specifically, higher HFs enhance flame stability and accel­

capacity, directly impacting emissions of species such as O2 and CO. 
Additionally, the velocity ratio plays a crucial role by influencing swirl 
intensity, internal flow structures, and shear strength between main and 
pilot streams, thereby affecting the mixing quality and residence time of 
reactants, and consequently, the combustor’s emission performance. As 
demonstrated by previous analyses, stratified combustion in the DACRS 
technology effectively allows for lowering the main-stream equivalence 
ratio and extending operability limits, thus maintaining favorable 
environmental performance. In the following figures, combustion effi­

The present results show that as long as the flame is stable, oxygen 
fraction governs the combustion efficiency outcome, largely indepen­
dent of Vr. This insensitivity is evident in the overlapping trends for Vr 
= 2.27, 3.0, and 3.87 in Fig. 6b.
Fig. 6c illustrates combustion efficiency against the main-stream 
equivalence ratio ϕm (for different Vr), revealing that ϕm is the most 
dominant factor controlling ηc. As expected, leaner mixtures (lower ϕm, 
meaning fuel-lean overall) exhibit substantially lower combustion effi­
ciency, whereas mixtures closer to stoichiometric (higher ϕm) approach 
complete combustion. At very lean conditions, there is more oxygen 
present than the fuel can consume, so a considerable fraction of the 
oxidizer ends up unused in the exhaust. For example, at ϕm = 0.63 (a 
highly lean case), one observes a significant surplus of O2 in the flue 
gases. This corresponds to a low combustion efficiency since much of the 
oxygen (and thus chemical energy potential) is not utilized in oxidizing 
the fuel. As ϕm increases (i.e. the fuel flow is raised relative to oxygen), 
the flame consumes a larger portion of the available O2 and ηc rises 
sharply. The experimental data demonstrate this: holding OFm constant 
(e.g. 34 % OFm), increasing ϕm from about 0.63 to 0.73 caused the 
measured O2 in the exhaust to drop. In terms of efficiency, that means a 
much greater fraction of the oxygen consumed in burning the fuel at 
ϕm = 0.73 than at 0.63, indicating a more complete combustion as the 
mixture became less lean. Extrapolating this trend, at or near stoichio­
metric conditions the flame would be expected to use nearly all the 
oxygen to convert fuel into H2O and CO2, leaving virtually zero oxygen 
in the products. In other words, the combustion efficiency would 
approach 100 % in the limit of ϕm = 1 (barring minor losses due to 
dissociation or kinetics). This well-established inverse relationship be­
tween equivalence ratio and excess O2 (or, equivalently, between ϕm and 
combustion efficiency) is a fundamental characteristic of combustion 
systems: the leaner the mixture, the more oxidizer remains unused and 
the lower the combustion efficiency, and vice versa.
Importantly, Fig. 6c also indicates that the influence of ϕm on effi­
ciency is essentially independent of the velocity ratio. The data points 
for Vr = 2.27, 3.00, and 3.87 all collapse onto a single smooth curve, 
implying that swirl intensity has small effect on oxygen utilization when 
ϕm is varied. This is because ϕm directly dictates the global fuel/oxygen 
balance. At a given ϕm, each flame (regardless of Vr) has the same 
theoretical amount of oxygen available per unit fuel; thus, the fraction of 
O2 that can be consumed (and the resulting efficiency) is fundamentally 
set by the mixture ratio, not by aerodynamics. Stronger swirl may 
improve mixing and flame stability, but it cannot overcome a stoichio­
metric deficit or surplus of oxygen in terms of overall combustion 
completeness. For example, at ϕm = 0.63, all cases have abundant 
excess O2 and low ηc, even though the Vr = 2.27 flame is very well- 
stabilized; conversely at ϕm = 0.73, all cases achieve much higher ηc, 
even the high-Vr flame, because more fuel is available to consume the 
oxygen. The experimental observations confirm this convergence: 
changing Vr had no discernible effect on exhaust O2 levels at a given 
equivalence ratio. Thus, equivalence ratio governs the combustion ef­
ficiency above any flow-driven influences. Ensuring sufficient fuel 
relative to oxygen (avoiding overly lean operation) is paramount for 
high ηc, whereas operating too lean will incur efficiency penalties no 
matter the swirl strength or burner design.

Stronger main flames at lower velocity ratios (Vr = 2.27) are also 
insusceptible to the value of their oxygen fraction OFm in terms of the 
effluent CO concentration, see Fig. 7b. As mentioned earlier, the OFm has 
a convoluted impact on the combustion process. A higher OFm increases 
the strength of the main flame by boosting its oxygen supply and sup­
pressing the negative impact of dilution CO2. This boosts flame tem­
perature and CO oxidation, but it also fosters the dissociation of CO2 at 
higher flame temperatures. Lower OFm, on the other hand, deteriorates 
the strength of the main flame, reduces its temperature, and magnifies 
the impact of dilution CO2. The concentration of effluent CO should 
increase due to poor oxidation at lower temperatures and scarce oxygen 
supplies, but it should decrease due to diminished CO2 dissociation at 
lower temperatures. The result of these contradicting effects is that the 
main flame is immune to changing OFm, but only if it is aerodynamically 
strong enough at lower Vr. At higher Vr (3 or 3.87), however, the main 
flame becomes weaker, and reducing the OFm becomes detrimental, as 
evident by the higher effluent CO concentrations at OFm = 30 %. The 
effect of poor oxidation at lower temperatures and scarce oxygen sup­
plies prevails, and the combustion completeness is sacrificed. These 
findings align well with the experimental findings of El-Adawy et al. [40,
52], who showed similar reductions in CO emissions with higher oxygen 
fractions due to intensified combustion kinetics and temperature.

3.4. Effects of velocity ratio and hydrogen/oxygen fractions on flame 
macrostructure
Beyond the stability limits, Vr also influences the flame’s macro- 
structure (overall flame shape and size). Lower Vr (stronger main- 
stream swirl) tends to produce a more open, V-shaped flame with a 
larger expansion angle. This occurs because greater swirl promotes the 
flame to spread radially (aided by the Coand˘a effect), shortening the jet 
penetration and keeping the reaction zone attached near the burner exit 
[40]. A larger expansion angle has been correlated with improved lean 
blowout performance, as a broadly spreading flame anchors more 
effectively and is less prone to blowout. By contrast, at higher Vr 
(weaker swirl), the flame is narrower and farther downstream, with a 
weaker central recirculation zone. This narrower flame is less stable, and 
the lean blowout threshold shifts to higher equivalence ratios (i.e. the 
flame blows out sooner as the mixture is made leaner). In general, a low 
Vr (2.27) yields a compact flame with robust recirculation stabilization, 
whereas a high Vr (3.87) leads to a longer, lift-prone flame that blows 
out at richer conditions. These findings align with experimental obser­
vations that optimizing swirl (via Vr) is crucial for maintaining flame 
attachment and avoiding blowout in stratified swirl burners.
The flame macrostructure in the DACRS combustor exhibits sys­
tematic changes with velocity ratio, fuel composition, and oxidizer 
composition. The visual flame images in Figs. 8–10 characterize how the 
flame’s geometry responds to variations in HF, OFm, and ϕm, respec­
tively (with other parameters held constant in each case). A well- 
anchored, compact flame typically indicates efficient mixing and 
robust combustion, whereas a lifted or elongated flame suggests weaker 
stabilization, potentially leading to incomplete combustion or blowout. 
In this experiment, the flame’s appearance transitioned from broad, V- 
shaped profiles to narrower, cone-shaped profiles depending on the 
operating regime, consistent with changes in swirl intensity and reaction 
kinetics. Generally, flames that are broader (wider opening angle) tend 
to have better anchoring near the burner, while very narrow flames 
concentrate heat along the centerline and may form hotspots further 
downstream.

4. Conclusions
This work presented an experimental investigation of stratified oxy- 
hydrogen-methane combustion in a dual annular counter-rotating swirl 
(DACRS) gas turbine combustor, focusing on how velocity ratio, 
hydrogen enrichment, and oxygen fraction affect flame stability, tem­
peratures, and emissions. The key findings are summarized as follows. 
• Lean Blowout Limits: Increasing the hydrogen fraction and the 
main-stream oxygen fraction (OFm) significantly extended the lean 
blowout limit. at Vr = 2.27, and 34 % OFm, the minimum sustainable 
main-stream equivalence ratio dropped from ϕm = 0.50 (at 0 % HF) 
to ϕm = 0.346 (at 15 % HF). In global terms, the overall lean blowout 
limit improved from ϕg = 0.50 to ϕg = 0.361, a ~28 % expansion of 
the lean operating window. This enhancement is attributed to hy­
drogen’s high flame speed and reactivity, which stabilizes the flame 
at ultra-lean mixtures, and to oxygen enrichment, which raises flame 
temperature and burning rates. The combination of HF and high OFm 
allowed the flame to remain anchored and burning even when the 
main mixture was extremely diluted.
• Swirl Intensity (Velocity Ratio): The velocity ratio emerged as a 
critical parameter governing flame behavior. Lower Vr (e.g. 2.27, 
meaning a stronger main swirl) greatly improved flame anchoring 
and stability, while higher Vr (3.87, weaker swirl) led to elongated, 
less stable flames. At Vr = 2.27 the internal recirculation zone was 
stronger, which broadened the high-temperature flame region and 
helped resist blowout at lean conditions. In contrast, Vr = 3.87 
produced a narrower flame with a delayed heat-release peak and a 
greater tendency to lift off. As a result, the optimal operating con­
dition in this study was at Vr = 2.27 (with high HF and OFm): this 
yielded the lowest blowout equivalences (ϕm = 0.346) and a very 
stable, attached flame. At Vr = 3.87, by comparison, the flame blew 
off at richer mixtures and struggled to remain attached when lean.
• Flame Temperature and Structure: With hydrogen and oxygen 
enrichment, flame temperatures increased substantially. For 
instance, at HF = 15 % and OFm = 34 %, the peak centerline flame 
temperature reached ~1704 ◦C, compared to ~1330 ◦C with 0 % HF 
under the same conditions. The higher flame temperatures indicate 
more complete heat release and were accompanied by a more 
compact flame shape. High-speed images confirmed that flames with 
H2 were brighter and more anchored near the burner (often forming 
a tight conical shape), whereas flames without H2 or with lower O2 
were more diffuse (open V-shape) and extended further downstream. 
These observations highlight a synergistic effect: hydrogen’s fast 
chemistry and added heat release, together with enhanced O2-driven 
kinetics, produced a strongly anchored, high-intensity flame. 
Notably, the measured flame temperatures with enrichment 
approached the theoretical adiabatic flame temperatures, suggesting 
minimal heat losses in those cases. For context, the theoretical 
adiabatic flame temperature for the tested global conditions is 
~2137 ◦C. The measured peak temperatures (1704 ◦C with hydrogen 
enrichment and 1330 ◦C with methane-only fuel) are lower, as ex­
pected from heat losses, CO2 dilution, and incomplete equilibrium. 
The closer agreement of the H2-enriched flame with the adiabatic 
value confirms its higher combustion efficiency and stronger stabi­
lization relative to the methane baseline.
• Emissions and Combustion Efficiency: Under optimal conditions 
(Vr = 2.27, HF = 15 %, OFm = 34 %, ϕm ≈0.63), the combustor 
achieved nearly complete combustion, with CO emissions <5 ppm 
and only a few percent O2 in the exhaust (ηc = 87 %). Even at ultra- 
lean settings, the H2-enriched flames had very low CO (reflecting 
efficient oxidation of the fuel). By contrast, at high Vr = 3.87 with 
lower HF, incomplete combustion was evident that CO emissions 
rose to 50–65 ppm in those cases and significant O2 (10–15 %) 
remained unused in the flue. This underscores the importance of 
optimizing all parameters (swirl, HF, OFm) to avoid partial burning. 
Hydrogen enrichment proved crucial at high Vr: it cut CO emissions 
by strengthening the weak flame, whereas without H2 the high-Vr 
flame left much fuel unburned (high CO). Likewise, sufficient oxy­
gen levels (32–34OFm%) were needed to fully consume the fuel; and 
at 30 % OFm, combustion efficiency dropped. Overall, the lowest 
emissions and highest combustion efficiency coincided with the 
same conditions that gave the best stability, namely, strong swirl, H2 
enrichment, and oxygen-rich operation.
• Limitations of the present work: This study was conducted at at­
mospheric pressure in a laboratory-scale DACRS combustor and 
emphasizes macro-scale trends in flame shape, lean stability, tem­
perature, and emissions/combustion efficiency. Field-resolved di­
agnostics (e.g., OH* chemiluminescence, OH/CH2O-PLIF, (stereo-) 
PIV) were not deployed, and detailed reacting-flow CFD validation 
against measured scalar and velocity fields will be conducted in 
future works. The CFD model was done only for the temperature 
correction and. Gas temperatures were obtained from a centerline R- 
type thermocouple with a radiation correction (Blevins–Pitts with 
Whitaker’s correlation) but without complementary optical ther­
mometry. The operating matrix, velocity ratio, hydrogen fraction, 
oxygen fraction, and equivalence ratio was intentionally bound to 
isolate key couplings in an oxy-fuel methane/hydrogen flame; 
therefore, extrapolation beyond these ranges, high-pressure condi­
tions, or alternative injector geometries should be made with 
caution. Within these constraints, the results provide consistent, 
physically grounded trends that are expected to persist at elevated 
pressure when non-dimensional parameters (e.g., swirl number, 
Reynolds, Damk¨ohler, Karlovitz) are matched, but quantitative sta­
bility margins and detailed flame-flow interactions remain to be 
established under engine-relevant conditions.
• Recommendation and future outlooks: The trends observed here 
suggest clear directions for designing better oxy-fuel combustors. 
Strong swirl flows (lower Vr) are recommended to enhance flame 
holding and reduce blowout risk. Fuel enrichment with hydrogen (or 
other high-reactivity fuels) can substantially widen the lean limit and 
improve combustion completeness, especially in CO2-diluted envi­
ronments. Oxygen fraction in the oxidizer should be high enough 
(30–34 % by vol.) to compensate for CO2’s diluent effect. This en­
sures the flame temperature and burning velocity remain in a range 
that supports stable combustion. By following these guidelines, it is 
possible to achieve stable, lean, and clean combustion even without 
nitrogen. Importantly, since NOx formation is negligible in oxy-fuel 
combustion, future systems could consider operating at slightly 
higher global equivalence ratios than conventional air-fired gas 
turbines, to maximize efficiency and reduce excess O2 in the exhaust. 
Nonetheless, some degree of lean operation (or CO2 dilution) is still 
necessary to avoid excessively high flame temperatures. Therefore,

of swirl intensity, hydrogen blending, and oxygen enrichment, DACRS 
combustors can achieve enhanced flame stability, higher thermal effi­
ciency, and substantially lower pollutant emissions, paving the way 
toward cleaner and more sustainable combustion technologies.
