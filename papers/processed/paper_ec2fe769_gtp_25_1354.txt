Experimental Identification of the 
Operability Limits in Exhaust Gas 
Recirculation Rates Under 
Transient Control for a Microgas 
Turbine
Exhaust gas recirculation (EGR) has been identified as a key adaptation for gas turbines to 
mitigate the efficiency penalty of amine-based postcombustion carbon capture. By reducing 
the exhaust gas flow rate and increasing CO2 concentration, EGR enhances capture 
efficiency while also significantly lowering NOx emissions and residual O2, both of which 
influence amine stability. However, the impact and limitations of EGR in dynamic operation 
remain largely unexplored, representing a critical gap in operability assessments. 
Understanding these aspects can facilitate the development of control strategies and 
optimization algorithms where EGR serves as an additional control variable to mitigate 
pollutant emission peaks. To address this, a 3 kWe microgas turbine (mGT) setup with 
enhanced EGR was tested under sudden EGR variations, while monitoring emissions. 
Additionally, emissions were measured during hot starts with different pre-applied 
recirculation levels. Results indicate that NOx peaks occur during EGR transitions, and 
high EGR rates prevent successful turbine startup. Identifying these transient behaviors, in 
terms of emissions and combustion stability, provides initial insights into defining 
operability boundaries for EGR application. The development of control algorithms to 
manage EGR levels is left for future work. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4069725] 
Keywords: microgas turbine, exhaust gas recirculation, emissions, CO, NOx, transient 
operation 
Introduction
On the technological pathway to carbon neutrality, exhaust gas 
recirculation (EGR) increases the amine-based carbon capture 
efficiency by decreasing the mass flow of exhaust gases and 
increasing the CO2 concentration, as asserted by De Paepe et al. [1]. 
Additionally, EGR also enables to decrease NOx and O2 levels, both 
responsible for amine degradation as presented by Fosta˚s et al. [2]. 
However, high EGR rates are associated with conditions close to 
stoichiometry leading to carbon monoxide emission and risk of 
flameout as experimented by Thielens et al. [3]. Even if Verhaeghe 
et al. [4] have shown the strong benefit of EGR to carbon capture, the 
transient factors limiting recirculation have still to be investigated.
The impact of transient operations on gas turbine emissions is not 
well documented in the literature. Environmental policies, such as 
those developed by the European Union [5], regulate gas turbine 
emissions based on daily or monthly averages, even though 
continuous monitoring is required. As a result, peak emissions 
receive less attention due to the averaging process, and emissions 
during transient operations have not been a primary focus. However, 
growing environmental concerns are likely to increase the 
monitoring of transient emissions. In the context of carbon capture, 
peaks in NOx and CO concentrations are particularly relevant as they 
can affect amine stability and overall system performance.
Rosfjord and Cohen studied the impact of transient operation on 
turbine combustors and highlighted the risk of blowout in staged 
combustors [6]. When staging is used to reduce NOx emissions by 
distributing the temperature field, reductions in fuel flow can create 
excessively lean regions, leading to flame blowout. However, their 
study focused only on monotonic and oscillatory variations of the 
air/fuel flow rate, without considering exhaust gas recirculation [6].
In their report, Pavri and Moore [7] stated that while CO 
emissions can remain below 10 ppmvd during steady-state 
operation, higher levels are observed during ignition and load 
increases. However, as the share of renewable in the grid continues 
to grow, such load variations are expected to become more frequent.
Tenango-Pirın et al. numerically investigated combustion 
instabilities under varying operating conditions [8]. Their study 
revealed that pollutant formation, particularly CO and NOx, is 
highly sensitive to inlet air pressure. Variations in air mass flow 
influence flame dynamics, leading to the formation of intermediate

Thielens et al. [3] experimentally investigated advanced EGR 
rates up to the point of flameout. Their study first highlighted a key 
distinction between emissions and concentration. For NOx, the 
concentration follows a parabolic trend, peaking at around 60% 
EGR before declining. However, due to the reduction in exhaust 
mass flow enabled by EGR, total NOx emissions (the product of 
concentration and exhaust flow rate) continuously decrease as 
recirculation increases. Regarding carbon monoxide, its concen­
tration rises steadily with increasing EGR, driven by O2 depletion, 
which promotes incomplete combustion.
While some studies have examined pollutant evolution during 
transient operations, their interaction with exhaust gas recirculation 
has never been experimentally investigated or documented. To 
address this gap, a modified microgas turbine (mGT) setup with 
enhanced EGR capabilities has been equipped with an exhaust gas 
analyzer to assess the impact of hot startup and EGR variations on 
emissions. First, the steady-state concentration levels of O2, CO2, 
NOx, and CO are characterized, followed by an analysis of their 
evolution across a wide range of operating conditions (e.g., hot start 
and EGR change).
Methodology
Since conducting the experimental campaign on industrial-scale 
gas turbines is not economically feasible, an mGT was chosen for its 
adaptability and lower cost. The 3 kWe EnerTwin from MTT
V
R is 
equipped with an EGR valve (11–12 in Fig. 1) controlled by the 
machine’s internal regulation system. However, this built-in valve 
alone is insufficient to achieve high EGR rates. To overcome this 
limitation, the setup was modified with an external EGR loop 
(13–14) and a back-pressure valve (15–16). The external EGR loop 
is made with 100 mm diameter spiral tubes to provide a section high 
enough to decrease pressure losses through the recirculation loop. 
Both the internal (11–12), external (13–14), and back-pressure 
(15–16) valves are of the butterfly type. While butterfly valves do not 
provide precise closure angle or flow control, they offer a compact 
and cost-effective solution. When fully open, their disk aligns with 
the flow, minimizing pressure drops.
In the modified setup described in Fig. 1, incoming air (1) is 
preheated using an external electric resistor (2–3). The air is then 
compressed from atmospheric pressure (4) to 2.4 bar (5) and further 
preheated in the internal recuperator before entering the combustion 
chamber. Fuel from the gas line (17) is compressed (18) and injected 
into the combustion chamber. After combustion, the exhaust gases 
expand through the radial turbine (8), and their residual energy is 
utilized to heat the inlet water (14) to the desired temperature for 
cogeneration (15). Following the heat exchanger, the cooled exhaust 
gases can be recirculated either after the electric resistor (11) or 
before it (13).

The test campaign was conducted to analyze emissions across

Results and Discussion
To assess the impact of EGR on transient operations, the exhaust 
gas composition is first measured in steady-state for each 
configuration described in Table 2. These steady-state measure­
ments provide a reference for evaluating the changes induced by 
transient conditions. Emissions are then monitored during both EGR 
ratio transitions and hot starts to analyze their dynamic effects.
Steady-State. After the preheating phase, different EGR levels 
are applied, according to Table 2, and the concentrations of O2, CO2, 
CO, NO, and NO2 are measured with the gas analyzer. The dry molar 
concentrations of CO2 and O2 follow the theoretical law as 
represented in Figs. 2 and 3, respectively.
The CO2 concentration starts at 1.3% with no EGR and reaches a 
maximum of 7.3% at 81% EGR (Fig. 2). Due to the high excess air 
used (k ¼ 8:5) at 0% EGR, the oxygen content in the exhaust gases 
remains relatively high, at 18.8%, as shown in Fig. 3. At the highest 
tested EGR level, the dry molar O2 concentration decreases to 8.5%, 
resulting in a normalized air-fuel ratio of 1.6. Under stoichiometric 
conditions, the CO2 concentration could theoretically reach 12.1%, 
with oxygen levels dropping to zero. However, operating at such 
rich conditions would be too restrictive for the combustor. The 
combustor architecture divides the chamber into a primary and a 
secondary zone, with dilution air introduced in the latter. Supplying 
the inlet with a stoichiometric air–fuel mixture would lead to locally 
incomplete conditions in the primary zone, given the air fraction 
allocated to dilution in the secondary zone [10].

measured at 27 ppm, CO concentrations remain below 30 ppm up to 
60% EGR. Beyond this point, CO levels increase sharply due to 
oxygen depletion in the combustion chamber, as previously reported

Exhaust Gas Recirculation Transient. During steady-state 
operation, the exhaust gas recirculation rate is suddenly adjusted 
between different levels. The changes in EGR rates are detailed in 
Table 3, and the corresponding O2, CO2, NOx, and CO concen­
trations in the exhaust gases (16 in Fig. 1) are presented. Both CO2 
and O2 concentrations do not exhibit peak emissions when the EGR 
rate is adjusted. Instead, their values increase gradually from one 
steady-state to the next. However, the time required for stabilization 
increases as the target EGR level becomes higher. The settling times 
for these transitions are presented in Table 4, showing the time it 
takes for the exhaust gas concentrations to stabilize. The settling 
time, t0:2, is defined as the duration from when the dry O2 
concentration begins to change until it stabilizes within a 0.2% 
absolute window around the final steady-state concentration. As 
shown in Table 4, the settling time t0:2 can reach up to 62 s for the 
largest transitions and decreases as the change in EGR rate becomes 
smaller. This increase in stabilization time is linked to the time 
required for the recirculation process to fully establish itself.
Changes in the EGR ratios lead to distinct NOx emissions during 
the transient phase, categorized into one of the three types presented 
in Fig. 6. Starting from zero recirculation, various EGR levels were 
applied, and the corresponding NOx emissions are summarized in 
Table 5. For transitions to EGR rates below 30%, the NOx emissions 
evolve smoothly from the previous steady-state level to the next, 
following the behavior depicted by type 1 in Fig. 6. However, 
experiments revealed a peak in NOx emissions when transitioning 
from 0% to EGR levels above 60%. This peak increases as the EGR 
rate is raised. For EGR levels exceeding 70%, resulting in a NOx 
concentration lower than that observed without EGR at steady-state 
(as shown in Fig. 5), the emission evolution follows type 3, as 
defined in Fig. 6. For intermediate levels, before reaching 70% EGR, 
the emission evolution corresponds to type 2.

No significant change in electric power production is observed 
during the transition between EGR levels. Thielens et al. [3] 
previously demonstrated that increasing EGR can slightly enhance 
power production due to the cooling effect of exhaust gases when 
mixed with ambient air. However, in this setup, the preheating 
system ensures a constant temperature at the compressor inlet, 
eliminating this effect. Additionally, since no significant pressure 
drop occurs in the recirculation loop, the power output of the 
microgas turbine remains unchanged throughout the transition 
between EGR levels.
Hot Start. A hot start is defined as the startup of the mGT 
immediately after a shutdown, meaning that all components (e.g., 
heat exchangers) remain preheated. Compared to a cold start, hot 
start testing is more convenient, as no cooling time is required 
between experiments at different EGR levels. Additionally, hot 
starts provide a lower bound for CO emissions since the preheated 
combustion chamber enhances combustion efficiency, reducing the 
amount of incomplete products.
During the startup phase, the shaft is driven electrically to 
pressurize the combustion chamber. Once the required conditions 
are met, fuel is injected and ignited, causing the oxidizer 
concentration to drop from the ambient 21%, as illustrated in Fig. 
7. Following the acceleration phase, the mGT stabilizes at its 
nominal operating conditions. As the gas flow gradually decreases, 
the oxygen concentration in the exhaust gases rises until it reaches 
the steady-state value, as shown in Fig. 3.
Regarding CO2, its evolution follows an opposite trend to the one 
of oxygen, as shown in Fig. 8. Before combustion begins, no carbon- 
containing species are present in the exhaust gases. However, during 
the acceleration phase, the additional fuel consumption leads to a 
sharp increase in CO2 concentration, resulting in a peak significantly 
higher than the steady-state value.
For both oxygen and carbon dioxide, the stabilization time of their 
concentrations in the exhaust gases increases as a larger fraction of 
exhaust gases is recirculated from the startup phase. Additionally, 
the two-zone architecture of the combustion chamber prevents 
ignition at extreme EGR rates (e.g., 81%). In the highest EGR case 
referenced in Table 2, a flameout was observed during startup due to 
insufficient oxygen availability in the primary combustion zone.
The evolution of CO emissions is depicted and justified in Fig. 9. 
Before ignition, CO is absent from the exhaust gases as no 
combustion occurs. Upon ignition, a sharp peak in CO emissions 
appears, followed by a rapid decline as fresh air enters the 
combustion chamber and increased heat release enhances combus­
tion completeness. This initial CO peak is attributed to the low 
equivalence ratio at ignition. As the turbine shaft accelerates, more 
air flows into the mGT, reducing CO formation. Once sufficient air is 
available to sustain complete combustion, flame dilution and the 
resulting temperature decrease become the dominant factors 
influencing CO production. Consequently, CO levels evolve with 
the air–fuel ratio, as greater combustion dilution lowers flame 
temperature, promoting incomplete combustion and increased CO 
formation.
The impact of EGR on CO emissions is illustrated in Fig. 10, 
where the initial peak is clearly represented. EGR increases CO 
emissions during the starting phase. Although no CO should be 
present before ignition, some was detected at the highest EGR level 
due to noise in the gas analyzer. Since CO is toxic to the analyzer 
measurement cells, a dilution factor of five is applied to protect 
them, which increases baseline noise. Additionally, if residual CO

The evolution of NOx concentration in the mGT during the startup 
phase is shown in Fig. 11. A peak in NOx emissions occurs at 
ignition, primarily due to the extended residence time caused by the 
low air flow rate and suboptimal combustion conditions. Limited air 
flow results in poor mixing and the formation of hot spots, both of 
which contribute to increased NOx production. Following this initial 
peak, the additional air dilutes the combustion process and lowers 
the flame temperature. Since NOx formation is primarily governed 
by the Zeldovich’s mechanism, the temperature reduction directly 
decreases NOx emissions. Consequently, NOx levels evolve 
inversely with the equivalence ratio.
Although exhaust gas recirculation slightly reduces the initial 
NOx peak during the starting phase (Fig. 12), the effect remains 
minimal. This is because, at ignition, the combustion process has not 
yet fully experienced the impact of recirculated exhaust gases, such 
as the reduction in oxygen concentration and the increased presence 
of CO2 in the combustion chamber. While considering the 
maximum EGR of 73%, the reduction in NOx becomes visible due 
to reduced O2 contents.
Good Practices on Control Strategies. Environmental policies 
are expected to become increasingly stringent in the coming years, 
regulating not only the daily average emissions but also peak 
emissions. Moreover, the growing share of renewable energy in the 
grid will force gas turbines to operate more frequently as peak units, 
significantly increasing transient operations. Additionally, NOx and 
CO have been identified as pollutants that impact the chemical 
stability of amines. While their exact influence has yet to be 
quantified, experimental work has highlighted best practices and key 
considerations for managing EGR effectively.
 Maximizing EGR under the CO emission limit in steady-state 
operation: Achieving the highest feasible level of exhaust gas 
recirculation during steady-state operation helps reduce the 
overall exhaust gas mass flow, increase CO2 concentration, and 
lower O2 and NOx concentrations. These effects benefit amine- 
based carbon capture but are constrained by CO emission 
limits.
 Starting without EGR: Although initiating gas turbine 
operation with EGR does not impact NOx emissions, it 
increases the peak in CO emissions. To avoid unnecessary 
CO peaks, it is preferable to start without EGR and gradually 
increase the recirculation rate after the initial CO peak has been 
encountered.

Conclusion
Exhaust gas recirculation is employed in amine-based carbon 
capture to increase CO2 concentration and reduce the exhaust gas 
mass flow rate. However, EGR also influences NOx and CO 
emissions, both in steady-state and transient conditions. To address 
the lack of experimental data on EGR’s impact on emissions during 
transient phenomena, a 3 kWe mGT from MTT
V
R was modified and 
equipped with an external EGR loop. Seven operating states, each 
characterized by a different EGR rate, were defined. Emissions were 
measured using a gas analyzer during transitions as well as during 
startup.
In steady-state operation, the dry molar concentrations of O2 and 
CO2 in the exhaust gases align with theoretical predictions. However, 
EGR significantly affects NOx and CO production. Regarding NOx, 
increasing the EGR rate initially leads to a rise in NOx concentration 
before a significant reduction due to oxygen depletion. This depletion 
also causes a sharp increase in CO levels at high EGR rates.
During transient operation, NOx peaks occur when transitioning 
between EGR levels, with peak intensity increasing as the difference 
between EGR levels grows. In contrast, CO emissions transition 
more gradually between steady-state values corresponding to 
different EGR levels.
While no CO2 or O2 peaks are observed during EGR transitions, 
startup conditions lead to peak emissions. Both CO and NOx exhibit 
peaks during startup, but while the NOx peak remains unaffected by 
the initial EGR level, the CO peak intensifies with higher EGR levels.
These experiments highlight challenges related to CO and NOx 
emissions during startup and phase transitions when applying EGR. 
Depending on environmental policies and the tolerance of the 
carbon capture unit, EGR implementation may need to be 
constrained to minimize or mitigate transient pollutant emissions.
Future Work
While EGR has demonstrated limitations in terms of emissions 
during transient phases, this study has only focused on the increase 
in EGR and the startup phase. However, transient phenomena also 
occur during shutdown and EGR reduction, and they remain 
unexplored.
With respect to performance, this study has been limited to 
emissions, and the impact of transients on combined heat and power 
production and efficiency has not been investigated. Nevertheless, 
the impact on the heat production is expected to be limited due to the 
thermal inertia of the system during transients.
Furthermore, given the growing interest in hydrogen, future 
studies should explore how transitioning from natural gas to 
hydrogen-containing blends affects cycle emissions. Finally, the 
research could focus on how O2 or H2 enriched combustion could be 
beneficial toward a further extension of the EGR ratio and as such an 
enhanced CO2 concentration in the flue gases. A further enhance­
ment of the CO2 concentration in flue gases will facilitate amine- 
based post carbon capture systems worldwide.
