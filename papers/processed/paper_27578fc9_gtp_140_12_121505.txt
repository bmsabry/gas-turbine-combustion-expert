Design of Acoustic Liner in
Small Gas Turbine Combustor
Using One-Dimensional
Impedance Models
The side-wall cooling liner in a gas turbine combustor serves main purposes—heat trans-
fer and emission control. Additionally, it functions as a passive damper to attenuate ther-
moacoustic instabilities. The perforations in the liner mainly convert acoustic energy into
kinetic energy through vortex shedding at the oriﬁce rims. In the previous decades, sev-
eral analytical and semi-empirical models have been proposed to predict the acoustic
damping of the perforated liner. In the current study, a few of the models are considered
to embody the transfer matrix method (TMM) for analyzing the acoustic dissipation in a
concentric tube resonator with a perforated element and validated against experimental
data in the literature. All models are shown to quantitatively appropriately predict the
acoustic behavior under high bias ﬂow velocity conditions. Then, the models are applied
to maximize the damping performance in a realistic gas turbine combustor, which is
under development. It is found that the ratio of the bias ﬂow Mach number to the porosity
can be used as a design guideline in choosing the optimal combination of the number and
diameter of perforations in terms of acoustic damping. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4040765]
Introduction
In the areas of modern aero-engines and industrial gas turbines,
it is a necessity not only to maintain high performance but also to
cut down fuel consumption and reduce pollutant emissions. One
of the promising solutions for achieving these requirements is to
adapt a lean premixed combustion technology. However, this type
of combustion is intrinsically susceptible to a combustion instabil-
ity, which is a coupling mechanism between acoustic pressure
ﬂuctuations and unsteady heat release oscillations. Consequently,
this can lead to a crucial damage to the combustor and turbine
parts, and reduce the system efﬁciency and life of the engine com-
ponents, such as rotor blades [1].
To minimize damages resulting from the combustion instability
in a gas turbine combustor, one typical approach is to optimize
the acoustic absorption capabilities of the perforated combustor
liner. It is well known that perforations can generally be applied
to damp acoustic perturbations. The original functions of the liner
are to contain the combustion process and to facilitate the distribu-
tion of air to all various combustor zones. Of the several combus-
tors currently in service, up to 20% of the total combustor air ﬂow
is employed in liner wall cooling [2]. As previously mentioned,
another particular feature of the liners is that they are proven to
have a signiﬁcant effect on the damping mechanisms of the com-
bustor acoustics. The liner damping mainly involves vortices gen-
erated over the rims of the perforations. When incident sound
waves interact with and destabilize shear layers formed at the ori-
ﬁce rims, vortex rings are formed and the sound energy is con-
verted into kinetic energy, which is dissipated by the surrounding
ﬂuid [3–7].
A number of research groups have reported their results on the
damping effect of cooling liners or oriﬁces, because they were
proven to be effective in suppressing combustion instabilities in
rocket engines in the 1960s [8,9]. Most recently, Zhao and Li [5],
Lahiri and Bake [10], and Lahiri [11] presented excellent reviews
summarizing the status, challenges, and progress of implementing
acoustic dampers, such as perforated liners on various systems.
According to their reviews, most acoustic liner designs in the
practical ﬁeld are conducted using analytical or semi-empirical
approaches because the holes involved are extremely small to
accurately simulate them with most computational codes (Typi-
cally, the hole diameters range from 0.64 to 8.82 mm [12].).
There exists a few analytical or semi-empirical modeling
approaches to calculate the damping characteristics in perfora-
tions. The least cumbersome and simplest analytic modeling
method is to use a zero-dimensional approach [5,6] where the
acoustic impedance terms are educed from measurements of mul-
tiple liner geometries. Since Howe [4,13] presented the ﬁrst model
for a perforated plate with bias ﬂow based on Crandall’s acoustic
theory [14], there have been comprehensive impedance model
developments. His model is regarded as classical standards for
modeling bias ﬂow oriﬁces or perforations. Jing and Sun [15,16]
and Bellucci et al. [17,18] presented an extended and/or modiﬁed
Howe model and conducted various parametric studies on perfo-
rated plates with bias ﬂow. Similarly, combining various empiri-

However, these zero-dimensional models cannot globally con-
sider the effect of the acoustic ﬁeld in a duct where traveling or
standing waves exist. Especially, in a gas turbine combustor,
acoustic damping characteristics of liners are determined by vari-
ous interactive inﬂuences of acoustic ﬁelds in the combustor (or
duct), annulus (or cavity), and in-between perforations. These
complex interactions between duct acoustics and perforation
impedance can generally be modeled by using the transfer matrix
method (TMM), which was formulated from the electrical circuit
theory [21]. In this method, acoustic ﬁelds in the duct and cavity
are described by their respective differential equations. Thereafter,
they are combined by several small control volumes.
The objective of the current study is to brieﬂy summarize vari-
ous zero-dimensional impedance models for perforated liners with
bias ﬂow. Subsequently, the selected impedance models are
applied to the one-dimensional (1D) TMM model to evaluate the
acoustic damping performance of side-wall liners. To validate our
liner acoustic model, Lahiri’s [10,11] measurement results are

Definition of Perforation and Acoustic Damping Parame-

impedance in perforated liners with bias ﬂow. The combustor

Transfer Matrix Method for One-Dimensional Acoustic
Analysis. The plane wave reﬂection and absorption coefﬁ-
cients of a perforated liner with a normal sound incidence can
be directly obtained when its impedance is given. This is the
situation under which the aforementioned impedance models
are validated. A typical example of an acoustic experiment
setup in an impedance tube with a perforated plate is shown
in Fig. 2.
However, when the same liner with an unpartitioned cavity is
mounted as a side-wall with a grazing sound incidence, such as in
a gas turbine combustor liner, the sound propagation within the
cavity is required to be taken into account. For this type of liner,
the acoustics inside the duct and cavity are coupled through the
impedance boundary of the perforated liner in-between them.
These complex interactions between duct acoustics and perfora-
tion impedance can be generally modeled by using TMM, which
is a convenient approach for modeling one-dimensional, linear
dynamic systems and is well described in several textbooks
[21,37] and papers [10,11]. In this paper, the general description
is summarized as follows.

Model Validation
Selected Validation Cases. In order to suggest an optimal
acoustic impedance model for a perforated liner with a bias ﬂow
and to validate the suggested model, Lahiri’s measurement results
[10,11] are chosen in the current study. For characterizing the

Conclusions
Four popular zero-dimensional impedance models, such as the
models of Jing, Bauer, Betts, and Elnady for a perforated plate are
incorporated into the transfer matrix method in order to analyze
the acoustic damping in a two-duct system with a perforated ele-
ment. Thereafter, the models are validated against experimental
data from the literature under high bias ﬂow velocity conditions.
The validation results show that all of the impedance models con-
sidered in this study can appropriately represent the quantitative
effects of the bias ﬂow Mach number and porosity of the perfo-
rated liner against the measurement. The impedance models are
then applied to a small aircraft gas turbine combustor under devel-
opment to ﬁnd an optimal liner geometry in terms of the maxi-
mum acoustic energy absorption at a given design combustor
conﬁguration and operating condition. The Elnady model predicts
slightly lower damping characteristics under a given condition
than the other impedance models do. However, this deviation is
negligibly small. Moreover, it is found that the ratio of the bias
ﬂow Mach number to the porosity can be used as a design guide-
line in choosing the optimal combination of the number and diam-
eter of perforations in terms of the acoustic damping. The current
ﬁnding will be further optimized through the consideration of
other design disciplines of the combustor liner, such as heat trans-
fer and emission in our ongoing development processes.
