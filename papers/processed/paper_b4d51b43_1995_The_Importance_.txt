The Importance of the Nitrous 
Oxide Pathway to NOx in 
Lean-Premixed Combustion 
This study addresses the importance of the different chemical pathways responsible for 
NOx formation in lean-premixed combustion, and especially the role of the nitrous 
oxide pathway relative to the traditional Zeldovich pathway. NOx formation is modeled 
and computed over a range of operating conditions for the lean-premixed primary zone 
of gas turbine engine combustors. The primary zone, of uniform fuel-air ratio, is 
modeled as a micromixed well-stirred reactor, representing the flame zone, followed by a 
series of plug flow reactors, representing the postflame zone. The fuel is methane. The 
fuel-air equivalence ratio is varied from 0.5 to 0.7. The chemical reactor model permits 
study of the three pathways by which NOx forms, which are the Zeldovich, nitrous 
oxide, and prompt pathways. Modeling is also performed for the well-stirred reactor 
alone. Three recently published, complete chemical kinetic mechanisms for the C1-C2 
hydrocarbon oxidation and the NOx formation are applied and compared. Verification 
of the model is based on the comparison of its NOx output to experimental results 
published for atmospheric pressure jet-stirred reactors and for a 10 atm. porous-plate 
burner. Good agreement between the modeled results and the measurements is obtained 
for most of the jet-stirred reactor operating range. For the porous-plate burner, the 
model shows agreement to the NOx measurements within a factor of two, with close 
agreement occurring at the leanest and coolest cases examined. For lean-premixed 
combustion at gas turbine engine conditions, the nitrous oxide pathway is found to be 
important, though the Zeldovich pathway cannot be neglected. The prompt pathway, 
however, contributes small-to-negligible NOx. Whenever the NOx emission is in the 15 
to 30 ppmv (15 percent 02, dry) range, the nitrous oxide pathway is predicted to 
contribute 40 to 45 percent of the NOx for high-pressure engines (30 atm), and 20 to 35 
percent of the NOx for intermediate pressure engines (10 atm). For conditions 
producing NOx of less than 10 ppmv (15 percent 02, dry), the nitrous oxide 
contribution increases steeply and approaches 100 percent. For lean-premixed combus-
tion in the atmospheric pressure jet-stirred reactors, different behavior is found. All three 
pathways contribute; none can be dismissed. No universal behavior is found for the 
pressure dependence of the NOx. It does appear, however, that lean-premixed 
combustors operated in the vicinity of 10 atm have a relatively weak pressure 
dependence, whereas combustors operated in the vicinity of 30 atm have an 
approximately square root pressure dependence of the NOx.

Chemical Reactor Model 
The baseline chemical reactor model employed consists of 
a micromixed well-stirred reactor (WSR), followed immedi-
ately by a series of plug-flow reactors (PFR). Usually, the 
WSR is assigned the smallest volume that will permit reac-
tion for a given mass flow rate of reactants. This is termed 
"incipient blowout." It is defined to be 101 percent of the 
volume for blowout of the WSR. 
The WSR provides for the initiation and stabilization of 
the combustion process, and represents the flame zone. The 
PFR provides for the continuation of the combustion pro-
cess, and given adequate mean residence time, permits the 
combustion process to approach completion. It represents 
the post flame zone. Together, the WSR and PFR, of uni-
form fuel-air equivalence ratio, are taken to simulate the 
complete lean-premixed combustion process. 
A variation of the baseline model is used to assess the 
sensitivity of the NOx to the fraction of the combustor 
volume assumed to be the WSR. In this variation, the WSR 
component is increased progressively until it comprises the 
complete combustor, at which point the PFR component is 
eliminated. In an actual combustor, this variation would 
require the inlet streams to penetrate increasingly deeper 
into the combustor, and thus, produce an increasingly larger 
region that is intensely mixed and is high in free radical 
concentrations. The effect of this, as shown later, is to 
increase the NOx. 
Another variation of the model involves removal of the 
WSR. Combustion stabilization is then accomplished by in-
cluding one or more recycle zones, by which hot, burned 
gases are brought forward to the initial plug-flow reactor 
stage. In a previous study of lean-premixed combustion for 
gas turbine engines [4], this variation was found to cause little 
change in the NOx compared to that predicted by the base-
line model, and thus, is not reconsidered herein. 
In order to validate the baseline model, and the WSR 
model alone, test cases were computed and compared to 
datasets for laboratory combustors. These datasets include 
atmospheric pressure jet-stirred reactor measurements from 
two laboratories, and porous-plate burner data for 10 atm. 
Following this phase of the study, the baseline model was 
applied to a range of lean-premixed gas turbine combustor 
operating conditions in order to determine the following: 
1 The effect of varying the volume of the flame zone (i.e., 
the WSR) relative to the volume of the postflame zone 
(i.e., the PFR). 
2 The effects of varying the combustor pressure (1, 10, and 
30 atm) and inlet temperature (300, 600, and 800 K).

The Chemical Kinetic Mechanisms 
Three complete chemical kinetic mechanisms of hydrocar-
bon oxidation and NOx chemistry are used in the present 
study. These three mechanisms are those of Drake and Blint 
[1], Miller and Bowman [2], and Michaud et al. [3]. 
The kinetic mechanism of Drake and Blint, hereafter 
referred to as the DB mechanism, has been used without 
deletions. Reaction (9), with rate data as estimated in [2], has 
been added to the DB mechanism; and the pressure sensitivi-
ties of reaction (4) and the CH4 dissociation reaction have 
been extended from 20 to 35 atm. The rate data selected for 
reaction (12) are from [5], since these have been found to 
give the best overall agreement between the model results 
and the jet-stirred reactor and porous-plate datasets consid-
ered. The DB kinetic mechanism, which was developed from 
verification of laminar, premixed flame data taken at the 
burner surface, shows an enhanced importance of the prompt 
NOx pathway [1]. 
The kinetic mechanism of Miller and Bowman, hereafter 
referred to as the MB mechanism, has been used without 
deletions. The dissociation reactions for C2H6, C2H5, C2H3, 
CH4, and reaction (14) listed below, have been corrected for 
elevated pressure. The MB kinetic mechanism is the succes-
sor to the Glarborg et al. mechanism [5], which was based on 
jet-stirred reactor data. 
CH2CO + M = CH2 + CO + M 
(14) 
The kinetic mechanism of Michaud et al., hereafter referred 
to as the MWF mechanism, has been used without deletions. 
The dissociation reactions for C2H6, C2H5, C2H3, CH4, 
reaction (14), as well as reactions (15) and (16) listed below, 
have been corrected for elevated pressure. The MWF kinetic 
mechanism was developed using porous-plate burner data 
and includes the addition of the singlet O atom chemistry to 
the Zeldovich and nitrous oxide pathways.

These three kinetic mechanisms have been selected for this 
study, since they represent recent developments in NOx 
chemistry for combustion sources. The MB mechanism has 
been used in several studies, the DB mechanism provides 
enhanced prompt NOx rates, and the MWF mechanism 
provides alternative possibilities regarding the O atom attack 
on N2 and the formation of N20. These mechanisms cover a 
useful range of chemical kinetic possibilities for NOx forma-
tion in gas-fired, high-pressure combustors. 
Laboratory Reactor NOx Measurements 
In the next two sections of this paper, results for NOx are 
shown and discussed for two distinctly different types of 
laboratory combustion reactors. Results obtained by the 
model applied to the conditions of these reactors are exam-
ined. The two types of reactor are jet-stirred reactors and a 
porous-plate burner. 
Jet-stirred reactors, when operated at short residence times 
(of a few milliseconds) can approach the well-stirred reactor 
(WSR) condition. At this condition, the rate of mixing is fast 
compared to the rate of chemistry for the process being 
studied, which in this case is the formation of NOx. The 
condition is characterized as zero dimensional. Results for 
NOx are shown in the next section, which appear to satisfy 
the WSR condition. Results are also shown for jet-stirred 
reactors operated so that the mixing is not fast compared to 
the chemistry. In this situation, the finite mixing rate, to-
gether with wall heat loss, promotes gradients and structure 
within the reactor and influences the NOx formation. In-
creases in NOx because of these influences are shown. How-
ever, decreases in NOx because of a finite mixing rate can 
also occur, and are shown. 
The porous-plate burner, on the other hand, is essentially 
a one-dimensional plug-flow reactor (PFR). Although the 
initial region of burner requires backmixing and back-diffu-
sion for stabilization of the flame (the model uses a WSR 
zone), under lean-premixed conditions only a relatively small 
amount of the NOx emitted from the burner is formed in this 
region. This region generally has a short mean residence time 
compared to that of the overall burner. Thus, the NOx is 
formed predominantly in the bulk of the burner, which is 
well modeled as a PFR. 
Jet-Stirred Reactor NOx 
Jet-stirred reactors permit NOx to be examined under 
conditions of intense recirculation of the burning gases into 
the fresh reactants. Furthermore, they provide databases for 
the development of NOx mechanisms, e.g., see [5]. 
Published results on NOx formation for lean, premixed, 
methane-fired jet-stirred reactors are reviewed and com-
pared to the model predictions. The model predictions are 
based on the measured temperatures, pressures, mean resi-
dence times, and inlet reactant concentrations. The NOx 
yields are in the low (< 10 ppmv) and modest (< 100 ppmv) 
ranges. The data are based on the actual 0 2 (dry) concentra-
tions. The reactor pressure is ambient, which is either 1.0 or 
0.92 atm (depending on the elevation of the laboratory site). 
Three types of results are shown, including: (1) NOx trends, 
(2) comparison to the model, and (3) NOx pathways.

1 The importance of the nitrous oxide pathway to NOx is 
demonstrated by the results for the combustion of the CO 
fuel. The nitrous oxide pathway contributed nearly all of 
NOx. Furthermore, the rate of NOx formation for the CO 
combustion was markedly greater than for the methane com-
bustion (at similar temperatures). Because of the modest 
temperatures of the CO combustion, the Zeldovich pathway 
was weak. Model predictions indicate that less than 10 per-
cent of the NOx measured for the CO combustion was 
caused by the Zeldovich pathway (see Table 1). The CO 
combustion with the nearly dry air gave high O atom levels, 
which promoted N20 formation by reaction (4 reverse), fol-
lowed by N20 oxidation to NO by reaction (5). The nitrous 
oxide was not destroyed by reaction (7), because of the low 
levels of the H atom. For the methane combustion, however, 
the O atom levels were lower, and the H atom levels were 
considerably greater. Thus, less N20 was formed, and that 
which was formed was destroyed by reaction (7). Markedly 
less NO, was formed. 
An interesting additional aspect of the results for the CO 
combustion is the double-valued behavior of the NOx. The 
lower part of the curve corresponds to the usual behavior of 
the reactor: i.e., the reactor temperature increased with 
decreasing mean residence time. The upper part of the 
curve, however, corresponds to the short mean residence 
times. The reactor was near blowout. The temperature (and 
free radical concentrations) increased with increasing mean 
residence time, resulting in the maximum in the NOx forma-
tion rate at the temperature of about 1450K. 
2 For the methane combustion, typical behavior is shown 
in Fig. 1 for temperatures greater than about 1600 K (corre-
sponding to the shortest residence times run). That is, the 
rate of formation of the NOx increased with increasing 
temperature, and the measured and modeled NOx agree 
within a factor of about two. However, for temperatures 
below 1600 K, involving residence times of about 15 ms and

temperatures (and longest residence times) examined, the 
rate of NOx formation measured actually increased with

the heat loss at the walls of the reactor, the temperatures in 
the center of the reactor, in the vicinity of the inlet jets, were 
hotter than the temperatures measured for the bulk of the 
reactor. This effect is explored in detail in [7]. 
Measured and modeled values for selected cases from Fig. 
1 are given in Table 1. The predictions for the methane cases 
show that all the pathways contribute to the NOx, except for 
a very weak Zeldovich contribution for the 1445 K case. As 
explained above, close agreement between the measured and 
modeled results is not expected for the methane cases be-
cause of the relatively long mean residence time run. On the 
other hand, the CO combustion more nearly satisfied the 
WSR condition, because of the modest temperatures and low 
H 20 concentrations. For these cases the lack of agreement 
between the measured and modeled results (using the MB 
and MWF mechanisms) is caused by uncertainty in the 
measured H 20 inlet air concentration. The CO combustion 
is very sensitive to changes in the H 20 content of nearly dry 
air. In [6], close agreement is obtained between the measured 
and modeled results for the NOx when the measured O atom 
concentrations are used in a kinetic mechanism involving the 
nitrous oxide and Zeldovich pathways. 
For the methane combustion, the DB, MB, and MWF 
mechanisms predict about the same total NOx. The differ-
ence evident is the favoring by the DB mechanism of prompt 
NOx over NOx formed by the nitrous oxide pathway. For the 
CO combustion, the reduced prediction by the DB mecha-
nism of the NOx formed by the nitrous oxide pathway is 
significant. The MB and MWF mechanisms appear to offer 
the best representation of the nitrous oxide pathway.

Porous-Plate Burner NOx 
Attention is now directed to the porous-plate burner oper-
ated at elevated pressure. The questions are (1) whether the 
model and chemical kinetic mechanisms are valid for this 
burner, and (2) whether the NOx formation is similar to that 
for the jet-stirred reactor. 
Experimental Conditions. The porous-plate burner data 
are from Leonard and Correa [11], hereafter termed mea-
surements by LC. The fuel was methane, premixed with air. 
The burner pressure was 10.4 atm, and the mixture inlet 
temperature was 616 K. (Leonard and Correa also conducted 
experiments for lower pressures and mixture inlet tempera-

authors [4].) The length of the burner, i.e., the distance from 
the porous-plate to the sampling point, is 11.4 cm, and the 
burner diameter is 5 cm. This database considered covers a 
range of <f> from 0.5 to 0.6. 
Comparison to the Model. The burner is modeled by 
applying the baseline model (i.e., the WSR operating at 
incipient blowout, followed by the PFR). Adiabatic behavior 
is assumed.

With respect to the first question posed above, it appears,

Comments on the Residence Time Distribution 
The particle residence time distributions of the two types 
of laboratory reactors examined are much different, and 
cover the limiting possibilities. For the jet-stirred reactors, 
the individual particles have residence times from very short 
to very long compared to the mean residence time; whereas, 
for the porous-plate burner, all of the particles reside in the 
burner for approximately the mean residence time. It is 
noteworthy, as demonstrated above, that these distinctly dif-
ferent situations both exhibit good agreement between the 
measured and modeled NOx. This provides confidence in the 
model and the chemical kinetics. 
The lean-premixed primary zone of gas turbine engine 
combustors should have a particle residence time distribution 
that lies between the limiting distributions of the laboratory 
reactors. The tendency, however, may be toward a relatively 
narrow residence time distribution (centered around the 
mean residence time). This distribution is provided by the 
WSR/PFR baseline model (described above and used to 
simulate the porous-plate burner). In order to examine the 
effect a wider residence distribution has on the NOx, the 
fraction of the combustion process treated as a WSR is 
enlarged (as described above). Although there is virtue in 
identifying a chemical reactor model that best simulates the 
actual combustor, the objective of this study regarding the 
importance of the nitrous oxide pathway is well met by 
applying the simple WSR/PFR model, and by varying the 
WSR to PFR ratio.

Conclusions 
In this paper, total NOx and the contributions of the 
nitrous oxide, Zeldovich, and prompt pathways in lean-pre-
mixed combustion have been presented. The kinetic mecha-
nisms of Drake and Blint [1], Miller and Bowman [2], and 
Michaud et al. [3] have been compared over a range of 
operating conditions. The computer model has been com-
pared to laboratory data. The modeled results for NOx 
showed good agreement to the laboratory data for jet-stirred 
reactors operated at short mean residence times, and for the 
porous-plate burner operated at minimum heat loss condi-
tions. 
A comparison of the relative importances of the nitrous 
oxide, Zeldovich, and prompt pathways for gas turbine en-
gine conditions shows that the three kinetic mechanisms 
considered give similar results for most of the conditions 
examined. All three mechanisms predict comparable total 
NOx amounts. The DB mechanism consistently predicts the 
largest prompt NOx contribution. The MB and the MWF 
mechanisms, which use identical hydrocarbon chemistry, pre-

tions examined, the major source of the NOx exiting the 
combustor is formed by the nitrous oxide pathway. At richer 
conditions, all three kinetic mechanisms show that the largest 
NOx source is the Zeldovich pathway, but that the nitrous 
oxide pathway is an nonnegligible route. 
The results indicate that whenever the total NOx emission 
is in the 15 to 30 ppmv (15 percent Oz, dry) range, the 
nitrous oxide pathway contributes 40 to 45 percent of the 
NOx for a high-pressure engine (30 atm) and 20 to 35 percent 
of the NOx for an intermediate pressure engine (10 atm). For 
engines producing less than 10 ppmv (15 percent 0 2, dry) 
total NOx, as is the goal with ultra low NOx combustors, the 
nitrous oxide contribution increases steeply and approaches 
100 percent. 
Although this study has examined NOx formation from the 
chemical kinetic viewpoint, the results indicate that the fluid 
mechanics of the lean-premixed combustor should be ar-
ranged so that the flame zone(s) are as thin as possible. 
Broad zones of high-intensity, recirculating combustion are 
likely to carry elevated free radical concentrations, and thus, 
increase the NO. formation.
