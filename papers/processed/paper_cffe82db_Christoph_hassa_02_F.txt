FLOW FIELD MIXING CHARACTERISTICS OF AN AERO-ENGINE
COMBUSTOR - PART I: EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
C. Hassa*, P. Voigt*, B. Lehmann+, R. Schodl*, M. Carl*
German Aerospace Center DLR, Germany

MTU AeroEngines, Munich, Germany

Abstract
A model combustor with wide optical access was
built for the investigation of isothermal mixing. The
planar Quantitative Light Scattering Technique
(QLS) was used for the measurement of the concen-
tration of fluid injected through the dilution jets into
the combustor liner. The velocity distribution in the
combustor was measured with a 2-D Laser Doppler
Anemometer. The derivation of the Mie scattering as
a quantitative measure for concentration distributions
is given, the image corrections methods are described
and the experimental setup used for the investigation
is discussed together with an error estimation. The
errors of the measured mean concentrations can be
limited to 5%. The combination of the measurement
of the velocity and the concentration of jet fluid as a
passive scalar qualified the experiment as suitable test
case for the verification of the capability of various
CFD codes to model turbulent mixing in the presence
of swirling recirculation. The results show a strong
coupling of the jet mixing to the structure of the pri-
mary zone flow. The variation of the jet to main-
stream momentum ratio with the position of the jet
relative to the burner nozzles and the pressure distri-
bution created by the recirculation influences the jet
penetration and backmixing of jet fluid in the primary
zone notably.

Model Combustor
The model combustor for investigations of the com-
bustor characteristics under isothermal conditions at
ambient pressure was a scaled down planar sector
element of the annular aero-engine combustor devel-
oped by MTU AeroEngines within the Engine3E
project. The scaling was necessary in order to fit a
sector combustor with a minimum of three segments,
each comprising one burner, into an existing test
facility with an open test section of 180 mm. The
underlying principle of the scaling was to keep the
mass distribution (main/secondary), the pressure drop
and the momentum ratios between the dilution jets

Fig. 1 shows the model combustor assembly in three
different views. The combustor casing, the separation
walls between the main and secondary flow as well as
the combustor liner were made from aluminum. The
combustor top wall as well as the combustor top and

straighten the flows before entering the combustor. In
Fig. 2, a photograph shows the model combustor

The combustor consisted of a primary zone with
constant cross section and a convergent dilution zone.
At the combustor head primary air was introduced by
means of three airblast atomizer nozzles and through
slots at the top and bottom end of the heat shield
representing the combustor cooling air. The dilution
air was injected into the main flow at the transition
from the primary zone to the dilution zone through
three rows of dilution holes which were arranged in a
staggered configuration in order to achieve a rapid
mixing with the main flow.
LDA-Measurements
The velocity measurements were performed under
isothermal and atmospheric conditions using a
modular two-component DANTEC laser-Doppler
anemometer. The velocity components were meas-
ured with the green (λ=514.5 nm) and blue (λ=488
nm) light of an argon laser driven with less than 0.5W
total light power.
The intersection angles of the two couples of light-
beams were seven degrees. The signal light was re-
ceived under near forward-scatter to obtain a high
signal rate together with good signal quality.
The solid scattering particles consisted of SiO2. They
were nearly ball-shaped and fairly monodisperse with
diameters of 0.8 micrometers with a size distribution
width of 20% of the mean value. The powder was
dispersed into a low bypass air flow which was intro-
duced into the hoses for the main air flow upstream of
the model combustor.
Two DANTEC BSA’s were available for signal ac-
quisition and analysis. A computer-controlled three-
axis traversing system scanned the flow field by
moving the optics in a horizontal plane and the model
combustion chamber vertically.
All measurements were made without any coincident
condition of the BSA’s . Thus we were able to realise
the necessary high signal rates especially for an FFT-
analysis of the velocity fluctuations along a frequency
range of at least some KHz.
The measuring light beams were introduced into the
model chamber through one of the end plates of the
chamber and the scattered signal light was received
from the opposite direction. In the case of the meas-
urements near the nozzle exit the optical system was
inclined against the chamber base plate to prevent the
blocking of the axial-velocity measurement beam.
The reported measurements refer to the flow field of
the central sector only. The other sectors served to
simulate the flow interactions of a multi-sector. The
measurements were additionally extended to the near-
wall region downstream in order to study the flow
field around the dilution jets.
Mixing fraction measurements
Many of the existing techniques for mixing fraction
measurement are based on point wise measurements,
like chemical or thermal probe sampling.2 It is obvi-
ous that probe sampling underlies severe drawbacks
in combustion research, since the method is time
consuming and the probe influences the mixing proc-
ess where it is most intense: in the high gradient re-
gions. Optical techniques are promising, since they
have the potential to give planar information about
the mixing process without disturbing the flow field.
Light sheet techniques based on Mie-scattering or
laser induced fluorescence (LIF) have been demon-
strated to be basically suited for planar mixing frac-
tion measurements of isothermal flows. Being de-
pendent on high power lasers to overcome the com-
paratively low-signal level, the LIF-technique has not
achieved an importance comparable to the more effi-
cient Mie-scattering technique in the past. Today LIF
becomes increasingly attractive due to the progress-
ing development of lasers. The first system was set up
by Long et al. 3 and later employed by several other
researchers.4,5 In spite of the advantages of high sig-
nal level and ease of optical set up, the Mie-scattering
technique requires quite a lot of effort in data proc-
essing to provide accurate, quantitative data. Al-
though the aforementioned authors have already in-
troduced image-processing procedures to correct for
background, sensitivity vignetting effects, etc. and for
the light sheet intensity distribution, they did not take
into account extinction and scattering direction ef-
fects, which are very significant in practice. The Mie-
scattering system introduced in this paper accounts
for these additional effects thus improving measure-
ment quality, making the method a quantitative tech-
nique.

Measurement Principle of the Quantiative Light Sheet
(QLS) Technique
The QLS-technique described in this chapter was
earlier mentioned by P. Voigt and R. Schodl 6 , P.
Voigt 7 and C. Hassa et al. 8. The light sheet method
uses scattering of light by small particles marking the
flow. The inflow must consist of at least two separate
streams, allowing to inject tracer particles to only one
of them: the other streams must remain unseeded.
The mixing of the seeded injection flow (index 1)
with the unseeded main flow (index 2)  is analyzed by
illuminating the interaction region with a laser light
sheet and recording the scattered light from this re-
gion on a camera film or CCD-chip. Under the test
condition, which will be specified in the following,
the gray values of the detected image correspond to
the main concentration of the seeded flow in the
mixture.

chip is shown schematically in Fig. 3. Depending on
the imaging scale, a single pixel on the chip with the
area ∆xim • ∆yim corresponds to a certain area element
∆x • ∆y in the light sheet at position (x,y). The thick-
ness ∆z of the light sheet then determines a volume

by each pixel is proportional to the scattering power
Psca(x,y) collected by the camera lens from all parti-

∆t. In the simplified case of uniform particle distribu-

Measurement Errors
One effect is extinction, which is the attenuation of a
propagating light beam by the scattered light emitted
from particles. As shown by P. Voigt7 one can correct
for this intensity loss along the x-coordinate when the
laser power distribution PL(y) is known at the en-
trance and exit of the measurement region and when
extinction is the only existing effect.

However in the case of light scattering on particles
there are in parallel effects of multiple scattering
overlapping the extinction, rendering these correc-
tions impossible. Multiple scattering is a secondary
effect in scattering experiments. It denotes the fact
that scattering particles can be light sources for adja-
cent particles. This effect depends on the particle
load. The existence of secondary scattering has been
proven in a number of experiments and different
aspects have been simulated numerically. An exam-
ple of multiple scattering effects is shown in Fig. 6 by
the virtual thickening of a laser beam: A laser beam
passes through a chamber with homogeneous seed-
ing. The laser beam enters from the left side. On the
outlet side the transmission ratio τ is measured by a
photodiode. The chamber is seeded with four differ-
ent particle loads and images have been taken. In the
upper left image no extinction could be measured.
The beam looks thin. For the following experiments
the seeding was intensified successively. As a result
the laser beam in the image increases it's thickness
accordingly. But this expansion is not constant along
the beam. In the first quarter of the path the expan-
sion increases, due to the strong secondary scattering
in the forward direction. After reaching the maximum
diameter, the expansion decreases, caused by the
coexisting extinction effect on the centerline.
The correction of multiple scattering is the most
challenging part of the post processing, since the
effects are fully three dimensional. A number of dif-
ferent approaches have been tested already, but none
of them with sufficient results yet. Since extinction
and multiple scattering are simultaneous effects,
correction algorithms are even more complicated and
not available yet. A full correction being impossible,
there is only one solution: to keep these errors small.
For that reason the seeding must be adjusted to the
lowest possible level which can be accepted with
regard to the camera sensitivity. In practice the seed-
ing level should be kept so small that extinction along
the light sheet path does not exceed 10%. Predicted
by a detailed error analysis given in Voigt9 and
proved by comparative tests, the remaining measure-
ment error does not exceed 5% and reaches this
maximum value where multiple scattering effects are
most important in regions of steep mixing fraction
gradients.

Experimental Set-Up
The experimental set up consists of an Ar+-laser (1W
power) fiber linked to a light sheet generating box in
which a polygon scanner with 20 facets is rotating
with controllable speed. The scanning laser beam
generates the light sheet propagating with constant
height (max. height ≈ 120 mm). A Peltier-cooled
CCD camera with a dynamic range of 12 bit takes the
picture viewing normally to the light sheet. When
light reflection on model surfaces cannot be avoided,
these were painted with fluorescent dye and a laser
line filter was inserted to the camera objective to
reduce these reflections to a great extent.
The measurements start with a background image
taken with the conditions: laser on and seeding off.
From the signal image taken with seeding on in one
flow component the background image is subtracted
and different correction procedures for pixel sensitiv-
ity, image distortion and scaling, scattering direction
and laser power distribution and if possible extinction
are applied. After that, the reference value g(x0,y0)
indicating the 100% level in the in the image is de-
termined and the corrected mass concentration map is
established. Further application of various graphical
procedures deliver the final mass concentration con-
tour plot.
Being restricted to low seeding levels, even a highly
sensitive cooled CCD camera requires several sec-
onds of exposure time to achieve results of good
quality supposing an Ar+-laser is used as light source.
That limits this set up to mean value measurements.
Instantaneous measurements with much shorter expo-
sure time (~70µs) have also been carried out but
require an intensified CCD camera and are less accu-
rate.10
Results
Velocity
The position of the LDA optics as depicted in Fig. 2
allowed to obtain measurements of the axial and
radial velocity. Measurements were performed in the
center plane and in the plane of the axis of the first jet
rows in the near field of the jets. The measurement
grid was 2 mm in the axial direction  up to x = 15 mm
and from then on 5 mm and 0.5 mm in the y and z
direction.
In Fig. 7 the mean axial velocity U and radial velocity
V are depicted as arrows, the U velocity is addition-
ally reproduced by the false color scheme. The con-
tour of the liner shows the position of the nozzle,
which is not in the middle of the combustor height,
as well as the position of the holes of the circumfer-
entially staggered mixing jets.
 The typical features of an aeroengine combustor
flowfield can be discerned: The expanding, swirling
nozzle flow with an inner and outer recirculation
zone. The inner recirculation zone is closed by the
jets from the first row of dilution holes. However the

mixing zone flow is rather peculiar as the three axi-
ally and tangentially staggered rows of jets produce
large three-dimensional flow patterns, which should
lead to the desired quick mixing of the dilution air
and thus to improved homogenization. The high
penetration of the jets into the opposite half of the
combustor volume is a desired feature for tangentially
staggered jet rows,2 but would be less in the com-
busting case, as the jet to mainstream momentum
ratio decreases with the acceleration of the main flow
connected to the combustion in the primary zone. To
some extent, this can be simulated by changing the
repartition of mass flows and thereby velocities ac-
cording to the zonal temperature levels and this has
been done for the QLS measurements, but it will not
be presented here, as the aim of this contribution is
the generation of validation data for the CFD calcula-
tions of Part II.
A closer look on the nozzle near field of Fig. 8 re-
veals a recirculation, that extends upstream across the
first measurement location at x = 1mm. The swirling
flow expands with a moderate rate up to x = 14 mm.
Here the recirculation zone widens quickly under the
influence of the backpressure that is exerted by the
perpendicular jets of the mixing zone. The corre-
sponding picture for the circumferential velocities in
Fig. 8 has a zero velocity contour which is deflected
due to the influence of the three dimensional flow
pattern of the jets producing an asymmetric recircu-
lation zone. The upstream movement of jet fluid in
the upper half of the combustor suppresses the toroi-
dal recirculation zone that is present for symmetric jet
configurations, as in this plane only the lower nozzle
flow recirculates, whereas the upper part is deflected
by the first jet and it is mostly jet fluid filling up the
upper half of the recirculation. The asymmetric posi-
tion of the nozzle slightly above the symmetry line of
the liner also influences the penetration of the jets, as
the momentum ratio on the downside is slightly
higher, leading to a lower jet angle, higher negative
axial velocities of the first jet in the upper half and
finally a jet angle of the second jet toward the pri-
mary zone, which is even more negative than the
angle of the liner wall. The variation of the momen-
tum ratio of the jets along the lateral direction de-
pending on the distance to the nozzle produces a
variation in jet angles in the axial and to a lesser ex-
tend in the lateral direction. The resulting spatial
distribution of jet cores tends to widen the effective
jet spacing and thus the intensity of shear and mixing
and might inhibit the desired jet penetration. The
effect could in theory be corrected or even be used to
enhance mixing by creating counterswirling flows
when the jet diameters are varied accordingly in the
lateral direction, as has been suggested by11.
As the lateral velocity component was not measured,
a turbulent quantity Uturb = (u’2 + v’2)0.5/U was con-
structed, that is represented in a false color scheme in
Fig. 9 together with the mean velocity vectors. Not
surprisingly, comparison with Fig. 7 shows the high-
est intensities at the boundaries of the inner and outer
recirculation as well as in the region between the first
and second and second and third jets. Since the meas-
ured values are very high, note the logarithmic scale
on Fig. 9, these regions are candidates for coherent
large scale fluctuations or even periodic oscillation,
and a periodic instability was actually found for the
used burner nozzle,12 however for a systematic inves-
tigation it would be necessary to make simultaneous
or phase averaged 2-D velocity measurements, which
is beyond the scope of this contribution.
Concentration Measurements
The concentration measurements were made in 61
axial-radial planes with a separation of 1 mm cover-
ing the middle sector of the model combustor. Be-
cause of the convergent part of the combustor, 3 light
sheet directions were necessary to cover the full cross
section of the combustor with light sheets entering
from the combustor exit: 1 following the upper liner
contour, one following the lower liner contour and
one parallel to the burner axis. The resolution in the
axial and radial direction is about 0.25 mm, however
for the computational ease of the post processing 4*4
elements were integrated to give a 1 mm2 point in the
plots.
Fig. 10 shows the mean concentration field of the jet
fluid in the central plane of the combustor. Compari-
son of the vector field of Fig. 7 gives a good impres-
sion of the backmixing of jet fluid in the primary
zone, which is partly due to the deflection of the
mean streamlines of the first jet and partly to the
turbulent diffusion of jet fluid into the recirculation in
the high shear regions at the lower part of the jet. In
the dilution zone, the large scale jet mixing works
quite well, as can be seen by the rapid homogeniza-
tion downstream of the third jet. At x = 100 mm, only
50 mm downstream from the first jet, the bandwidth
of the concentration of mixture has decreased to 15%.
The threedimensional character of the jet mixing is
depicted in Fig. 11 which shows a contour of the jet
fluid concentration in the middle sector where C is
0.5<C<0.55. The contourplot of C at z = 0 is in-
cluded. Differences in the contour of the 2 jets from
the second row in the positive y and z direction can
be seen. They correspond to the variation of jet pene-
tration according to the local variation of momentum
flux.
Fig. 12 shows the RMS values of the concentration as
computed from 10.000 pictures produced with a sin-
gle sweep of the scanning laser beam at 70 µs per
sweep. This representation exposes very nicely the
potential core region of the first jet. Regions of high
concentration fluctuation are connected with the wake
of the first jet and the lower and upper part of the

Conclusions
The present contribution summarized the results of an
experimental effort, which was directed towards a
realistic and complete characterization of the steady
state isothermal flow and mixing in a model com-
bustor geometry exhibiting the complexity of modern
aeroengine combustors. The Mie-scattering technique
was improved to yield quantitative results for the
concentration of dilution jet fluid in the combustor
within a 5% error margin. This accuracy and the
superior spatial resolution of the planar technique
make it fit to be used for a comparative judgement on
the ability of various CFD codes to predict turbulent
mixing. However the experimental expenditure in
terms of the required expertise and care is also rather
high. Comparison of experimental and numerical
results shows the limits of  a purely steady state ap-
proach. Capturing the non steady state and periodic
effects and weighting their relative importance for the
description of the macroscopic features of the com-
bustion will be a task for the years to come.
