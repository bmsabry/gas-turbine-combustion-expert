ABSTRACT
A new model for the merging of co-flowing jets is presented. The behavior of the
merging flows is governed by the conservation of momentum and tracer fluxes, and
the nature of velocity and tracer profiles is determined based on the summation of
these fluxes at each location. This model differs from previous attempts, which have
employed an additive assumption to model the velocity and tracer profiles. It is
shown that the additive assumption is consistent with the summation of momentum
and tracer fluxes for the merging of strongly-advected jets, but that the two
approaches are inconsistent for the merging of weakly-advected jets.
Comparisons are made between the predictions of a model based entirely on the
additive assumption and the new model. In addition predictions from the models are
compared with available experimental data. The comparisons indicate that, although
the additive assumption is inconsistent with the summation of momentum and tracer
fluxes for the merging of weakly-advected flows, the errors created by making this
assumption are not significant when the predictions are compared with experimental
data.
To gain a better understanding of the errors generated by the additive assumption in
the weakly-advected region, the two models are applied to the merging of jets in a
still ambient fluid (the appropriate limiting case). Taking velocity as an example,
there are notable differences in the cross-sectional velocity profile predictions from
the two models. While both models conserve the overall momentum flux of the flow,
the distribution of the momentum flux within the jet cross-section differs. The additive
assumption re-distributes the momentum flux in jet cross-section more rapidly and
hence merging occurs over a relatively short distance. In addition with the additive
assumption model the centerline velocity reduces more rapidly, so that overall
momentum flux remains constant. Comparisons of tracer distributions from the two
models show similar discrepancies. Although the differences are small, considerable
care must be exercised when making use of the additive assumption.
Keywords: merging jets, co-flowing ambient, model predictions, velocity and
concentration distributions
INTRODUCTION
A common component of the outlet structure for a wastewater disposal scheme is the
multiple port diffuser. This component is normally employed to begin the process of


--- Page 2 ---

dispersing the effluent in the receiving water, so as to provide high degree of initial
dilution. Since the discharge ports are usually round, the wastewater is discharged
from a multiple port diffuser as a series of axisymmetric flows. Interaction between
neighboring flows can have a significant influence on the dilution efficiency of the
diffuser section and is of increasing concern.
Here we consider the interaction of an infinite line of non-buoyant jets, which are
released in the same direction as the ambient current. Improvements in our
understanding of this relatively simple problem will provide the basis for dealing with
more complex and practical interaction scenarios.
The released fluid behaves initially as a single axisymmetric co-flowing jet, and then
the individual axisymmetric co-flowing jets merge to form a two dimensional co-
flowing jet. In addition for a single non-buoyant co-flowing jet, there exists two
distinct regions of behavior: the weakly-advected (or strong jet) region, where the jet
behavior is dominated by the jet excess momentum, and the strongly-advected (or
weak jet) region, where the jet behavior is strongly affected by the ambient flow. This
characteristic behavior of a single co-flowing jet is also reflected in the behavior of
merging co-flowing jets. There is the possibility of strong jet and weak jet behavior in
both the axisymmetric and two-dimensional regions. Therefore there are four limiting
cases for merging co-flowing jets: axisymmetric strong jet, axisymmetric weak jet, two
dimensional strong jet and two dimensional weak jet. The merging process may
occur in the strong jet region, the weak jet region or during the transition from strong
jet to weak jet behavior.
Davidson (1989) combined Knudsen’s (1988) axisymmetric co-flowing jet model and
Patel’s (1971) two dimensional co-flowing jet model, and developed a model for the
merging of pure jets, near the center of a long line of jets, in a co-flowing ambient
flow. In this way edge effects were avoided. Conservation of momentum and tracer
fluxes, along with a spread assumption were adopted as the governing equations in
this model.
Davidson’s model provides correct predictions for variations of velocity, spread and
dilution along the jet trajectory in both the axisymmetric and two-dimensional
regions. To predict the jet behavior during merging, Davidson assumed both the
velocity and concentration distributions of the merging jets were additive. However,
the distribution of the velocity and tracer within the jet cross-section should be based
on the summation of the conserved quantities (momentum and tracer fluxes). In this
paper we discuss the similarities and differences between model predictions based
on the additive assumption and those based on the summation of the conserved
quantities.

Relatively simple expressions for Iq and Ic can then be developed for merging in the
weak jet region, based on these distributions. Note that in the weak jet region the
distributions are the same as those obtained from the additive assumption. Thus the
additive assumption introduces errors for merging in the strong jet region, but not for
merging in the weak jet region. We therefore focus our attention on merging in the
strong jet region, where there are inconsistencies between the models.

A merging co-flowing jet model has been developed using the above definitions for
Im, Iqc, Iq and Ic. The application of the correct forms of Im and Iqc during strong jet
merging and Iq and Ic during weak jet merging, largely eliminates the inconsistencies
created by the summation of velocity and concentration tracer present in Davidson’s
model. However small errors are introduced because of the inaccurate forms of Iq
and Ic during strong jet merging and Im and Iqc during weak jet merging.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSIONS
Predictions from the new model have been compared with those from Davidson’s
model for merging in the strong jet region. In figure 1 it can be seen that the two
models provide the same predictions for the axisymmetric and two-dimensional
limits. However during the merging process there are differences between the
predictions. Experimental data from Wang (1996) has been added to figure 1 for
comparative purposes. Based on comparisons with the experimental data, the
difference between the two model predictions and experimental data is relatively
small. Both models appear to provide reasonably accurate predictions for the
behavior of jets merging in the strong jet region. These results indicate that the
errors created by using the additive assumption for velocity distributions during
merging are not significant in the strong jet region
Davidson’s model and the new model have also been applied to the simpler problem
of merging jets in a still ambient fluid. These comparisons provide an opportunity to
explore the differences between the models in more detail. Knystautas (1964) also
studied this problem and predictions from his model, which is based on momentum
addition and is therefore essentially the same as the new model under these
conditions, are also included for comparison. Vertical and horizontal velocity profiles
from all three models are shown in Figure 2. As expected the predictions of jet
merging from Knystautas’ model and the new model are the same. However, the
predictions of jet merging behavior from Davidson’s model show notable variations
from the other two models. This is because the added velocities are squared in the
governing momentum equation and hence additional terms appear in this equation.
These terms result from the multiplication of velocities from different jets.
Physically the use of the additive assumption results in the velocities increasing
more rapidly near the planes of symmetry between the jets when compared to the
momentum addition models. As the momentum between the planes of symmetry
must be conserved, the central velocities must therefore decrease more rapidly.
Hence Davidson’s (1989) model has tendency to under-predict the centerline
velocities and over-predict the velocities near the planes of symmetry, during
merging. The velocity profiles based on the additive assumption therefore flatten
relatively quickly and merging occurs more rapidly than in the momentum addition
models. These features can be observed in figure 2.
CONCLUSIONS
The additive assumption used in Davidson’s model of merging co-flowing jets has
been shown to be inconsistent with the addition of the conserved quantities, at a
given location, during merging in the strong jet region. The two approaches are
however equivalent in the weak jet region. Comparisons with experimental results


--- Page 6 ---

show the errors created by the additive assumption are relatively small. Velocity
profile comparisons indicate that the additive model has a tendency to under-predict
the centerline velocities and over-predict the velocities near the planes of symmetry,
during merging. The velocity profiles from this model therefore flatten relatively
quickly and merging occurs more rapidly relative to the momentum addition models.
