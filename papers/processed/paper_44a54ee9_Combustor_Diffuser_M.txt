Combustor Diffuser Modeling 
Part I: Inlet Profiles & 2-D Calculations 
 
H. MongiaC, G. Hsiao, and D. Burrus, GE Aircraft Engines, Cincinnati,  
PSVS Sreedhar, A. Rao, and P. Naik, EACoE, Bangalore 
 
ABSTRACT 
2-D axisymmetric simulation of a typical gas turbine 
diffusion system is conducted with two turbulence 
models, the standard k-ε, Shear Stress Turbulence, SST 
k-ω including its low-Reynolds number formulation. 
Effect of diffuser inlet profile, center-, inner- and outer-
peaked on pressure recovery and loss coefficient is 
modeled with two approaches for specifying the inlet 
conditions: 1) Measured inlet mean axial velocity with 
reasonable assumptions for turbulence quantities; 2) 
Through-the-rig inlet, profiler, duct, prediffuser and 
dump region 3-D CFD simulation to calculate the pre-
diffuser inlet profiles. Best agreement with data is 
obtained with the standard k-ε model starting with the 
calculated diffuser inlet conditions. 
 
INTRODUCTION 
 
2-D and 3-D CFD calculations are generally done 
during the gas turbine combustion system design process 
to aid in the optimization of the diffusion system. Early 
work by Karki et al1 as well as the follow-on 
publications, e.g., Reference 2 have shown that the 
standard k-ε model with wall functions overpredicts CP 
by 5-15% compared to data. A preliminary assessment of 
various advanced turbulence models indicates3 that SST 
k- ω turbulence model has shown potential for giving 
better agreement with data for flows subject to adverse 
pressure gradients including separation. However due to 
lack of systematic validation of CFD-based approach 
with data of combustor diffusion systems of practical 
interest to designers, the current design process relies on 
empirical data collected on simulated full-scale 3-D 
diffuser rigs coupled with qualitative guidance from 
CFD.  
Let us review a typical 3-D diffuser experimental 
investigation in order to establish an outline of what 
CFD-based diffuser model validation effort needs to 
undertake so that “CFD-friendly combustor designers” 
don’t need to rely on diffuser rig testing as part of the 
design process. 
Karki et al1 made a significant contribution to the art 
of combustor diffuser modeling even though there still

DATA SETS 
A typical annular diffuser rig, Figure 1a, 
consists of a cylindrical annular inlet section, which 
extended from a rounded entrance to the diffuser inlet. 
The length of this inlet section from the bell mouth 
entrance to the prediffuser inlet was ~12.0”or ~16 
passage heights. The axial distance from the inlet section 
entrance to the diffuser inlet measurement plane was ~ 
8.7” or ~11 passage heights. The distance from the 
measurement plane to the pre-diffuser inlet was ~3.2” or 
~4 passage heights. Within the inlet section at a distance 
of ~2.5” downstream from the rounded entrance were 
located the inlet profilers. In the initial rig configuration 
the profiles consisted of a pair of 360-degree rings 
designed to protrude 0.10” into the flow stream from 
both the inner and outer flow path walls, called opposed-
ring profiler.  The location of these rings placed them 
~12 passage heights upstream of the diffuser inlet. The 
testing reported in this paper used “bow tie” profilers, 
Fig. 1b. The three shown produce center-, inner- and 
outer-peak profiles, respectively. 
The prediffuser exit angle, exit height, L/H and 
geometric area are  ~10.25O, 1.17”, 2.27 and 74.8 in2, 
respectively. Cowl opening height, Hcowl, geometric area, 
axial location, Lcowl and (L/H)cowl are 0.729”, 100 in2, 
2.125” and 2.92, respectively. Outer and inner annuli 
areas at stations 3.5 (toward the farther end of the 
annulus) are 39.4 and 30.5 in2, respectively. The airflow 
splits between the three streams (dome, inner and outer 
annuli) are controlled independently in order to evaluate 
the effect of the flow splits on CP and Ω. 
 
A total of 118 static and total pressure taps were 
installed on the diffuser model test rig, shown 
schematically in Fig. 1a. Of these 64 were located in the 
rig inlet and prediffuser sections, the remaining 54 were 
located in the dump regions, the combustor dome, and 
upstream of the metering orifices. As part of the inlet 
instrumentation there were four 5-element total pressure 
rakes along with wall static pressures for each rake. The 
elements of these rakes were located at y/h = 0.1033, 
0.3081, 0.5095, 0.7078, and 0.9031, bottom to top. At 
the diffuser discharge plane another set of four 5-element 
total pressure rakes along with associated wall statics 
were located.  The radial positions of these total pressure 
elements were at y/h = 0.1045, 0.3136, 0.5227, 0.7365, 
and 0.9233, bottom to top. As part of the combustor 
dome instrumentation two mixer cups were each 
instrumented with five total pressures and five static 
pressures equally spaced around the cup circumferences. 
These taps were placed to quantify the pressure variation 
around the mixer cups. Pt3 is based on the calculated 
value determined from the measured diffuser inlet static 
pressure and the known inlet mass flow rate, assuming 
CD of 1.0. The dome total pressure is taken from the 
average of the dome pressures mounted onto the dome 
plate facing upstream, assuming stagnation flow 
conditions.

be compared with predictions in this paper and 
References 7-9, a total of 10 good quality runs  (Table 1) 
are used to quantify the effect of the following on

and Ω: 
1. Diffuser inlet profiles, Runs 3, 4, 5 and 8

2. Diffuser area ratio and length, Runs 3 and 9; 
3. W/ and w/o combustor dome cowling, Runs 3 
and 6; 
4. Cowling inlet area, Runs 3 and 7; 
5. Nozzle tip diameter, Runs 3 and 11; 
6. Blockage due to nozzle stem, Runs 3 and 12; 
7. Airflow splits between dome, inner and outer 
passages that was conducted for all the 10 test

CP = (Ps-Ps3)/q3

Subscripts s, t and 3 denote static and total pressures,

Tables 2 and 3 list the average CP, total pressure loss 
coefficients, Ω and their root mean square error 
(σ) values for each of the 10 runs at the pre-diffuser exit 
plane. Each run included a set of 18 test conditions 
involving variations in airflow splits between the three 
streams, namely outer passage, inner passage and the 
dome. As noted in Table 1, Run #10 was a repeat of Run 
#3 for establishing typical test-to-test variability. From 
this we observe the following: 
1. 
Sum of root mean squares, so-called pool σ for 
CP and Ω at the pre-diffuser exit of 2.08% and 
8.05% are comparable with the test-to-test 
variability of 2.64% and 9.71%. 
2. 
On the other hand, the max to min variation in 
CP for the test Runs 3, 4 and 5 (conducted to 
quantify the effect of inlet profile) is 13.3%, which 
is ~5 times the test to test variability σ or ~6.4 
times the pool σ. The overall σ  for the 10 runs is 
~8% or 4 times the pool sigma. Essentially similar 
ratios may be observed for Ω. 
3. 
It is a reasonable assumption to make that the 
pool σ’s for the dome, inner and outer passages 
should be ≥ those of the diffuser exit plane.

(of ~40k cells) was locally refined to 475k cells within 
the solver, FLUENT CFD, to achieve the required y+ 
~1, for all the walls.

Starting w/ calculated profiles 
Since the bow-tie profiler geometries are 3-
dimensional in nature (Fig. 1b), a 3d analysis of each of 
center, inner and outer peaked profilers were taken up by 
simulating the test conditions.   GAMBIT software was 
used for gridding the inlet geometry through the 
prediffuser. For the through the profiler (TTP) analysis, 
an all hexahedral mesh comprised of ~512k cells was 
used, Fig. 1d.  A total of 245 grid points were used in the 
axial direction over a physical length of 9.2” starting 
from the upstream dump exit to the diffuser inlet. 
Similarly 20×96 cells were employed at the inlet section 
(fig. 1c) over a 180 sector with a channel height of 0.76”.  
A very fine mesh was employed near the profiler walls 
with the minimum cell size of 0.0155” and 0.033” in the 
axial and radial direction respectively.  The dimensions 
of the upstream dump were 8h*5h in the radial and axial 
direction respectively, where h is the height of the 
profiler (0.764”).  Subsequent studies involving different 
reasonable assumptions for the plenum upstream of the 
bell mouth entrance did not make any noticeable impact 
on the calculated diffuser inlet conditions.

CP of Outer and Inner Passages 
Fig. 4 shows typical comparison between CP 
data and predictions with the standard k-ε model in 
conjunction with the use of standard wall function. The 
calculations are presented with the measured inlet 
profiles 
(identified

stdke_wallfunc_meas) 
and

Abscissa represents flow through the outer or inner 
annuls as appropriate, expressed as fraction of the total 
flow; y-axis presents CP. 14 out of 18 measured points 
were used for the model calculations so that the trend 
lines for predictions and data can be compared 
qualitatively. Data and calculations are presented for 
both the outer and inner passages for stations 3.4 (closer 
to annulus inlet), and 3.5, toward the end of the annulus. 
With the measured inlet profiles, the standard k-
ε model over predicts outer and inner passages CP with 
considerable error defined as [(Data-Prediction)/Data]. 
On the average, this most popular current practice 
approach over-predicts CP for the two axial stations of 
the outer passage by 26 and 30%, respectively; see 
entries in Table 4.  On the other hand, model when used 
with the through-the-profiler calculations (so-called with 
calculated inlet profiles for mean velocity components, k 
and ε) over-predicts CP by 6% and 13%, respectively. 
Similarly for the inner passage, model’s over-predictions 
of 30 and 26% (w/ the measured profiles) reduce to 12% 
and 10%, respectively. This means, with calculated inlet 
profiles, the standard k-ε model w/ standard wall 
function over predicts annuli CP by ~10% compared to 
28% w/ measured profiles; see Table 5. Perhaps, a 
plausible explanation for this unexpected conclusion is 
that for most of the engineering-purpose diffuser rigs we 
don’t have sufficiently resolved measurements for the 
mean quantities; turbulence quantities are rarely 
measured for these set-ups. One should not expect four 
5-element Pt rakes with wall static pressures to pick up 
representative flow field characteristics. Moreover, it is 
not easy to measure combustor diffuser inlet profiles in 
gas turbine engines. We therefore feel that is better to 
have a process for numerically calculating inlet profiles 
through a well posed through the inlet simulation than to 
rely 4-5 Pt rakes to specify inlet conditions. 
Figure 5 shows back-to-back comparison 
between the standard k-ε/wall function and SST low-
Reynolds number k-ω models, both w/ measured inlet 
profiles. The SST model appears to have slight 
advantage over the k-ε model, the former w/ average 
over predictions of ~25% compared to ~28% for the k-ε 
model. However, when compared for the calculated inlet 
profile, Figure 6, the average over prediction by SST is 
~16% compared to ~10% by the k-ε model. This is rather 
a disappointment considering: 1) the SST calculations 
are with ~475k cells compared to ~40k cells used by the 
k-ε model; 2) SST was specifically formulated and 
validated for flows involving adverse pressure gradients. 
However when appropriate, we are in favor of using SST 
for diffuser calculations.    
Figure 7 compares the standard k-ε model 
predictions with the two wall functions (standard and 
non-equilibrium), measured and calculated inlet profiles. 
Even though the use of non-equilibrium wall function 
did not show any advantage over the standard wall 
function (average over-prediction for the passages being 
~11% compared to ~10% w/ the standard wall function), 
we recommend its use because in future investigations 
we plan to replace it with wall functions appropriate for 
flows involving reverse-flow and separation with the 
hope of getting better correlation between model and 
data.  The built-in advantage of including stream-wise 
pressure-gradient term in the non-equilibrium wall 
function is quite evident even though it has not shown 
any benefit for Run 3.

Summary 
 
 
This is a 1st paper in the planned ten papers to 
conduct systematic assessment of the available CFD and 
turbulence 
modeling 
capabilities 
for 
predicting 
performance of combustor diffusion systems used in gas 
turbine engines. As summarized in Table 5, we have 
conducted 2-D diffusion system simulations with the 
standard k-ε (with two different wall functions – 
standard and non-equilibrium) and SST k-Ω turbulence 
models. 3-D CFD simulation through the three profilers 
(that give respectively center-, inner- and outer-peak 
diffuser inlet profiles) clearly showed that the measured 
inlet profile (with four 5-element total pressure rakes 
along with wall static pressures) couldn’t capture the 
complexity of the flow field. Consequently, the model 
predictions starting with measured profiles performed 
poorly against the simulation with the calculated inlet 
profiles. 2-D CFD for the diffusion system with high-
dome flow predicted the dome pressure recovery poorly.

gives good agreement. 
 
Prediffuser CP was predicted rather very well

~12% for Run #5, outer-peak profile, which we have not

Run #5 compared to approximately ~10% (CP over-

Future Diffuser Modeling Plan 
 
In follow-on publications 7 through15 we plan to 
study the effect of the following on CP and Ω: 
 
1. 
2-D versus 3-D simulations 
2. 
Different turbulence models and near-wall 
treatments to capture boundary layer losses for 
diffusers close to or beyond incipient flow 
separation.  
3. 
Diffusion system design parameters including 
inlet profiles, pre-diffuser area ratio, L/H, cowling, 
blockages due to dome swirlers, nozzle stem and tip, 
airflow splits between the dome, inner and outer 
annuli of the combustor liner. 
4. 
Multi-passage diffusers validation 
5. 
Engine to rig correlation.
