Compressor/Diffuser/Combustor
Aerodynamic Interactions in Lean
Module Combustors
The paper reports an experimental investigation into the possibility of increased interac-
tions between combustor external aerodynamics and upstream components, e.g., predif-
fuser, compressor outlet guide vane (OGV), and even the compressor rotor, caused by the
trend in lean module fuel injectors to larger mass ’¼éows entering the combustor cowl. To
explore these component interaction effects, measurements were made on a fully annular
rig comprising a single stage compressor, an advanced integrated OGV/prediffuser, fol-
lowed by a dump diffuser and a generic combustor ’¼éametube with metered cowl and
inner/outer annulus ’¼éows. The ’¼éow split entering the cowl was increased from 30% to
70%. The results demonstrate that, with ’¼üxed geometry, as the injector ’¼éow increases, the
performance of the prediffuser and feed annuli suffer. Prediffuser losses increase and at
high injector ’¼éow rates, the diffuser moves close to separation. The substantial circum-
ferential variation in cowl ’¼éow can feed upstream and cause rotor forcing. Notable
differences in performance were observed inline and between injectors at the OGV exit,
suggesting that geometry changes such as an increased dump gap or nonaxisymmetric
prediffuser designs may be bene’¼ücial. DOI: 10.1115/1.2747646
Introduction
In future aeroengine gas turbine combustor designs, lean mod-
ule combustion will be essential in order to achieve required NOx
targets. Consequently, up to 70% of the ’¼éow issuing from the
compressor may be required to enter the ’¼éametube through the
fuel injectors, compared with typically 30% in current combus-
tor designs. To accommodate this increased mass ’¼éow require-
ment while maintaining an acceptable pressure drop, lean module
fuel injectors will be signi’¼ücantly larger than their conventional
air-spray counterparts. This will necessitate geometry changes to
the combustor external aerodynamic layout such as a deeper
’¼éametube, potentially increasing the aerodynamic loss due to in-
creased turning within the dump region. In addition to affecting
the turning losses incurred by the residual air’¼éow entering the
feed annuli surrounding the ’¼éametube, these geometry and ’¼éow
split changes and the strongly 3D circumferential distribution of
the ’¼éow into the combustor head compared to current practice are
likely to increase the coupling or interaction between the combus-
tor external aerodynamics and upstream components such as the
compressor outlet guide vane OGV/prediffuser combination and
possibly even lead to forcing of the ’¼ünal compressor rotor stage.
Upstream modi’¼ücation of turbomachinery ’¼éow due to the po-
tential ’¼üeld introduced by downstream components is by no
means a new phenomenon. It has led, for example, to work on
optimization of the aerodynamic interaction between the fan and
by-pass OGVs Shahpar et al. 1. Equally, the two-way interac-
tion between the prediffuser, upstream OGV, and downstream
combustor is well known. The impact of OGV wakes on the per-
formance of a downstream located prediffuser has been fully
documented Stevens et al. 2, Barker and Carrotte 3,4, as has
the bene’¼ücial effect of the blockage caused by the downstream
combustor cowl stabilizing the prediffuser ’¼éow Klein 5. Re-
cent works by Barker et al. 6 and Walker et al. 7 have shown
how, by adopting an integrated approach whereby the design of
the OGV and prediffuser are considered simultaneously, the OGV
wake ’¼éows can be manipulated to advantage in achieving im-
proved prediffuser performance. Finally, computational ’¼éuid dy-
namics CFD simulations have also recently addressed the topic
of interaction between closely coupled components such as com-
pressor and combustor Schlueter et al. 8. In this work, the main
effort was placed on identi’¼ücation of appropriate techniques for
coupling the unsteady Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes predic-
tion of the compressor ’¼éow with the large-eddy simulation pre-
diction of the prediffuser/combustor combination. It seems that
the coupling was essentially one way downstream in the engine,
so that although combustor cowl/fuel injector pressure and ’¼éow
characteristics could in’¼éuence the prediffuser, it was assumed that
these did not penetrate further upstream and in’¼éuence the com-
pressor calculation. While this is certainly the design intent, and is
a good assumption for present designs, the present paper is aimed
at addressing the question as to how strong this upstream effect
might be for lean module type geometries and injector ’¼éow splits.
The present investigation extends the study of Walker et al. 7
to consideration of combustor cowl fuel injector ’¼éow in addition
to the compressor/prediffuser components. An experimental inves-
tigation has been undertaken to characterize the extent that the
component interactions described above are modi’¼üed when in-
creasing the injector air’¼éow from conventional levels to those
expected in lean module combustors, using a combustion system
layout typical of current design practice. The experimental study
utilizes the same rig as used by Walker et al. 7. This comprises
a fully annular generic lean module combustor downstream of a
single stage axial compressor. The OGV/prediffuser coupling is
effected via an advanced integrated OGV/prediffuser geometry
4,5. Several traverse planes are used to gather ’¼üve-hole probe
data that allow the ’¼éow structure to be examined through the
rotor, OGV, prediffuser, and in the inner/outer annulus supply
ducts, quantifying the extent of the component interactions and
associated system performance. Several con’¼ügurations are inves-
tigated where the injector mass ’¼éow has been increased system-
atically from 30% to 50% and ’¼ünally 70%.

Instrumentation
