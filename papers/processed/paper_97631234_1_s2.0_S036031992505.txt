A B S T R A C T
Hydrogen (H2) combustion plays a pivotal role in decarbonizing energy systems including gas turbines and in­
dustrial burners due to its zero-carbon emissions and high energy density. However, inherent high diffusivity of 
H2 may wrinkles the flame front and causes local extinction, and the impact of this thermodiffusive instabilities 
(TDI) increases at lean combustion conditions. This study systematically investigates turbulent premixed H2/air 
jet flame utilizing OH planar laser-induced fluorescence (OH-PLIF) and spontaneous Raman scattering (SRS) 
techniques to resolve flame fronts and temperatures, respectively. The experiments were conducted with 
equivalence ratios (ϕ) spanning over 0.5–1.0, Reynolds number (Re) ranging from 6000 to 12000, as well as 
different co-flow conditions. It was revealed that increasing Re or reducing ϕ enhances the flame front wrinkling, 
promotes the reactant pocket formation, and increases flame brush thickness. Additionally, the flame temper­
ature was observed to exceed the adiabatic temperature in the post-flame zone due to the molecular diffusion in 
the stretched H2-air premixed turbulent flames. This super-adiabatic temperature effect diminishes with 
increasing Re, as a result of the improved reactant pocket formation. A stabilization mechanism for turbulent 
premixed H2/air flames was analyzed based on a dimensionless Damk¨ohler number. Non-piloted turbulent jet 
flames require Da >4.18 for stabilization, whereas the surrounding pilot flame can enhance this Da criterion by 
preheating the combustible mixture and providing a considerable radical pool, thereby significantly improving 
the flame stability through both thermal and kinetic effects. These findings bridge the critical knowledge gap in 
understanding combustion across turbulent flame regimes, offering essential insights for the design of lean-burn 
H2 combustors.
1. Introduction
Hydrogen (H2) brings in a unique opportunity to decarbonize the 
thermo-chemical energy transition process by substituting conventional 
hydrocarbon fuels while leaving great potential for optimization. For 
transition towards sustainable combustion systems, lean premixed tur­
bulent flame is advantageous for the desirable merits of high efficiency 
and low nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions [1,2], making it a preferred 
approach for the utilization of H2 in industrial applications such as the 
gas turbines [3–5], internal engines [6–8] and gas condensing boiler [9]. 
Significant challenges exist for the development of such burners in the 
flame instability [10], including flashback and local extinction, as H2 
flames burn differently from conventional fuels. These inherent diffi­
culties in maintaining hydrogen flame stability under fuel-lean condi­
tions are amplified in practical combustion systems due to the turbulent 
flow interactions and transient operational demands, necessitating the 
development of H2 flame stabilization mechanism.
To enhance the stability of highly turbulent lean flames, pilot flames 
have served as one of the most common solutions from both practical 
and scientific perspectives. During the startup or low-power operating of

a combustion device such as gas turbine or engine [11], pilot flames can 
anchor the main turbulent flames through continuous ignition and 
thermal support. In addition, scientific-wise, piloted flames have been 
developed as a standard configuration and extensively adopted to 
generate benchmark datasets for advancing the turbulent combustion 
model validation, across a broad range of turbulence levels, such as the 
Sandia piloted premixed flames [12].
The stabilization dynamics of piloted turbulent lean flames have 
attracted considerable research attention due to their fundamental 
importance in flame anchoring and practical relevance to clean com­
bustion technologies. Guibert et al. [13] pointed out that the adiabatic 
flame temperature of the coaxial pilot flames significantly increases the 
stability of the central flame while the H/C ratio has negligible effects. 
This finding is consistent with that of Yu et al. [14] found that the main 
premixed jet flame is insensitive to the hot gas composition from the 
pilot flame but significantly depends on the heat transfer from the pilot 
flame. Meares et al. [15] studied the stabilization mechanism of turbu­
lent jet flame, but with a focus on understanding the impact of the ho­
mogeneity of the CH4/air in mixture flow, by changing the premixing 
length of the fuel and air flows. Cabra et al. [16] studied the autoignition 
behavior of a lifted turbulent H2/N2 jet flame in the coflow of hot 
combustion products, and they proposed that the small-scale recircula­
tion and the mixing of hot products into reactants rapidly trigger the 
ignition below the liftoff height. While significant progress has been 
made in piloted premixed flame research, existing studies mainly focus 
on hydrocarbon-based or diluted H2 flame [13–16], leaving pure H2 
turbulent premixed flames unexplored.
With respect to the piloted turbulent premixed flame structure, Zhou 
et al. [17] conducted simultaneous laser-based measurements and 
visualized the detailed flame structure of piloted stoichiometric CH4/air 
turbulent jet flames in a distributed reaction zone. Wang et al. [18] 
performed a DNS study on the inner structure and the stabilization 
mechanism of piloted CH4/air premixed flames and found a negative 
displacement speed on the flame front in the near field. Shi et al. [19] 
measured the internal flame structures of lean premixed H2/air flames in 
the distributed reaction zone. However, it remains insufficient for a 
comprehensive understanding of piloted lean premixed H2/air flame 
structures especially those crossing turbulent flame regimes.
Apparently, great distinctions of flame stability and structure can be 
expected between the hydrocarbon fuels and H2, given the facts that: (1) 
H2 exhibits much higher laminar flame speed and molecular diffusivity 
that preclude direct extrapolation of hydrocarbon-based combustion 
dynamics; (2) sub-unity Lewis number of H2 fuel-lean premixed gases 
induces super-adiabatic flame temperatures and more wrinkled flame 
surfaces [19,20] due to strong thermodiffusive instabilities [21], which 
accordingly results in (3) unique combustion dynamic behaviors [22]. 
Insufficient understanding of the flame structure and stability hinders 
not only the design of hydrogen combustors but also the development of 
accurate combustion models. The unique features of flame corrugation 
and high temperature in pure H2 flames pose significant challenges for 
conventional intrusive measurement techniques. Systematic in­
vestigations based on multi-laser-based diagnostic techniques for flame 
front and temperature measurements are therefore urgently needed to 
gain deeper insights into the flame-turbulence coupling in pure H2 
flames, especially when flames are located in different turbulent 
regimes.
Given the limited understanding of piloted lean H2 flames and the 
scarcity of experimental data, this work focuses on the flame structure 
and stability of the turbulent premixed H2/air flames using in-situ laser 
diagnostics. The objectives of this study are: (1) to establish an experi­
mental database for pure H2 turbulent flames in the moderate turbulent 
flame regimes, using OH-planar laser-induced fluorescence (OH-PLIF) 
and Spontaneous Raman Spectroscopy (SRS); (2) to quantify the influ­
ence of coflow conditions on flame structure, particularly in the vicinity 
of the flame base; and (3) to reveal the stabilization mechanism for both 
non-piloted and piloted H2/air flames through dimensionless 
Damk¨ohler number analyses and kinetics studies via CHEMKIN II [23].
2. Experimental methods and supporting calculations
2.1. Burner configuration and flame conditions
The axisymmetric burner configuration is illustrated in Fig. 1, con­
sisting of a concentric nozzle assembly. A central stainless-steel tube 
with a 4-mm inner diameter and 6-mm outer diameter delivered pre­
mixed gas flow of H2/air, while a coaxial channel with an inner diameter 
of 60 mm supplies the pilot flow. A ceramic honeycomb ring, with an 
outer diameter of 60 mm, an inner diameter of 6 mm, and a thickness of 
13 mm, was positioned 1 mm upstream of the nozzle outlet plane to 
establish a uniform flow profile. To further stabilize the pilot flow, a 
sintered metal mesh and 1-mm-diameter glass beads were installed 200 
mm upstream of the nozzle exit. Air was supplied by an air compressor, 
while H2 and CH4 were delivered from pressurized gas cylinders. High- 
purity gases (H2: 99.95 %, CH4: 99.95 %, and O2: 99.999 %) were 
supplied and regulated by mass flow controllers (Seven star) with ac­
curacy of 0.5 % of the set point plus 0.35 % of full scale. The burner 
assembly was mounted on a three-axis translational stage with resolu­
tion of 0.01 mm for spatial-resolved laser diagnostics. Flame images 
were captured by a digital camera (Canon EOS 600D, 32 mm focus 
length, ISO 3200, exposure time of 1/20 s, f/5 aperture) to record the 
flame morphologies under varying conditions.
Turbulent premixed H2/air flames with different equivalence ratios 
(ϕ), Reynold numbers (Re), and coflow conditions have been investi­
gated. Three coflow conditions were investigated: (1) a CH4/air pre­
mixed flame with an equivalence ratio fixed at 0.8 was adopted as the 
pilot flame to stabilize the central H2/air premixed flame, producing a 
vitiated coflow with a temperature of 1620 K, as to be described in 
section3.5; (2) a cold air coflow at room temperature flows with the 
same outlet velocity to that of the piloted CH4/air mixture; (3) quiescent 
air without any coflow. Lean premixed H2 flames were studied with 
equivalence ratios ranging from 0.5 to 0.8 and Reynolds numbers 
spanning over 6000–12000. This Re range not only allows capturing the 
coflow condition effect on the flame stability, but also aligns with typical 
DNS capabilities as demonstrated in Ref. [21], enabling the interests in 
the influence of both turbulence and the intrinsic flame instability.

2.2. OH-PLIF setups
OH-PLIF diagnostics were employed to trace flame dynamics. A 
tunable pulsed dye laser (Sirah Precision Scan), pumped by a pulsed Nd: 
YAG laser (Q-Smart 850), was used as an excitation source for the Q1(8) 
transition of the OH A-X (ν’ = 1, ν’’ = 0) band near 283.55 nm, deliv­
ering 18.92 mJ/pulse at a repetition rate of 10 Hz. The laser beam was 
transformed into a planar laser sheet with a thickness of approx. 0.5 mm 
and a height of 500 mm using cylindrical lens, covering the entire 
measurement region. After excitation, fluorescence signals at around 
308 nm were captured using a CCD camera (LaVision SX 6 M, dynamic 
resolution: 12-bit, chip size: 2752 × 2200 pixels) equipped with a gated 
intensifier (IRO X intensifier, Spectral range: 190–900 nm, Gain: 90 %, 
Gate width: 300 ns) and a Nikkor UV lens (focal length: 85 mm, f 
number: 2.8). A bandpass filter centered at 310 nm with a FWHM of 10 
nm was installed in front of the UV lens to isolate OH fluorescence 
signal. Given the above laser parameters and light sheet dimensions, also 
considering that laser linewidth is less than 0.03 cm−1 (referring to the 
Sirah specification), the normalized irradiance can be calculated as 
25.33 W/(cm2 cm−1), which is much lower than the saturation limit of 1 
× 105 W/(cm2 cm−1) reported by Dunn and Masri [29]. Therefore, it is 
believed that the LIF saturation was avoided and the fluorescence signal 
is in the linear regime [30]. The vertical energy profile of the incoming 
laser sheet was measured separately by capturing fluorescence intensity 
of a cuvette filled with Rhodamine 6G (dye solution). The spatial reso­
lution was measured as 0.0399 mm, which is sufficient to resolve 
wrinkled flame structures across all equivalence ratios (ϕ = 0.5–1.0), 
and comparable with reaction zone thickness (0.023–0.076 mm). The 
PLIF images were processed for background noise and shot-to-shot 
fluctuation of laser energy. For each experimental condition, 500 indi­
vidual images were recorded to allow statistical information to be 
retrieved. The signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) for the OH-PLIF measurements 
was higher than 30, which was determined by obtaining a ratio of the 
averaged fluorescence intensity and averaged non-fluorescence 
intensity.

2.4. Data processing
2.4.1. OH-PLIF image processing
Following background subtraction and energy calibration of the OH- 
PLIF data, the flame front characterization was conducted through 
MATLAB-based image processing as outlined in Fig. 4. Turbulent flame 
structures, including the primary reaction zone and fragmented pockets 
of reactants and products, were identified using a hybrid filtered Canny 
algorithm with adaptive filtering [31]. Statistical quantification of 
pocket distributions was performed across 500 consecutive laser shots, 
with spatially averaged radii computed through geometric centroid 
analysis. The refined single-pixel flame contours were used to binarize 
the images into burnt (assigned a value of 1) and unburnt (assigned 0) 
regions. Averaging of 500 binarized images yielded the mean progress 
variable c, where c = 0 and c = 1 correspond to unburnt reactant mix­
tures and fully burnt products, respectively. In this study, the c = 0.05 
iso-contour line was designated as the flame leading edge to demarcate 
thermally undisturbed fresh mixtures, while the flame brush thickness, 
δT, was defined as the spatial distance between this leading edge and the 
half-burning iso-surface (c = 0.5). At the region of the flame brush tip, 
the energy variations of the laser sheet were confirmed to be within 3 %, 
and thus, the impact of the energy inhomogeneities on the flame brush 
was minimal. To take the asymmetric feature of the averaging flame 
structure into account, δT was calculated by averaging the left and right 
flame wings.

2.5. Supporting calculation method
In this study, laminar flame speed, SL, and laminar flame thickness, 
δL, were calculated based on 1D freely propagating laminar premixed 
flame (PREMIX) incorporated in CHEMKIN II. Burke et al. mechanism 
[35] was adopted for kinetics modeling, based on which the thermal 
diffusivity was derived. To accurately predict the transport properties of 
H2 flames, multicomponent transport properties and thermal diffusion 
were employed. Grid refinement criteria (GRAD and CURV) were both 
set to 0.05 to make sure that the solution is grid-independent.
When calculating Damk¨ohler number, two chemical time scales were 
taken into account to isolate the thermal effect and the kinetic effect: 
τchem,therm was obtained based on α and SL of H2/air mixtures with only 
mixture temperature elevated, while τchem,coup incorporates both the 
thermal effect and the kinetic effect, by additionally specifying an initial 
OH concentration in the combustible mixture. The initial OH concen­
tration was set as 20 % of the OH maximum of the corresponding H2/air 
PREMIX simulation, and this percentage was determined according to 
OH-PLIF signal to be discussed in section 3.2.
3. Results and discussions
3.1. Flame appearance
Fig. 5 illustrates the images of the piloted turbulent premixed H2/air 
flames across varying equivalence ratios (ϕ). A distinct evolution in 
flame structure is observed with increasing ϕ, the flame tip becomes 
progressively obvious, transitioning from an open-like tip shape at ϕ =
0.5 to a well-defined closed configuration near stoichiometric condi­
tions. Although the direct imaging exhibits an apparent open tip for the 
very lean case of ϕ = 0.5, it should be underlined that the closure of 
inner cone tip still exists but the flame tip is too weak to be captured by a 
digital camera, which was later validated by the following OH-PLIF 
images. Readers could also refer to the case of ϕ = 0.6 for some hints 
in Fig. 5 (b), where the faint closure of the inner cone becomes 
discernible. Besides, a secondary post-flame zone enveloping the inner 
reaction layer was observed for the piloted flames, which grows spatially 
and gets brighter as ϕ increases. During the experiments, it was also 
found that despite the imaging challenges inherent to H2 combustion, a 
darkroom environment without any ambient lighting is applicable for 
the visualization of H2 flames. The bule radiation from the flame front is 
reported as a broadband radiation spectrum which peaks at approxi­
mately 440 nm [36] and the red radiation at the flame tip is attributed to 
the vibrationally excited H2O molecules in the wavelength range from 
600 to about 900 nm [37]. The pink color in the pilot flame region arises 
from thermal radiation of the ceramic honeycomb, which reaches in­
candescent temperatures during sustained operation.
Fig. 6 compares the appearance response of the turbulent premixed 
H2/air flames to the coflow variations at a jet Re of 8000. A reduction in 
Re (from 10000 to 8000) shortens the flame length, as seen in Fig. 5(a, c) 
compared with Fig. 6(a and b), consistent with the expected propor­
tionality between flame height and fuel injection velocity. Apparently, 
coflow conditions exert a pronounced impact on flame appearance. 
Replacing the pilot flame with a room-temperature coflow or entirely 
eliminating the coflow collapses the post-flame zone, leaving only the 
internal flame cone visible as a result of the eliminated thermal and 
radical enhancement from the hot product of the pilot flame. In contrast, 
the distinctions in the H2/air flame appearance between air coflow cases 
and quiescent ambient air cases are negligible as shown in Fig. 6(c and 
d), suggesting the minimal aerodynamic interaction at the tested coflow 
velocities, and this point will be validated again by the flame brush 
thickness in section 3.3.

Fig. 7 exhibits the normalized OH-PLIF signal which clearly shows 
the complete 2D internal flame structure, and the maxima of the aver­
aged raw OH intensity are also identified. The internal flame front 
consistently shows the increasing flame height as Re increases, similar to 
that demonstrated in the flame photos as discussed in section 3.1.

convection-controlled regime. Larger Pe indicates stronger convective 
effect, where the greater vortex-flame interaction promotes more

The dependence of peak OH-PLIF intensity on Re exhibits distinct

from the pilot flame region envelops the anchoring position produced. 
The OH radical from the pilot flame is checked to be above 19.5 % of the

bility through kinetic enhancement. Based on the current laser-related 
experimental configuration, the OH-PLIF signal is assumed to exhibit a 
linear relationship with the OH radical concentration, as introduced in 
section 2.2; therefore, this proportionality serves as the basis for the 
ignition delay time calculation in section 3.5.
3.3. Pocket formation
Pocket formation results from the flame front winkling and curving 
in the middle height and at the flame tip, which significantly influences 
the lean blow out limits, flame surface densities, the local heat release 
rates, and the temporal average flame temperatures. To quantitatively 
characterize the internal flame front characteristics and gain deep in­
sights into the turbulent premixed H2 flame, statistical evaluations of 
pocket amount and mean radius were conducted, as presented in Figs. 8 
and 9.
Fig. 8 depicts the reactant pocket properties as a function of ϕ and the 
remarkably decreasing trend underlines the significant impact of ϕ on 
the reactant pocket formation, this can be attributed to the transition in 
the turbulent flame regimes, shown in Fig. 2, as flames shift from the 
flamelets zone to the thin reaction zone as ϕ decreasing. The product 
pocket formation across the examined ϕs was also checked which is not 
shown here and its influence is less profound compared with that on the 
reactant pocket. The reactant pocket occurs near the flame tip while 
product pocket appears inside the inner flame wings, as shown in Figs. 4 
and 7. As Tyagi et al. [39] attributed the formation of the reactant 
pocket and product pocket to the flame folding and tearing, respectively, 
it can be concluded that the shift from the flamelet zone to the thin 
reaction zone has a more profound influence on flame tip surface folding 
than the flame wing tearing.
Fig. 8 compares the effects of Re on the reactant pocket properties for 
flames at equivalence ratios of 0.5 and 0.8, and more pocket formation 
for the leaner conditions correlates well with the increased flame front 
corrugation observed in Fig. 7. More significantly, pocket properties in 
the thin reaction zone (flames with ϕ = 0.5) exhibit a stronger non-linear 
dependence on Re than these in the flamelets zone (flames with ϕ = 0.8). 
In the flamelets zone, increasing Re exerts a relatively constant effect on 
both the pocket amount and radii; in contrast, within the thin reaction 
zone, the influence of Re on pocket radii becomes more profound at 
higher Re, while its impact on pocket amount weakens. These two

Apart from these general behaviors, the post-flame region exhibits 
super-adiabatic temperature phenomena, as a result of TDI. For H2/air 
mixtures of ϕ = 0.5 and 0.8, the calculated adiabatic temperatures (Tad) 
are 1642 K and 2162 K, respectively. However, experimentally 
measured flame temperatures reaching 1718 (±41.4) K and 2189 
(±53.4) K, considering that the temperature was under-estimated due to 
the flame fluctuation, the real temperature of flame is beyond the 
calculated adiabatic temperatures. This observation aligns with findings 
by Shi et al. [19] and Chen et al. [42], and they attributed this TDI 
impact on super-adiabatic temperature to the differential diffusion of H2 
that drives H2 diffuses toward the products, enhancing local heat 
release. Besides, the more profound super-adiabatic temperature 
behavior at low ϕ indicates the stronger TDI at leaner conditions, and 
this behavior agrees with finding in Berger et al. ’study [38] that the 
impact of instabilities increases if the equivalence ratio is decreased. 
Notably, differences in post-flame temperature are observed across the 
four investigated Re numbers, i.e., increasing Re correlates with a 
reduction in peak flame temperature. This trend is attributed to the 
improved reactant pocket formation due to more wrinkling as Re in­
creases. These pockets entrain unburnt mixtures into the post-flame 
zone, increasing the possibility of a local low-temperature region and 
thus reducing the average temperature in Raman measurements. This 
cooling effect of these pockets is more profound under a leaner condi­
tion, as compared in Fig. 9.
In addition, for Re = 10000 and Re = 12000 in Fig. 8, it is observed 
that once the centerline temperature reaches its peak, it decreases at 
larger HAB, indicating the mixing of ambient air with the hot products. 
This behavior could be addressed by applying a shielding coflow in the 
future to separate the reacting flow from the surrounding air.
Fig. 13 (a) compares the flame centerline temperature between 
piloted cases and air coflow cases. For the two studied flame cases, non- 
piloted flames consistently exhibit higher temperatures at HABs larger 
than 10 mm. This can be attributed to the slightly upstream flame tip in 
the non-piloted cases, as demonstrated in Fig. 13 (b), where the peak 
OH-PLIF signal in a piloted flame clearly occurs in a downstream posi­
tion compared with these in non-piloted cases. The less distance to the 
tip of the non-piloted flames causes the higher flame temperature in the 
unburnt side. Since the flame tip is only slightly shifted by the pilot 
flame, as shown in Fig. 13 (b), the resulting discrepancy in the centerline 
temperature remains relatively small, especially in the positions above 
the flame tip, where measurement uncertainty is amplified. Again, only 
minimal differences emerge between flames in coflow air and those in 
quiescent environments, resulting in the near-identical stability map as 
will be discussed in the next section.
In addition to the influence of the pilot flame on the flame centerline 
temperature, the coflow temperature at the burner exit plane is greatly 
elevated in the piloted cases. To quantitatively examine this inlet 
change, the temperature of the pilot flame flue gas was measured by 
Raman scattering at the HAB = 5 mm, and it was found that the radial 
temperature distribution is fairly uniform at 1620 (±35.6) K. Enveloping 
the central H2/air flames, the pilot flame preheats the reactant flows 
through the heated ceramic honeycomb and tube wall. The Raman 
scattering is no longer applicable to this preheating effect measurement 
because of the near-wall reflection interference; instead, a 1-mm-diam­
eter type- K thermocouple was inserted at the center of the burner exit 
and on the top surface of the burner nozzle immediately after stopping 
the pilot flame while keeping an airflow in the central tube with a cor­
responding outlet velocity to the tested H2/air flame. Experimental 
measurement of the temperature profile at the burner exit is challenging 
due to the small size of the tube. However, under the investigated flow 
turbulence, it has been checked that the thermal boundary layer is 
considerably thin, and the center-point measurements were therefore 
used to characterize the mixture temperature at the burner exit. Results 
show that the central flow is about 10 K heated by the hot ceramic 
honeycomb and the tube wall, while the temperature of the burner tip is 
421 K, and the central flow rate variation does not change these numbers 
noticeably.
3.6. Flame stability
The flame stability under coflow conditions of pilot flame case, air 
coflow case and quiescent air case were examined in this study. A flame 
was identified stable if it remained burning without blow-out or local 
extinction for at least 20 min following ignition or transition from a 
previous operating condition. It has been confirmed that a stable flame 
could persist for several hours otherwise it extinguishes within this 
transition period. Besides, 20 min are sufficient to establish a new 
thermal equilibrium at the studied thermal power with a maximum of 
1.5 kW (Case 9 in Table 1), thereby ensuring the reliability of the 
measurements.
The matrix stability map of H2/air flames with and without pilot 
flame assistance is presented in Table 2. For non-piloted H2 flames, the 
stability cannot be achieved within the investigated Re range if it is very 
lean, such as ϕ = 0.5–0.7. At high equivalence ratios, such as ϕ = 0.8 and 
1.0 listed in Table 2, flames are stable; besides, Table 2 also shows that 
stability range can be extended by increasing ϕ, as flame shifts to be 
stable at Re = 10000 when increasing ϕ from 0.6 to 0.7 to 0.8 and 1.0. 
No noticeable discrepancy was found between the flames in air coflow 
and quiescent air, and the similar stability behaviors in these two non­
piloted flames are correlated well with the minor differences in flame 
appearance, flame brush thickness, and OH signal, as discussed in the 
previous sections. Particularly, with the pilot flame assistance, flames

mixtures is 40. In this respect, to identify this Da threshold for concentric 
H2 jet flames, more H2/air flames beyond Table 2 were explored under 
the air coflow condition and a Da-Re stability map can be obtained as 
shown in Fig. 14, where Da was calculated based on the τchem of an

the non-piloted flame stabilizes, whereas at lower values, extinction 
occurs. To the best of the authors’ knowledge, this is the first report of 
Da criterion for flame stability of H2 turbulent flames in a round tube jet. 
Due to the differences in the burner configuration and the formula of Da 
calculation, it is important to note that the Da stability limit in this study 
is not directly aligned with those in Refs. [24,26]. It should be noted that 
the Da criterion is burner configuration-dependent, and a thorough 
analysis should be made for a given burner configuration with geometry 
or coflow air velocity varied, which is beyond the scope of this study.
Now we discuss how the pilot flame stabilizes H2/air flames that are 
extinguished under non-piloted conditions, such as those with ϕ = 0.5. 
As discussed in sections 3.2-3.5, the pilot flame alters the flame struc­
ture, and profound influences are found especially near the burner exit, 
including the preheating effect of the heated burner configuration 
(temperature of central mixture flow at the burner exit is ~310 K, and 
burner wall tip is ~421 K), the presence of considerable OH radical 
(~20 % of the maximum in the central H2 flame), and the directly 
heating by the hot flue gas (~1620 K) of the pilot flame at the flame 
base. Experimentally, it is very difficult to examine how much the 
combustible mixture at the flame base is heated by the pilot flame, 
therefore, Da numbers under different thermal conditions were 
calculated and results for ϕ = 0.5H2/air flames with Re = 6000 and 
12000 are demonstrated in Fig. 15. The heating effects with five 
increasing levels are compared, namely the room temperature of 300 K, 
the burner outlet temperature preheated by the hot tube and ceramic 
honeycomb, i.e. 310 K, the temperature identical to the burner wall, and 
the temperature of one-third-way heated by the pilot flame, as well as a 
higher temperature of 800 K. Datherm and Dacoup were calculated based 
on chemical time scales of τchem,therm and τchem,coup, respectively, as 
described in section 2.5, and the differences between these two Da 
numbers are caused by the kinetic enhancement of OH radical.
It is clear that the mixture temperature has an exponential effect on 
increasing Da number, as a result of the much enhanced laminar flame 
speed. For example, as the temperature increases from 300 K to 745 K, SL 
is significantly enhanced from 48.06 cm/s to 627.64 cm/s. The pre­
heating effect of the hot solid wall is slight. Although all the piloted H2/ 
air flames attach to the nozzle tip, as exhibited in Figs. 5 and 6, the real 
heating effect of the pilot flame must exceed the burner wall tempera­
ture since under this circumstance Da is still lower than 4.18. For a one 
third way direct heating of the pilot flame, corresponding to a mixture 
temperature of 740 K, the thermal enhancement is quite remarkable and 
all Da numbers are on the order of 10. Further increasing the mixture 
temperature makes this thermal stabilization effect stronger. In addi­
tion, it can be observed that the participation of OH radical exerts a 
kinetic stabilization effect, which gets more important at higher mixture 
temperatures. Based on the 20 % basis of OH radial pool adopted in this 
kinetic analysis, the kinetic stabilization effect is less profound than the 
thermal stabilization effect, within the investigated thermal range.
Based on the analysis of Da numbers, the stability of turbulent pre­
mixed H2/air flames can be promoted by two synergistic mechanisms: 
(1) thermal contributions from the hot solid wall and pilot flame 
exhaust, and (2) kinetic enhancement via the radical pool. The expo­
nential stabilization effect offers practical insights into industrial

4. Conclusions
This study experimentally investigates the flame stability and 
structure of turbulent premixed H2/air flames in both cold co-flows and 
piloted flames. OH-PLIF and Raman spectroscopy were utilized to 
measure the flame front structure and temperature profile along the 
centerline. By examining the spontaneous internal flame front, pocket 
formation, and temporally-averaged characteristics such as flame brush 
thickness and temperature profile, this study has established a 
comprehensive database for turbulent premixed H2 flame and eluci­
dated the stabilization mechanism of piloted flames. Based on the dis­
cussions and analyses, the following conclusions can be drawn. 
(1) Increasing the Reynolds number or decreasing the equivalence 
ratio enhances flame front corrugation, which can be attributed 
to the stronger convective effect and the enhanced impact of the 
TDI. Reactant pocket properties in the thin reaction zone exhibit a 
non-linear relationship with increasing Re, while in the flamelets 
zone, the effect of Re is relatively constant, implying the 
competing mechanisms of chemical reaction and turbulence.
(2) The flame brush thickness of turbulent premixed H2 exhibits a 
quadratic dependence on height above burner. For non-piloted 
flames, δT increases monotonically throughout the entire HAB 
range. However, the pilot flame exerts a noticeable influence on 
the central flame, leading to an initial thickening of δT near the 
burner exit, followed by a subsequent decline. For both flame 
configurations, δT increases quadratically on the height above the 
burner.
(3) Super-adiabatic temperature was observed in the post-flame re­
gion as a result of TDI, and TDI impact on this phenomenon be­
comes stronger at leaner conditions. Increasing Re decreases the 
post-flame temperature of turbulent premixed H2/air flames, as a 
result of the improved cooling effect of more reactant pocket 
formation. The presence of a pilot flame preheats the central 
mixture flow at the exit, but lowers the temperature at higher 
HABs due to the upstream-shifted flame tip.
(4) The presence of a pilot flame alters the flame structure incon­
stantly at different axial locations. While the impact is minor at 
the flame tip, it is profound near the burner exit. These remark­
able structure changes in the vicinity of the flame base, which 
accordingly enhances the stability in terms of: (i) the preheating 
effect of the heated burner configuration, (ii) the considerable OH 
radical, and (iii) the directly heating by the hot flue gas.
(5) Kinetic study further shows that the stability of turbulent pre­
mixed H2 flames is controlled by the Da mechanism with Da 
>4.18 as the stabilization criterion; while for the piloted flame 
cases, the thermal contribution and kinetic enhancement from 
the pilot flame increase Da exponentially and thereby enhance 
the stability of turbulent premixed H2/air flame.
