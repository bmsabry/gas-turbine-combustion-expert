A B S T R A C T   
Recent experimental studies suggest that lean-premixed pure hydrogen combustion plays a central role in 
generating self-sustained pressure oscillations, preferentially coupled with high-frequency tangential modes in 
land-based gas turbine combustors. The triggering of such high-frequency combustion dynamics is known to be 
closely connected with relatively small, flashback-resistant, characteristic nozzle dimensions, and the structural 
and kinematic properties of the premixed hydrogen flames. To identify important trends in the transverse modal 
dynamics of hydrogen flames, here we conduct detailed measurements of multislit injector-driven hydrogen 
flame dynamics. We analyze the measured data using the wave decomposition technique for PDF-based statis­
tical assessments of bimodality and nodal line orientation. Experimentally, the planar-to-transverse mode 
transformation is revealed to be controlled by the combined influence of flame temperature and thermal power, 
which ultimately leads to the development of well-defined standing modes at around 4.69 kHz under the most 
severe test condition. Using analytic modeling and rigorous validation measurements, we also investigate 
Helmholtz resonator-type passive stabilization and its impact on the integrated system’s thermoacoustic states 
with respect to the change in purge air flowrate. Our results show that the coupled system does not retain many 
of the initial thermoacoustic properties, but instead undergoes prominent changes in the first tangential modal 
dynamics, progressively evolving from a large-amplitude bimodal to a low-amplitude unimodal state.   
1. Introduction 
With its high relevance to the decarbonization of power generation 
systems, hydrogen-fueled gas turbine combustion has been the subject of 
extensive research [1–3]. Significant progress has been made in recent 
years, with the development of flashback-resistant dry low-NOx 
hydrogen combustion technologies, and basic design principles and 
unconventional nozzle architectures have been demonstrated under 
engine-relevant conditions [4–6]. Even though extensive efforts have 
been made to understand nitrogen oxides emission trends and heat 
transfer-dependent boundary layer flashback mechanisms [7–14], pre­
mixed hydrogen flames’ mechanistic role in the triggering of 
high-frequency self-excited instabilities remains relatively underex­
plored. Detailed characterization and physical understanding of the 
transverse modal dynamics at a fundamental level, including multidi­
mensionality and local flame-acoustic interactions, are expected to be a 
crucial ingredient for the development of passive stabilization methods 
tailored to meet gas turbine system-level requirements. A series of recent 
experimental and numerical investigations indicate that the instabilities 
of premixed hydrogen flames are characterized by the excitation of 
higher-order acoustic modes [15,16], even including the triggering of 
high-frequency combustion instabilities coupled to the first tangential 
mode [17–19]. The physical understanding of the transverse modal 
dynamics and their passive stabilization are the main focus of the cur­
rent study. 
O’Connor et al. [20] provided a comprehensive review of transverse 
mode instabilities, highlighting the transverse acoustic field, hydrody­
namic mode excitation, and flame response to these disturbances. A 
series of experimental investigations conducted by Sattelmayer and 
colleagues [21–25] are concerned with high-frequency transverse 
thermoacoustic interactions. Based on the detailed observations, they 
developed a theoretical model for linear stability analysis, and identified 
local flame displacement as the primary mechanism. Using a similar 
reduced-order modeling approach, M´ery [26] studied the stability of 
transverse modes in a simplified flow configuration, and concluded that 
the spatial distribution of thermal power relative to the acoustic field 
plays a crucial role. Recently, Acharya and Lieuwen [27,28] utilized a 
level-set-based theoretical framework to analyze the growth rates of

Passive damping devices including various forms of acoustic 
dampers [30–35] and perforated liners [36,37] are often used in gas 
turbine combustion systems to suppress pressure oscillations, mainly 
due to their structural simplicity and proven robustness. For example, 
Helmholtz resonators consisting of a narrow flow passage (neck) and a 
relatively large cavity (volume) are well known to be very effective in 
controlling intense pressure fluctuations by dissipating coherent 
acoustic oscillations via non-resonant vortex shedding. These dampers 
are generally cooled by means of a purge flow (also called bias flow) to 
decrease the risk of hot gas ingestion-induced frequency detuning and 
related hardware failures; this method requires a careful performance 
tradeoff between the minimum purge mass flowrate required for suit­
able damper operation and the potential increase in nitrogen oxides 
emissions [30–32]. The remaining considerations affecting the perfor­

systematically characterized the stabilization capabilities of generic

and purge mass flowrate. The authors observed that the Helmholtz 
resonators provide better stabilization performance at comparatively 
low purge flowrate, but are more prone to frequency detuning than 
other resonators of comparable volume. Mazur and his colleagues [39] 
investigated the role of the circumferential placement of Helmholtz 
resonators in annular combustor configurations, and they found that 
standing modes are predominantly observed when the dampers are 
clustered in a certain segment of the annulus, while spinning modes 
become more pronounced in uniform distribution circumstances. A 
comprehensive study was conducted by Scarpato and coworkers [43,44] 
to examine specifically how various parameters affect the absorption 
properties of acoustic dampers with perforated plates as a function of 
dimensionless Strouhal number. 
Although substantial progress has been achieved in the character­
ization of lean-premixed hydrogen combustion dynamics, thermoa­
coustic interactions with Helmholtz resonators, especially in regard to 
high-frequency transverse instabilities, are not well understood; they 
are generally hypothesized to reflect modal dynamics-invariant passive 
stabilization. To clarify important trends that apparently different from 
the well-established planar acoustic behaviors, here we investigate the 
modal dynamics of an acoustic damper-coupled lean-premixed com­
bustion system that is primarily driven by multislit hydrogen flames’ 
heat release rate fluctuations. This study demonstrates experimentally 
that the implementation of multiple Helmholtz resonators plays an 
important part in fundamentally altering the modal dynamics of the 
coupled system, and greatly attenuates the intensity of the system 
instability.

2.1. Hydrogen combustion and impedance tube test setups 
The cross-section of the atmospheric-pressure test facility is shown in 
Fig. 1a, together with flashback-resistant slit-type injectors developed 
for operations with pure hydrogen fuels. This test rig, modified from the

preheated lean-premixed reactant mixtures, ultra-high purity 99.999% 
hydrogen and dry air, are supplied to the inlet plenum with a perforated 
plate to remove large-scale flow structures and provide a well-defined 
acoustic boundary condition. The multislit injector assembly displayed 
in Fig. 1b consists of radial and circumferential injectors with a constant 
slit width of 1.5 ± 0.02 mm, and the whole nozzle assembly is directly 
connected to a 109 mm diameter optically-accessible quartz tube section 
and a variable-length combustor section. Note that the length of the 
quartz tube is shortened to facilitate the integration of the damper 
(Fig. 1a inset). The external surface of the quartz tube is actively cooled 
with jet impingement of room temperature air to prevent thermal 
damage. The use of a water-cooled tunable piston permits change of the 
effective combustor length, defined as the distance between the 
combustor dump plane and the piston head. Further details about the 
experimental setup and operational information are provided in Refs. 
[45,46]. 
The purge flowrate-controlled impedance tube depicted in Fig. 1c is 
used to measure absorption coefficients of Helmholtz resonators (inde­
pendent experiment). A fully shielded neodymium magnet-type loud­
speaker (Morel model EW428) is positioned on the left side of the 
impedance tube, operated with serially connected power amplifier 
(InterM model R500Plus) and function generator (Tektronix model 
AFG31000). The air temperature inside the impedance tube during ex­
periments is monitored with a type-K thermocouple, and pressure fluc­

2.2. Measurement techniques and experimental conditions 
The mass flow rates of air and hydrogen are controlled using two 
thermal mass flow meters: Sierra Instruments model FlatTrak 780S for 
air and Teledyne Instruments model HFM-D-301 for hydrogen. The flow 
meters have measurement ranges of 6.8 kg/min for air and 0.058 kg/ 
min for hydrogen, with an accuracy of ±0.5% for air and ±0.6% for 
hydrogen at full scale. Seven piezoelectric pressure transducers (PCB 
model 112A22, sensitivity 14.5 mV/kPa and uncertainty ±1%) are used 
to measure pressure data. These pressure transducers are installed in 
water-cooled adapters with 16 mm long waveguides, and are mounted at 
two longitudinal positions (p′
us and p′
tp in Fig. 1a) and five non- 
equidistant azimuthal locations at the injector face (Fig. 1b). The 
analog signals are conditioned by an amplifier (PCB model 482A16), and 
recorded using a high-speed data acquisition system. The sampling 
frequency is set to 48 kHz (16 s duration), which is sufficiently high to 
prevent any aliasing of the instability frequencies discussed below. 
Spectral analysis of the recorded signals is performed using the fast 
Fourier transform technique with the Hanning window function, and the 
Hilbert transform is utilized to calculate instantaneous phase 
information. 
Here we tested two different nozzle velocities of 25 ± 0.25 m/s and 
50 ± 0.51 m/s, while the inlet temperature was kept at 473 ± 5 K 
throughout our experiments. For each nozzle velocity condition, the 
equivalence ratio was varied between 0.325 and 0.615; these values are 
equivalent to adiabatic flame temperatures ranging between 1400 and 
2000 K, confirmed by Cantera calculation [48]. The experimental un­
certainty in measuring the equivalence ratio is estimated to be approx­
imately 1.5%, taking into account all elemental uncertainties related to 
mass flow rates and inlet temperature measurements. For the test con­
ditions summarized in Table 1, stationary measurements of self-excited 
instabilities were carried out in the absence of acoustic dampers by 
adjusting the effective combustor length from 1200 to 1800 mm in 50 
mm increments. Subsequently, the coupled resonator-combustor sys­
tem’s thermoacoustic responses were investigated over a range of purge 
mass flowrates up to 6.4 g/s, when other experimental conditions 
remained unchanged at the most severe test condition. 
3. Results and discussion 
This section will begin by analyzing pressure amplitude spectra ac­
quired for the entire range of test conditions. Of particular interest are 
inlet condition-dependent growth patterns and consequential influence 
on longitudinal-to-transverse mode transition. We will concentrate on 
the transverse modal dynamics of high-frequency self-induced in­
stabilities using wave decomposition-based phase space reconstruction. 
Finally, we will examine how the laboratory-scale combustion system 
integrated with multiple Helmholtz resonators undergoes prominent 
changes in oscillatory state.

instead triggers the first tangential mode instabilities. Experimentally, 
this confirms the existence of a planar-to-transverse mode transition that 
depends critically on the combination of flame temperature and mean 
nozzle velocity; longitudinal and transverse modes, signified by sub­

flame temperature condition depicted in Fig. 2d, showing clearly that 
the first tangential mode near 4.6 kHz is markedly intensified, while the 
resonance phenomenon remains unaffected by the variation of

simultaneous excitation of high-order longitudinal eigenmode at 2.60 
kHz and its second harmonic component at 5.20 kHz is detectable 
particularly under relatively short combustor length conditions, and 
these spectral peaks appear to remain unassociated with the preexisting 
transverse mode. In summary, we identify the predominant trend in the 
multislit hydrogen flame dynamics as the replacement of low-frequency 
planar modes by high-frequency transverse modes in relation to the 
overall increase in the adiabatic flame temperature, characteristically 
posing serious obstacles to the development of lean-premixed hydrogen 
combustion systems.

3.2. Helmholtz resonator design, validation tests, and data interpretation 
Having reviewed several key aspects of the transformation from 
bimodal to unimodal transverse dynamics, we turn to one of the main 
questions of the present study: passive stabilization and concurrent in­
fluences on the modal dynamics, particularly when Helmholtz 
resonator-type acoustic dampers are integrated into the hydrogen 
combustion system. The major dimensions of the Helmholtz resonators 
were obtained by the well-established design principles discussed in 
Refs. [51–53], and specific information about orifices and cavity di­
mensions is summarized in Table 2. The model prediction was directly 
validated against absorption coefficient measurements using the purge 
flow-controlled impedance tube illustrated Fig. 1c. The modeling results 
for the two frequencies of interest, namely 1.2 and 4.5 kHz, are dis­
played in Fig. 5a and b in the form of heatmaps to visualize the variation 
of frequency-dependent absorption coefficient over a range of purge air 
flowrate. For these results, it is reasonable to expect that the Helmholtz 
resonators function properly, provided that the requirement for the 
purge air flowrate is accurately satisfied; the maximum sound absorp­
tion can be achieved at approximately 0.6 and 3.0 g/s, respectively. The 
calculated absorption coefficients corresponding to the horizontal gray 
dashed lines are replotted in Fig. 5c and d for direct comparison with 
their respective experimental data. The prediction is in good agreement 
with the measurement, supporting the design principles and relevant 
interpretations. It should be noted, however, that the lower and upper 
cutoff frequencies are in principle determined by the distance between 
microphones specified by flower = 0.05c/s, and the condition required to 
maintain planar acoustic waves for a fixed impedance tube diameter, 
that is, fupper = 0.58c/d, where c is the speed of the sound, s is the 
microphone spacing, and d is the diameter of the impedance tube [47]. 
In parallel with detailed characterizations of the Helmholtz resona­
tors in the isothermal flow environment in isolation, we also investigate 
the thermoacoustic response of the coupled Helmholtz resonator- 
combustor system to high-frequency combustion dynamics. The air- 
purged Helmholtz resonators designed for the mitigation of 4.5 kHz 
transverse mode are installed in close proximity to the hydrogen flames, 
and they are positioned at six equally spaced azimuthal locations – 
forming a three-dimensional network perpendicular to the crossflow 
direction – to rule out the possibility that the dampers are accidently 
aligned along the pressure nodal line (Fig. 1d). Fig. 6a plots pressure 
spectrograms measured for varied purge air flowrates up to 6.4 g/s, and

unaffected by the further increase in the purge flowrate. The fact that 
these behaviors are accompanied by progressive variations of the degree 
of preferred modes (illustrated in Fig. 6b) suggests unambiguously that 
the resulting transverse modal dynamics are not invariant. The transi­
tion of the preferred mode from inconsistent standing to coherent 
spinning mode is clearly visible in Fig. 6d (bar plots); the rightmost 
graph included in Fig. 6b shows a non-trivial structure akin to a canted 
elliptical 
orbit 
reflecting 
the 
pure 
spinning 
mode. 
Such 
resonator-coupled transverse modal dynamics are previously unre­
ported in the literature. The details of the underlying mechanisms 
remain uncertain; in-depth explorations will be indispensable for further 
characterization. 
4. Conclusions 
The development of hydrogen-fueled gas turbines has garnered 
notable interest in the quest to achieve the global energy sector’s 
decarbonization goals. A series of recent experimental studies, however, 
suggest that lean-premixed combustors operating with pure hydrogen 
fuels are characteristically susceptible to the excitation of high- 
frequency transverse instabilities that can have direct and critical im­
pacts on the architecture and functioning of can-annular combustion 
systems. Addressing this fundamental problem is crucial, not only to 
advance our understanding of the multidimensional thermoacoustics, 
but also to propose the effective passive stabilization strategies that will 
be required to account for their transverse modal dynamics. To this end, 
we carry out several independent experiments to measure Helmholtz 
resonator-coupled system-level modal dynamics driven by the heat 
release rate fluctuations of multislit hydrogen flames. In particular, we 
combine pressure spectrum analysis results with wave decomposition- 
based phase space reconstruction to provide a systematic approach to 
mode identification, given that the morphological features entail 
detailed information about amplitude-phase modulations. By clarifying 
distinct modal dynamics patterns, we find that the transverse mode 
constitutes the dominant part of the pressure amplitude spectra under 
high-temperature and high thermal power conditions. For passive sta­
bilization of the transverse fluctuations, Helmholtz resonators are 
theoretically modeled and experimentally validated under isothermal 
flow conditions in isolation and in a fully coupled damper-combustor 
configuration. The integration of multiple Helmholtz resonators is 
observed to produce noticeable changes in the modal dynamics, 
including the subsequent generation of two adjacent modes. Taken 
together, the findings from this research underscore the importance of 
passive stabilization approaches, especially taking into account purge 
flow-induced detuning and unpredictable non-planar behaviors like 
nodal line dislocation and distortion.
