Modern industrial gas-turbine spray combustors feature multiple swirlers and distributed fuel
injection for rapid mixing and stabilization. The present paper is one of a sequence of papers that aim to
study the flow field of such combustor, the related combustion characteristics and their control. The
present paper focuses on experimental investigation of the velocity flow field downstream of a Triple
Annular Research Swirlers (TARS). Multiple combinations of swirlers were tested in cold flow under
atmospheric conditions with and without confined combustion chamber. The experiments showed that a
central recirculation zone, an annular jet with internal and external shear layers characterize the flow field
downstream of TARS. The Central Toroidal Recirculation Zone (CTRZ) region is axisymmetric but the jet
contains imprints of the internal flow and has some nonaxisymmetric features. Comparison of experimental
results with CFD simulations are discussed. Numerical results confirmed these observations and showed
that the asymmetry relates to the effect of the internal swirling vanes on the jet flow. Compared to
unconfined case, flow with confined tube shows an enlarged CTRZ region and a recirculation region in the
expansion corner and reduced concentration of turbulence intensity in the jet region.
Introduction
Many modern combustion systems are
characterized by multiple annular counter-
rotating [1] and co-rotating [2] axial swirlers
with distributed fuel injection points. This design
results in efficient combustion with extremely
low 
emissions 
and 
improved 
combustor
performance. 
These systems are usually
operated near the lean flammability limit where
they may exhibit combustion instability [1]. The
various mechanisms that may trigger combustion
instabilities have been studied extensively in
dump combustors, bluff body stabilized system
and single swirler combustors [3,4]. 
Flow
related 
instabilities, 
equivalence 
ratio
fluctuations, acoustic coupling and unstable
spray dynamics were identified as the dominant
mechanisms 
that 
drive 
thermoacoustic
oscillations when Rayleigh's instability criterion
[5] is satisfied. Studies of the fluid dynamics of
multi-swirlers 
systems, 
their 
mixing
characteristics, the flow instabilities and the
potential driving mechanisms of combustion
instabilities are quite limited. 
To achieve
optimum 
combustion 
performance 
while
minimizing 
the 
propensity 
to 
develop
thermoacoustic instabilities it is important to
study the cold and hot flow patterns in a
geometry that simulates the design of modern
multi swirl combustor systems.
The 
Triple Annular Research Swirler
(TARS), which is the topic of the present
investigation, features three swirling air passages
and distributed fuel injection pattern. The nozzle
is developed by Delavan Gas Turbine Products
(a 
division 
of 
Goodrich 
Aerospace) 
in
cooperation 
with General Electric Aircraft
Engines (GEAE). The purpose of the study is to
investigate the effect of the internal design of the
fuel injector on the mixing pattern in the
combustion chamber.
Numerous efforts have been devoted to
study swirling flows in various combustion
systems such as gas turbine engines and diesel
engines. The swirling flows are used to improve
and control the mixing process between fuel and
oxidant streams and in order to achieve flame
stabilization and enhanced heat release rate [6].
Velocity field characteristics of swirling flow
combustors have been investigated extensively
but limited to specific geometry and were
primarily focused on swirling jet flows [7] or on
the interaction of two co- or counter swirling
streams [8]. One of the main features that are
usually observed in swirling flows is the
formation of a Center Toroidal Recirculation
Zone (CTRZ) around the axis of the jet. This

CTRZ is used to stabilize the combustion

within the

chamber. 
Experimental 
results
showed that the CTRZ is a quasi-axisymmetric
bubble developed by vortex breakdown which is
associated with swirling flow exceeding a certain
swirling strength [7,8]. 
Multiple factors,
including inlet conditions and geometry [9],
tangential velocity profile and axial velocity [10]
and downstream conditions [11] affect the
process of vortex evolution and breakdown
process.
In a complex geometry such as TARS in
which several co-swirling or counter-swirling
flows interact, the vortical evolution and
breakdown are complex and detailed velocity
measurements are necessary to resolve the
physical processes that involve mixing and
interaction of three distinct air streams.
While detailed measurements are possible
downstream of the swirier, the internal passages
arc more difficult 
to probe. 
Numerical
simulation is preferred for investigating the
internal flow structure. The numerical results can
be compared at the nozzle exit with the
experiments for validation. Accurate numerical
simulation can also be used as an effective
design tool of complex cocnbustor geometry.
Due to the three dimensionality and periodic
character of the swirling 
flow, accurate
prediction is challenging and requires adequate
approach. 
Spall and Gatski (12J have used
RANS to simulate velocity/vorticity field and
predict the turbulent vortex breakdown. Their
simulations were compared with experimental
results.
The present paper compares the flow field of
four different swirier combinations under the
same operating conditions. Simulation results
will 
be 
presented 
and 
compared 
with
experimental data at the same locations in the
flow field The information obtained here will
subsequently be used to study the fluid dynamics
effect on the flame structure in a multiple swirl
stabilized spray combustor and to develop
passive/active control strategies for improved
performance, 
such 
as 
acoustic 
instability
abatement and emission reduction in industrial
combustors.

The time averaged velocity field of air flow
downstream of the TARS, including axial and
tangential 
mean 
and 
turbulent 
velocity
components were measured using a 2-D TSI
Model 3200L/P Phase Doppler Particle Analyzer
employing a forward scatter mode with a scatter
angle of 30 degree. In order to measure the axial
and 
tangential 
velocity 
components
simultaneously, the test rig was mounted on a 2-
D transverse system, Velmex Model 34D-
9109A. The transverse system, controlled by
Labview software, was used to move the test rig
stably along the horizontal and vertical directions
relative to the LDV beams. The step length and
motor speed were controlled by the software.
An argon-ion laser system made by Ion
Laser Technology, Inc. provided the coherent
laser source with wavelengths of 514.5 and 488
nm. The system was configured as a 2-D LDV,
using the green beam (514.5 nm) to measure the
axial velocity parallel to the axis of the nozzle
and the blue beam (488 nm) to measure the
tangential velocity in the horizontal plane
perpendicular to the nozzle axis. 
The focal
length of the transmitter was 500 mm/250mm
and the receiver was 300 mm. When the test rig
traversed horizontally along a radius that was
aligned with the axis of the transmitter probe,
axial and tangential velocity components were
measured along this radius. 
By rotating the
TARS mounting plate, data along a set of radii
was obtained and was used to construct the
velocity distribution in a cross-sectional plane.
By measuring along the same radius at different
vertical positions, the velocity distribution in a
streamwise plane was obtained.
To improve the signal quality and data rate
of the LDV measurements, a seeding system was
built to seed the entire airflow through the
nozzle. The seeder included a ROSCO 1500 Fog
Machine which was installed inside a high
pressure vessel. Compressed air mixed the fog
inside the vessel and delivered it into the test rig.

Velocity Mapping of Swirler 504545
A complete mapping of the axial and
tangential velocity components was performed in
a plane perpendicular to the axis at z/R = 0.1 and
in the streamwise plane which included the axis
of the nozzle. The mean and RMS axial and
tangential velocity components mapping at
z/R=0.1 for swirler504545 combination is shown
in Figure 4. The following flow structures are
observed in this figure:
1) The axial velocity mapping shows a
central axisymmetric recirculation zone that was
formed around the axis with a diameter of about
IRat this plane.
2) Close observation revealed that at z/R =
0.1 the flow region outside of the CTRZ is not
fully axisymmetric and several peaks can be
discerned near r/R=0.9 in the mean and r/R=0.6
in RMS axial velocity mapping and in the
tangential velocity mapping.
These peaks may be correlated to the
number of vanes in the inner, intermediate and
outer swirlers which were 4, 4, and 8,
respectively. At the location of z/D=0.05 the
non-axisymmetric flow induced by the swirler
vanes was not fully mixed to produce uniform
flow and some locations (corresponding to the
vane geometry) have larger velocity components
than others.
Similar behavior is shown in 
Figure 5,
which depicts the velocity mapping on a
downstream cross-sectional plane at z/R=0.4.
Distinct peaks at mean and RMS velocity
mapping can be clearly observed.
The contour plots of Figure 4 were averaged
circumferentially to produce radial profiles of the
axial and tangential velocity components as
shown in Figure 6 for swirler 504545 at z/R=0.1.
The locations of the peak velocity of the mean
and tangential components are at r/R=0.9 and
r/R= 0.95 The peaks of the RMS velocity of the
two components are at nearly the same locations
at r/R=0.6 where the shear layer of annular jet is
located. The CTRZ has a radius of r/R=0.5
which is primarily contributed by the inner
swirler and partially by the intermediate swirler.
The mean and RMS axial and tangential
velocities in the streamwise plane for this swirler
are shown in Figure 7. This plane extends from
z/R=0.1 to z/R=4.7. The negative region of mean
axial velocity near the centerline shows that the
region of the center recirculation zone extends to
about z/R = 5 (extrapolated from the data). The
momentum of the strong airflow in the annular
jet is gradually reduced as the jet merges into the
main stream at about z/R=2. High magnitude
mean tangential velocity is confined to a small
region near the nozzle exit. A secondary region
of increased level tangential component is
centered at about z/R=1.5. This amplified
tangential 
velocity 
may be related 
to a
circumferential 
flow 
instability mode. This
phenomenon will be further investigated by PIV
and hotwire to capture the unsteady nature of the
flow. The RMS mapping shows that the multiple
swirling flows mix well before reaching the
z/R=2 plane.
Comparison 
of 
Different 
Swirler
Combinations
The circumferentially averaged mean and
turbulent 
axial 
and 
tangential 
velocity
components were measured at an axial distance
of z/R =0.1 for the different swirler combinations
listed in Table 1 (Figure 8). The important flow
features that can be discerned in these figures
are:
1) The 
counter-rotating 
sense 
of
intermediate swirler causes significant reduction
in the diameter of the recirculation zone from
r/R=0.55 to r/R=0.35 while reducing the peak
axial velocity and spreading the peak over a
wider range of radial distance. It seems that the
intermediate counter-clockwise rotating swirling
flow pushes the inner flow inside and mixes with
the outer flow in the radial direction better than
the other swirlers. The intermediate flow has
significant effect on the inner flow field as can
be seen from the RMS of both axial and
tangential components that increase significantly
in the inner flow region. This enhanced mixing
suggests that counter rotating swirler will be a
good choice to improve the inner region fuel
atomization 
but 
may 
reduce 
the 
flame
stabilization.
2) The swirling sense of the inner swirler
has a negligible effect on the flow field because
the inner flow is mostly fully developed. This
can be seen from the nearly identical profiles of
swirlers 304545 and 304545c.
3) The larger swirling angle of the outer
swirler (504545) compared to swirler 304545,
leads to higher magnitude of both the axial and
tangential velocity components. There was no
apparent effect of this increased swirl on the
axial or tangential RMS levels.

Comparison of Data With /Without Confined
Tube
Data for swirler 504545 with and without a
confining tube that simulates the combustion
chamber were taken under the same operating
condition for streamwise plane (Figure 9) and
cross section plane (FigureiO). Figure 9 (a) and
(c) show that the jet and CTRZ are significantly
enlarged by the tube both in radial and axial
direction There is an external recirculation zone,
which is located at the corner of the expansion
tube. Another important observation is that the
maximum axial velocity area, which is spread
between r/R= 1-1.2 and z/R=0~1.2 in the
unconfined 
case, 
tends 
to 
be 
damped
immediately after exiting the nozzle. The
turbulent axial velocity component (Fig. 9b) is
reduced and is more uniformly distributed in the
center region of the jet. This suggests that the
confined tube acts damping the turbulence by
creating a larger uniform CTRZ. This cold flow
observation may have significant effect on the
combustion stability in reacting flow studies.
Circumferential averaged velocity profiles in
Figure 10 confirm this observation quantitatively
by showing the magnitude reduction for confined
case in the mean and RMS velocity compared
with unconfined case.
Comparison of experimental data with CFD
simulation
CFD 
simulations based on the same
operating conditions were performed for swirlers
304545, 3045c45 and 304545c by using Fluent
5.5 3D solver and realizable k-e model. The
comparison of the circumferentially averaged
mean axial and tangential velocities obtained
from CFD simulations and those obtained from
experimental data, 
for swirler 304545 at
z/R=0.25 is shown in Figure 11. The magnitude
and peak location of tangential velocity agrees
well with experimental measurements. The
features of the axial velocity profile calculated
using CFD 
follow 
closely 
those of 
the
experimental data, except the location of the
peak jet velocity which is shifted from r/R=l
(experimental) to r/R=0.75 (CFD). The CFD
simulations of the entire flow field at the exit of
the nozzle (not shown here) showed a region of
separated flow at the boundary between the
intermediate and outer air passages. The flow
separation predicted by the simulations caused
the annular jet to shift inwards resulting in inner
displacement of the velocity peak. 
No such
separation was observed in the experiments
which showed that the flow remained attached to
the wall between the inner and intermediate
swirlers. 
This 
discrepancy 
between 
the
simulations and experiments is the result of the
inability of the CFD to predict the proper
behavior of a turbulent swirling flow near a
diverging wall.
The internal flow in the TARS swirler was
simulated using CFD (Fig. 12). The series of
figures at different axial cross-sections (z/R
locations) show the flow evolution process. The
initial contours at z/R=-0.4 show the imprint of
the eight vane passages. As the flow moves
towards the exit, the eight streams merge, and at
the exit plane (z/R=0) there were four high
velocity regions, similar to those observed in the
measurements presented in Fig. 4. 
The four
spots of the intermediate flow seem to split out
from the axisymmetric flow in the inner passage.
Conclusion
LDV measurements were performed on a
Triple Annular Research Swirler (TARS) in cold
flow test rig that simulates an identical
combustion rig. Axial and tangential velocity
mapping of the flow in different cross sectional
planes and along a centerline streamwise plane
revealed several important flow structures in the
triple annular swirling flow with co-and counter
swirling cases. An axisymmetric recirculation
zone was formed in the center of the flow but the
outer regions showed some non-axisymmetric
features due to incomplete internal mixing. The
strong RMS magnitude induced by the counter-
clockwise intermediate swirler suggests that this
configuration could be a good choice to obtain
enhanced fuel and air mixing. 
However, the
same configuration resulted in reduced central
recirculation zone which may lead to flame
destabilization.
The comparison between confined and
unconfined results for swirler 504545 shows the
significant effect of the confining tube on the
swirling flow in terms of jet expansion and decay
rate, enlargement of the CRTZ and reduced
turbulent intensity which becomes more uniform
in the center jet region.

3-D 
CFD 
simulations
confirmed features that were observed in the
experiment, including the formation of the
central recirculation zone and the four lobed flow
at the nozzle exit. 
The simulations provided
information on the internal flow development
from an initial region which is mostly affected
by the vanes geometry to the external zone that is
governed by the interaction between the three
streams. 
Reasonable agreement between the
velocity profiles predicted by the CFD and the
experimental data suggest that the simulation
approach used here can be an effective design
tool to determine the flow field in fuel nozzles
with complex geometry. Issues of wrong
prediction 
of 
flow 
separation 
should 
be
addressed. Additional measurement will be
performed to resolve the dynamic nature of the
flow field associated with the present complex
geometry.
