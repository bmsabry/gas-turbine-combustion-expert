The thermo-acoustic response of a premixed swirl
burner
C J Lawn
Department of Engineering, Queen Mary and Westﬁeld College, Mile End Road, London E1 4NS, UK
Abstract:
Application of the theory of thermo-acoustic excitation of ﬂames requires a detailed
knowledge of the ﬂame response to plane-wave velocity ﬂuctuations of a given frequency through the
burner. A model of the type of swirl burner used in some designs of industrial gas turbine is
investigated, chemiluminescent emission being used as a marker of the heat release from both methane
and propane premixed ﬂames. The effects of the equivalence ratio and the withdrawal of the swirler so
as to allow an annulus of unswirled premixture are also examined.
It is found that there are two distinct modes of self-excitation. The response of different burner
conﬁgurations in the mode with the higher frequency is analysed in terms of four parameters. These
characterize the position and spread of the axial distribution of heat release ﬂuctuations, and the origin
and effective velocity of convection of disturbances.
From these parameters, the Rayleigh integral can be calculated and this deﬁnes the frequency range
over which excitation is possible. If the acoustic conditions require that the ﬂame is lengthened,
retracting the swirler, reducing the equivalence ratio or changing from propane to methane are all
shown to be possible control measures for this particular burner.
Keywords:
premixed ﬂame, thermo-acoustics, Rayleigh criterion, swirl burner

1
INTRODUCTION
The current practice of ﬁring lean premixtures of natural
gas in gas turbines has led to a need for greater under-
standing of the problem of combustion-induced vibra-
tions. This is due to the susceptibility of such systems
(which are operated in the lean mode to suppress NOx
formation) to thermo-acoustic interactions, and the
possibility that major structural damage will result. The
susceptibility arises from the acoustic characteristics of
the premixture supply system and the combustion
chamber, and from the sensitivity of the ﬂame to the
velocity ﬂuctuations associated with plane acoustic
waves.
The principles of excitation are well known, having
been enunciated by Lord Rayleigh. His criterion for
excitation at a particular frequency may be reﬁned to the
form

ﬂuctuations will build up until non-linear processes in
the ﬂame limit the heat release ﬂuctuation and the
acoustic losses become such that the equality is satisﬁed.
At frequencies close to (but not identical with) the
resonant frequencies of the combustion chamber, little
energy input is required for very high levels of pressure
ﬂuctuation to be attained.
While the principles are established, application of the
theory to a practical system demands detailed knowledge
of the ﬂame response. A number of contributions in the
literature have presented semi-empirical models of the
response of simple ﬂames, and they have been reviewed
by Raun et al. [1]. An early noteworthy example was the
work of Merk [2] on a Bunsen-type ﬂame, since he
introduced a convective
time-delay
concept.
More
recently, Keller and Saito [3] and Buechner et al. [4]
have correlated data from dump combustion systems.
Shih et al. [5] presented detailed measurements of the
Rayleigh integral for such a system but did not attempt to
correlate them. Macquisten and Dowling [6] postulated a
model for a V-shaped ﬂame and used it in conjunction
with the acoustic characteristics of their system to
produce a complete solution, which compared favourably
with experimental results.
This must be the goal for more complex systems, and
the mathematical framework has been set out in papers by
Lieuwen and Zinn [7], Hobson et al. [8], Abbott [9] and
Dowling and Hubbard [10]. Sivasegaram and Whitelaw
[11] have noted the complex effects that swirl can have
on the regimes of excitation. Thus, realistic ﬂame models
are required for swirl burners. Although computational
ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) may make a contribution to this
(see, for example, reference [12]), comprehensive data on
the ﬂame response, such as those recently presented by
Broda et al. [13] and Paschereit et al. [14], are needed.
The present contribution is directed to this end, with the
correlation of a large number of sets of measurements on
a small model. The model was of the type of swirl burner
found in some commercial combustion systems.
Most of the
data
in the
investigations
already
mentioned were obtained by recording chemiluminescent
emission as a marker of the heat release. This principle
was also used here, building on the advanced techniques
and analysis which have previously been deployed on a
bluff-body stabilized ﬂame [15, 16]. These are brieﬂy
described and then the analysis of the acoustics is out-
lined, before the presentation of the results for the various
ﬂames. Finally, these are discussed in the context of a
semi-empirical model which allows calculation of the
Rayleigh integral.
The data are unique in that the advanced ultraviolet
(UV) techniques have been used to demonstrate the
structure (both axial and radial) of the response, at
speciﬁc frequencies, of ﬂames that are close in con-
ﬁguration to those of practical relevance. From this
knowledge base, the effect of changes in fuel, equiva-
lence ratio and burner geometry on the acoustic response
of the ﬂame have been investigated and related to the
acoustic properties of the premixture supply system. Such
a comprehensive approach to the thermo-acoustics of a
swirl burner has not been published previously.

APPARATUS
A diagram of the burner, air and fuel supply system, and
combustion chamber is shown in Fig. 1. The air was
supplied from the laboratory compressed air system
through a constriction to the side of a pipe of 51 mm
diameter with the end blanked off. This effectively
ensured an acoustic velocity node at the end of the pipe.
The air then ﬂowed round a bend to the burner mouth
through a leaf valve and a tapered section in which a 16-
vane swirler was located. The swirler operated in one of
four positions for different experiments:

This is the original conﬁguration, not
fully seated on the tapered section.
Conﬁguration 2.
The swirler in this conﬁguration was
fully seated.

Conﬁguration 4.
Finally the swirler is further retracted

When the swirler was properly seated, the swirl number
was calculated to be about 0.4.
The fuel was piped to a small plenum below the swirler
from which it was injected into the air ﬂow through a tree
of eight pipes, each branch of which had ﬁve down-
stream-facing holes in it. The fuel was thus entrained into
the air in the wake of the branch before the mixture was
accelerated through the swirler. By the time that the air
and fuel left the burner through an oriﬁce of 19 mm
diameter, full premixing was assumed, there being no
ﬁne structure in the resulting ﬂame. In addition, there was
a 1.2 mm hole in the top of the plenum through which
undiluted fuel passed into the recirculation zone in the
centre of the swirler to feed a small diffusion ﬂame. In
conﬁgurations 3 and 4 this pilot ﬂame consumed 12 per
cent of the total fuel, but slightly less in conﬁgurations 1
and 2. Similar fuel injection arrangements are a feature of
some commercial designs. A ﬁfth conﬁguration, desig-
nated conﬁguration 3*, in which the swirler was retracted
to position 3 but the pilot hole was almost completely
blocked, was also investigated.
Fuel and air supplies were measured by rotameters,
corrections to the calibrations being applied to account
for the pressures in them being greater than atmospheric.
An absolute accuracy of 2 per cent FSD (full-scale
deﬂection) may be assumed for these ﬂowrates, giving
the equivalence ratio typically to about 5 per cent. For
convenience in discussion, the indicated volumetric
ﬂowrate Qa is used to label the results. With 1.2 l/min
of propane, Qa  60 l/min corresponded to a burner exit
velocity of about 4 m/s.
The combustion chamber also had a diameter of
51 mm, and it was a straight pipe, 651 mm long, for
these experiments. The bottom section was made of
quartz so as to transmit UV radiation efﬁciently. At the
top, the combustion gases were exhausted to the
laboratory.
Measurements were made of the chemiluminescent
UV emission from excited OH radicals at a wavelength of
307 nm, as discussed in detail in another paper [16]. A
lens mounted on a telescopic tube directed radiation from
speciﬁc regions of the ﬂame on to a photo diode mounted
behind a UV ﬁlter. Stops placed in front of the lens, and
the relative positions of the lens and photodiode,
determined the dimensions of the collection volume.
Three arrangements were employed in the current
programme:
Arrangement (a).
A 2 mm horizontal slit (with the lens
66 mm and the photodiode 180 mm from the centre of
the combustion chamber) collected UV emission from
a thin slice of the ﬂame which was 1.1 mm thick at the
front and less than 0.1 mm thick at the rear. With a
horizontal width varying from 25 mm to 1 mm over the
same distance, this collection volume provided a
reasonable sample of the total cross-section, and the
telescope was traversed vertically to measure the axial
distribution of UV emission.
Arrangement (b).
A circular stop of 10 mm diameter
(with the lens 256 mm and the photodiode 320 mm
from the centre of the combustion chamber) led to a

the results averaged over 60 samples of 0.25 s.

An attempt was made to correlate the principal

4
RESULTS
4.1
Spatial distributions of the magnitude of heat
release
Most of the qualitative results recorded here from the
quantitative UV data were also evident by observation of
the (blue) visible spectrum. Initial measurements were
made on a methane ﬂame with the valve open, the swirler
in the ‘original conﬁguration’, designated conﬁguration 1
in Section 2, and with the UV collection arrangement
designated arrangement (b) in Section 3. Taking the full
spectrum of ﬂuctuations into account, the evolution of the
radial UV intensity distributions with axial distance at
f  0.42 was as shown in Fig. 5a. The ﬂame spreads out
from the region surrounding the pilot ﬂame at x  5 mm,
the UV intensity reaches a peak near the axis between 15
and 20 mm, and then the proﬁle ﬂattens and decays.
The result of seating the swirler to produce burner
conﬁguration 2, and closing the valve, can be seen by
comparing Fig. 5b with Fig. 5a. In Fig. 5b the con-
tributions above and below 300 Hz have been separated
at two axial distances but, since these contributions have
similar distributions in both cases, comparison with Fig.
5a is valid. Clearly, eliminating the annulus of high-speed
unswirled air around the swirler has produced a much
hollower ﬂame, with virtually no heat release on the axis
above the pilot ﬂame and considerable combustion taking
place in the outer region at an axial distance from the
burner outlet plane of x  25 mm. While the deconvolu-
tion analysis makes the radius of cut-off of the ﬂame
(r  20 mm or 4°) at x  10 mm sharper than it should be,
there was in fact very little emission outside this position,
reﬂecting the ‘mushroom’ shape of the ﬂame.
The same results for x  10 mm are shown in Fig. 5c,
but there they are compared with some for a higher
equivalence ratio. This shows that raising the premixture
to f  0.59 by reducing the air ﬂow for the same fuel
ﬂow has the effect of initiating combustion in the
premixture around the pilot ﬂame much earlier.

excitation frequency (Fig. 6a). (The contribution of the
pilot ﬂame to the heat release, below x  7.5 mm, is

4.2
Spatial distributions of the phase of heat
release
The phases of the UV ﬂuctuations relative to the
combustion chamber pressure at the same excited
frequency for methane are shown in Fig. 6b. These
phases are as recorded along chordal lines of sight and
hence are not strictly those at the local position shown in
the plot. However, a deconvolution of the real and
imaginary components of the signal for one axial position
in which there was a substantial variation (x  5 mm)
showed that the error involved in ascribing the phases to
the closest approach of the line-of-sight to the axis, as
here, is likely to be small.
It is seen that the outer regions of the ﬂame are
approximately in phase and lagging the pressure by 100–
120°. The pilot ﬂame lags by slightly more but, in the
region of greatest magnitude, the UV emission is almost
in phase with the pressure. Further along the ﬂame, the
phase lag increases steadily.

The effect of varying the excitation frequency

those in the higher-frequency band associated with the
background turbulence [16]. This was also true of the
frequency-integrated axial distributions, although these
have not been presented. However, in Figs 5c and 5d
there is evidence of a tendency for the integral over the
acoustically excited frequencies to be relatively smaller
on the axis than that over the higher frequencies.

5.3
Explanation for the different ﬂame parameters
It is possible to explain some of these observations for the
higher-frequency modes in terms of the overall pattern of
combustion. Although the pilot ﬂame functions in a way
which anchors the main ﬂame and broadens the limits of
ﬂammability, it appears from comparing conﬁgurations 3
and 3* in Fig. 9c that it delays the combustion in the main
zone. Certainly, the premixed ﬂame could be seen to burn
back into the burner exit when there was no pilot.
The effect of reducing the equivalence ratio appears
from most of the data for propane in Fig. 9b, and from
those for methane in Fig. 5c, to be to lengthen the ﬂame.
This is in agreement with the results of computation and
observation on a swirled ﬂame by Bradley et al.[18], who
explained it in terms of the quenching of the ﬂame in the
shear layer when the laminar burning velocity is small, as
in lean premixtures. The ﬁt of the model equation to the
data for f  0.47 and 0.58 is shown in Figs 9c and d.
As the swirler is retracted progressively, the ﬂame also
lengthens (Figs 9a and d), coming closer to the axis (Figs
5a and b). The high-velocity unswirled premixture
around the periphery of the swirler is carried further
downstream before it can ignite than when all the ﬂow is
swirled.

also gave rise to an increased average convection

Buechner et al. [4] apparently needed no such correction
to the exit velocity, while those on an annular jet showed
convection to be slightly slower than the mean burner exit
velocity [15].
The possibility that some of these modes were in fact
helical, as observed by Paschereit et al. [14], was
speciﬁcally investigated by looking for signiﬁcant phase
shifts between perpendicular UV lines of sight. None was
found.
Finally, Fig. 9bii indicates that the lower laminar
burning velocity for methane than propane at f  0.6
(0.07 as opposed to 0.13 m/s [19]) also results in a longer
ﬂame. Jones and Leng [20] found signiﬁcant differences
in the behaviour of a pulse combustor with just 20 per
cent additions of propane to methane. However, the
possibility of relating the results for the two fuels at
different equivalence ratios through the laminar burning
velocity seems to be eliminated by the fact that the
methane ﬂame was stabilized well below the normal limit
of lean ﬂammability (f  0.5) by the pilot.
5.4
Conditions for excitation
For each of these ﬂame conditions, the contribution to the
Rayleigh integral was evaluated as a function of fre-
quency at each axial location. Similar data were also
generated directly from the measurements; these showed
the same trends and that the contributions from the pilot
ﬂame were small. These model data are presented in Fig.
10a in order to illustrate the mechanism for producing a
positive value of the integral more clearly. At the chosen
frequency of 272 Hz and a velocity corresponding to
Qa  80 l/min, both the swirler-seated (conﬁguration 2)
and
swirler-retracted
(conﬁguration
3)
cases
have
positive integrals. However, the negative contribution
arising from the convection time delay in association
with the later heat release due to retracting the swirler
further (conﬁguration 4) prevents a positive integral.
Similarly, the reduction in Qa to 60 l/min with con-
ﬁguration 3 also reduces the net energy input at this
frequency because the smaller convection delay puts the
peak heat release out of phase with the pressure. Broda et
al. [13] have produced a complete map of the contribu-
tions to the Rayleigh integral for one combustion
condition, and then integrated the data to yield an axial
variation similar to that of conﬁguration 2.
These mechanisms lead to the complex variation in the
Rayleigh integral with frequency illustrated in Fig. 10b.
Excitation is possible in the bands where the integral of
these curves is sufﬁciently positive to overcome the
acoustic dissipation, although its realization depends on
satisfying the phase relationships of the system acoustics
as well. Since the magnitude of the acoustic impedance
for this supply system does not vary dramatically up to
the Helmholtz resonance frequency, and the combustion
chamber impedance is similarly ﬂat up to the quarter-
wave resonance at about 260 Hz, the variation in the
Rayleigh integral may be expected to be reﬂected in the
magnitude of the acoustic pressures.
The computed maxima for these conﬁgurations lie in
the region 150–250 Hz, consistent with the observations.
With propane, only the conﬁguration with the swirler
seated is predicted to excite above 300 Hz and to have a
maximum at about the observed frequency of 248 Hz. At
higher frequencies the positive and negative contribu-
tions from different regions of the ﬂame tend to cancel, as
seen in Fig. 10a. However, with methane in conﬁguration
1, the greater distance between the effective origin and
the peak heat release allows enough time for the Rayleigh
integral (not plotted here) to become positive again in the
range 300–400 Hz, which is where the second strong
peak in the spectrum (at 368 Hz) was observed for these
conditions.
A similar model could be constructed to account for
the lower-frequency mode. However, this is obviously
not described by the same correlations for the magnitude
and phase of the heat release as the higher-frequency
mode, and so is not predicted by the Rayleigh integrals
calculated for Fig. 10b.

with equivalence ratio ﬂuctuations being the mechanism

Another well-known mechanism for acoustic interac-
tion with ﬂames is the generation of large-scale coherent
vortical structures in the shear layers at the exit from the
burner, as discussed by Coats [21]. Lin et al. [22] have
studied such interactions in a lifted jet ﬂame, and Poinsot
et al. [23], McManus et al. [24], Yu et al. [25] and
Schadow and Gutmark [26] and others, the interactions in
shear layers. It is hypothesized that this is the mechanism
for the higher-frequency mode of excitation, the coherent
structures generated in the burner when the acoustic
velocity is at the peak of its cycle giving rise to an
enhancement of the heat release downstream which is in
phase with their passage. This speculation can only be
conﬁrmed by visualizing or measuring the details of the
ﬂow structure, and this is the next phase of the
investigation.
6
CONCLUSIONS
1. The ﬂame in this particular swirl burner was suscep-
tible to two distinct modes of self-excitation. These
were characterized by different distributions of ﬂuc-
tuating heat release and different relationships be-
tween its phase and the acoustic velocity ﬂuctuations
at the burner exit.
2. The modes were distinguished by their Strouhal
numbers, since the frequencies scaled approximately
on the mean burner exit velocity, and so they could be
loosely designated ‘lower-frequency’ and ‘higher-
frequency’ modes.
3. The response of different burner conﬁgurations in the
higher-frequency mode were analysed in terms of four
parameters, which characterize the position and spread
of the axial distribution of heat release ﬂuctuations,
and the origin and effective velocity of convection of
disturbances.
4. The combination of these parameters with the acoustic
impedance of the supply system in the Rayleigh
integral was used to determine the frequency bands
within which excitation was possible, and these were
consistent with the data.
5. Depending on the precise combination of thermo-
acoustic parameters, it may be desirable either to
lengthen or to shorten the axial heat release distribu-
tion, so as to change the convective time delays.
Retracting the swirler to produce an annular sheath of
unswirled premixture, reducing the equivalence ratio,
and changing from propane to methane all lengthened
the distribution.
6. It is possible that the lower-frequency mode was due to
ﬂuctuations in the equivalence ratio associated with
the acoustic ﬂuctuations in the air supply, and that the
higher-frequency mode was due to the periodic
generation of large scale vortical structures in the
shear layer. Further measurements are required to
conﬁrm this.
