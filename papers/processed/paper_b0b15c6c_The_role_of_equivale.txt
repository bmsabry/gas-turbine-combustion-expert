THE ROLE OF EQUIVALENCE RATIO OSCILLATIONS IN DRIVING
COMBUSTION INSTABILITIES IN LOW NOx GAS TURBINES
TIM LIEUWEN and BEN T. ZINN
Schools of Aerospace and Mechanical Engineering
Georgia Institute of Technology
Atlanta, GA 30332-0150, USA
This paper presents a theoretical investigation of combustion instabilities in low NOX gas turbines
(LNGT) that burn fuel in a lean premixed mode. It is shown that these instabilities may be caused by
interactions of combustor pressure oscillations with the reactants’ supply rates, producing equivalenceratio
perturbations in the inlet duct. These perturbations are convected by the mean ﬂow to the combustor
where they produce large-amplitude heat-release oscillations that drive combustor pressure oscillations. It
is shown in this study that in contrast to earlier analyses, which assumed a uniform instantaneous heat
release throughout the ﬂame region, the heat release within the ﬂame may exhibit strong spatial depen-
dence that can signiﬁcantly affect the combustor stability. The proposed instability mechanism is incor-
porated into a model that is used to predict LNGT stability limits. The model results show that LNGT
are highly prone to combustion instabilities, especially under lean operating conditions, and that the regions
of instability can be approximately described in terms of a ratio of the reactants’ convective time from the
fuel injector to the combustor and the period of the oscillations (with some modiﬁcations that account for
the structure of the combustion region). Signiﬁcantly, the developed model’s predictions are in good agree-
ment with available experimental data, strongly suggesting that the proposed mechanism and the developed
model properly account for the essential physics of the problem.
Introduction
Increasingly stringent emissions legislation has in-
creased the demand for low NOX gas turbines
(LNGT) that employ a lean, premixed mode of com-
bustion. However, LNGT are prone to detrimental
combustion instabilities in various operating ranges,
especially under the lean conditions where they are
designed to operate. To develop rational approaches
for preventing these instabilities, an understanding
of the underlying controlling mechanism and capa-
bilities for predicting the conditions under which
these instabilities occur must be developed. Deter-
mining this mechanism is complex because it may
involve interactions between a number of different
phenomena, such as unsteady ﬂame propagation,
vortex–ﬂame interactions, acoustic-wave propaga-
tion, etc. [1]. The objective of this paper is to outline
a mechanism that may be responsible for these in-
stabilities and to develop an instability model that
can predict the conditions under which they occur.
Instabilities in LNGT have been observed under
operating conditions both very near lean blowout
(LBO) [2] and under lean conditions that are re-
moved from LBO [3]. There is increasing evidence
that these instabilities may be at least partially due
to a time-varying fuel and air mixture entering the
combustion zone. Although in Ref. [3] it was found
that reactants’ unmixedness broadened the range of
unstable combustion in the latter case, it is possible
that reactants’ unmixedness also caused the instabil-
ities reported in the former, where variations in
equivalence ratio (f) caused periodic ﬂame extin-
guishment and, thus, a periodic heat addition pro-
cess that drove the reported instabilities. These ob-
servations are supported by the authors’ studies [4,5]
showing that f perturbations (i.e., unmixedness)
produced large heat-release oscillations that could
drive combustion instabilities under lean operating
conditions. These studies strongly suggested that f
ﬂuctuations in combustors operating under lean con-
ditions, even away from LBO, could produce very
large heat-release oscillations that could drive insta-
bilities.
To determine the role of unmixedness in LNGT
instabilities, it is necessary to consider how it can
arise and to elucidate the feedback mechanism be-
tween the f and combustion process heat-release
oscillations

Model Development
This section describes the development of a model
to predict the linear stability limits of an LNGT,
based on the mechanism described in the previous
section. Though a description of some elements of
this model is provided elsewhere [5], an overview of
the model and its assumptions are provided here for
completeness.
Similar to Yang and Culick’s model [7], the com-
bustor system is assumed to consist of three regions:
(1) an inlet duct and fuel-feed system, (2) a com-
bustion region, and (3) a combustion products re-
gion that includes the remainder of the combustor
and exhaust nozzle. To determine the system’s sta-
bility, it is necessary to solve the First-order conser-
vation equations in regions 1–3, satisfy boundary
conditions at the upstream and downstream ends of
these regions, and satisfy conservation laws at the
region interfaces. The solution yields the complex
eigenvalue xn 4 xn,r ` ixn,im that determines the
frequency (xn,r) and damping and ampliﬁcation
(xn,im) of the nth mode. When xn,im , 0, the mode
will grow in time, indicating that the LNGT is un-
stable and vice versa.

Conclusions
The agreement between the model and the data
strongly suggests that f oscillations indeed play a key
role in the mechanism that drives LNGT instabilities
and that the developed model properly accounts for
the essential steps in the driving mechanism. As
there appear to be no reported measurements of f
oscillations during unstable combustion, such mea-
surements would be of interest for a more direct
conﬁrmation of this proposed mechanism.

Joachim G. Wu¨nning, WS Inc, Germany. The author
pointed out that equivalence ratio oscillations are a main
cause for combustion instabilities. Did you try to decouple
mixing and combustion, for example, by mixing far up-
stream and having constant equivalence ratio?
Author’s Reply. At this point, decoupling the mixing pro-
cess from the chamber acoustics by moving the fuel injec-
tion point far upstream has not been attempted at the
Georgia Tech facility (or in practical systems) due to safety
considerations. Although it would be interesting to examine
the effect of moving the fuel injectors upstream upon the
combustor’s stability, such modiﬁcations raise a number of
additional issues that will have to be considered.
First, our analysis suggests that no matter how large the
distance is between the location of fuel injection and the
combustor, as long as sufﬁciently large coherent oscillations
in equivalence ratio persist at the ﬂame region, an insta-
bility can occur. Thus, without actually measuring the ﬂuc-
tuations in the equivalence ratio, it is unclear how far the
injector must be moved upstream to actually attain a com-
pletely homogeneous mixture. For example, recent mea-
surements by Mongis et al. [1] suggest that oscillations in
fuel mass fraction on the order of 6% of the mean and took
up to 35 cm to be smeared out. Although a linear extrap-
olation of these ﬁgures may not be valid, if we run the
numbers simply to get an upper bound, their data suggest
that for typical instabilities with pressure amplitudes of 10–
15% of the mean, a distance of up to 10 m may be required
to ensure that the mixture that enters the combustor is
uniform.
Second, it should be noted that increasing the mixing
length will not necessarily cause a monotonic decrease in
the amplitude of the reactant composition ﬂuctuations.
That is, assuming a standing wave structure in the inlet
section, the amplitude of the acoustic pressure and velocity
at the fuel injector will depend on its location. Thus, it can
be seen that local equivalence ratio oscillations due to ﬂuc-
tuations in airﬂow rate past the injector will decrease as the
injector is moved toward a velocity node and then increase
again as it is moved past it. Similarly, modulations in fuel
ﬂow rate will decrease and subsequently increase as the
injector is moved through a pressure mode.
