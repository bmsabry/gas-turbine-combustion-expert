Suppression of Thermo‑acoustic Instability inside a Rijke Tube 
using an Adaptive Passive Helmholtz Resonator
Nilaj N. Deshmukh1   · Ajinkya Kulkarni1 · 
Afzal Ansari1 
Received: 24 August 2020 / Accepted: 27 April 2024 / Published online: 15 May 2024 
© The Institution of Engineers (India) 2024
Abstract  Thermo-acoustic instability (TAI) inside a lean 
premix combustor is a major hurdle towards operation of 
gas turbines for both aerospace and land based operations. 
It arises due to the coupling between heat release fluctuation 
and pressure fluctuation within the combustor. It is exhibited 
in longitudinal, radial, tangential and bulk mode. The Rijke 
Tube (RT) is a canonical example to study this phenomenon 
on a laboratory scale in which longitudinal modes of TAI 
are seen. In this work a passive approach of using Helmholtz 
Resonator (HR) as an acoustic damper is proposed for sup-
pression of TAI inside the RT. An attempt has been made 
to suppress TAI corresponding to second mode of instabili-
ties. To abate different modes using same HR, its tuning 
is proposed by changing cavity volume with a hinge and 
use of different neck orifice diameters. The designed HR is 
fixed at a certain longitudinal position on RT, to suppress 
TAI of multiple modes. To analyse effectiveness of HR for 
suppressing TAI, wall pressure is measured with pressure 
transducer. To acquire data and analyse it, data acquisition 
system from National Instruments and LabVIEW software 
is used. The obtained result shows around 16 dB reduction 
in sound pressure level with HR of 5 mm neck diameter at 
150° angle of cavity. The designed HR will help to reduce 
TAI at the design stage.

Introduction
With an intention to reduce pollutants like ­NOx, utilization 
of lean pre-mix (pre-vaporised) burners in combustors began 
in the decade of 1980. These pollutants were also respon-
sible for acid rain. Unlike, the earlier combustors involving 
diffusion flame, these produce more power and are more 
efficient. Such combustors are used in gas turbines for both 
aerospace and land based operations. But the major problem 
associated with these combustors, is that they are prone to 
combustion instabilities or TAI (TAI) [1]. Deshmukh and 
Sharma [2] presented a method for TAI characterization.
TAI is self-excited resonant oscillation due to coupling 
between unsteady heat release and acoustic field within 
the combustor. An unusual humming sound or roar dur-
ing the operation of a combustor implies it is subjected to 
TAI. This is undesirable and is accompanied with various 
adverse effects like excessive vibrations, high noise level, 
high heat transfer rates, localized burning, possibility of 
component melting, damage to flame holders and flame 
flashback [3–5]. Heat release dynamics, duct acoustics 
and flow dynamics are main elements of TAI. Combustion 
chambers can be viewed as organ pipes in which acoustic 
pressure and velocity oscillations can be sustained [6]. The

effect of heat source on the boundary of TAI was studied 
[7]. The acoustic waves generated due to monopole heat 
source interfere with the reflected ones forming standing 
waves. These resonant oscillations exhibit pressure varia-
tions near about 1% of static pressure and couple with heat 
release forming a positive feedback loop. In extreme cases 
this static pressure may go high up to 50 bar [1]. Factors 
like vortex shedding, equivalence ratio fluctuations, phase 
difference between heat release and pressure fluctuation, 
liquid fuel atomization with droplet evaporation, location 
and design of fuel injector, inlet temperature and veloc-
ity of gases are responsible for occurrence of TAI. Heat 
release dynamics, duct acoustics and flow dynamics or 
hydrodynamics are main elements of TAI [8]. Rayleigh 
found that it mainly depends upon the phase between heat 
release and pressure fluctuations [9]. To overcome the 
problem of arbitrary scale and unit while quantifying the 
Rayleigh Index, Deshmukh and Sharma [10] proposed a 
coefficient of Rayleigh Index based on normalizing the 
acquired data.
Control of TAI can be achieved by various passive and 
active methods. Passive methods which don’t involve use of 
power are low-cost with no movable parts and effective at a 
particular frequency. Active methods are much more com-
plicated involving use of sensors and actuators. Apart from 
reducing sound pressure level, these ensure that the overall 
combustion properties such as emission levels, flame blow 
out behaviour, and flashback properties are not deteriorated. 
An external perturbation is introduced to the system via an 
actuator, which manipulates the acoustic field or the heat 
dissipation rate. This prevents the onset of TAI or damp-
ens it. These are effective at low frequency. Deshmukh and 
Sharma [11] presented simplest open loop active control 
technique for suppression of TAI using radial micro-jets. 
The net heat gain with control of TAI increases by 21.6% 
[12]. Unlike passive control, active control methods are 
more ‘adaptive’, and have great potential to stabilize com-
bustors even at off-design conditions. Important passive 
techniques to abate TAI include use of acoustic dampers 
like HRs (HR) [13–15], Quarter wave tubes (QWT) [16], 
perforated liners [17], absorbing material [18] and baffles 
[19] were investigated.

Bedout et al. presented algorithm to control noise using 
self- tuning HR [20]. Zalluhoglu and Olgac studied effects 
of the cavity volume, neck length and neck orifice diameter 
on TAI [21]. To achieve low frequency TAI attenuation and 
over broad frequency range, it is better to increase the cavity 
volume or decrease neck length or increase neck diameter 
[21]. Location of HR and heat source also plays a crucial 
role in abating TAI [22]. Krebs et al. [23] used two sets of 
axisymmetric HR of different sizes on annular gas turbine 
to dampen thermo-acoustic. Selamet and Lee [24], analyzed 
the effects of extended neck of HR into the cavity. It was 
found that such an extension acted like a quarter wave reso-
nator in the cavity. Extension of neck into the cavity lowered 
the resonance frequency substantially. Furthermore, it was 
observed that with total extended neck length retained, neck 
expansion or contraction and porosity also affected transmis-
sion loss and resonance frequency [14]. The use of sound 
absorbing lining inside HR was analyzed and it was found 
that its thickness and density affect the acoustic character-
istics of the resonator [14]. Keeping density fixed it was 
seen that increase in thickness lower transmission loss but 
increased stability envelop. The experimental investigations 
of an adaptive control for suppressing TAI in RT was carried 
out by Wei et al. [25].

This paper reports use of adaptive passive HR to supress, 
TAI of different modes. Tuning of HR is proposed by chang-
ing cavity volume with a hinge and use of different neck 
orifice diameters. The designed HR can be fixed at a certain 
longitudinal position on RT, to suppress TAI of multiple 
modes. The effectiveness of adaptive passive HR was evalu-
ated for different neck diameter and volume of HRs. The 
proposed design of resonator can be used to suppress the 
TAI inside the combustor as active or passive method.

Design of Adaptive Passive Helmholtz Resonator 
(APHR)
This study encompasses two aspects: variation of cav-
ity volume of HR using a hinge arrangement and use of 
different neck diameters. The APHR is designed in such 
a way that TAI frequencies in the range of 400 Hz to 
900 Hz can be dampened. Second and third modes fall in 
this region. The cavity of this HR is formed between fixed 
wall attached to the cylinder and movable wall attached 
to the lid. The movable wall rotates about a central hinge. 
The volume of cavity is product of cavity face area and 
constant height of 46 mm. The cavity face area is in the 
form of a sector of radius 25.75 mm with central angle.

Experimentation
Extensive study has been carried out to identify location 
of burner and flow rate of burner on which strong insta-
bility occurred. Therefore, for air flow rate as 9 lpm, LPG 
flow rate as 0.2 lpm (A/F ratio as 45 and equivalence ratio 
as 1.6) and burner position at x/L = 0.06(42 cm) from 
left end, a thermo-acoustic mode was captured. When 
spectral analysis of this signal was done, a dominant peak 
at 608 Hz was observed. This FFT signal was then post 
processed to get SPL in frequency domain as shown in 
Fig. 3. The fundamental, second and third classical har-
monics are of this RT are 272 Hz, 543 Hz and 814 Hz 
respectively. The frequency 608 Hz is near to 543 Hz and 
less than 814 Hz. Hence it is clear that acquired FFT 
signal is second thermo-acoustic mode. To dampen this 
mode, this APHR was placed at antinode point on the RT 
as shown in Fig. 4.
Result and Discussion
With TAI prevailing within the RT, the cavity volume was 
increased by rotating movable wall from 60 to 270 degrees 
with increments of 30 degrees. This procedure was followed 
for all the three neck diameters. From the acquired data fre-
quency spectra are plotted as shown in Figs. 5, 6 and 7 for 
different neck diameter. To check the effectiveness of APHR, 
the obtained results are compared with background noise 
and without APHR for different volume. Considering use 
of APHR with all three neck diameters it was clearly seen 
that there is reduction in SPL with the use of APHR with a 
shift in frequency. The spectra clearly show, sound pressure 
level decrease till effective volume required for suppression 
and then increases. The availability of additional volume of 
HR not helping to supress the TAI.
The peak SPL and corresponding frequencies are cap-
tured from Figs. 5, 6 and 7. Figures 8 and 9 indicates the 
sound pressure level variation and peak frequency for dif-
ferent neck diameter and volume of APHR. Figure 8 shows 
the effectiveness of APHR increases (with decrease in SPL) 
when its cavity volume is increased from its (1/6)th part to 
its half. It is also clear that with use of 5 mm neck orifice 
diameter, maximum reduction in SPL is achieved which is 
about 16 dB. Volume corresponds to 150 degrees is more 
effective as it is closed to effective volume required for sup-
pression. Nagaya et al. [27] proposed technique was limited 
to only second mode of instability, whereas the proposed 
method can be used for suppressing TAI of any mode. From 
Fig. 9, it is seen that with increase in cavity face angle, peak 
frequency increases due to availability of excess volume and 
this is observed for all three diameters. Around 60 Hz fre-
quency shift were observed as the APHR volume increased 
from 60 to 270 degrees. The result obtained by Bedout et al. 
[20] indicates rise in frequency up to certain angle and then 
decrease.
Conclusion
An adaptive passive HR was designed and fabricated which 
has helped in suppression of second and third mode of TAI. 
This study helped in deciding the position of the resona-
tor along the length of the RT for its effective use. It has 
been observed that the tuning of the resonator by variation 
of the sector angle results in increase of the cavity volume 
and hence suppression of sound pressure level (SPL). The 
second mode of TAI corresponding to frequency of 608 Hz 
has been captured and a reduction of SPL by 16 dB has been 
achieved, whereas study carried out by Zhao [14] able to 
reduce SPL by 5–11 dB. With greater neck diameter size, 
greater suppression of sound has been achieved as the vol-
ume of the cavity is changed from (1/6)th of total volume to 
half. With greater sector angle, increment in peak frequency 
has been observed for all neck diameters. This type of HR 
can be accepted as an alternative to conventional resona-
tors which become bulky due to increase in volume with 
decrease in frequency. This type of HR design can be use 
as active control technique, if change in volume regulated 
through mechanism. Thus help in suppressing the instability 
of any mode present inside the combustor. The effectiveness 
of HR can be increased by using porous material.
