PREDICTION OF TURBULENT PLANE JET IN CROSSFLOW
Kalyan Kalita, Anupam Dewan, and Anoop K. Dass
Department of Mechanical Engineering, Indian Institute of Technology,
Guwahati, North Guwahati, Guwahati, India
In this article numerical predictions of turbulent plane jets discharged normal to a weak or
moderate cross stream are presented. The Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes equations with
the standard k± e turbulence model have been used to formulate the  ow problem. The
governing equations that are elliptic in nature are solved using the  nite volume method.
The predictions are presented to illustrate the  ow pattern involved and to assess the per-
formance of the standard k± e model by comparison with available experimental data for
three different jet to cross stream velocity ratios (six, nine, and ten) and the agreement is
found to be satisfactory.
INTRODUCTION
Turbulent jets in a cross¯ ow have been used in many engineering ® elds. Among
various con® gurations of jets in a cross¯ ow, turbulent plane jets injected normally
into a uniform cross stream have numerous applications. Film cooling, eƒ uent
discharged into the atmosphere and into water bodies, and industrial mixing are a
few examples of such applications.
The problem of a turbulent plane jet discharged through a slot into a cross¯ ow
has received considerably less attention in comparison to the round or rectangular jet
in a cross¯ ow. A turbulent plane jet in a cross¯ ow di ers from the turbulent round
or rectangular jet as in the former the jet stream completely blocks the path of the
cross stream in the near ® eld of the jet discharge. According to Demuren [1] the ¯ ow
® eld of the plane turbulent jet is nominally three dimensional, and the computation
of a two-dimensional ¯ ow ® eld is adequate for such a ¯ ow con® guration.
Carter [2] studied heated turbulent plane jets in a con® ned cross¯ ow where he
measured the temperature trajectories, de® ned as the locus of local maximum tem-
perature, for three di erent values of jet to cross stream velocity ratios (R).
Ramaprian and Haniu [3, 4] reported their experimental ® ndings of turbulent plane
nonbuoyant isothermal and heated jets in a mild cross¯ ow for R = 6:0; 9:0, and
10.0. They have measured mean velocity components and their ¯ uctuations together
with the mean temperature, temperature ¯ uctuations, and heat ¯ uxes for heated jets.
They also checked the two dimensionality of the ¯ ow ® eld and concluded that ¯ ow
can be considered as two dimensional (within 5% of the mean velocity variation).

Computational Domain and Boundary Conditions
A rectangular domain is considered for the present computation. In the direc-
tion of cross¯ ow, the upstream (left) boundary is considered su ciently far away
from the jet slot, as suggested by Amer et al. [20]. The downstream (right) boundary is
placed su ciently away from the jet slot such that a zero gradient condition for all the
variables can be applied [21]. The wall from which the jet emanates constitutes the
bottom boundary of the domain, and the top boundary is placed su ciently away
from the bottom so that an initial cross stream velocity condition prevails there [18].
The size of the computational domain depends upon R. For example, for R = 10 we
consider a domain of (5D ‡ 120D) along the y-direction and 240D along x-direction.
Except at the jet discharge, the bottom boundary is considered as a solid wall
where the boundary conditions are obtained using the wall function [15]. It is as-
sumed that the universal logarithmic velocity pro® le prevails in the vicinity of the
wall. Turbulent kinetic energy is zero at the wall, and its dissipation rate in the near
wall region is assumed to be inversely proportional to the distance from the wall [16].
For the other three boundaries and at the jet origin we have used the conditions as

NUMERICAL METHOD
In the current study we have used the ® nite-volume method with nonuni-
form staggered grids where the velocity components are positioned in the middle
of the sides of a control volume, and other variables (p, k, and e) are positioned
in the center of the control volume [22]. The equations involved in this study are
elliptic in nature. To solve these equations a computer code is developed based on
the SIMPLER algorithm [22]. The convection terms together with the di usion
terms of the transport equations for all the variables are discretized using the
power-law scheme [22]. The systems of linear algebraic equations are solved by
alternating the direction of the line-by-line iteration method using the tri-diagonal
matrix algorithm. At each iteration, the variables u, v, k, and e are underrelaxed.
The values of the underrelaxation parameters are taken as 0.45 for u, k, and e,
and 0.75 for v.
To validate the present computer code, we have predicted the centerline
velocity decay of the turbulent con® ned impinging slot jet ¯ ow and compared the
results with those of Seyedein et al. [23] and the agreement is found to be good. The
di erence between the two pro® les is less than 4% at any location. For this purpose
we have used the wall functions [15] for both the top and the bottom boundaries.

CONCLUSIONS
It is shown that the important ¯ ow con® guration of turbulent plane jets in
