Abstract
A new experimental method is described that
provides high-speed movies of turbulent premixed ﬂame
wrinkling dynamics and the associated vorticity ﬁelds.
This method employs cinema stereoscopic particle image
velocimetry and has been applied to a turbulent slot
Bunsen ﬂame. Three-component velocity ﬁelds were
measured with high temporal and spatial resolutions of
0.9 ms and 140 lm, respectively. The ﬂame-front loca-
tion was determined using a new multi-step method
based on particle image gradients, which is described.
Comparisons are made between ﬂame fronts found with
this method and simultaneous CH-PLIF images. These
show that the ﬂame contour determined corresponds well
to the true location of maximum gas density gradient.
Time histories of typical eddy–ﬂame interactions are
reported and several important phenomena identiﬁed.
Outwardly rotating eddy pairs wrinkle the ﬂame and are
attenuated at they pass through the ﬂamelet. Signiﬁcant
ﬂame-generated vorticity is produced downstream of the
wrinkled tip. Similar wrinkles are caused by larger
groups of outwardly rotating eddies. Inwardly rotating
pairs cause signiﬁcant convex wrinkles that grow as the
ﬂame propagates. These wrinkles encounter other eddies
that alter their behavior. The effects of the hydrodynamic
and diffusive instabilities are observed and found to be
signiﬁcant contributors to the formation and propagation
of wrinkles.
1 Introduction
Turbulent
premixed
combustion
has
gained
renewed
interest recently due to its importance in the development
of low emission engines for transportation and power
generation (Cabot et al. 2004; Hayashi et al. 2005; Johnson
et al. 2005). However, there are many unanswered ques-
tions regarding the fundamental dynamics of such ﬂames.
It is generally believed that for a wide range of conditions
the ﬂame front resembles an unsteady, stretched, laminar
ﬂame. In this ﬂamelet regime, the ﬂame consists of thin
sheets that are wrinkled by the turbulence. That is, the
instantaneous topography of these sheets is determined by
the detailed history of the turbulent eddies that interact
with them. Important properties such as the fuel con-
sumption rate, statistical ﬂame shape and position, and the
local chemical reaction details are determined by this
topography and the related unsteady strain rate on the ﬂame
surface. Hence, in order to properly understand and model
turbulent ﬂamelets, we must understand the dynamics of
the unsteady turbulence–ﬂame interaction.
The fundamental unit of the turbulence–ﬂame interac-
tion is the eddy–ﬂame interaction. If we consider the ﬂame
to be a wave propagating at a ﬁxed speed, the simplest
wrinkling mechanism is apparent: velocity gradients in the
reactants will cause certain ﬂame segments to travel faster
or slower in the laboratory ﬂame. In a quasi-steady case,
the ﬂame will position itself such that the reactant velocity
normal to the surface equals the propagation speed,

resulting in wrinkles. Damko¨hler (1940) originally postu-
lated that the velocity gradients implied by a simple
theoretical vortex would produce a pair of conical wrinkles.
The increase in fuel consumption rate between turbulent
and laminar ﬂames was predicted to be due to the increase
in reaction surface area. Since then, this concept has been
modiﬁed to reﬂect more practical scenarios by including
phenomena such as inefﬁciencies in the stretching process
(Meneveau and Poinsot 1991; Colin et al. 2000; Charlette
et al. 2002), the effects of strain and curvature (Chen and
Im 2000; Hawkes and Cant 2000; Chakraborty and Cant
2005), and instabilities arising from hydrodynamic or dif-
fusive effects (Kobayashi and Kawazoe 2000; Yuan et al.
2006).
In order to understand these phenomena and the modi-
ﬁcations they make to the wrinkling process, canonical
studies have been performed involving the interaction
between simple 2D vortex structures and a laminar ﬂame
front. Common conﬁgurations for premixed ﬂame experi-
ments include planar wake generated vortices impinging on
a V-ﬂame (Lee et al. 1993, 1995; Nye et al. 1996;
Hargrave and Jarvis 2006), and interactions between a 2D
outwardly rotating vortex pair and a laminar ﬂat ﬂame
(Roberts and Driscoll 1991; Mueller et al. 1998; Sinibaldi
et al. 1998) or counterﬂow ﬂame (Renard et al. 1998).
These experimental studies have quantiﬁed the effects of
unsteady strain and curvature on both the topography and
chemistry of the ﬂame. Furthermore, the attenuation and
generation of vorticity by the ﬂame front has been inves-
tigated. The intrinsic instabilities of laminar premixed
ﬂames have also been studied by many authors (e.g.
Sivashinsky 1977; Denet and Haldenwang 1992; Kadowaki
1999).
While these studies provide information about funda-
mental processes, studies of fully turbulent ﬂames are
needed to understand the more complex case of turbulent
ﬂame dynamics. In real turbulence the isolated vortex pair–
laminar ﬂame interaction does not occur. Instead, a chaotic
sequence of turbulent eddies impinge on a dynamically
curved and strained ﬂame front. Long chains of eddies may
create large scale wrinkles or may have counteracting
effects and cause negligible wrinkling. Inwardly rotating
eddy pairs may stretch the ﬂame in unpredicted ways.
Parameters such as the interaction angle and inter-eddy
spacing may also play an important role. Furthermore, the
2D isolated vortex conﬁguration does not allow for many
of the effects controlling the evolution of 3D turbulence;
there is no inter-scale energy transfer or vortex line
stretching. Without these effects, the behavior of the vor-
tices, particularly at small scales, will not be accurately
represented. Hence, in order to understand the ability of
real turbulence to wrinkle premixed ﬂames, we must study
their interaction directly.
Recently, Filatyev et al. (2005) obtained instantaneous
images of eddy–ﬂame interaction in a turbulent slot Bunsen
ﬂame using simultaneous PIV and CH-PLIF diagnostics.
They showed what may be isolated cases of wrinkling
phenomena that are similar to those found in the laminar
studies. Filatyev et al. (2007) went further, using double
pulsed stereoscopic PIV to determine statistical ﬂuxes of the
mean progress variable. Acetone PLIF was used to locate
the ﬂame contour. Tanahashi et al. (2005) combined
simultaneous CH-OH PLIF with stereoscopic PIV in a tur-
bulent premixed swirl-stabilized ﬂame. They demonstrated
the need for three-component velocity measurements in
order to interpret ﬂame wrinkling phenomena.
However, these studies only recorded isolated or double
shot images; they did not obtain the time-resolved infor-
mation needed to understand the unsteady dynamics of a
turbulent ﬂame in detail. Wrinkling has been observed at
scales ranging from the turbulence micro-scales (Tanahashi
et al. 2005) to that of the full burner (Filatyev et al. 2005).
Hence, an understanding of the mechanisms causing ﬂame
wrinkling over this entire range is needed. That is, we must
observe the causality between the turbulence ﬁeld and the
ﬂame front dynamics with both high temporal and spatial
resolution. Furthermore, all velocity components should be
measured in order to avoid erroneous interpretations. These
velocities should be measured simultaneously in the reac-
tants and products, allowing the full interaction history to
be recorded. These objectives have not been accomplished
by previous studies.
To achieve them, a new cinema stereoscopic particle
image velocimetry (CS-PIV) system, capable of recording
three-component velocity ﬁelds at several kilohertz with a
spatial resolution of 140 lm (interrogation boxes of
280 lm) has been developed. This system has been applied
to a 2D methane–air slot Bunsen ﬂame and is described
below. A predictor–corrector method of mapping the ﬂame
surface topography from the gradient of particle image
density is presented and its accuracy analyzed using
simultaneous Mie scattering/CH-PLIF images. The global
ﬂame and ﬂow statistics are reported and the effects of
diffusive stability shown to be signiﬁcant. Typical eddy/
ﬂame interaction events are presented and important phe-
nomena discussed. Outwardly rotating eddy pairs are
observed to cause wrinkles concave towards the reactants
that burn through into cusps. The eddies are attenuated by
the ﬂame and ﬂame-generated vorticity (FGV) is created in
the products. Similar wrinkles are formed by larger groups
of eddies. Inwardly rotating eddy groups are found to
create convex wrinkles that grow, encountering further
eddies that affect their behavior. The hydrodynamic and
preferential diffusion effects on the ﬂame stability are
observed and found to be signiﬁcant in the formation and
propagation of wrinkles.

2 Cinema stereoscopic PIV
Development of the cinema stereoscopic PIV system
involved simultaneous implementation of various conven-
tional PIV techniques. Cinema PIV measurements in
turbulent non-premixed ﬂames have been reported by
Upatnieks et al. (2004). Cinema-stereoscopic PIV has been
used by Ganapathisubramani et al. (2007) with the laser
plane normal to the ﬂow direction for the study of non-
reacting turbulence. Employing CS-PIV for the detailed
study of ﬂame dynamics demanded a balance between
conﬂicting requirements while achieving sufﬁcient accu-
racy all three velocity components. That is, we required a
ﬁeld of view and frame rate sufﬁciently large to track the
progress of eddies and capture the resulting ﬂame motion,
but sufﬁcient resolution to identify the smallest scale tur-
bulent structures. The signal quality was dependant on the
particle image quality, and hence on the focus and light
collection ability of the camera/lens system. This was
greatly effected by the low pulse energy of high-rep-rate
lasers and the requirement to ﬁlter chemiluminescence
from the ﬂame. Hence, it was necessary to optimize several
factors including camera resolution, camera and laser
frame rate, magniﬁcation, ﬂow speed, stereo conﬁguration,
sheet dimensions, and PIV particle properties.
A schematic of the CS-PIV system is shown in Fig. 1a.
An angular arrangement satisfying the Scheimpﬂug crite-
rion was employed. The angle between the laser sheet
normal and the lens axis was 35. The cameras were placed
in a dual forward scatter conﬁguration to increase light
gathering and maximize the useful ﬁeld of view. The two
cameras used were Phantom v9.0 s (Vision Research),
which posses a maximum resolution of 1,632 9 1,200
11.5 lm square pixels at frame rates of over 1 kHz. Higher
frame rates are possible at lower resolution. A pair of
ORC-1000 high-rep-rate Nd:YAG lasers (Clark-MXR),
capable of producing 8 mJ per pulse of 532 nm light at
several kilohertz, were used to create the light sheets. The
beams from the two lasers were polarized orthogonally to
each other and combined using a polarization based
system. This avoided the high losses common to non-
polarizing systems. To eliminate the difference in scatter-
ing from the S- and P-beams, the polarization was then
rotated 45 by a half wave plate. A sufﬁciently thin sheet
was produced by passing the laser beam through a 59
Galilean telescope. This provided sufﬁcient vertical and
horizontal expansion to create a sheet with a full-width at
half-maximum of approximately 300 lm and a usable
height of 20 mm.
A balance between ﬁeld of view, frame rate, resolution,
and ﬂow speed was found with the cameras operating at a
resolution of 672 9 1,120 pixels and frame rate (rf) of
2,222 Hz. Particle image pairs of a 12.8 mm 9 18.2 mm
ﬁeld of view were obtained every 0.9 ms. With the long
axis aligned with the principal ﬂow direction, this allowed
approximately 13 images of an eddy convecting at 1.5 m/s
to be obtained. Frame straddling was employed and the
time delay (DtPIV) between correlated particle images was
set by the laser pulses as 40 ls. A timing diagram
describing the operation of the system is provided in Fig. 1.
Data was collected in spans of approximately 1 s, resulting
in over 4,000 images and 3.5 GB of data per run.
The ﬂow was seeded with silica coated titanium dioxide
particles (Huntsman A-PP2). These particles had a nominal
diameter of 0.15 lm and survived passage through the
ﬂame. It was found that these particles possessed a much
narrower range of particle diameters than other solid, high
temperature ﬂow tracers (e.g. alumina)
due to less
agglomeration. The seed density was adjusted in real-time
to provide an optimal balance between valid PIV vectors in
the reactants and products. That is, the seed density was set
to be slightly higher than optimal in the reactants, resulting
in a slightly lower than optimal level in the products after
expansion. This scheme, combined with an adaptive multi-
pass interrogation method, provided sufﬁcient particles for
accurate vector determination throughout the ﬂow ﬁeld.
Scattered light from the particles was collected by the
cameras using Micro-Nikkor 105 mm lenses operating at
f/8.0. Narrow bandpass ﬁlters were placed in front of the
lenses to remove chemiluminescence from the ﬂame. A

The angular conﬁguration of the CS-PIV system created
a strong perspective distortion. That is, in order to maintain
focus across the laser sheet at an acute viewing angle, the
object, lens, and camera planes had to meet at a single
point as described by the Scheimpﬂug criterion (Raffel
et al. 1998). The differences in orientation between these
planes resulted in varying distances between the laser sheet
and camera sensor. This resulted in non-uniform magniﬁ-
cation across the ﬁeld of view. To calibrate the cameras for
this, a target was placed in the light sheet and imaged at
various z-locations. A specially designed target was
required, as standard targets were found to cause unac-
ceptable errors due to two system attributes. The dual-
forward
scatter
conﬁguration
required
simultaneous
imaging from opposite sides of the target. Additionally, the
high resolution of this system required high magniﬁcation
collection optics, resulting in a limited depth of ﬁeld.
Standard two-sided targets created noticeable translation
and focusing errors due to their thickness. Transparent
targets marked on a single side caused similar errors due to
light bending effects in the target medium. To avoid these
errors, a thin, translucent, symmetric calibration target was
used. This was comprised of a thin ﬁlm, marked with
300 lm crosses on a 750 lm grid and sandwiched between
100 lm thick glass ﬂats. This created equal in-plane shifts
for both cameras and out-of-plane shifts an order of mag-
nitude smaller than the depth of ﬁeld.
The distorted images from the cameras were corrected to
a normal coordinate system and registered using the pin-
hole camera model in the commercial DaVis software
package by LaVision. This process mapped equivalent
pixels from each camera to the same physical position in
the measurement plane. Minute adjustments were made to
ensure proper registration of the cameras using the DaVis
stereoscopic self calibration method, which ensures that
simultaneous particle ﬁelds from both cameras contain
identical information. The spatial cross correlation and
consequent velocity components were computed using the
same software. A multi-pass vector evaluation technique
was employed with interrogation box sizes decreasing from
32 9 32 to 16 9 16 pixels with a 50% overlap. This
resulted in a roughly cubical interrogation volume with a
box size of approximately 280 lm and a vector spacing of
approximately 140 lm. The adequacy of the dynamic
range to accurately measure both reactant and product
velocities was conﬁrmed by systematically varying DtPIV.
It was found that the conditional statistics were unaltered
by this parameter in the range of the selected value. The
number of spurious vectors was less than 1.5% in both the
reactants and products.

determined that accurate tracking of eddies with a char-
acteristic size of 0.5 mm was required. The temporal
Taylor scale was determined by ﬁtting an osculating

Flame front location was accomplished by measuring
the dilatation of the gas from the gradient of seed particle
number density. Typical methods of mapping a ﬂame front
from seed density have generally involved threshold tech-
niques (Stella et al. 2001; Pfadler et al. 2005). However,
these techniques rely on an ad hoc threshold value and are
constrained by the spatial ﬁlter selected, which is typically
isotropic. These problems are greatly compounded when
using solid seed; the particles survive the ﬂame, creating a
gradient of number density instead of a sharp cutoff.
However, this gradient itself can be used to accurately
identify the ﬂame surface. That is, we can associate the
ﬂame surface with an iso-contour of particle image density
gradient. Here we use the maximum gradient contour.
However, the highly non-uniform nature of the particle
ﬁeld made accurate determination of these gradients from a
raw image very difﬁcult; isolated seed density variations
and high-frequency noise appeared as false expansion
contours in the reactants. Therefore, a two-step method was
used. First, an initial predictor step with a standard ﬁlter
scheme was applied along with an adaptive threshold
technique. The second step repositioned the ﬂame to
coincide with the center of the local high gradient region
using an edge ﬁnding ﬁlter aligned normal to the ﬂame
boundary predicted in the ﬁrst step. As the density gradient
curve had an S-shaped proﬁle, this corresponded to the
location of the maximum gradient. This two-step procedure
effectively eliminated noise due to variations in particle
seed density as shown in Fig. 3.
In order to quantify the accuracy of this method, com-
parisons were made between ﬂame fronts found from the
particle density gradient with those corresponding to the
center of the CH-layer. This was done using simultaneous
Mie scattering/CH-PLIF images originally taken by Fila-
tyev et al. (2005). As can be seen from Fig. 3, the particle
gradient contour is slightly offset from the CH-layer
towards the reactants. The mean offset from the center of
the CH-layer was 435 lm and the standard deviation was
50 lm. The relatively high standard deviation was caused
by local thickening of the CH layer due to out-of-plane
curvature. In regions where the CH layer thickness was
within 25% of the mean, the mean offset was 425 lm with
a standard deviation of 25 lm.
This difference between the location of the particle
image ﬂame front and the CH-layer is expected. The par-
ticle density is proportional to the gas density. Therefore,
the center of the high particle density gradient region
should correspond to that of the high gas density gradient
region. Chemkin simulations of a laminar methane–air
ﬂame indicate that the maximum density gradient should
occur approximately 415 lm upstream of the center of the
CH-layer. The simulations also indicate that the high
density gradient region occurs slightly ahead of the high
temperature and velocity gradient regions due to dissocia-
tion. This, combined with the low Stokes number of the
sub-micron seed, resulted in an expected accumulated
particle lag due to thermophoretic forces (Sung et al. 1994)
and acceleration of less than 20 lm. Thus it was concluded
that the location of the measured maximum particle gra-
dient contour corresponds to the maximum gas density
gradient contour with an uncertainty of less than 25 lm.
5 Results
5.1 Flame characterization

6 Conclusions
The unsteady dynamics of turbulent premixed ﬂame
wrinkling was observed for the ﬁrst time using cinema
stereoscopic PIV. This system is capable of obtaining three
component vector ﬁelds at rates greater than a kilohertz
with spatial resolution of 140 lm and an interrogation box
size of 280 lm. Various wrinkling phenomena were
recorded in turbulent slot Bunsen ﬂames burning methane–
air mixtures at various equivalence ratios. The ﬂame sur-
face was accurately determined from the gradient of the
PIV particle image density. This contour of maximum
particle density gradient was found to correspond to the
maximum in gas density gradient.
Flame wrinkling caused by outwardly rotating eddy
pairs was found to occur. A wrinkle was formed around the
eddy pair, which was attenuated by the ﬂame. Flame-
generated vorticity having an opposite sign to that of the
original eddies was created downstream of the negatively

The hydrodynamic instability was observed. Flow

negatively curved ﬂame regions and away from those

theory. The velocities thus induced were found to be larger

in the development of ﬂame wrinkles. Further work is
needed to quantify this effect. Preferential diffusion effects
on the ﬂame stability were also observed. The diffusively
stable case exhibited marked resistance to high curvature
wrinkling. This resulted in less ﬂame surface area and a
taller ﬂame than the equivalent diffusively neutral case.
Wrinkling events involving large numbers of eddies
were also observed. These occurred both as a series of
inwardly rotating eddy groups causing a large positive
curvature wrinkle and a foursome of outwardly rotating
eddies causing a large negative curvature wrinkle. The
signiﬁcance of these events in generating ﬂame surface
area indicates that the canonical studies performed using
isolated vortices may be insufﬁcient to describe turbulent
ﬂame dynamics.
