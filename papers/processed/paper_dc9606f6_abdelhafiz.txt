In this study a comparison between the premixed methane oxygen-enriched-air (CH4/O2/N2) and oxy-
methane (CH4/O2/CO2) in a gas-turbine model combustor that imitates pre-mixers in operational air-
fuel dry low emissions gas turbines is presented. The comparison and analyses that follow in this
study are based on the results of experiments conducted. The combustion stability maps were obtained
through the estimation of acoustic limits and measurement of blowout limits within the space of
equivalence ratio (4) e oxygen fraction (OF). The stability maps obtained were superimposed on the
contour plots of constant adiabatic temperature (Tad), Reynolds number (Re), and power density (PD) of
the combustor. Effects of Tad on ﬂame macrostructure, ﬂame stability, ﬂame speed, and blowout
mechanism were investigated. The temperature distributions were also measured. The study results
indicated that blowout of CO2 as well as N2 ﬂames occur at constant Tad. These results are more pro-
nounced in the case of the multi-hole burner because, such burner is not characterized with sporadic
nature of ﬂame lifting and reattachment that dominate the occurrence of a blowout in swirl burner; for a
given OF, 4 at which CO2 ﬂames blowout is higher than that of N2 ﬂames due to the poorer resistance of
oxy-ﬂames to blowout as compared to air ﬂames; the stable combustion zone of CO2 ﬂames is larger than
that of N2 ﬂames.

1. Introduction
To guarantee a complete combustion process in an engine even
with the newest form of technologies an engine has to operate on
excess air [1]. Operating on excess air means more than required
(stoichiometric) air containing 21% of oxygen by volume is sent to
the engine via the intake valve. This will enable the fuel molecules
to have the required amount of oxygen needed for complete
combustion. Although utilization of excess air increases the rate of
air-fuel mixing and hence guarantees a complete combustion
process, heat energy is wasted when excess air is used through the
ﬂue gas [2] due to a large amount of diluent (Nitrogen) present in
the air [3]. The addition of diluent in a combustion process signif-
icantly affects the ﬂame properties in many ways [4] and the effects
of CO2 (carbon-dioxide) dilution on the ﬂame characteristic have
been established [5e7] including the works of the authors [8e10].
Since the cost of producing energy is getting on the high side,
enhancing energy efﬁciency should be of paramount concern [11].
The use of oxygen-enriched air is among the effective energy saving
methods in a combustion process. Since nitrogen draws off part of
the produced energy through the exhaust gas which eventually
lowers the efﬁciency of the fuel-utilization, combustion with
oxygen-enriched air reduces the heat energy loss, fuel consump-
tion, improves fuel variability, power density, and hence improve
the thermal efﬁciency by lowering the concentration of nitrogen
involved. Also, since nitrogen is the basis of NOx formation
decreasing it will reduce pollutants concentration in the ﬂue gas
[3,12e17]. One of the prices to pay when adopting the oxyfuel
technology is the reduction in stability zone when compared with
that of the air/fuel ﬂame [18]. Rashwan et al. [18] reported a
reduction of about 20% in the ﬂammability limits for oxyfuel ﬂame
with a 36% oxygen fraction, and a ﬂashback was reported when the

Runyon et al. [19] studied the effect of diluents (CO2 and N2) on
the combustion stability of premixed oxy-methane combustion in a
generic swirl combustor under atmospheric and elevated pressure
conditions. They reported that for low thermal power and atmo-
spheric condition, the stability range of CO2 diluted ﬂames is wider
than that of the N2 diluted ﬂame considering the total concentra-
tion of oxygen in the premix. Gillon et al. [20] and Chahine et al.
[21] studied the effect of oxygen enrichment on the combustion
characteristic of methane-air laminar diffusion ﬂame. The per-
centage of oxygen fraction was varied from 21% to 30%. They re-
ported that the ﬂame lift-off height is reduced when the oxygen
enrichment method is considered which can be justiﬁed by the
increase in laminar ﬂame velocity and heat release as a result of an
increase in the ﬂame temperature. Merlo et al. [22] investigated the
ﬂame stability of non-premixed turbulent methane ﬂame with
oxygen-enriched air under swirling conditions. They reported that
increasing the percentage of oxygen fraction in the air while
maintaining the same oxidizer ﬂow rate improves ﬂame stability
and combustion efﬁciency. Li et al. [23] investigated the oxyfuel
combustion of methane in three different fast mixed cylindrical
ﬂame burners. The mole fraction of oxygen was varied between
0.25 and 1.0. They reported that a steady ﬂame was achieved when
the mole fraction of oxygen was below 50% for lean to rich range of
equivalence ratio. They also reported that for combustion of a high
mole of oxygen fraction (above 70%) to be considered the width of
the tubular burner should be extremely thin. Cacua et al. [24]
studied the impact of oxygen-enriched air on combustion charac-
teristic for a dual fuel engine. One of the fuel compositions
considered was 60% vol. and 40% vol. of methane (CH4) and CO2,
respectively, and the percentage of oxygen fraction was varied from
21% to 27%. They reported that as the percentage of oxygen fraction
is increased; the thermal and combustion efﬁciencies were
increased, and the combustion stability of the dual-fuel engine was
also improved.
Belohradský et al. [25] compared the combustion characteristics
of oxygen-enhanced combustion techniques which include premix
enrichment and oxygen-enriched-fuel. The considered combustion
characteristics were; nitrogen oxides (NOx) formation, heat ﬂux to
the combustor wall, the temperature of the ﬂue gas, dimensions
and shape of ﬂames, and stability. They reported that the oxygen-
enriched-combustion method; signiﬁcantly reduces the emission
of NOx when compared to the premix enrichment technique;
obtained ﬂames were sharp and stable during the tests, and ﬂames
were elongated for about 25% when the percentage of oxygen
fraction was increased to 35% which is contrary to Baukal [12].
Watanabe et al. [26] examined the structures of lean premixed
turbulent air and oxy-ﬂames in a swirl-stabilized reactor at a macro
(large) and micro (small) scale. They reported that at a macro scale,
the total length of the oxy-ﬂame was noticeably shorter compared
to the air-ﬂame, and that oxy-ﬂames show a higher ﬂame-surface
area density. Evans and Kyritsis [27] investigated the stability of
oxyfuel premixed ﬂame in a narrow duct under the atmospheric
condition. Oxy-methane and Oxy-propane ﬂames were studied and
compared. They reported that several formations of steady and
oscillating ﬂames regimes were noticed and that, the boundaries
between these regimes for oxy-methane ﬂames were solely
dependent on the equivalence ratio rather than the Reynolds
numbers. They also reported that increasing the length of the tube
gives room for more thermal interaction and hence, reduces the
oscillations’ stability for both fuels, which was more evident in the
case of methane. Baigmohammadi et al. [28] studied the effect of
oxygen enrichment, equivalence ratio, and ﬂow rate of the reacting
mixture on the premixed ﬂame dynamics of the propane-air in a
non-adiabatic reactor. They reported that increasing the ﬂow rate
of the reactive mixture, equivalence ratio, and oxygen fraction up to
40% increase the ﬂame stability while, ﬂame instability may occur
when the level of oxygen is beyond 40%. Vega et al. [29] studied the
effect of the electric ﬁeld on the ﬂame shape of a laminar premixed
methane ﬂame. They reported that the ionic wind effects that
initiate the deformation in the shape of the ﬂame reduce when the
ﬂow rate and oxygen enrichment level are increased. Abdelhafez
et al. [30] studied the dependence of ﬂame stability and structure
on the adiabatic ﬂame temperature of premixed oxygen-enriched
and oxy-methane ﬂames (CH4/O2/N2 and CH4/O2/CO2 respec-
tively) in a gas turbine model swirl combustor. They reported that
stable ﬂames of the same adiabatic ﬂame temperature irrespective
of equivalence ratio and oxygen fraction have the same shape and
that, the adiabatic ﬂame temperature is enough to characterize the
stable combustion region of both CH4/O2/N2 and CH4/O2/CO2
ﬂames.
Luo et al. [31] studied the effects of oxygen-enrichment on the
combustion characteristic of a biomass micro fuel. They reported
that increasing the concentration of oxygen under atmospheric
combustion improves combustion performance by reducing the
burning time and enhances the combustion temperature of the

The literature shows that there's still a lot to be done in the area
of lean premixed oxygen-enriched air combustion. Some of the
studies reported in the literature investigated the effects of oxygen-
enrichment on combustion characteristics while some were spe-
ciﬁc on ﬂame stability. A study carried out in a swirl combustor that
relates the oxygen-enrichment level, ﬂame stability, and adiabatic
ﬂame temperature is brieﬂy referred to in the literature. To the best
of the knowledge of the authors, no study on the dependency of
ﬂame stability and structure on adiabatic ﬂame temperature for
premixed methane oxygen-enriched air ﬂames in a micromixer
burner has been reported in the literature. Hence, the objective of
the present study is to investigate the effect of adiabatic ﬂame
temperature, oxygen fraction, and equivalence ratio on ﬂame sta-
bility and microstructure of premixed CH4 ﬂames in a gas-turbine
model combustor using a micromixer like multi-hole burner.

2.3. Experimental procedure
The experimental procedure carried out in this study is pub-
lished in detail by the authors [10]. The main features of the pro-
cedure are the jet velocity was kept constant at 5.2 m/s for all the
considered ﬂames while the mixture ﬂow rates (i.e. CH4, CO2, and
O2) were varied for required combinations of 4 and OF and the
stability map of the combustor was obtained by scanning the
blowout limit for one OF at a time while varying 4. Procedure to
achieve stability map of a combustor is also published by the au-
thors [10].
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Stability maps
Varying the value of oxygen fraction in a diluted O2/N2 oxidizer
is not expected to cause a signiﬁcant change in the properties of
oxidizer because, the properties of the two elements (i.e. O2 and N2)
constituting the oxidizer are similar in some aspects. To be speciﬁc,
their heat capacities and densities are comparably the same with
little difference in their densities been attributed to a small varia-
tion in their molecular weight. Contrary to these, Carbon dioxide is
characterized by higher heat capacity and density. These two
properties are about 150% greater than those of O2 and N2 at a given
temperature and pressure. Therefore, oxygen fraction is expected to
signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the characteristics of oxyfuel ﬂame. One of
the major combustion parameters that are signiﬁcantly affected by
oxygen fraction is the adiabatic ﬂame temperature (Tad), this
parameter is of paramount importance when analyzing premixed
ﬂames as it has been reported for a swirl burner [30,40]. A similar
analysis will be presented here for micromixer ﬂames.
The ﬁrst set of results pertains to stability maps. Figs. 2 e 4 show
the stability maps of the studied gas-turbine model combustor for
both CH4/O2/CO2 and CH4/O2/N2 ﬂames at a constant jet velocity on
the background of adiabatic ﬂame temperature (Tad), power density
(PD), and Reynolds number (Re) respectively. The color scale for the
two maps are the same, this is to ensure that identical comparisons
were achieved. The continuous line and circular marks represent
the blowout limit and points respectively while the dashed line
represents the acoustic limit. In every case, the stable combustion
region lies between these two lines (i.e. continuous and dash lines)
thus, the stable combustion zone is the area established above the
blowout limit and below the acoustic limit. Unlike what was re-
ported for swirl burner [30], blowout points for a set of cases (i.e.
given 4 and OF) are almost consistent meaning the sporadic nature
of ﬂame lifting and reattachment that dominate the occurrence of a
blowout in swirl ﬂames does not occur in the case of multi-hole
(micromixer like) burner. Therefore, the absence of sporadic lift-
ing and reattachment of ﬂame before the blowout in the multi-hole
burner settings makes it easy and accurate to predict the blowout
points and hence, a stable combustion zone.
Several observations can be made by examining Fig. 2 closely.
The inﬂuence of oxygen fraction on adiabatic ﬂame temperature for
an oxyfuel ﬂame can be seen through the steepness in slopes of
constant adiabatic ﬂame temperature contours for CH4/O2/CO2
ﬂames. Also, the value of this temperature (Tad) for these ﬂames is
always lower than that of their counterparts (i.e. CH4/O2/N2 ﬂames)
for a given set of OF and 4. These observations are due to the higher
heat capacity of CO2 diluent as compared to N2 diluent as reported
in the previous study [30]. The CO2 diluent tends to absorb more
heat released from a combustion process thereby leaving a ﬂame
with lower temperature.
An observation that both acoustic and blowout limit consis-
tently follow curves of constant Tad for CH4/O2/CO2 ﬂames reported
by the authors [10] were also observed here for CH4/O2/N2 ﬂames at
the same jet velocity. This can be justiﬁed from Fig. 2 through the R2
value of the exponential ﬁt curves of both ﬂames been greater than
99.7%. Therefore, this study is proving that ﬂame stability is pri-
marily controlled by adiabatic ﬂame temperature, irrespective of
the diluent (either CO2 or N2) used in premixed CH4 combustion
and burner geometry (multi-hole, micromixer, or swirl), with this
ﬁnding more pronounced in the case of the multi-hole burner,
compared to swirl burners. This phenomenon is attributed to the
reaction kinetics because for a similar constant inlet jet velocity, the
blowout and acoustic limits are inﬂuenced mostly by reaction ki-
netics [30]. Since a sensitive balance exists between the ﬂow and
ﬂame velocity at the blowout limit, the constant jet velocity
considered in this study implies that this limit takes place at a
constant ﬂame velocity which strengthens the major role played by
reaction kinetics. It can be seen from Fig. 2 that for a premixed
combustion system running on natural gas and operating at either
constant bulk swirl velocity [30] or jet inlet velocity, the stability
maps are best described by adiabatic ﬂame temperature.
Considering the above concept, it will be good to reiterate that
the design and operation of a premixed oxyfuel gas-turbine
combustor should be based on adiabatic ﬂame temperature
similar to what is attainable in state-of-the-art lean premixed air-
fuel gas-turbine combustors. Even though the equivalence ratio is
used to control the temperature of a ﬂame, the design, operation,
and evaluation of gas-turbine combustors are not based on equiv-
alence ratio but rather ﬂame temperature hence, this study en-
dorses this existing tradition for oxy-fuel gas-turbine combustors.
Some other important observations that can be made from Fig. 2
and when also compared with the previous study [30] are as
follow:
 Stable ﬂames were achieved for CH4/air (OF ¼ 21%) at 4 < 0.50
which is lower than what was reported for swirl ﬂames, this
same condition falls in the blowout zone for oxyﬂames. Either
for CH4/O2/CO2 or CH4/O2/N2 ﬂames, a multi-hole burner dis-
plays a greater performance over a swirl burner [30] through its
ability to function at a lower Tad of 1430 K as compared to 1870 K
[30] before the blowout in case of oxy-ﬂame as shown in
Fig. 2(D). This is beneﬁcial in extending the turndown capability
(low-load limit) of oxy-fuel gas turbines. Oxyfuel ﬂames are
already known for their inferior stability (which is responsible
for its relatively small stable combustion zone) in comparison to
air-fuel ﬂames [39], so using a burner technology that widens
the operability window on the blowout side is of critical
importance to the operation of the gas turbine.
 The equivalence ratio at which CO2 ﬂames blowout is higher
than that of N2 ﬂames at a given OF which conﬁrms the poorer
resistance of oxyﬂames to blowout as compared to air ﬂames.
However, the stable combustion zone of CO2 ﬂames is larger
than that of N2 ﬂames which is contrary to earlier ﬁndings re-
ported for swirl ﬂame [18,30,41,and [42] but is in agreement
with the ﬁndings of Runyon et al. [19]. The situation, in this case,
is because of the negative inﬂuence of CO2 on reaction kinetics
which offers better retardation to the onset of acoustic in-
stabilities, thereby increasing the upper limit of the stable
combustion zone. This ﬁnding agrees with what was reported
by Jerzak and Kuznia [43].
 This study shows that even as high as 70% OF the CO2 and N2
ﬂames are not identical, although the limits are coming closer.
This seems logical because (no matter how small it may be) the
two ﬂames have different chemical kinetics, and the multi-hole
burner shows this difference in their combustion characteristics.

number which houses mixture properties. Differences in slopes of
curves of blowout limits and acoustic limits were due to signiﬁcant
differences in mixture properties (e.g. densities) of CO2 and N2.
Curves of CO2 ﬂames are steeper than those of N2 ﬂames because of
the higher-density of CO2 as compared to that of N2. Hence, using
mixture properties or Reynolds number to explain the stability
maps of premixed CH4 ﬂames in a gas turbine combustor working
under a constant jet inlet velocity is not appropriate.

The second set of results pertains to ﬂame macrostructure.

3.5. Temperature measurements
Fig. 10 e 12 show the ﬂame temperature distributions along the
combustor centerline and in the radial directions at two axial po-
sitions, respectively for both ﬂames as indicated. The purpose is to
show the relationship between Tad, OF, 4, and ﬂame structure. The
temperature measurements’ domain has been well explained by
the authors in their previous study and equations for normalization
were also presented [10]. Operating parameters and Tad of selected
ﬂames are shown in Table 2.
Looking at Fig. 10 closely, it can be observed that the tempera-
ture distribution along the axis of the combustor follows a linear
relationship for both ﬂames. The normalized axial temperature, in
any case, is less than 1.00 due to heat transfer by radiation to the
surroundings and convection to the quartz combustor conﬁne-
ment. Flames with lower Tad tend to have higher normalized tem-
perature values when compared with those with higher value of
Tad. The reason is that the rate at which heat is lost from the lower
temperature ﬂame is less compared to the higher temperature ones
and it is more obvious here due to the dominance of radiation heat
transfer. Even though ﬂames 5, 10, and 13 have the same structure
in both CO2 and N2 ﬂames, there are still small differences in their
temperature distributions which show a silence roles play by OF
and 4 as previously explained [10]. Figs. 11 and 12 show the radial
temperature proﬁles at two different axial positions of 0.97 (close
to the combustor exit) and 0.70 (close to the ﬂame). In any case, the
maximum temperature is obtained at the axis and decreases as the
thermocouple is moved outwardly with a sharp drop when the
combustor wall is approached. The reason for the sharp drop is as a
result of high convective and radiation heat transfer taken place
through the wall.
4. Conclusion
The study results indicate that:
1. Blowout of CO2 and N2 ﬂames occur at constant adiabatic
temperature
2. The outcomes are more pronounced in the case of the multi-
hole burner because, where such burner is not characterized
with sporadic nature of ﬂame lifting and reattachment that
dominate the occurrence of a blowout in swirl burner
3. For a given OF, the 4 at which CO2 ﬂames blowout is higher than
that of N2 ﬂames due to the poorer resistance of oxy-ﬂames to
blowout as compared to air ﬂames
4. The stable combustion zone of CO2 ﬂames is larger than that of
N2 ﬂames; with no similarity observed in the blowout limits of
CO2 and N2 even at maximum OF considered in this study
5. N2 ﬂames follow a contour of constant PD to some extent while
CO2 ﬂames do not
6. There is a limit to what Tad can do when it comes to macro-
structure of N2 ﬂames; and that a linear correlation exists be-
tween the logarithm of ﬂame speed and the inverse of Tad for
oxyfuel ﬂames.
7. The outcomes of this study reinforce the recommendation that
the design and operation of combustors of oxyfuel gas-turbine
should be based on Tad rather than the percentage of exhaust
gas recirculation or dilution ratio.
