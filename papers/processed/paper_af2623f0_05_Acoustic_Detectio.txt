depositions and vane-blockages in swirling flows by utilizing spectral decomposition and
comparison.
I.
Introduction
Many gas turbine engines for propulsion systems feature swirl combustors to achieve stable, efficient, and clean
combustion1-4. Swirling flows often exhibit instabilities characterized by vortex breakdown and the emergence of a
precessing vortex core5 (PVC). The PVC is a naturally occurring three-dimensional time-dependent hydrodynamic
instability that rotates about the central vortex core axis. This phenomenon has been the subject of various
investigations1-3, 6-8. The combustion dynamics in such systems are caused by interactions between the unsteady heat
release with the temporal and spatial inhomogeneity, and unsteady fluid mechanical phenomena of PVC and vortex
breakdown. The PVC is altered by these interactions9-12, and may also in turn modify the combustion processes13-16.
Premixed combustion excites the PVC to higher frequencies and intensities than those of the isothermal flow;
whereas diffusion controlled combustion suppresses the PVC intensities by two orders of magnitudes10. This
damping is primarily caused by a decrease in swirl number due to the axial acceleration of the flow by the
combustion process12. The PVC was also damped by axial and tangential entry natural gas flames due to large radial
density gradients; but it was still detected on the boundary of the reverse flow zone10. Recently, the presence of PVC
in a natural gas lean premixed flame, with an equivalence ratio of Φ = 0.887, has been reported for a wide range of
swirl characteristics16. The PVC is characterized by periodic fluctuations of flow velocity, pressure, and temperature.
The heat release rate oscillates nearly in phase with pressure. The pressure oscillations may couple to, and get
amplified by, the natural acoustic modes of the combustor, leading to feedback loop type combustion instabilities17,

18. Using bluff-body type stabilization, the plenum and flame zone pressure fluctuations, and flame zone and exit
pressure fluctuations for an enclosed flame have been found to be very coherent19. The sources of noise in a swirl
burner can be broadly classified as aerodynamic or turbulence-generated noise, combustion driven oscillations,
combustion noise, and resonant coupling with one of the modes of enclosure. Combustion driven oscillations are
generally linked to system, acoustical, and transition instabilities. Resonant coupling can be witnessed in flows with
strong degree of swirl20. Combustion noise is generated by the unsteady characteristics of the turbulent combustion
process21-23. The sound generation mechanism is a function of rate of change of heat release rate, and does not
depend on the actual heat release rate24.
Gupta et al25 measured radiated noise from swirling flows which were dominated by PVC tones. Singh et al26-27
showed that swirler configuration; single, co- and counter-rotating double swirlers; have substantial effect on PVC,
and the correlation between external sound measurements and internal plenum pressure fluctuations. The objective
of the current experimental study was to detect the flow anomalies in swirling flows through acoustic measurements.
The proposed mechanism was based on the influence of vane-thickness and vane–blockage on PVC and its
associated frequency. Radial swirlers with two different thicknesses were used to evaluate the potential to detect
vane depositions whereas three consecutive vanes, out of 16, were blocked to verify detection of vane-blockage.
Simultaneous external far-field sound and internal plenum pressure fluctuations measurements were performed.
Spectral decomposition technique, which can isolate source characteristics from system response, was employed to
characterize different configurations. The velocity dependent sources features and constant frequency system
response features were obtained from both external sound and internal pressure measurements.
II.
Experimental Arrangement
The experiments were conducted in a lean premixed combustor facility, shown schematically in Fig. 1(a). The
facility is installed in an enclosure with dimensions of 1.3 m (L) X 1.2 m (W) X 3.0 m (H) with an exhaust hood at
the top. The premixer with swirlers was assembled on a plenum, which supplies air and fuel to the premixer. The
plenum was mounted on a three-dimensional traverse system. A buffer plate was installed inside the plenum to
create a uniform upstream airflow.
The air was supplied through the bottom and directly into the plenum. The
maximum achievable air flow rate was 0.15 kg/s.
A radial swirler with a converging-diverging nozzle was employed. A schematic diagram of the radial swirlers
used in this study is shown in Fig. 1(b). The swirling premixer was made up of four parts: nozzle head, upper
swirler, lower swirler, and base plate. The nozzle throat diameter was Dt = 3.81 cm, and the exit diameter was De =
8.64 cm. Both swirlers had an inner diameter of 5.08 cm, with sixteen straight vanes.
The upper swirler was 28.8 mm long, and the lower swirler was 7.6 mm long. The blade angle was 210. The
lower swirler could be installed with different orientations, to form co-rotating or counter-rotating double swirlers.
However, for the present study single swirler configuration with only upper swirler was utilized. The swirl intensity
is characterized in terms of the swirl number, S, defined as3

A miniature piezoelectric ICP pressure transducer, PCB Model 112A22 was mounted on the sidewall of the air

uniform up to 100 kHz, within 1%. The radiated sound pressure measurements were made using microphone from a
Bruel & Kjaer 3599 Intensity Probe, which included a phase-matched pair of 1.27 cm (½”) diameter Bruel & Kjaer

22A, a PC-based data acquisition and analysis system. The system was capable of acquiring data up to sampling
rate of 50 kHz, with anti-aliasing filters and different time domain windows. The same system was used for spectral
analysis, including auto-spectrum, cross-spectrum, and coherence. All external sound measurements were made at
one far field location approximately r/Dt = 9 and x/Dt = 1 away from the combustor. A Hanning time-domain
window was used and 300 averages were performed for all measurements. The bandwidth was 2 kHz, the frequency

III.
Spectral Decomposition Procedure
The acoustic frequency response of the system was identified and the source spectral distribution was de-
convolved from measured far-field and internal sound pressure spectra using a spectral decomposition method28-32
based on kinematical similarity laws.
The spectral decomposition method is based on the assumption that the
acoustic problem may be described using a linear, uncoupled source-filter system with no feedback. The sound
generated by swirling flows is the source and the acoustic response of the system is considered a filter. Firstly,
radiated sound pressure spectral density data were expressed in non-dimensional form. The non-dimensional spectra,
E, for any fixed location may be expressed as the product of two functions G and F as shown in Eq. (2)

Figure 5 presents plenum pressure fluctuations for all three configurations 1.27 mm vanes, 2.54 mm vanes and
with 3 vanes blocked of 2.54 mm vanes swirler over a range of Re value. These are simultaneous measurements with
external sound spectra of Fig. 3. The plenum pressure fluctuations are represented as dB using the same reference
pressure of 20 µPa used for sound. This has been done only to facilitate their comparison. Not all pressure
fluctuations in the flow-field are transmitted as sound and are termed as pseudo-sound. These pseudo-sound pressure
fluctuations do not propagate through the fluid but are convected with the eddy structures in the flow at subsonic
speeds.
The plenum pressure fluctuations spectra differ substantially from the external radiated sound pressure
spectra. The PVC frequencies can be seen in plenum pressure fluctuations spectra as well, with higher amplitudes
compared to the amplitudes in external sound spectra. Despite being upstream of the PVC structure, a strong
pressure fluctuation was present at the pressure transducer locations. The PVC structure generates substantial
pressure fluctuations in the flow-field. However, not all PVC frequencies at different flow rates can be seen in the
plenum pressure fluctuations. Some of them are probably hidden beneath the high-amplitude surrounding spectral
features or may not be getting transmitted. Internal plenum pressure fluctuations are dominated by plenum circular
modes visible at frequencies about 1000, 1350, and 1650 Hz. Figure 6 presents the spectra of Fig. 5 as a function of
St.
The transmission of PVC upstream internally is confirmed by the presence of same characteristic Strouhal
number.
Figure 7 shows the PVC frequency for all three cases as a function of Re. The linear variation confirms the origin
of these frequencies from PVC. At same Re, the swirler with 2.54 mm thick vanes has a higher PVC frequency
compared to one with 1.27 mm vane thickness. The swirler with 2.54 mm vanes has a vane exit area 1.2 times less
than the swirler with 1.27 mm which in turn increases the overall average velocity by a factor of 1.2 for 2.54 mm
vane-thickness swirler. This can be compared to an increase of 1.3 in PVC frequency for 2.54 mm vane swirler
compared to 1.27 mm vane swirler. The results show the importance of vane exit area in establishing the velocity-
field and in determining the precession speed of PVC. This is further confirmed by the results when 3 vanes are
blocked. With further reduction in vane exit area the velocities and in turn PVC frequency is increased further at
same flowrate (Re). The rate of increase in PVC frequency with Re is also in the same order. The blocking of 3
