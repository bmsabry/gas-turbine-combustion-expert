Control of oscillating combustion and noise based
on local ﬂame structure
Gyung-Min Choia, Mamoru Tanahashib,*, Toshio Miyauchib
a School of Mechanical Engineering, College of Engineering, Pusan National University, 30 Jangjeon-dong,
Geumjeong-gu, Busan 609-735, Republic of Korea
b Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering, Tokyo Institute of Technology, 2-12-1 Ookayama,
Meguro-ku, Tokyo 152-8552, Japan
Abstract
To control combustion oscillations, the characteristics of an oscillating swirl injection premixed ﬂame
have been investigated, and control of oscillating combustion and noise based on local ﬂame structure
has been conducted. The r.m.s. value of pressure ﬂuctuations and noise level show signiﬁcantly large values
between / = 0.8 and 1.1. The beating of pressure ﬂuctuations is observed for the large oscillating ﬂame
conditions in this combustor. Relationship between beating of pressure ﬂuctuations and local ﬂame struc-
ture was observed by the simultaneous measurement of CH/OH planar laser induced ﬂuorescence and
pressure ﬂuctuations. The local ﬂame structure and beating of pressure ﬂuctuations are related and the
most complicated ﬂame is formed in the middle pressure ﬂuctuating region of beating. The beating of pres-
sure ﬂuctuations, which plays important roles in noise generation and nitric oxide emission in this combus-
tor, could be controlled by injecting secondary fuel into the recirculating region of oscillating ﬂames.
Injecting secondary fuel prevented lean blowout, and low NOx combustion was also achieved even for
the case of pure methane injection as a secondary fuel. By injecting secondary fuel into the recirculating
region near the swirl injector, the ﬂame lifted from the swirl injector and its reaction region became uni-
form and widespread, hence resulting in low nitric oxide emission. Secondary mixture injection, fuel diluted
with air, is not eﬀective for control of combustion oscillations suppression and lean blowout prevention.

1. Introduction
It has been considered that most of combustion
oscillations or combustion instabilities are caused
by the feedback interaction between natural acous-
tic modes of a combustor and oscillations of heat
release rate [1]. Since such combustion oscillations
or instabilities may cause harmful emissions or
break down of the combustor, a number of studies
have been conducted to elucidate the mechanism
and the control strategies of combustion oscilla-
tion [2–11]. From the viewpoint of pollution for-
mation, NOx has a signiﬁcant impact on the
environment and studies related to NOx reduction
by passive or active control have also been con-
ducted [12–14]. According to Rayleigh [1], we
may easily control combustion oscillations by sim-
ply introducing an energy source out of phase with
oscillations of heat release rate. However, it had

combustors [7–11]. Therefore, a detailed under-
standing of the combustion oscillations and/or
instability mechanism is necessary for an eﬀective
and robust active combustion control.
To obtain important factors for control of com-
bustion oscillations, several experimental studies
have been conducted for ﬂame–acoustic interac-
tions in unstable combustors using phase-locked
measurements [2,3]. Quantitative measurements
of the ﬂames response to acoustic perturbations
have also been conducted [15,16]. These measure-
ments are heavily inﬂuenced by the combustion
system. Since the heat release rate plays important
roles in the sound generation mechanism in the
turbulent combustion ﬁeld [17], it is very informa-
tive to identify the dominant sound source through
the understanding of spatial and temporal ﬂuctua-
tions of the heat release rate of the combustor for
the development of active control schemes.
Recently, the eﬀect of secondary fuel injection
location on the eﬀectiveness of active combustion
control was studied in an atmospheric pressure
using laboratory-scale dump combustor [18]. Sim-
ulation studies have been conducted to evaluate
the control law under wide-range operation of a
generic combustor using secondary fuel injection
[19]. Combustion control using secondary fuel
injection has two advantages: one is that successful
combustion control is possible using considerably
small amount of energy, and the second is that it
reduces the actuator requirements. From these
advantages, the secondary fuel injection method
is desirable to suppress pressure ﬂuctuations and
to prevent lean blowout of the ﬂame. On the other
hand, because it had been considered that second-
ary fuel injection might produce a large amount of
nitric oxide, optimum design has been required to
minimize the amount of secondary fuel. Therefore,
development of control scheme of secondary fuel
injection with low nitric oxide is needed for eﬀec-
tive and wide-range operation.
In this study, the relationship between pressure
ﬂuctuations and local ﬂame structure has been
investigated by simultaneous measurements of
CH/OH planar laser induced ﬂuorescence (PLIF)
and pressure ﬂuctuations to ﬁnd most dominant
factors that generate large pressure ﬂuctuation
and noise. CH radicals are produced at the ﬂame
front and have very narrow width enough to rep-
resent the reaction zone [20,21], where the OH
prevails in the burned side [22]. Furthermore, it
is well known that distribution of CH radicals
agrees with that of the heat release rate [23,24].
This is why we conducted CH and OH PLIF mea-
surement to investigate local ﬂame structure. Con-
trol using secondary fuel injection has been
conducted to suppress ﬂame oscillating and noise
and to prevent lean blowout. Variations of emis-
sion index with secondary fuel injection were also
measured. The results of the present study demon-
strate the relationship between local ﬂame struc-
ture and pressure ﬂuctuations, and successful
control strategy using secondary fuel injection.
2. Swirl injection premixed combustor and exper-
imental method
Figure 1 shows schematics of swirl injection pre-
mixed combustor and details of secondary fuel
injection nozzle. This combustion rig consists of
contraction section, swirl injection section, and
combustion chamber. The inner diameter of
120 mm in the contraction section is reduced to
40 mm diameter in the swirl injection section. The
inner cross-section of combustion chamber was
120 · 120 mm, and the outlet of combustion cham-
ber was contracted to 60 mm · 60 mm. The total
length of the combustion chamber was 590 mm.
On each side of combustion chamber, a silica glass
plate of 120 mm · 170 mm and 5 mm thickness
was installed to allow optical access. The swirl
injector has swirl vanes of 14 mm inner diameter
and 40 mm outer diameter, which inclined 45 from
the streamwise direction. The secondary fuel nozzle
was mounted at the center of the swirl vanes, and its
exit has 8 injection holes (ID = 0.6 mm) inclined
45 from streamwise direction.
The pressure transducer (TOYODA, PD104)
was used for measuring pressure ﬂuctuations in
the combustion chamber. A water-cooling con-
nection tube was used to mount the pressure
transducer at the position 250 mm downstream.
Sound level was measured with

3. Combustion mode of swirl injection premixed
ﬂame
Figure 2 shows pressure ﬂuctuations and noise
as functions of ﬂow rate of main mixture, Qm, and
equivalence ratio, /. Regardless of mixture ﬂow
rates, the r.m.s. value of pressure ﬂuctuations
and noise level show signiﬁcantly large values
between / = 0.8 and 1.1. The power spectrum of
the pressure ﬂuctuations at Qm = 300 L/m and
time-series pressure ﬂuctuation signal at / = 0.8
are shown in Fig. 3. For the large pressure ﬂuctu-
ation and noisy region, most of the pressure ﬂuc-
tuation energy is in the 117–130Hz range. In the
present case, the dominant frequencies can be
attributed to excitation of a quarter-wave mode
of the combustion chamber, when we assume that
the mean temperature of combustion chamber in-
side is 1000–1300K. In the power spectrum of the
pressure ﬂuctuations, we can observe a second
peak near the most unstable mode. Frequency of
the second peak is about 30Hz higher than that
of the most unstable mode. The diﬀerence of these
modes results in the beat tone as shown in Fig. 3B.
We can see that the pressure signal ﬂuctuated with
frequency of the beating as well as the dominant,
and the beat frequency is equal to the absolute va-
lue of the diﬀerence in frequency between the most
unstable mode and neighboring one.

Because the sound source is dominated by heat
release rate in the turbulent reacting ﬂow [17], it is
thought that this complicated local ﬂame struc-

combustion noise. To avoid the large amplitude

adopted secondary fuel injection for the control
of the local ﬂame structure. The ﬂow rates of sec-
ondary fuel, Qsf, are changed from 0.5% to 1.5%
of Qm. For cases injecting secondary fuel, the
equivalence is calculated by considering both the
amounts of main and secondary fuels. Figure 5
shows r.m.s. of pressure ﬂuctuations and noise le-
vel with secondary fuel injection. From 0.5% to
1.0% of secondary fuel injection, the r.m.s. of
pressure ﬂuctuations increased with secondary

ever, the r.m.s. of pressure ﬂuctuations decreased
drastically for the 1.5% secondary fuel injection.
On the other hand, noise level decreased signiﬁ-
cantly when the secondary fuel exceeds 1.0% for
the large oscillating region even though there are
some increments of noise level for lean conditions.
We can see that the noise level decreased over

level reduction about 20dB is observed for 1.0%

pressure ﬂuctuation is larger than that without
secondary fuel condition. To investigate the mech-
anism of noise level reduction, power spectrum of
pressure ﬂuctuations with and without secondary
fuel injection was compared [in Fig. 6]. As inject-
ing secondary fuel 1% of Qm, the neighboring
peak in the power spectrum disappeared, where
the energy at the dominant frequency with sec-
ondary fuel injection is larger than that without
secondary fuel [in Fig. 6A]. The case of / = 0.9
also shows this tendency. The noise level reduc-
tion in Fig. 5 is ascribed to the elimination of beat-
ing of pressure ﬂuctuations through the control of
local ﬂame structure by injecting secondary fuel.
It is thought that the loud noise is generated in
the large pressure ﬂuctuation region. This is why
loud noise is generated for the case without sec-

pressure ﬂuctuations without secondary fuel injec-

tion. From the result of little eﬀect in pressure
ﬂuctuations and noise level for 0.5% secondary
fuel injection, it can also be seen that there is a
minimum ﬂow rate of secondary fuel to control
the local ﬂame structure in the present swirl injec-
tion premixed combustor.

5. Conclusions
We have carried out investigations to make clear
the relationship between pressure ﬂuctuations and
local ﬂame structure in oscillating swirl injection
premixed ﬂame, and controlof local ﬂame structure
has been conducted using secondary fuel injection
to suppress combustion oscillations, noise level,
and nitric oxide production. Conclusions obtained
by the present study include:
1. Beating
of
pressure
ﬂuctuations,
strongly
related with local ﬂame structure, causes high
level of combustion noise and a large amount
of emission index in the present swirl injection
premixed combustor.
2. Beating of pressure ﬂuctuations can be con-
trolled by secondary fuel injection, and this
control is eﬀective on suppression of combus-
tion oscillation, prevention of lean blowout,
and reduction of emission index.
3. Secondary premixed mixture injection is not
eﬀective for control of combustion oscillations
suppression and lean blowout prevention.
