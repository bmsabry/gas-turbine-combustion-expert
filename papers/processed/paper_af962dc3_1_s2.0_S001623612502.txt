A B S T R A C T
Against the backdrop of the continuous global surge in energy demand, excessive reliance on fossil fuels has led 
to increasingly severe environmental pollution and climate issues, making the promotion of sustainable energy 
transition urgently needed. As a hydrogen energy carrier with zero carbon emission characteristics, ammonia 
demonstrates significant application value in the power generation sector and transportation systems, thanks to 
its superior energy density and convenient storage and transportation advantages. Currently, the development of 
ammonia-fueled gas turbine technology still faces numerous challenges, including increased nitrogen oxide 
(NOx) emissions, poor combustion performance, insufficient flame stability, and material performance degra­
dation. This paper systematically integrates the complete technical chain of ammonia as an energy car­
rier—covering synthesis, combustion, emission control, and material technologies—and sorts out the 
development context, current status, and future trends of ammonia gas turbine technology. The core advantages 
of this technology lie in its clean combustion characteristics and compatibility with existing infrastructure; 
however, technical barriers such as NOx control and combustion stability still need to be overcome. Innovative 
solutions, including ammonia-hydrogen co-combustion, ammonia-methane co-combustion, selective catalytic 
reduction (SCR), rich-lean combustion technology, plasma-assisted combustion, and the development of new 
materials, are expected to address these issues. Representative practical cases such as the IM270 Ammonia Gas 
Turbine Project and the Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Demonstration Project mark breakthrough progress in the 
commercial application of ammonia gas turbine systems. Looking ahead, the integration of renewable energy 
systems and artificial intelligence-driven system optimization will significantly enhance the efficiency of 
ammonia gas turbine technology.
1. Introduction
As a cornerstone of modern civilization, energy underpins every 
facet of human life, influencing economic dynamics, industrial output, 
and daily activities. Global economic expansion, particularly in 
emerging and developing nations, has fueled a relentless rise in energy 
consumption. Despite the growing adoption of renewables, fossil 
fuels—coal, oil, and natural gas—still dominate the energy landscape. 
However, escalating energy demand and fossil fuel overexploitation 
have triggered critical environmental crises, including climate disrup­
tion, air pollution, and ecosystem degradation, threatening Earth’s 
ecological balance. Amid this urgency, renewable energy and 
alternative fuels like ammonia have emerged as pivotal solutions to 
address both energy security and sustainability challenges.
1.1. Energy demand and environmental challenges
The global energy landscape remains dominated by fossil fuels, 
which account for 81 % of energy consumption, a ratio unchanged over 
the past 30 years [1]. Despite renewable energy growth, fossil fuels still 
contribute over 75 % of global greenhouse gas emissions [2]. In 2023, 
global energy consumption grew by 2.3 %, far exceeding the 2010–2019 
average of 1.3 % [3], reaching 580 million terajoules annually [4]. 
Projections indicate demand will rise to 660 trillion Btu by 2050, driven

Fossil fuel reliance exacerbates air pollution, acid rain, and climate-

1.2. The importance of ammonia as an alternative energy source
Ammonia, with its energy density of ~3 kWh/L, offers the dual role 
of a zero-carbon fuel and hydrogen carrier [7]. Unlike hydrogen, it le­
verages existing infrastructure for storage and transport via ships, 
pipelines, or rail, overcoming hydrogen’s logistical barriers [7,8]. Green 
ammonia, produced using renewable energy, eliminates fossil fuel use, 
while blue ammonia reduces emissions through carbon capture [7] 
(Fig.1).
As an essential feedstock for synthetic ammonia, hydrogen is an 
energy source closely intertwined with ammonia. Recent studies have 
extensively analyzed the lifecycle and economic aspects of both 
ammonia and hydrogen [9–11]. The lifecycles of ammonia and 
hydrogen are crucial for understanding their roles in decarbonizing 
energy systems and industrial processes. Both energy carriers are central 
to the transition toward a sustainable, low-carbon economy, yet their 
lifecycles exhibit significant differences in terms of production, storage, 
transportation, and end-use applications.
Hydrogen production predominantly relies on steam methane 
reforming (SMR), which is highly carbon-intensive, although growing 
attention is being paid to green hydrogen produced via water electrol­
ysis powered by renewable energy. On the other hand, ammonia pro­
duction heavily depends on the Haber–Bosch process, which combines 
nitrogen and hydrogen under high pressure and temperature, 
consuming substantial energy and emitting carbon dioxide [12]. 
However, advances in electrochemical and photocatalytic ammonia 
synthesis technologies are emerging as sustainable alternatives, partic­
ularly when integrated with renewable energy sources [13,14]. Notably, 
lithium-mediated nitrogen reduction and calcium-mediated processes 
have shown potential for decentralized, low-carbon ammonia produc­
tion [8,15]. These innovations highlight the potential for sustainable 
production of both molecules, though ammonia synthesis currently faces 
greater challenges in achieving high efficiency and scalability compared 
to hydrogen production [16].
The storage and transportation of hydrogen are hindered by its low 
energy density and high volatility. Common methods include high- 
pressure gas storage, cryogenic liquid hydrogen, and chemical storage 
in carriers such as ammonia or methanol [17]. In contrast, ammonia has 
a higher energy density and can be stored and transported more easily in 
liquid form under moderate pressure or temperature, making it a prac­
tical hydrogen carrier [14,16]. Recent studies suggest that using 
ammonia as a medium for long-distance hydrogen transport is feasible, 
especially in scenarios where renewable resources are abundant but 
geographically distant from demand centers [18,19]. Ammonia’s dual 
role—as both a fuel and a hydrogen carrier—underscores its unique 
advantages in the energy transition [14].
The environmental footprints of these two molecules vary signifi­
cantly depending on the production methods. Green hydrogen, pro­
duced via electrolysis using renewable electricity, has very low carbon 
emissions, while blue hydrogen, produced via SMR coupled with carbon 
capture, offers a transitional solution [17]. Similarly, green ammonia 
produced with renewable energy has a negligible carbon footprint, 
whereas conventional ammonia production remains a major source of 
CO2 emissions [16]. Lifecycle assessments (LCA) indicate that inte­
grating renewable energy into ammonia production can substantially 
reduce its carbon intensity, although challenges remain in scaling up 
these technologies [19]. In comparison, the environmental impact of 
hydrogen is more closely tied to its production pathway, with green 
hydrogen having the lowest emission levels [17].
Key applications of hydrogen include fuel cells for transportation, 
energy storage, and industrial processes such as steel production and

From a lifecycle perspective, ammonia and hydrogen offer comple­

Globally, ammonia’s energy sector penetration remains low (1 %), 
but its potential is vast: meeting 5 % of primary energy demand would 
require 1.6 billion metric tons of ammonia, significantly curbing emis­
sions [22]. The ammonia market reached 194.8 MMT in 2023 and is 
projected to triple by 2050 [20], driven by Asia—particularly Japan, 
where power generation demand may surge from 3 MMT/year (2030) to 
30 MMT/year (2050) [23].
The air transportation industry is one of the most challenging sectors 
in the global decarbonization challenge. Its carbon emissions account for 
approximately 2.5 % of the world’s total emissions and are on a 
continuous upward trend with the growth of air travel demand. The 
International Air Transport Association (IATA) has set the goal of 
achieving net −zero carbon emissions by 2050 in “Minimizing residual 
CO2 emissions in 2050″ [24]. The alternative to traditional aviation fuel 
(kerosene) is the core key to achieving this goal. Among numerous 
alternative energy options, green ammonia (ammonia produced from 
renewable energy) stands out as an important candidate for aviation gas 
turbine fuel due to its zero −carbon characteristics, high energy density, 
and relatively mature storage and transportation infrastructure.
The use of ammonia gas turbines in the fields of transportation and 
mobile devices is increasingly receiving interest. Ammonia can either be 
combusted directly or transformed into hydrogen for utilization in fuel 
cells, effectively substituting conventional fossil fuels. This property 
positions ammonia as a viable fuel with significant potential for various 
applications in heavy-duty transport and mobile machinery.
1.3. Research background and significance of ammonia gas turbines
1.3.1. Development background of ammonia gas turbine technology
Ammonia’s potential for gas turbines lies in its low-carbon profile 
and compatibility with existing systems, requiring minimal retrofits. 
However, its slower combustion speed (20 % of natural gas) and higher 
NOx emissions pose challenges [25–34]. Early research in the late 20th 
century laid groundwork for understanding ammonia’s combustion 
properties, with breakthroughs in the 21st century driving commer­
cialization. For example, a 50 kW micro gas turbine achieved stable 
ammonia operation with 89–96 % combustion efficiency but emitted 
1000 ppm NOx [35].
Major corporations are accelerating development: Mitsubishi Power 
aims to launch a 100 % ammonia-fueled gas turbine by 2025, using 
selective catalytic reduction (SCR) to reduce NOx [36]. GE and IHI plan 
to retrofit existing turbines for 100 % ammonia by 2030, while IHI’s 
low-N2O burner (2022) addresses emissions in high-ammonia co-firing 
scenarios [37,38].
Currently, existing low-carbon solutions besides ammonia-fired gas 
turbines include hydrogen-fired gas turbines, methanol-fired gas tur­
bines, and carbon capture systems, among others.
Methanol offers numerous advantages, including emission reduction, 
favorable ecotoxicological properties, and ease of handling, trans­
portation, and storage. Siemens Energy and the New Zealand Tur- 
bomachinery Centre (NZTC) successfully retrofitted an SGT-A20 gas 
turbine to operate on methanol and conducted tests [39]. Direct com­
bustion of methanol results in approximately 10 % lower CO2 emissions 
compared to kerosene. The project also demonstrated the use of bio- 
methanol as a fuel, achieving an overall CO2 reduction of 60 %. These 
tests demonstrate the significant potential of methanol-fired gas turbines 
in reducing carbon emissions. Furthermore, Pashchenko [40] proposed 
a method to utilize low-grade heat for thermochemical decomposition of 
methanol prior to combustion in methanol-fired gas turbines. Analysis 
indicated that this approach improves gas turbine efficiency by 4 %, 
further enhancing the application potential of methanol-fired gas 
turbines.
Hydrogen-fired gas turbines also exhibit favorable low-carbon 
emission characteristics. Using pure hydrogen as fuel in the combus­
tion chamber of a gas turbine results in zero CO2 emissions. Research 
also shows [41]that using a methane-hydrogen mixture containing 75 % 
(by volume) hydrogen as fuel can reduce CO2 emissions by 51.1 % 
compared to methane fuel, demonstrating commendable low-carbon 
performance.
However, it must be emphasized that, compared to methanol-fired 
and hydrogen-fired gas turbines, ammonia-fired gas turbines still 
possess unique advantages. When methanol is used as a fuel, CO2 
emissions are always inevitable; in this regard, ammonia fuel demon­
strates superior zero-carbon emission characteristics. Additionally, the 
application of hydrogen faces challenges such as high transportation and 
storage costs. In contrast, ammonia fuel is easier to transport and store 
due to its better stability and physical properties. Moreover, adapting 
existing gas turbine equipment for hydrogen use requires significant 
structural modifications, whereas ammonia fuel exhibits higher 
compatibility with existing infrastructure.
1.3.2. Application prospects of ammonia gas turbines
1.3.2.1. Power generation industry. Ammonia gas turbines offer a scal­
able pathway for decarbonizing power grids. Mitsubishi Heavy In­
dustries’ projects in Singapore and Indonesia target medium-sized plants 
and isolated islands, leveraging 100 % ammonia fuel [42]. Micro gas 
turbines (e.g., Iki’s 50 kWe system) demonstrate feasibility for small- 
scale applications, though NOx control remains critical [35]. 
Combined-cycle systems, like Mitsubishi’s H-25 series, integrate 
ammonia with steam turbines to enhance efficiency [43].
1.3.2.2. Transportation industry. In aviation, ammonia’s energy density 
suits short-haul flights and drones. Australian firm Aviation H2 plans to 
retrofit aircraft for green ammonia by 2030 [44]. Maritime applications 
benefit from ammonia’s liquid storage advantages, with IMO backing its 
use as a clean fuel [45,46]. Heavy-duty trucks, responsible for 23 % of 
transportation emissions, could adopt ammonia for its range and low 
carbon footprint, with projects like Hydrofuel Inc.’s ammonia-driven 
freight systems advancing feasibility [47,48].
Ammonia gas turbines represent a transformative technology in the 
global energy transition, bridging the gap between decarbonization 
goals and practical infrastructure needs. While challenges like NOx 
emissions and combustion efficiency persist, ongoing research and in­
dustry partnerships—such as those by GE, IHI, and Mitsubishi—are 
driving solutions. As outlined in this paper, ammonia’s role as a 
hydrogen carrier and zero-carbon fuel positions it to reshape power 
generation and transportation, offering a viable pathway toward a

2. Fundamental theory of ammonia as an energy carrier
2.1. Physicochemical properties and combustion characteristics of 
ammonia
Ammonia (NH3) is a carbon-free compound containing 17.6 % 
hydrogen. Under identical volume conditions, liquid ammonia exhibits a 
higher hydrogen content than pressurized or liquefied hydrogen (H2), 
positioning it as an effective hydrogen carrier. Its key advantages as an 
energy carrier include ease of storage, compact space requirements, and 
the ability to be stored at low pressures (8 bar) under ambient temper­
ature (25 ℃).
In the natural world, ammonia predominantly occurs as ammonium 
salts, whereas in its uncombined state, it is found as a colorless gas that 
emits a strong, pungent smell, commonly referred to as anhydrous 
ammonia. Regarding energy storage and transportation, ammonia offers 
distinct advantages: functioning as an effective carrier for hydrogen, 
each mole of ammonia can release 1.5 mol of hydrogen gas, and a cubic 
meter of liquid ammonia has the capacity to store up to 107 kg of 
hydrogen, making it extremely valuable in hydrogen storage applica­
tions. Moreover, the physical characteristics of ammonia render it 
especially appropriate for application in harsh environments, as it has a 
freezing point as low as −77.73 ℃, which allows it to remain in liquid 
form and serve reliably as fuel even in extremely cold conditions [49]. 
From the perspective of environmental sustainability, ammonia serves 
as a representative of zero-carbon fuels, as it does not emit greenhouse 
gases like carbon dioxide during its use and has a minimal effect on the 
depletion of the ozone layer and global warming. These qualities make 
ammonia particularly advantageous in efforts to combat climate change 
and meet carbon neutrality objectives. Given these attributes, ammonia 
is becoming increasingly significant in strategies for energy transition 
and sustainable development.
Ammonia’s physicochemical properties are critical for its application 
in gas turbines. In the gas phase, its density at 1 atm and 15 ℃ is 0.73 kg/ 
m3, with a specific gravity (air = 1) of 0.597, indicating it is lighter than 
air and dissipates rapidly in case of leaks. The liquid phase has a boiling 
point of –33.5 ℃ and a vapor pressure of 8.88 bar at 21 ℃, enabling 
liquid storage under moderate pressure. Notably, ammonia’s flamma­
bility range in air is 15–28 % by volume, with an autoignition temper­
ature of 630 ℃ (930 K), which is higher than gasoline (503 K) and 
natural gas (859 K), reducing the risk of spontaneous ignition during 
storage and transport [49].

Ammonia’s laminar burning velocity at stoichiometric conditions (φ 
= 1) is only 0.07 m/s, significantly lower than methane (0.38 m/s) and 
hydrogen (3.51 m/s). This low velocity poses challenges for stable flame 
propagation in gas turbine combustors, often requiring design modifi­
cations such as increased turbulence or hydrogen co-firing to enhance 
flame stability [50].
Unlike fossil fuels, ammonia combustion produces NOx primarily 
from fuel-bound nitrogen (NH3 → HCN → NO), accounting for over 70 % 
of emissions, rather than thermal NOx from air nitrogen at high tem­
peratures. In gas turbines, this necessitates specialized emission control 
technologies like selective catalytic reduction (SCR) or lean-burn com­
bustion strategies to mitigate NOx output. For example, studies show 
that at 1500 K, fuel-type NOx constitutes 82 % of total emissions, while 
thermal NOx contributes only 18 % [50].
Ammonia has a lower heating value of 18.8 MJ/kg and volumetric 
energy density of 11.3 GJ/m3 (approximately one-third that of gaso­
line). This means that, for the same volume, ammonia stores less energy. 
Therefore, transportation vehicles using ammonia as fuel require higher 
fuel storage capacity. In contrast, traditional fuels such as gasoline and 
diesel are more efficient in terms of transportation and storage. Addi­
tionally, its combustion efficiency is generally lower than traditional 
fuels, necessitating higher fuel consumption rates and potential modi­
fications to turbine designs for optimal performance.
Although ammonia has a lower energy density, its specific energy 
cost provides a notable benefit compared to conventional fuels. For 
large-scale implementations, using ammonia as fuel could be more 
financially appealing. The prices of traditional fossil fuels often experi­
ence market volatility, whereas the production of ammonia can harness 
renewable energy sources like wind and solar, which may lessen 
dependence on fossil fuels and enhance economic stability.
Ammonia is safer than other fuels (such as hydrogen, gasoline, and 
propane) in terms of safety standards. Its strong odor makes leaks 
quickly detectable, and since ammonia is less dense than air, it will 
dissipate rapidly after leakage. Furthermore, ammonia’s low reactivity 
makes it less dangerous than other fuels in the event of accidental fires or 
explosions. However, under high-concentration conditions, ammonia 
vapor may be toxic and corrosive, and it may even be fatal. Therefore, 
appropriate preventive measures still need to be taken during use.
Notably, ammonia’s high octane rating (130 RON) allows gas tur­
bines to operate at higher compression ratios, potentially improving 
efficiency. However, its 1higher minimum ignition energy (8 mJ vs. 
gasoline’s 0.14 mJ) requires enhanced ignition systems, such as plasma 
assist or hydrogen co-firing [50].
Furthermore, combustion efficiency is one of the criteria for evalu­
ating fuels, which is influenced by combustion conditions such as the 
equivalence ratio, combustion equipment, and heat loss due to different 
exhaust temperatures. Wang et. al [51] calculated the waste heat in

2.2. Ammonia synthesis under moderate conditions
Researching the development and application of ammonia gas tur­
bine technology necessitates a focused examination of ammonia syn­
thesis. The traditional method of ammonia synthesis is dominated by the 
Haber-Bosch process, which requires high temperatures of 400–500 ℃ 
and extreme pressures exceeding 100 bar. This approach is energy- 
intensive and environmentally unfriendly. In recent years, numerous 
researchers have been dedicated to developing technologies for 
ammonia synthesis under milder conditions, such as room temperature 
and atmospheric pressure, to reduce energy consumption and environ­
mental impact. The following are several cutting-edge technologies and 
their progress in ammonia synthesis under mild conditions.
2.2.1. Electrochemical ammonia synthesis
The electrocatalytic nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR) has gained 
recognition as a promising alternative to the energy-demanding Haber- 
Bosch method for the sustainable synthesis of ammonia (NH3). 
Numerous outstanding studies have previously analyzed and compiled 
information on the reaction mechanisms, theoretical approaches, 
catalyst design techniques, and methods for ammonia detection in the 
context of NRR [52,53]. Particularly in recent years, substantial progress 
has been achieved in enhancing the NH3 production efficiency of NRR 
electrocatalysts [54,55]. For example, in 2020, comprehensive analyses 
conducted by both Shi’s [56] and Yang’s [57] teams focused on effective 
strategies for the development of highly active electrocatalysts aimed at 
NH3 production. Additionally, in 2021, Pang and his team [58] 
reviewed the performance of two-dimensional nanomaterials in the 
context of NRR. Within this section, we will primarily examine the 
mechanisms underlying NRR, recent theoretical advancements related 
to the reaction.
The current proposals for the mechanisms involved in the reduction 
of nitrogen gas (N2) to ammonia (NH3) encompass several distinct 
pathways: dissociative, associative, enzymatic, and Mars-van Krevelen 
(MvK), as illustrated in the relevant Fig. 3 [59]. In the dissociative 
mechanism, the process begins with the breaking of the triple bond 
between the two nitrogen atoms in N2, requiring a substantial input of 
energy. This breaking results in the formation of two nitrogen atoms, 
referred to as N-adatoms. These N-adatoms are then hydrogenated in a 
series of steps to ultimately produce ammonia, a process that is well 
established through the Haber-Bosch method used for industrial 
ammonia synthesis. In contrast, the majority of electrochemical nitrogen 
reduction reaction (NRR) systems operate under an associative mecha­
nism, wherein hydrogenation takes place before the actual cleavage of 
the N≡N bond. In this scenario, ammonia is produced only after the final 
nitrogen-nitrogen bond has been cleaved. The associative mechanism 
can be categorized further into two specific pathways: the distal and 
alternating pathways, which are based on the order in which the

2.2.2. Mechanochemical ammonia synthesis
Ammonia is synthesized via ball milling using iron powder as the 
catalyst. The entire synthesis process can be divided into two steps: ni­
trogen dissociation and subsequent hydrogenation. In the first step, 
stable N2 is adsorbed and dissociated into atomic nitrogen on the defects 
of the iron particles [Fe(N*)], where Fe(N*) denotes nitrogenated iron 
particles. The low-coordinated defects, which are created by repeated 
collisions during ball milling, are considered highly active for nitrogen 
dissociation. In the next step, N* is hydrogenated into NHx* species (x =
1–3). With the aid of additionally transferred energy, the strongly 
adsorbed NHx* species are subsequently detached from the iron surface. 
Finally, the product ammonia is released [61].
As illustrated in Fig. 4, the ammonia synthesis cycle comprises two 
key steps: nitrogen dissociation and hydrogenation. Active defects on 
the surface of the activated iron catalyst, generated through mechanical 
activation such as repeated collisions, are highly effective for nitrogen 
dissociation. During the hydrogenation step, the energy provided by 
intense impacts promotes the desorption of strongly adsorbed NHx (x =
0–2) intermediates. Throughout these cycles, the iron catalyst exhibits 
adaptive behavior reminiscent of a smart material, as its particle size is 
dynamically self-tuned. Larger iron particles produce more active de­
fects via fragmentation, while smaller Fe(N*) particles exhibit a higher 
capacity for N* adsorption.
Using this mechanochemical method, ammonia was synthesized 
under moderate conditions, with the temperature as low as 45 ℃ and the 
pressure as low as 1 bar. Eventually, a final ammonia concentration as 
high as 82.5 vol% was achieved, which is much higher than that of the 
current state −of −the −art Haber −Bosch process (25 vol% at 450 ℃ 
and 200 bar) and the electrochemical method (10–2,900 ppm, that is 
0.6–170 μM in electrolyte).
2.2.3. Light-driven ammonia synthesis
The process of photocatalytic fixation of nitrogen (N2) is essentially 
dependent on the properties of light absorption, the dynamics of pho­
togenerated charge carriers, and the catalytic mechanisms at the inter­
face of semiconductor materials. Typically, the catalytic process at the 
interface during the photocatalytic nitrogen reduction reaction (NRR) 
involves three key stages [62]: (1) the adsorption and activation of ni­
trogen, (2) a series of hydrogenation steps that lead to ammonia pro­
duction, and (3) the desorption of ammonia molecules from the surface 
of the catalyst. From a perspective of chemical bonding, various pho­
tocatalysts can promote nitrogen activation through five distinct path­
ways: these include dissociative modes, terminal termination modes, 
terminal bridging modes, lateral bridging modes, and terminal lateral 
bridging modes (Fig. 5) [63]. When light is absorbed, electrons from the 
semiconductor’s valence band are excited to the conduction band, 
resulting in the generation of photogenerated electrons and leaving 
behind holes in the valence band. There is a competitive interaction 
between the recombination of carriers and their migration, wherein 
electrons that remain unrecombined are gradually moved into the an­
tibonding orbitals of N2 via surface-active sites, while the holes engage 
in the oxidation of water to produce O2 or H2O2. Due to the considerable 
bond dissociation energy (approximately 941 kJ mol−1) of the N≡N 
triple bond, its reduction involves a multistep proton-coupled electron 
transfer (PCET) mechanism: N2 first experiences a single-electron 
reduction, generating N2
−intermediates, followed by a series of hydro­
genation processes leading to the formation of NH3.
The process of photocatalytic reduction of nitrate (NO3
−) into 
ammonia (NH3) occurs via a sequential mechanism of deoxygenation 
and hydrogenation (Fig. 6) [64]. Initially, nitrate undergoes a two- 
electron reduction yielding nitrite (NO2
−), which is followed by a six- 
electron transfer process that involves alternating cleavage of N–O 
bonds and formation of N–H bonds. This sequence produces metastable 
intermediates like NH2O and NHO. It is important to recognize that 
hydroxyl radicals (–OH) produced by holes generated from light can 
lead to the unwanted oxidation of these intermediates. Thus, efficient 
catalysts are required to possess strong electron-donating abilities along 
with selective adsorption properties to inhibit side reactions while 
facilitating the final reduction stage. In the conversion of gaseous ni­
trogen oxides (NOx), the described mechanism integrates both oxidative 
and reductive pathways. The initial step involves the photocatalytic 
oxidation of NOx to NO3
−, which is essential for the following production 
of NH3. The resulting nitrate then experiences the previously mentioned 
reduction pathway, characterized by carefully controlled electron 
transfers and proton coupling to transform nitrogen-containing com­
pounds into ammonia. Successfully achieving these sequential reactions 
necessitates photocatalysts that demonstrate not only outstanding cat­
alytic performance but also exceptional stability and selectivity, 
ensuring the efficient and precise synthesis of the desired ammonia 
product.
In photocatalytic ammonia synthesis, a fundamental research chal­
lenge persists in designing advanced catalysts that simultaneously 
exhibit broad-spectrum light absorption and effective N≡N bond acti­
vation capabilities. Recent studies have demonstrated progress in this 
field, with several promising photocatalytic systems being developed 
through innovative material design and precise structural engineering. 
There are roughly the following categories: metal oxides, metal sulfides, 
bismuth-based materials, carbon-based materials, metal–organic 
frameworks (MOFs), covalent organic frameworks (COFs), and some 
emerging catalytic material systems (such as perovskites and bio-hybrid 
systems) [66].

3. Technological status and challenges
3.1. Development history and research status
The origin of ammonia gas turbine technology is deeply rooted in the 
convergence of chemical engineering and power generation technolo­
gies. In the early 19th century, the Haber-Bosch process enabled 
industrial-scale ammonia production [103]. This landmark innovation 
not only facilitated the large-scale synthesis of ammonia from atmo­
spheric nitrogen but also laid the foundation for its subsequent role as an 
energy carrier. The core advantages of ammonia as a fuel lie in its 
relatively high energy density (14.3 MJ/kg, comparable to liquid fuels) 
and inherently low-carbon attribute, making it a highly promising key 
option in decarbonization strategies driven by global climate goals.
Meanwhile, the development of gas turbine technology also provided 
fundamental support for the application of ammonia fuel. In 1920, the 
first 370-kW gas turbine was commissioned [103], establishing core 
principles in the fields of thermodynamics, materials science, and 
combustion engineering. Early research focused on optimizing turbine 
design 
and 
material 
durability 
under 
high-temperature 
con­
ditions—technical challenges that resurfaced as key issues in the sub­
sequent development of ammonia-fueled systems. Although ammonia 
was not yet regarded as a priority fuel at that time, breakthroughs in 
aerodynamics, heat transfer technology, and creep-resistant alloys (such 
as nickel-based superalloys) during this phase laid an important tech­
nical foundation for the subsequent R&D of ammonia gas turbines.
Against the backdrop of the gradual consolidation of ammonia 
industrialization and gas turbine technology fundamentals, the first 
major breakthrough in ammonia gas turbine research emerged in 2014. 
Through optimizing premixing ratios and combustor geometry, re­
searchers achieved the world’s first ammonia-fueled gas turbine power 
generation [104], successfully overcoming two core combustion chal­
lenges of ammonia: its relatively low flame propagation speed (0.3 m/s, 
approximately 1/3 that of methane) and its tendency to produce NOx 
during combustion. This breakthrough not only verified the feasibility of 
ammonia as a standalone fuel but also highlighted the need for rede­
signing combustors for ammonia gas turbines based on the fuel’s unique 
properties—a requirement that differs significantly from conventional 
natural gas-fueled gas turbine systems.
In 2017, in-depth experiments on ammonia combustion further 
validated its practical application value [105]. Experimental data 
showed that compared with natural gas, ammonia fuel could reduce CO2 
emissions by 20 % while optimizing thermal efficiency to approximately 
35 %. Notably, this research pioneered the strategy of enhancing flame 
stability through partial hydrogen co-firing—a concept that provided 
important inspiration for the subsequent development of hybrid fuel 
systems and also clarified ammonia’s “dual role”: it is not only a low- 
carbon fuel but also a crucial platform for integrating renewable 
hydrogen and addressing its inherent combustion limitations.
Entering the 2020 s, the development focus of ammonia gas turbines 
shifted to optimizing material corrosion resistance and thermal man­
agement. To address corrosive byproducts generated from ammonia 
combustion (such as NOx and ammonia leakage) and achieve higher 
operating temperatures, a series of advanced cooling technologies 
emerged, including effusion cooling and thermal barrier coatings (TBCs) 
[106]. These technologies enabled the turbine inlet temperature to 
exceed the melting point of structural materials (e.g., Inconel 718, with a 
melting point of approximately 1300 ◦C) by a maximum of 300 ◦C. For 
ammonia gas turbines, this progress holds dual critical significance: 
higher temperatures not only help improve combustion completeness 
and reduce unburned ammonia emissions but also mitigate corrosion 
risks through precise temperature control.
The technical practice in 2022 further demonstrated the synergistic 
effect of such material optimization and combustion improvement—a 
2000-kW class ammonia gas turbine achieved over 99 % greenhouse gas 
emission reduction through the improvement of ammonia premixing 
and blade cooling technologies [107]. However, this practice also 
highlighted the persistent challenge of NOx emissions (approximately 
50 ppm without treatment), which requires solutions through advanced 
combustion strategies such as staged fuel injection or selective catalytic 
reduction (SCR).
The transition of ammonia gas turbines from laboratory research to 
commercialization accelerated significantly in 2021, with the official 
launch of the first fully ammonia-fueled gas turbine project [108]. This 
progress benefited both from the decline in green ammonia costs and 
policy incentives (e.g., the EU’s “Green Deal”). Deployed in a distributed 
power generation scenario, the project verified the feasibility of 
ammonia in grid-scale applications but also exposed gaps in infra­
structure such as dedicated fuel storage and supply chains.

3.2. Numerical simulation in the research of ammonia gas turbines
Numerical simulation plays a pivotal role in advancing the research 
and development of ammonia-fueled turbines, providing a robust 
framework for performance optimization, efficiency enhancement, and 
mitigation of operational challenges. As a carbon-free fuel, ammonia has 
garnered significant attention in the context of decarbonizing power 
generation and industrial processes. However, its combustion charac­
teristics—such as low flame speed and high nitrogen oxide (NOx) 
emissions—require detailed computational modeling to design turbines 
capable of effectively harnessing its potential. Numerical simulations 
enable researchers to investigate the complex fluid dynamics, heat 
transfer, and chemical kinetics associated with ammonia combustion, 
offering insights that are difficult to obtain through experimental 
methods alone.
Numerical studies on the mechanisms and characteristics of 
ammonia combustion have been extensively reviewed in the literature 
[102,111]. This subsection focuses specifically on the application of 
numerical simulations in the study of ammonia-fueled gas turbines.
The control of nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions and the enhancement 
of combustion performance in gas turbines powered by ammonia have 
emerged as key areas of contemporary research. In exploring the com­
bustion behavior and emission outputs of ammonia in gas turbines, a 
study by El-Adawy et al. [112] was performed under two scenarios: 
ammonia-air and ammonia-oxygen combustion conditions. This inves­
tigation offers a comparative assessment of ammonia combustion within 
gas turbine combustors, focusing on environments with both air and 
pure oxygen. By analyzing the impact of the equivalence ratio (ranging 
from 0.7 to 1.3) across both combustion systems, the research seeks to 
clarify the combustion behaviors and emission profiles while identifying 
possible advantages and challenges that each method presents. A 
comprehensive chemical mechanism was integrated into the flamelet- 
generated manifold (FGM) model to precisely simulate the combustion 
dynamics.
Numerical findings reveal that within the equivalence ratio (ϕ) range 
of 0.7–1.3, the combustion of ammonia-oxygen (NH3/O2) consistently 
achieves a greater peak flame temperature than that of ammonia-air 
(NH3/air). The highest temperature values for both combustion sce­
narios are attained under stoichiometric conditions, which reflects the 
ideal fuel-to-oxidizer ratio necessary for complete combustion. In these 
conditions, NH3/O2 combustion reaches a peak temperature of 2860 K, 
which is substantially higher than the 2059 K recorded for NH3/air 
combustion. Additionally, the swirling flow creates a unique V-shaped 
flame structure and establishes both inner and outer recirculation zones 
(Fig. 8). At elevated equivalence ratios, the reaction rates increase, and 
higher temperatures result in the combustion zone expanding and gas 
flow velocities increasing, a trend that is especially notable in the NH3/ 
O2 system.
As illustrated in Fig. 8, the velocities of flames across various 
equivalence ratios are depicted for combustion systems using NH3/O2 
and NH3/air. It is important to note that the maximum flow velocity 
observed in ammonia-oxygen combustion conditions is significantly 
greater. At stoichiometric ratios, the highest axial velocity in the NH3/ 
O2 mixture reaches roughly 17.45 m/s, compared to only 5.27 m/s in the 
NH3/air scenario. This difference can largely be explained by the lack of 
nitrogen in the oxidizer stream when oxygen is used. Nitrogen, the 
primary component of air, serves as a thermal diluent that captures a 
considerable amount of the energy released during combustion, which 
in turn lowers the adiabatic flame temperature (AFT). The absence of 
nitrogen in oxygen-enriched combustion allows for a more complete and 
efficient release of energy, resulting in a notable rise in the adiabatic 
flame temperature. This heightened heat release causes a greater ther­
mal expansion of the combustion products, subsequently creating 
steeper velocity gradients and elevated flow velocities within the 
combustor. As a result, the NH3/O2 combustion system displays more 
dynamic flame behavior and a defined flow field structure in contrast to 
the air-based system. The increased flow velocity not only boosts tur­
bulence, mixing, and overall combustion efficiency, but also modifies 
the recirculation patterns that are vital for stabilizing the flame. These 
results highlight the essential impact of oxidizer composition on the flow 
field, flame characteristics, and performance metrics of swirl-stabilized 
ammonia combustion systems.
Oxygen enrichment notably enhances the combustion of ammonia 
when considering peak heat release rates. At equivalence ratios (ϕ) of 
0.7, 1.0, and 1.3, there are increases in the peak heat release rates by 
factors of 12, 9, and 6, respectively. This improvement is linked to 
elevated flame temperatures, more rapid reaction kinetics, and greater 
stability, underscoring the efficiency potential of the NH3/O2 system.
Regarding NOx emissions, distinct differences are also observed be­
tween NH3/O2 and NH3/air combustion systems (Fig. 9). Under all 
equivalence ratios, NH3/O2 combustion results in significantly higher 
NOx emissions compared to NH3/air combustion, due to its higher flame 
temperature and stronger oxidation potential. At an equivalence ratio 
(ϕ) of 0.7, NOx emissions from NH3/O2 combustion reach 14,054 ppm, 
more than three times the value of 4,188 ppm for NH3/air combustion. 
At ϕ = 1.0, the NOx level for NH3/O2 combustion is 8,095 ppm, 
compared to 3,015 ppm for NH3/air combustion. Even under fuel-rich 
conditions (ϕ = 1.3), NOx emissions from NH3/O2 combustion remain 
as high as 2,728 ppm, whereas the value for NH3/air combustion is 
1,502 ppm. These differences indicate that the absence of nitrogen in 
NH3/O2 combustion eliminates the thermal buffering effect and pro­
motes NOx formation through both thermal and prompt NO 
mechanisms.
In conclusion, throughout various equivalence ratios, the combus­
tion of ammonia (NH3) with oxygen (O2) exhibits excellent flame sta­
bility, elevated peak temperatures, and significantly improved heat 
release rates. Nevertheless, these benefits are accompanied by a notable 
rise in nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions. Conversely, combustion of NH3 
with air presents a less energetic process, consistently resulting in lower 
NOx emissions, especially under stoichiometric and fuel-rich conditions. 
The ideal combustion approach hinges on the specific goals of the 
application, particularly whether prioritizing combustion efficiency or 
minimizing emissions is more critical. This highlights the importance of 
adopting a balanced optimization strategy when choosing between 
NH3/air and NH3/O2 systems for gas turbine applications. The com­
bustion of oxygen-enriched ammonia markedly enhances ammonia’s 
combustion characteristics, effectively tackling challenges such as low 
flame propagation speed and instability. However, this enhancement

The analysis of ammonia combustion performance and NOx emission

importance, as it provides critical insights into the feasibility of utilizing 
ammonia fuel in existing gas turbines. This work offers valuable guid­

concepts: lean-burn dry-low emissions (DLE); rich-burn, quick-quench,

The results indicate that the DLE combustion concept is not suitable for 
ammonia fuel due to the low flame propagation speed of lean ammonia/ 
air mixtures and extremely high NOx emissions, although it is worth 
noting that this concept produces negligible amounts of unburned 
ammonia (NH3). In contrast, the RQL combustion concept demonstrates 
excellent performance for ammonia fuel, ensuring flame stability under 
fuel-rich conditions while maintaining low NOx emissions. The fuel-rich 
flame generates hydrogen (H2), which is transported to the secondary 
combustion zone. In an ideal RQL configuration, combustion in the 
secondary zone occurs under MILD-like conditions, preventing the for­
mation of thermal NOx. Furthermore, the MILD combustion concept also 
shows great potential, with both low NOx and low unburned ammonia 
emissions. However, achieving MILD conditions and ultra-low NOx 
emissions in an ideal MILD configuration requires high levels of dilution, 
which may pose challenges in terms of design and operation.

To further validate the effectiveness of NH3/H2 combustion chem­
istry in practical gas turbine applications, Xiao et al. also investigated 
high-pressure conditions. The results showed that burning velocity de­
creases with increasing pressure, consistent with conclusions from pre­
vious studies [118,119]. The main reason for this effect is the change in 
sensitive elementary reactions under different pressure conditions. 
Therefore, the reduction in burning velocity can be attributed to the 
higher combustion rates observed under high-pressure conditions in 
experiments [119,125]. The mechanism model proposed by Xiao et al. 
provides an efficient, accurate, and computationally less expensive 
modeling solution for predicting various key characteristics of 
ammonia-hydrogen fuel blends under gas turbine-relevant conditions.
3.3. Technical challenges
One of the primary challenges in the application of ammonia as a fuel 
lies in its tendency to generate nitrogen oxides (NOx) during combus­
tion. When nitrogen chemically interacts with the fuel (particularly 
NH3), fuel NOx is produced through intermediates such as CN, HNO, 
HCN, and NHi, which are subsequently oxidized. Additionally, signifi­
cant thermal NOx can be formed under extremely high flame tempera­
tures [126]. These nitrogen oxides pose substantial threats to human 
health and have profound impacts on ecosystems, including contami­
nating drinking water sources [127], accelerating eutrophication pro­
cesses [128], and inducing or exacerbating respiratory diseases [129]. 
Notably, under certain conditions, ammonia combustion can also pro­
duce nitrous oxide (N2O), which has a global warming potential 
(GWP20) 273 times higher than that of carbon dioxide over a 20-year 
period [130]. Recent research indicates that N2O concentrations from 
ammonia/hydrogen mixed fuel combustion can reach 240 ppm, with a 
greenhouse effect comparable to CO2 emissions from a pure methane 
flame operating under dry low NOx (DLN) conditions [131]. Whether 
the nitrogen oxide emissions can be controlled will directly affect 
whether ammonia gas turbines can be widely used in industrial appli­
cations. Given these impacts, effective control of nitrogen oxide emis­
sions has become a critical focus in the development of ammonia-fueled 
gas turbine technology.
The mechanisms of NOx generation during ammonia fuel combus­
tion can primarily be categorized into two types: the Zeldovich thermal 
mechanism and the nitrogen-containing component conversion mech­
anism [132]. When the ambient temperature rises above 1800 K, the 
thermal mechanism becomes dominant, causing the nitrogen in the air 
to undergo oxidation reactions, thereby producing NO. In contrast, the 
nitrogen-containing component conversion mechanism describes the 
chemical process by which nitrogen-based compounds in the fuel (rep­
resented by NH3) ultimately generate NO through multiple intermediate 
reactions. In this conversion mechanism, several key parameters, such as 
fuel density, oxygen excess coefficient, and combustion conditions, 
significantly influence the efficiency of nitrogen-containing substances 
converting into NOx. Within the combustion system, active radicals such 
as HCN, NH3, N, CN, and NH interact in complex ways, and these re­
actions can either accelerate or hinder the formation of fuel-derived 
NOx.
The factors influencing nitrogen oxide emissions are complex and 
include pressure, temperature, fuel flow rate, equivalence ratio, and 
others.
Empirical evidence from sources [133,134] indicates that elevating 
the initial pressure within an NH3 combustion system in a fuel-rich at­
mosphere can significantly mitigate the creation of NO. Experimental 
findings [135] reveal that as the combustion chamber’s inlet pressure 
rises, there is a noticeable reduction in NO emissions. Through three- 
dimensional numerical simulation techniques, investigators [105] 
have conducted a thorough investigation into the combustion dynamics 
of ammonia/air mixtures, establishing that conditions of high pressure 
not only diminish NO formation but also lower the release of unreacted 
NH3. This occurrence is mainly due to the compression of ammonia 
molecules’ reaction times. The research further validates that increasing 
the system pressure from 1 bar to 5 bar can lead to a near 50 % decrease 
in NO produced by turbulent ammonia flames, a significant alteration 
closely linked to a reduction in OH radical concentration [136]. The 
conclusions of various researchers [137,138] bolster this finding, sug­
gesting that higher pressures suppress the formation of OH/O radicals, 
thus impeding the NOx formation pathway, which directly depends on 
OH levels. Additionally, another investigation [139] posits that envi­
ronments of high pressure can expedite certain three-body reactions, 
such as H + OH + M = H2O + M and H + O2 + M = HO2 + M. These 
reactions facilitate the oxidation mechanism of ammonia, ultimately 
leading to effective management of NOx emissions.

In ammonia gas turbines, temperature is one of the core factors 
influencing NOx emissions. NOx primarily includes NO (nitric oxide), 
NO2 (nitrogen dioxide), and N2O (nitrous oxide), and their formation is 
closely related to the temperature distribution within the combustion 
chamber. Ammonia (NH3) as a fuel has unique chemical properties, and 
its combustion process involves both fuel NOx (generated from nitrogen 
in ammonia molecules) and thermal NOx (generated from the oxidation 
of atmospheric N2), both of which are regulated by temperature 
(Fig.12). The typical operating temperature range of gas turbines (usu­
ally 800-1600 ℃, depending on inlet temperature and load) further 
amplifies the impact of temperature on NOx emissions.

Moreover, ammonia serves as a fuel but has considerable drawbacks 
regarding its combustion efficiency and stability. Research [144] shows 
that the ammonia-air mixture’s low combustion rate necessitates a 
decrease in inlet velocity to allow adequate reaction time within the gas 
turbine combustion chamber, ultimately impeding the improvement of 
turbulent mixing effects. Maintaining flame stability, which is essential 
for effective combustion, proves challenging due to ammonia’s slow 
laminar burning velocity. Relevant studies [142,145] indicate that this 
sluggish combustion rate makes the flame vulnerable to external dis­
turbances, which could lead to flame extinction or incomplete com­
bustion, thereby diminishing the operational efficiency of gas turbines 
powered by ammonia. From a flammability perspective, the range of 
ammonia’s combustibility is quite narrow; exceeding this concentration 
in the gas mixture results in an inability to maintain a stable flame 
[49,146]. Experimental findings [147,148] further illustrate that am­
monia’s low reactivity and slow oxidation rate are the main factors 
contributing to its poor combustion efficiency. This trait also results in 
higher emissions of unburned ammonia (NH3), which, in turn, decreases 
the overall energy conversion efficiency. The stability of ammonia 
combustion will directly affect the overall efficiency and operational 
safety of the gas turbine.
In comparison to conventional fossil fuels, the use of ammonia and 
hydrogen as fuels introduces specific challenges [149]. These gases are 
highly reactive and have the potential to corrode and damage compo­
nents of gas turbines, particularly under elevated temperatures. The 
occurrence of corrosion will add significant costs to the maintenance of 
the facilities and, to a certain extent, shorten the service life of the gas 
turbine. Therefore, materials utilized in gas turbines must possess the 
ability to endure these corrosive gases while preserving their structural 
integrity over time. Moreover, hot corrosion is commonly observed 
during gas turbine operations. This phenomenon is exacerbated by the 
presence of salt impurities, which can create molten deposits that 
compromise the protective oxide layers on various metal surfaces [150]. 
The extreme conditions inherent in ammonia gas turbines—charac­
terized by high temperatures, high pressures, and a chemically aggres­
sive atmosphere—demand materials that can withstand these conditions 
while ensuring structural integrity, functional efficiency, and long- 
lasting durability [151–156].
3.4. Solutions
3.4.1. Multi-fuel co-combustion
While there have been a few practical implementations of pure 
ammonia as a fuel source for gas turbines, the full potential of its utili­
zation necessitates additional investigation. Blending ammonia with 
other fuels in gas turbines not only improves combustion efficiency but 
also plays a crucial role in managing the emissions of harmful pollutants, 
such as nitrogen oxides. This is particularly important in the context of 
environmental regulations and the push for cleaner energy sources. 
Furthermore, ammonia’s energy density is relatively lower than that of 
traditional fuels like natural gas, which means that combining it with 
other fuels can considerably enhance its overall energy output. This 
blending strategy is not only beneficial for performance but also con­
tributes to solving some of the logistical challenges associated with the 
storage and transportation of pure ammonia fuel, thereby facilitating its 
broader adoption in the energy sector.
In recent years, there has been a growing interest in ammonia as a 
potential clean energy carrier, especially when used alongside a variety 
of traditional fuels such as hydrogen [121,157–161], diesel [161,162], 
methane [163–167], and pulverized coal [105] in combustion processes.

A great deal of research has also been conducted on the application 
of ammonia-hydrogen mixed combustion in gas turbines. Experimental 
data from da Rocha et al. [181] revealed that combustion systems uti­
lizing pure ammonia (NH3) exhibit significant ignition delays and sub­
optimal flame propagation efficiency. To address this issue, the research 
team demonstrated that introducing hydrogen (H2) can effectively 
optimize the combustion performance of ammonia-based fuels, signifi­
cantly improving ignition efficiency and enhancing flame stability. In 
another study, Valera-Medina et al. [157] employed a combination of 
numerical simulations and experimental methods to conduct an in- 
depth investigation of a gas turbine-specific NH3/H2 premixed com­
bustion chamber. Through experiments using a gas turbine with a power 
output of approximately 40 kW, the researchers discovered that a fuel 
mixture with a 60:40 vol ratio of NH3 to H2 exhibited unstable com­
bustion characteristics, whereas adjusting the ratio to 70:30 resulted in a 
more stable combustion process. The experiments also evaluated nitro­
gen oxide emissions under different combustion chamber configura­
tions, showing that when the air/fuel equivalence ratio exceeded 1.3, 
the concentration of NO2 in the dry components could be controlled 
below 10 ppm; when this ratio increased beyond 1.4, NO emissions 
could be reduced to extremely low levels.
In addition, many studies have confirmed that mixing hydrogen into 
ammonia fuel can increase the flame speed of ammonia combustion, 
make ammonia combustion more stable, and effectively control nitrogen 
oxide emissions [121,156,158] Kumar and Meyer undertook a 
comprehensive investigation that combined experimental methods and 
modeling techniques to explore the chemical kinetics of hydro­
gen–ammonia–air mixtures, as detailed in their publication [156]. Their 
study utilized numerical simulations derived from the GRI-Mech 3.0 
chemical mechanism, integrating it with the intricate chemical kinetic 
models created by researchers Tian and Konnov. The simulations took 
place using the advanced CHEMKIN software package, which allowed 
for the precise modeling of chemical reactions under various conditions. 
Notably, the model developed by Tian exhibited the highest level of 
accuracy, particularly in scenarios where the equivalence ratio sur­
passed a value of 1.0. This finding underscores the reliability of Tian’s 
model in predicting the behavior of ammonia-hydrogen mixtures. 
Additionally, the researchers revealed that ammonia–hydrogen mix­
tures, when composed with different ratios, can reach flame speeds that 
rival those observed in traditional methane–air combustion. A signifi­
cant conclusion drawn from their research is that the addition of 
hydrogen to ammonia not only enhances the flame speed but allows it to 
reach levels similar to those produced in methane–air combustion pro­
cesses. As a result, ammonia–hydrogen blends emerge as a practical fuel 
alternative for existing gas turbines, presenting the opportunity to uti­
lize them without requiring substantial modifications to the combustion 
chambers.
Focusing on flame speed and NOx emission characteristics, Xiao and 
Valera-Medina conducted a numerical analysis, complemented by 
experimental validation, of the reaction mechanisms governing 
NH3–H2–air combustion under gas turbine conditions [123]. Their 
measured flame speeds demonstrated strong agreement with findings 
reported by other researchers [126,182]. They confirmed that the 
addition of hydrogen significantly enhances the flame propagation ve­
locity of ammonia. This increase in flame speed facilitates the direct 
utilization of ammonia–hydrogen blends in existing gas turbine systems 
without necessitating extensive hardware redesign.
In order to further investigate the nitrogen oxides (NOx) emission 
characteristics of gas turbines that are fueled by ammonia–hydrogen 
blends, Aalrebei et al. [183] have developed a predictive algorithm 
specifically designed to evaluate the combustion and emission perfor­
mance of both industrial and experimental gas turbines. This advanced 
algorithm was executed through a custom code created in LabVIEW and 
was integrated with the GASEQ tool. By employing this computational 
tool, the authors conducted a detailed examination of a range of oper­
ational parameters associated with the combustion process. Their find­
ings revealed a significant concern with NOx emissions, which were 
found to be excessively high when ammonia–hydrogen mixtures reacted 
with air at equivalence ratios between 0.5 and 1.25. These results

Gas turbine systems based on ammonia-syngas blended fuels offer a 
potential alternative energy solution, the feasibility of which can be 
demonstrated from multiple dimensions [184–187]. From the perspec­

achieved through biomass conversion, effectively circumventing reli­

the synergistic effect of syngas and ammonia not only enhances the 
stability of the combustion process but also significantly reduces the 
generation of nitrogen oxides (NOx), carbon monoxide (CO), and un­

of compatibility with coal-fired power generation. However, current 
research on the application of ammonia-syngas blended fuels in gas 
turbines remains insufficient, primarily focusing on the exploration of 
combustion performance and pollutant emission characteristics.
A different investigation conducted by Han and colleagues [187] 
explored the combustion properties and emissions associated with 
ammonia-syngas blends within a swirl-stabilized combustion chamber. 
The researchers observed that a higher proportion of syngas in the 
mixture resulted in lowered flame temperatures as well as reduced NOx 
emissions. Conversely, they noted that incorporating syngas contributed 
to an increase in CO and unburned hydrocarbon emissions.
Cai et al. [188] examined how ammonia-syngas blended fuels 
influenced gas turbine combustor performance. The experimental find­
ings indicated that adding syngas to ammonia-based fuels not only 
enhanced combustion stability but also considerably reduced nitrogen 
oxide (NOx) emissions. Additionally, the research showed that as the 
amount of syngas in the blended fuel increased, the levels of carbon 
monoxide (CO) and unburned hydrocarbons tended to decrease. 
Importantly, while the inclusion of syngas positively affected emission 
levels, it also caused a notable decrease in the combustor’s thermal 
efficiency.
3.4.2. Ammonia cracking for hydrogen-enriched combustion
As mentioned in Section 3.4.1, many studies have confirmed that 
mixing hydrogen with ammonia can increase the flame speed of 
ammonia combustion, achieve stable and efficient combustion, and 
effectively control nitrogen oxide emissions. This method is also 
considered an effective means to improve the ammonia combustion 
process in ammonia gas turbines and enhance the overall efficiency of 
the gas turbine. However, using hydrogen as a secondary fuel compo­
nent mixed with ammonia requires the transformation of the fuel supply 
infrastructure, which involves all the issues related to using hydrogen as 
a fuel [163]. To avoid this problem, Pashchenko et. al [189] proposed 
another solution. As a good hydrogen carrier, ammonia can be thermally 
decomposed using an external heat source before combustion to produce 
hydrogen-rich gas, thereby improving the combustion process in the gas 
turbine.
In this framework, selecting the appropriate external heat source 
holds significant importance. To tackle this concern, Pashchenko et al. 
[189] introduced a method for thermochemical recovery of heat from 
exhaust gases, leading to the designation of gas turbines utilizing this 
method as chemically recuperated gas turbines (CRGT). The primary 
principle behind a chemically recuperated gas turbine involves utilizing 
the high-temperature exhaust gas generated post gas turbine operation 
for thermochemical processes, specifically the endothermic decompo­
sition of ammonia, to generate a hydrogen-rich gas. Furthermore, this 
hydrogen-rich fuel is utilized to create heat within the combustion 
chamber. Due to the highly endothermic nature of the ammonia 
decomposition reaction, an external heat source is essential. In a CRGT, 
this external heat is sourced from the heat in the exhaust gases following 
the gas turbine. The schematic diagram of an ammonia-fired chemically 
recuperated gas turbine is shown in Fig. 17.
In this research, Pashchenko et al. performed a thermodynamic 
assessment of CRG utilizing the Aspen HYSYS process simulation soft­
ware. The findings of the analysis indicate that the gas’s equilibrium 
composition after the ammonia decomposition reactor is significantly 
influenced by both pressure and temperature. As illustrated in Fig. 18, 
an increase in temperature results in higher hydrogen yields and a 
reduction in residual ammonia within the hydrogen-rich gas. At a 
temperature of 200 ℃, the H2 concentration remains below 50 %; 
conversely, at 400 ℃, hydrogen constitutes nearly three-quarters of the 
total gas volume. According to relevant studies [190], modern gas tur­
bines exhibit exhaust temperatures ranging from 500 to 800 ℃. This 
temperature range is evidently appropriate for facilitating the thermal 
decomposition of ammonia. Additionally, pressure is a crucial factor in

grating an ammonia-fired gas turbine with a solar power plant. This 
integration facilitates a cooperative approach between an ammonia- 
fired gas turbine and a concentrated solar power facility, utilizing 
solar energy to thermochemically decompose ammonia for generating 
hydrogen-rich gas. The hydrogen-rich gas produced on-site serves as a 
substitute for ammonia in fueling the gas turbine, which enhances both 
thermal and combustion efficiency.

reaction within the ammonia reformer (1), which is heated using solar

hydrogen-rich gas is incorporated into the design. The hydrogen-rich gas 
generated by the reformer is stored in a tank before being utilized as fuel

3.4.3. Selective catalytic reduction (SCR)
In the realm of regulating nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions from gas 
turbines, the main technical approaches include Selective Catalytic 
Reduction (SCR), Selective Non-Catalytic Reduction (SNCR), and Non- 
Selective Catalytic Reduction (NSCR). Of these, SNCR technology de­
pends on a high-temperature reaction environment (exceeding 900 ℃) 
due to its absence of catalysts, whereas NSCR systems encounter prac­
tical limitations stemming from the expense of reducing agents [195]. 
Conversely, SCR technology facilitates the targeted conversion of NOx 
through the use of reducing agents in an environment with surplus ox­
ygen, and has fostered a range of technical systems such as ammonia- 
based (NH3-SCR), hydrogen-based (H2-SCR), and carbon monoxide- 
based (CO-SCR), showcasing significant advantages in engineering 
applicability.
As the prevalent and well-established technique for selectively 
transforming nitrogen oxides (NOx) in flue gas into benign nitrogen (N2) 
and water (H2O) without the excessive use of oxygen (O2), NH3-SCR has 
emerged as the primary option for industrial denitrification, owing to its 
characteristic high selectivity for reduction (yielding N2 and H2O) and 
its ability to maintain stable operations in environments containing 
oxygen [195]. It is important to note that variations in the NO/NO2 ratio

The mechanistic pathway for H2-SCR can be distilled into two pri­

and dissociation of NO primarily examine how NO and H2 interact on

mechanism comprise the formation of nitrite/nitrate species, along with 
ammonia/ammonium ions. Studies have shown that basic supports are 
more predisposed to adsorb nitrite/nitrate as intermediates throughout 
the reaction, while acidic supports are more likely to hold ammonia,

3.4.4. Rich-lean combustion technology
The Okafor team [138] systematically explored the combustion 
characteristics and reaction mechanisms of CH4–NH3–Air blended fuels 
(with ammonia content up to 30 %) in gas turbine combustors. Exper­
imental data revealed that under single-stage combustion mode, with an 
equivalence ratio (Φ) ranging from 0.8 to 1.1, the NOx production of 
premixed CH4–NH3–Air mixtures exceeded 5000 ppmv, approximately 
double that of NH3–Air mixtures. This variation in emissions was closely 
related to fluctuations in the concentration of OH radicals within the 
combustor, as these radicals play a decisive role in the formation of fuel- 
derived NOx. The experiment employed a rich-lean strategy for emission 
control, significantly improving pollutant concentrations: NOx was 
reduced to 49 ppmv, CO decreased to 2 ppmv, while emissions of N2O, 
HCN, and NH3 were nearly zero, achieving a combustion efficiency as 
high as 99.8 %. The study revealed that the ideal equivalence ratio (Φ) 
for the primary combustion zone lies within the range of 1.30 to 1.35, 
with slight variations depending on ammonia content, confirmed as the 
optimal condition for minimizing NOx emissions. If the combustion 
conditions deviate from this optimal Φ range (whether towards rich or 
lean), the NOx production in either the primary or secondary combus­
tion zones increases, leading to an overall rise in emissions. Compared to 
NH3–Air combustion, the NOx emissions from CH4–NH3–Air using a 
rich-lean approach were lower, primarily due to the faster flame prop­
agation speed of CH4–NH3–Air mixtures, effectively suppressing NOx 
formation in the secondary combustion zone.
3.4.5. Liquid ammonia combustion
Over the last ten years, substantial advancements have been ach­
ieved in both basic research and the industrial implementation of 
ammonia combustion as a carbon–neutral fuel for gas turbines. The 
recent exploration of using liquid ammonia in gas turbines has emerged 
as a novel area of investigation. When compared to gaseous ammonia, 
liquid ammonia presents several significant benefits as a fuel source: 
Firstly, ammonia is usually stored as a liquid under pressure. Its ability 
to be used directly without the need for vaporization allows for the 
simplification of equipment, such as evaporators, electric heaters, and 
accumulators, which ultimately lowers system costs. Secondly, 
employing liquid ammonia directly leads to decreased energy re­
quirements associated with the vaporization process. Following vapor­
ization, gaseous ammonia may re-condense due to environmental heat 
loss, which necessitates the requirement for electric heaters to maintain 
temperature in pipes and accumulators—a consideration that does not 
arise with liquid ammonia. Additionally, gaseous ammonia requires 
larger or multiple parallel evaporators and accumulators to guarantee 
adequate fuel supply capacity; in contrast, controlling the flow rate of 
liquid ammonia under proper supply pressure is sufficient. Finally, in 
medium and large gas turbines, the combustion chamber pressure is 
typically above 0.8 MPa, at which point ammonia can be liquefied. 
Therefore, atomized liquid ammonia can be injected directly into the gas 
turbine [237].

The substantial latent heat associated with the vaporization of liquid 
ammonia presents obstacles for its use in gas turbines, which could 
result in combustion instability. This considerable latent heat contrib­
utes to significant heat dissipation, making the startup of gas turbines 
more complex and potentially leading to ignition failures or the 
extinction of flames. Like gaseous ammonia, maintaining stable flames 
in gas turbines that utilize liquid ammonia proves to be difficult. To 
achieve consistent flame propagation, it is crucial to implement well- 
designed fuel nozzles and effective air distribution strategies. Specif­
ically, reducing heat loss caused by air cooling and increasing swirl in­
tensity is vital for flame stability. Additionally, emission concerns pose 
significant challenges when applying liquid ammonia in gas turbines, 
necessitating careful consideration of the production of nitrogen oxides 
(NOx) and unburned ammonia (NH3).
Liquid ammonia is usually injected under high pressures through 
nozzles as a fuel. As a result, the pressure inside the nozzle drops, which 
leads to phenomena such as cavitation and flash boiling. Cavitation 
occurs in certain areas when the pressure of the fuel dips below its 
saturation vapor pressure, thereby boosting the fuel’s flow rate within 
the nozzle [238]. Flash boiling happens due to the reduction in pressure 
of superheated fuel, causing a portion of the fuel to vaporize and create 
bubbles in the remaining liquid [239]. These bubbles then expand 
quickly and burst almost instantaneously, gaining significant mo­
mentum in the radial direction. A schematic representation of the flash 
boiling process is provided in Fig. 27. The bursting of these bubbles 
during flash spray contributes to the enhancement of the atomization 
process [240].
In recent years, numerous experimental investigations have focused 
on liquid ammonia sprays. Pel´e et al. [241] were among the first to 
explore the characteristics of spray and the atomization process of liquid 
ammonia through the use of a gasoline direct injection (GDI) engine 
injector placed within a constant-volume chamber. Utilizing the 
Schlieren method, they obtained images depicting both the liquid phase 
and the liquid/vapor spray phases. A systematic comparison was made 
regarding the spray morphology, penetration length, and ammonia’s 
sensitivity to changes in temperature and ambient gas density, in rela­
tion to gasoline and ethanol. Furthermore, the effects of flash boiling 
were studied, highlighting the unique behavior of ammonia when con­
trasted with traditional fuels. Their results suggested that, under most 
conditions, the ammonia spray presents a longer and more slender 
structure, differing from the spray patterns of gasoline and ethanol. 
Additionally, the manner in which the ammonia spray responded to 
fluctuations in temperature and air density was distinct from the re­
sponses exhibited by gasoline and ethanol, which showed more similar 
trends.
Additional experimental investigations concerning single-phase 
liquid ammonia sprays have been documented. Fang and colleagues 
[242] examined the injection of high-pressure liquid ammonia through 
high-speed imaging, employing the diffuse back-illumination (DBI) 
technique. Their research measured the length of liquid penetration, 
width of the spray, and velocity at the tip under injection pressures 
reaching up to 100 MPa, covering both boiling and non-boiling condi­
tions. Similarly, Desclaux and associates [243] conducted experimental 
research to characterize the superheated ammonia spray emitted from a 
single-hole injector. They noted a significant sensitivity of the spray’s 
geometry and morphology in relation to the degree of superheating. 
Wang et al. [244] employed simultaneous OH Planar Laser-Induced 
Fluorescence (OH-PLIF) and Mie scattering techniques to investigate 
the flame structure and droplet–flame interactions in a liquid ammonia 
jet spray under hot and oxygen-deficient conditions. Their study pro­
vides the first detailed characterization of the liquid ammonia (LNH) jet 
spray flame structure in such an environment. In contrast to hydrocar­
bon fuels, which typically exhibit a “double flame” structure, the LNH jet 
flame manifests a “single flame” configuration, attributed to rapid 
droplet evaporation. The authors identified three distinct modes of 
droplet–flame interaction, with 97.46 % of droplets showing negligible 
interaction with the flame under typical conditions. The study also 
confirmed the concurrent presence of moderate evaporation and rapid 
flash boiling of LNH during combustion. Furthermore, the amount of 
carrier air was found to significantly influence droplet distribution and 
evaporation, thereby affecting both spray structure and flame 
characteristics.

3.4.6. Plasma-assisted combustion technology
Plasma, recognized as the fourth distinct state of matter from solids, 
liquids, and gases, is composed of a blend of charged and electrically 
neutral particles [246]. Generally, plasma systems are divided into two 
categories: thermal equilibrium plasma and non-equilibrium plasma. 
The former is defined by having a high energy density where heavy 
particles and electrons maintain equal temperatures. On the other hand, 
non-equilibrium plasma typically functions at lower pressures, exhibit­
ing decreased temperatures for both ions and neutrals (as shown in 
Fig. 30). Similar to a catalyst, non-equilibrium plasma stimulates envi­
ronmental gas molecules via high-energy collisions with electrons and 
dissociates chemical bonds, all while operating under standard tem­
perature and pressure conditions [247,248].
The mechanisms that enhance plasma-assisted combustion are rep­
resented through three main pathways, as illustrated in Fig. 31: ther­
modynamic effects, kinetic effects, and transport phenomena [249]. In 
terms of thermodynamics, plasma-mediated combustion is noted for 
significant increases in temperature within both the bulk gas and elec­
tron particles, due to effective energy conversion during the plasma 
discharge combined with exothermic chemical reactions. From a kinetic 
perspective, the plasma environment increases chemical reactivity via 
two key mechanisms: (1) lowering of activation energy barriers, facili­
tated by a plethora of high-energy electrons and reactive species, and (2) 
the creation of new reaction pathways and branching mechanisms 
within the established reaction network. Concerning transport phe­
nomena, the discharge of plasma creates localized ion winds, thus 
enhancing the turbulent mixing of reactant species.
Choe et al. [250] conducted experimental investigations on plasma- 
assisted ammonia combustion. Their findings demonstrate that plasma 
application simultaneously extends the lean blow-off limit of ammonia 
flames while significantly reducing nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions. This 
dual advantage of enhancing flame stability coupled with substantial 
NOx reduction positions plasma-assisted ammonia/air combustion as a 
promising frontier in renewable clean energy technologies.

rapid airflow entering the rotor results in considerable enthalpy losses, 
while reaction turbines demonstrate distinct energy conversion behav­
iors. For specific parts, the blades of the fan are primarily constructed 
from titanium alloys and polymer-based composites, whereas aluminum 
alloys are typically utilized for external static components. Within the 
compressor, the airflow compression process can raise temperatures to 
700 ℃, and its blades and disks are generally fabricated from titanium 
alloys [253]. As for the combustion chamber, it is predominantly con­
structed using high-temperature plates made of nickel-based and cobalt- 
based alloys.
In the design process of gas turbines, coating technology plays an 
irreplaceable and crucial role. By applying functional coatings to metal 
substrates, materials can be endowed with superior properties that 
surpass their intrinsic characteristics. Experimental studies have shown 
that without the protection of coatings, even single-crystal alloys with 
high strength and low creep properties struggle to maintain long-term 
stability under the harsh operating conditions of gas turbines. At the 
same time, purely oxidation-resistant alloys cannot meet the stringent 
mechanical performance requirements of structural components [252].

3.5. Technology Readiness Level (TRL) assessment and barriers
Technology Readiness Level (TRL) is a critical metric used to eval­
uate the maturity of a technology from its conceptual stage to full 
commercial deployment, typically categorized into levels 1 to 9 (with 
Level 1 representing basic research and Level 9 indicating proven 
operational application). As mentioned earlier, fundamental theories 
and principles related to ammonia combustion characteristics and ni­
trogen oxide formation mechanisms have been systematically investi­
gated (TRL 1–3). Numerous studies have also conducted extensive 
experimental testing and validation of various strategies for improving 
ammonia combustion stability and controlling nitrogen oxides (TRL 
4–6). Currently, several companies, including IHI and Mitsubishi, have 
developed and tested prototype ammonia gas turbines, though still on a 
limited scale (TRL 7–8). However, there remains a considerable distance

4. Future development trends of ammonia gas turbines
4.1. Synergistic utilization of ammonia with renewable energy
Renewable energy sources, including solar power, present effective 
strategies to reduce dependence on fossil fuels and the associated 
environmental pollution. However, solar energy systems frequently face 
challenges related to low efficiency, which necessitates backup energy 
to meet demand. The integration of solar energy with alternative sources 
has the potential to significantly lessen reliance on fossil fuels. For 
instance, a study conducted by Hosseini et al. [255] investigated a 
hybrid system that combines solar energy and fuel cells for residential 
heating and electricity, resulting in a discharge efficiency of 49 % and an 
overall energy efficiency of 55.7 %.
Conventional gas turbines powered by natural gas exhibit significant 
irreversibility, particularly within their combustion chambers, which 
results in decreased efficiency and increased emissions. To improve the 
efficiency of gas turbines and reduce emissions, incorporating solid 
oxide fuel cells (SOFC) into a combined cycle with gas turbines presents 
a valuable approach. In their research, Milewski et al. [256] performed 
simulations on hybrid SOFC systems operating under partial loads, 
demonstrating that these systems maintain stable thermal efficiency and 
provide considerable control flexibility. Haseli et al. [257] showed that 
their combined cycle setup enhanced both energy and exergy effi­
ciencies by 27.8 % and 26.6 %, respectively, when compared to tradi­
tional systems, while also achieving a reduction in emissions. 
Additionally, Chan et al. [258] created a model of a hybrid SOFC and gas 
turbine system with internal reforming, reaching over 60 % efficiency in 
single-generation and exceeding 80 % with the implementation of waste 
heat recovery. Finally, Calise et al. [259] examined a hybrid SOFC-steam 
turbine system with a capacity of 1.5 MW, which realized nearly 60 % 
electrical efficiency without the recovery of waste heat.
Developing multigeneration energy systems that generate various 
outputs aims to tackle the ineffectiveness of conventional renewable 
energy facilities. Al-Sulaiman et al. [260] investigated an integrated 
system that employs the organic Rankine cycle in conjunction with solid 
oxide fuel cells (SOFC), which can produce electricity, heating, and 
cooling, and discovered that it is considerably more efficient compared 
to single-output systems. Yan et al. [261] analyzed a configuration that 
integrates SOFC, a gas turbine, and an organic Rankine cycle powered by 
liquefied methane, achieving a remarkable efficiency of 67 % by effec­
tively utilizing waste heat. Malico et al. [262] created a tri-generation 
system that harnesses SOFC for the production of electricity, cooling, 
and heating, attaining a thermal efficiency of 68 %. Tse et al. [263] 
investigated a SOFC-gas turbine tri-generation setup, achieving a peak 
efficiency of 43.2 %, further enhanced through the recovery of waste 
heat.
These studies highlight the potential of integrating renewable energy 
with gas turbine systems for enhanced efficiency and reduced emissions.
Siddiqui et al. [264] propose a groundbreaking multigeneration 
energy system that is solar-driven, as illustrated by its fundamental ar­
chitecture in the state point distribution diagram (Fig. 33). This 
comprehensive system integrates a central solar tower receiver, along­
side multiple interconnected components including steam Rankine

also proposed an integrated concept of an ammonia-fueled gas turbine 
with a solar power plant. This system involves the synergistic integration 
of an ammonia-fueled gas turbine and a concentrated solar power plant, 
in which solar energy is utilized for the thermochemical dissociation of 
ammonia to produce hydrogen-enriched gas. The on-site generated 
hydrogen-enriched gas replaces ammonia as the fuel for the gas turbine, 
thereby enhancing its thermal efficiency and combustion performance. 
This study further confirms the promising prospects of integrating 
renewable energy systems with ammonia-fueled gas turbines.
4.2. Application of artificial intelligence in ammonia gas turbine systems
The rapid advancement of artificial-intelligence (AI) technologies 
has positioned their integration into gas-turbine engineering at the 
forefront of current research. The confluence of AI and ammonia-fueled 
gas turbines is now emerging as a pivotal route toward a zero-carbon 
energy paradigm. Notwithstanding ammonia’s merit of carbon-free 
combustion, its intrinsically low laminar flame speed, elevated NOx 
formation tendency, and corrosiveness impose stringent constraints on 
conventional design and operation strategies. AI offers a transformative 
capability to mitigate these challenges through (i) dynamic, model- 
predictive optimization of the combustion trajectory, (ii) real-time 
condition monitoring and anomaly detection, and (iii) data-driven 
refinement of component geometries and material selection, thereby 
enabling

As a landmark achievement in artificial intelligence, physics- 
informed neural networks (PINNs) are increasingly deployed in indus­
trial aerodynamics, with airfoil design representing one of the most 
active application areas.
Recently, Cao et.al [266] introduced NNfoil, a PINN–mesh-trans­
formation hybrid that learns the flow field in the leading-edge region of 
airfoils. By mapping complex aerodynamic geometries onto a uniform 
computational domain, the method solves the two-dimensional Euler 
equations with markedly higher efficiency. The coordinate trans­
formation substantially improves numerical accuracy and stability, 
especially in capturing the steep gradients characteristic of inviscid 
subsonic flows around curved surfaces, and NNfoil faithfully predicts the 
aerodynamic behavior of thin airfoils.
In parallel, Can Unlusoy et al. [267] accelerated and refined the 
compressor-blade design workflow by coupling PINNs with generative 
AI models. A Fourier projection layer was employed to parameterize 
inputs, and the two-dimensional incompressible viscous laminar 
Navier–Stokes equations were solved for a family of turning angles. To 
stabilize training, the authors proposed ReLoBRaLo (Relative Loss 
Balancing with Random Look-back), a novel adaptive loss-balancing 
strategy. Predicted lift and drag coefficients were in close agreement 
with high-fidelity CFD reference data. Hyperparameter studies revealed 
that adaptive activation functions, learning-rate scheduling, and signed- 
distance-function encoding exert dominant influence on convergence 
speed and accuracy. Furthermore, a systematic reduction in point-cloud 
density was shown to decrease prediction error, underscoring the po­
tential of adaptive sampling strategies to enhance PINN performance in 
turbomachinery applications.
Condition monitoring, fault diagnosis, and fault prognosis are pivotal 
in today’s hyper-competitive industrial landscape. The digitalization of 
plant assets has exponentially increased the volume of process data, 
while advances in artificial intelligence (AI) offer unprecedented op­
portunities to enhance the performance of industrial machinery. In the 
domain of heavy-duty gas turbines, traditional maintenance strategies 
are predominantly reactive: corrective actions are initiated only after a 
fault or anomaly has occurred, inevitably incurring substantial eco­
nomic penalties. Prognostic and health-management technologies are 
therefore indispensable throughout the gas-turbine life cycle and are 
driving a paradigm shift from reactive to predictive maintenance.
To enable early warning of compressor blade fouling and surge, Xie 
et al. [268] propose a hybrid thermodynamic–AI early-warning frame­
work for typical compressor faults. A modular, high-fidelity thermody­
namic performance simulator of the gas turbine is first established and 
dynamically calibrated against historical field data, yielding accurate 
real-time estimates of key indicators such as exhaust mass flow, turbine 
inlet temperature, and heat rate. Leveraging domain expertise on 
compressor failure modes, the dominant features that govern fouling 
and surge are extracted and abstracted into a prognostic model. A neural 
network is subsequently trained on healthy baseline data to learn the 
mapping between these features and the thermodynamic outputs, 
thereby generating a residual (deviation) trajectory. Continuous sur­
veillance of the residual enables early detection of incipient fouling or 
surge by identifying statistically significant deviations prior to conven­
tional alarm thresholds. The methodology is validated on measured data 
from a GE 9F-class gas turbine, demonstrating a substantial extension of 
the lead time compared with fixed-limit alarming. The resulting system 
is directly deployable at power-plant level, providing real-time decision 
support for maintenance and outage planning.
Cui et.al [269] have proposed a fault diagnosis method based on 
improved Deep Q-Network (DQN) for deep reinforcement learning. In 
this method, the one-dimensional original vibration signal is collected 
and used as the input of the DQN model, the fault sample set is applied to 
form the environment status of the DQN model, and the fault type of the 
rotor is considered to be the action set to input into the model. Then, the 
intelligent agent of the model uses the one-dimensional wide convolu­
tional neural network (WDCNN) to fit a Q network, and uses an ε-greedy 
strategy to make the decision actions, feedback rewards and next states, 
and save them into the experience pool; the temporal difference error 
(TD-error) is used in the intelligent agent to give a priority for experi­
ence playback, which makes the algorithm more stable and training 
convergent; successive exchange between the intelligent agent and the 
environment can determine the maximum reward and yield the output 
of the optimal fault diagnosis result.
Artificial intelligence (AI) is evolving from an auxiliary design tool 
into an endogenous engine of gas-turbine technology. Spanning multi- 
objective, real-time optimisation of ammonia-fuelled combustion; 
PINN-driven aerodynamic blade redesign executed in minutes; and 
thermodynamics-informed deep-learning models that forewarn of 
impending faults weeks in advance, AI is now being embedded across 
the entire life-cycle—requirements, design, manufacture, and oper­
ation—thereby establishing a self-evolving, closed-loop knowledge 
system. With the maturation of multimodal foundation models, 
quantum-accelerated training, and edge-intelligent processors, the gas 
turbine will metamorphose into a “cognitive” energy node: combustors 
that reconfigure emission–flame–trajectory maps within milliseconds to 
accommodate transient green-ammonia loads; turbine blades that 
“grow” optimal cooling channels in silico and in situ; and entire engines 
that autonomously switch among life-maximising, efficiency-max­
imising, or emission-minimising regimes dictated by real-time elec­
tricity price, carbon price, and grid-dispatch signals. In the forthcoming 
decade, AI is expected not only to raise unit-level efficiency by an 
additional 3–5 percentage points and drive NOx emissions towards trace 
concentrations, but also to aggregate distributed gas-fired assets into 
virtual zero-carbon power plants that dynamically co-optimise with 
renewable portfolios, thereby underpinning a paradigm transition of the 
global energy system from “fossil-fuel back-up” to “zero-carbon 
resilience”.
5. Conclusions
In the global energy transition, ammonia has emerged as a pivotal 
zero-carbon energy carrier and fuel, with ammonia gas turbine tech­
nology representing a highly promising solution for decarbonizing 
power generation and heavy-duty transportation. This paper systemat­
ically reviews the fundamental principles, current technological status, 
core challenges, and innovative solutions for ammonia gas turbines, and 
outlines their future development directions. Fundamentally, ammonia 
offers unique advantages as an energy carrier: its volumetric hydrogen 
density (107 kg H2/m3 in liquid form) is higher than that of compressed 
or liquid hydrogen, it is compatible with existing storage and trans­
portation infrastructure, and its combustion produces no carbon emis­
sions. However, inherent combustion characteristics of ammonia—such

as its low laminar burning velocity (0.07 m/s at φ = 1, only 20 % that of 
natural gas) and high fuel-derived nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions (over 
70 % originating from ammonia-derived intermediates)—pose intrinsic 
challenges. Recent advancements in mild-condition ammonia synthesis 
technologies, including electrochemical nitrogen reduction reaction 
(NRR), mechanochemical synthesis, light-driven photocatalysis, and 
single-atom/cluster catalysts, have laid a solid foundation for low- 
carbon ammonia production while addressing the high energy con­
sumption issues associated with the traditional Haber-Bosch process.
Technologically, the development of ammonia gas turbines has 
progressed from early laboratory-scale demonstrations (e.g., 50 kW 
microturbines achieving combustion efficiencies of 89 %–96 %) to 
prototype testing by industry leaders (e.g., Mitsubishi Power’s 40 MW 
ammonia-fueled turbine and IHI’s low-N2O combustors), reaching a 
Technology Readiness Level (TRL) of 6–7. Core challenges for this 
technology include difficulties in controlling NOx emissions, poor flame 
stability, and material corrosion in high-temperature, ammonia-rich 
environments. To address these issues, researchers have proposed a 
range of promising solutions: multi-fuel co-combustion (e.g., ammonia- 
methane blends doubling flame speed, ammonia-hydrogen blends 
achieving stable combustion at 70:30 vol ratios), ammonia cracking for 
hydrogen-enriched combustion (with chemically recuperated gas tur­
bines showing 8 %–9% efficiency gains), selective catalytic reduction 
(SCR), rich-lean combustion, direct liquid ammonia combustion, 
plasma-assisted combustion, and high-temperature composite mate­
rials/coatings (e.g., ceramic matrix composites with thermal/environ­
mental barrier coatings tolerating temperatures above 1500 ◦C). These 
innovations collectively overcome the bottlenecks in ammonia com­
bustion 
and 
emissions, 
advancing 
the 
technology 
toward 
commercialization.
Despite significant progress, several research gaps remain. First, 
mild-condition ammonia synthesis technologies (e.g., electrochemical 
NRR) face challenges in efficiency and industrial scalability, with high 
costs and low production scales hindering large-scale green ammonia 
production. Second, long-term operational data for ammonia gas tur­
bines under extreme conditions (e.g., high pressure, variable loads) are 
scarce, particularly regarding material corrosion resistance and com­
bustion stability over extended cycles. Third, NOx control technologies 
(e.g., SCR) lack systematic optimization for the unique exhaust condi­
tions of ammonia gas turbines (e.g., high humidity, residual ammonia), 
and the coupling mechanisms between combustion strategies and post- 
treatment systems require further exploration. Fourth, while direct 
liquid ammonia combustion shows promise, issues such as excessive 
vaporization heat loss and low atomization efficiency persist, and 
industrial-scale validation of spray and flame control strategies is 
limited.
Looking ahead, innovation in ammonia gas turbine technology will 
benefit from integration with renewable energy systems and the deep 
application of artificial intelligence (AI). Specifically, coupling ammonia 
gas turbines with clean energy sources like photovoltaics and wind 
power can enable efficient energy storage and peak-shaving systems, 
advancing the development of low-carbon energy networks. Meanwhile, 
the application of AI in combustion process optimization, emission 
control, and equipment condition monitoring will significantly enhance 
operational efficiency and intelligence. This multi-technology synergy 
provides robust technical support for the large-scale application of 
ammonia fuel.
Future development of ammonia gas turbines should focus on several 
key directions: first, holistic optimization of the green ammonia pro­
duction, storage, and turbine operation chain, which is critical for 
reducing costs and enhancing market competitiveness; second, syner­
gistic integration with renewable energy (e.g., solar-driven ammonia 
cracking in combined cycles), where combining ammonia gas turbines 
with clean energy sources like photovoltaics and wind power can build 
efficient energy storage and peak-shaving systems, promoting low- 
carbon energy networks; third, the application of AI in combustion 
process optimization, emission control, equipment condition moni­
toring, and gas turbine structural design, which will substantially 
improve system operational efficiency and intelligence.
