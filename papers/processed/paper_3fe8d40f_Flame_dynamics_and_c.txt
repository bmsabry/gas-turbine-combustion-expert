ABSTRACT
This article proposes a review of the state of knowledge in the field of combustion noise. The
survey comprises an initial discussion of indirect and direct noise sources and their general
characteristics, a summary of expressions devised to estimate combustion noise from turbulent
flames, a discussion of the fundamental equations describing sound emission from a reactive
region and an evaluation of scaling methods for combustion noise. An account is provided of a
set of experiments on noise radiation from perturbed laminar flames. Sources of intense radiation
of sound are identified and theoretical expressions of the pressure field are compared with detailed
measurements from well controlled experiments. These experiments indicate that flame dynamics
determine to a great extent the radiation of sound from flames. This is further demonstrated with
experiments dealing with effects of confinement. Links between combustion noise and
combustion instabilities are drawn on this basis. These two aspects are usually treated separately
but they are manifestations of similar processes. Much of the current effort in the field of
combustion noise focuses on numerical estimation techniques using modern computational tools.
The state of the art is less advanced than in computational aeroacoustics (CAA) but it is possible
to foresee that computational combustion acoustics (CCA) will progressively evolve into a well
established scientific field.
Key words: Combustion noise, Flame dynamics, Entropy noise
1. INTRODUCTION
It is now commonly admitted that combustion noise originates from two main sources.
The first, designated as “indirect noise” is produced when temperature fluctuations due
to the combustion process traverse an inhomogeneous region characterized by mean
flow gradients. Indirect noise also designated as entropy noise (Candel and Marble
(1977)) is generated by the coupling between a convective entropy mode and the mean

flow velocity or pressure gradients. The second source, “direct noise”, results from the
nonsteady processes of volumetric expansion in the reactive region (Bragg (1963);
Smith and Kilham (1963); Thomas and Williams (1966); Hurle et al. (1968)). The main
source of direct noise results from the rate of change of heat release rate associated with
the chemical conversion of the reactants (Strahle (1971, 1972, 1973); Dowling and
Ffwocs Williams (1983)). The first source of noise is of importance in high performance
propulsion systems because the combustor operates at elevated pressures and burnt
gases are exhausted through turbine bladerows or directly through a nozzle. The
coupling between hot spots generated by combustion and mean flow gradients then
gives rise to pressure perturbations. The amplitude level of the radiated pressure depends
in this case on the combustion mode and on the type and intensity of temperature
fluctuations reaching the combustor exhaust section and convected into the mean flow
velocity gradient. The second source depends more directly on the combustion process
and is controlled by the nonsteady heat release taking place in the combustion region.
This second process is also influenced by interactions with the boundaries, which
partially reflect sound waves generated by combustion. Under normal operating
conditions the noise sources are incoherent and radiate a broadband of frequencies
(“combustion roar” see Mahan and Karchmer (1991)). Under unstable operation a
feedback is established between the sound sources and pressure perturbations
propagating in the system. Noise radiation becomes more coherent as it is tuned on one
of the resonance frequencies of the system (see Candel (1992); Culick and Yang (1995);
Candel (2002); Lieuwen and Yang (2005) for reviews of combustion dynamics). This
usually involves a cyclic perturbation of the flow inducing a periodic motion of the
flame and associated fluctuations in heat release rate. Unstable combustion gives rise to
large amplitude sound pressure levels which are often detrimental to the operation of the
system. The heat fluxes to the combustor walls are intensified, structural vibration is
induced and this may lead in extreme cases to failure. Studies of combustion noise
generally focus on situations where the flow dynamics can be considered to be
independent of the radiated sound. It is implicitly assumed that the flow dynamics is
decoupled from the induced wave motion and the sound emission from unstable flames
is generally not considered when dealing with combustion noise. It is however not
always possible to obtain a complete decoupling between the flow and its acoustic
radiation because practical systems are confined and the boundaries reflect sound
towards the reactive region. One scientific challenge in the analysis of acoustically
coupled combustion is to estimate the level of oscillation when the unstable system has
reached a limit cycle. A possible solution to this fundamental problem proposed by
Noiray et al. (2008) consists in making use of the flame describing function to represent
the combustion response to incoming perturbations. It is then possible to solve the
system dispersion relation to obtain the modal growth rates as a function of amplitude
and predict the level of oscillation corresponding to the limit cycle.
In all cases the sources of combustion noise (indirect and direct) are related to the
flame dynamics and to unsteady rates of heat release. The analysis of combustion noise
is then intimately tied to an understanding of dynamics of perturbed flames. This
relationship has been explored in a set of recent studies. On the experimental side much

has been learnt from detailed experiments and more specifically from studies on
laminar flames. These investigations indicate that intense sound emission originates
from flame interactions. On the theoretical side, much of the work has relied on
classical expressions derived by Strahle. The pressure field is expressed in terms of an
integral of the time delayed rate of change of the nonsteady heat release rate. This
expression can be used to derive scaling rules or develop statistical estimates of
combustion noise. Alternatively it is possible to follow methodologies developed in
computational aeroacoustics (CAA). One idea consists in calculating the reacting flow
in an LES framework and determining the sound field from Strahle’s result. Another
approach uses an LES calculation to determine the sound sources which are then
inserted as input to a perturbed set of Euler equations. These equations are then
integrated to obtain the sound field. Another possibility consists in calculating the
reactive flow and the sound field with the same code. There are still many difficulties to
overcome to get clean and reliable estimates with these methods. One aspect which is
still not settled is the identification of the noise source terms. In some calculations of
noise from nonpremixed flames the source term is expressed as a function of the second
time derivative of the density while other calculations use alternative source
expressions. Indirect noise is also being investigated with modern computational tools
for wave propagation in nonuniform flow allowing direct estimations of the sound
radiation associated with this process. This article contains a review of progress in these
various areas and a discussion of current challenges. It focuses on direct combustion
noise and begins with an account of the classical theoretical developments in this area
(section 2). Equations of combustion acoustics are discussed in section 3 to sort out the
noise source terms. Standard expressions of the sound field radiated by open flames
are used to develop scaling rules which are briefly discussed in section 4. Direct noise
sources are examined by making use of systematic experiments on perturbed flames
(section 5). Five types of flame geometries are investigated and the radiated sound field
is linked to changes in flame surface area and to rates of change of heat release in these
flames. Confinement effects are briefly considered in section 6. It is indicated in
particular that confinement changes the acoustic response of the system to sources of
noise but also modifies the flame dynamics with a direct impact on the associated
nonsteady heat release source terms. The link between noise and instabilities is also
discussed when positive feedback between noise and sources leads to resonant regimes.
The final section contains a short survey of recent developments in computational
combustion acoustics.
2. CLASSICAL COMBUSTION NOISE THEORY
2.1. Early investigations
A classical investigation of noise generated by combustion was carried out by Thomas
and Williams (1966). The principle of this clever experiment was to fill soap bubbles
with a reactive mixture and record the pressure field radiated by the burning of each
isolated bubble. It was found that the pressure signal could be described as the sound
generated by a monopole source of strength d∆V/dt, where ∆V represented the volume
increase due to thermal expansion of the gases crossing the reactive front. In this early

This brief review indicates that sound radiation from flames results from the
unsteadiness of the combustion process. Intense levels of noise are produced by
interactions involving rapid changes of the rate of chemical conversion. When the flame
zone is compact with respect to the acoustic wavelength the time delays between the
different sources of sound can be neglected and noise radiation is essentially isotropic.
Some directivity can be due to propagation of sound through flow field inhomogeneities
and specifically temperature gradients (Strahle (1973)), from source convection effects
or from other sources of sound neglected in equation (7).

2.5. Nonpremixed flames
The sound field from non premixed and partially premixed flames is less well
characterized than that radiated by premixed flames. It is however the subject of some
recent experiments (Singh et al. (2005)) and of many numerical calculations (see for
example Klein and Kok (1999)). Theoretical analysis is more difficult because the rate
of heat release is less easily modeled in this case. This quantity also cannot be extracted
from direct measurements of free radical emission.
2.6. Nearfield effects
The observation point is in the nearfield when the distance r between the source region
and the detector is no longer large compared to the characteristic size L of the flame.
Sound radiation in this region is less well documented but of importance in practical
applications because the flame dimension can be quite large. Smith and Kilham (1963)
find that for a distance r < 30d, where d is the burner diameter, the acoustic intensity
ceases to vary like the inverse of the distance square 1/r2. Sound in this region is
influenced by the closest noise sources. These nearfield effects are of primary
importance for noise production in a confined environment. It will be shown later in this
article that results obtained in the farfield region can be extrapolated to some extent to
the nearfield. This can be used to analyse combustion instabilities coupled by noise
generation in the flame and emission. An example is given by Noiray et al. (2006) where
synchronized nearfield effects lead to a coherent amplification of the sound output from a
distribution of identical flames. Many current studies of combustion noise yield nearfield
results deduced from LES calculations in combination with acoustic perturbation
equations. There is also an attempt by Lee and Lieuwen (2002) to determine the nearfield
characteristics of perturbed conical flames based on a kinematic description.

Among all mechanisms limiting the flame surface area, three types are studied in

first concerns extinction by thermal losses of a flame impinging on a cold boundary.

atmosphere. As turbulent flames may be viewed as a collection of distributed
monopoles, the dynamics and the sound produced by a collection of small conical
flames anchored on a perforated plate are also compared to the dynamics and the sound
output of a single conical flame.

5.2. Spectral and time traces analysis
Without forcing, flames are stable for the operating conditions explored. Except for the
V-flame (Fig. 4, VF), there is no visible motion of the front. In the VF case, a small
disorganized motion of the flame limited to its free periphery is observed. This motion
can be attributed to interactions with small scale vortices convected in the mixing layer
between the premixed jet and the surrounding air. The mean background noise level in
the laboratory with all apparatus on is lower than 60 dB. Without combustion, the sound
level does not exceed 65 dB when the flow is submitted to modulations. This is also the

Unsteady flame-plate interaction is first investigated. The motion is periodic with a
frequency of oscillation equal to the forcing frequency f = 200 Hz. The velocity
perturbation at the burner outlet produces a deformation of the flame front (figure 5a)

the next image (figure 5d), a large portion of the flame front has disappeared and the
flame surface area is reduced compared to that available in the previous one. This
suggests that a sudden drop of flame surface area has occurred and that a large negative
rate of change of heat release has taken place. Flame extinction is due to thermal losses at
the cold boundary which is water cooled and has a temperature maintained at T ∼300 K.
The flame in the snapshot figure 5d has nearly retrieved the initial shape shown in the
top image of figure 5a. Because images were taken at regularly spaced instants, this
suggests that the rate of change of heat release takes higher value during phases of flame
destruction in the cycle than during the smooth increase of the flame surface area
(Schuller et al. (2002)).

Thermal losses during unsteady flame-wall interactions may constitute an intense
source of combustion noise, for example during extinctions of large coherent structures
entrained by the flow and impacting on solid boundaries (Smith and Zukoski (1985);
Kendrick et al. (1996)), or if the turbulent flame is simply stabilized close to a wall.
However in most combustors the flame is stabilized away from the boundaries and may
not reach the chamber walls. Other sources of combustion noise should be explored as
for example mutual flame interactions.
These interactions are investigated for a flame stabilized over the burner lips and the
central rod (MF in figure 4). The “M” shape adopted by the flame is well suited to
studies of flame-flame interactions which may take place between neighboring
branches (Schuller et al. (2003)). The case presented here corresponds to an equivalence
ratio Φ = 1.13, a mixture flow velocity υ– = 1.13m s−1, a modulation level fixed to υrum =
0.50 m s−1 and a modulation frequency f = 150 Hz. The description of the flame motion
over a cycle of excitation starts as in the flame-plate interaction. A velocity perturbation
originates at the burner lips and produces a deformation of the flame front at the base
of the burner (figure 7a). The perturbation mostly affects the outer branch of the “M”
flame. It is then convected by the mean flow towards the top of the flame. As the
deformation travels along the flame front, the two branches of the “M” are stretched in
the vertical direction and get closer (figure 7b-c), up to an instant in the cycle where the
flame surface area is maximum and two flame elements interact (figure 7d). The
outcome of this mutual annihilation depends on the spatial position of the first
interaction. In some cases pockets of fresh reactants may be trapped in a flame torus
while in other cases this will not occur. For some operating conditions not shown here,
up to two flame toruses can be produced. During interaction of these flame elements the
shape of the reactive front undergoes a strong alteration. As in the flame-plate situation,
after the mutual interaction, the flame quickly retrieves its initial shape at the beginning
of the cycle (figure 7a). The short phase in the cycle during flame surface destruction
produces a faster rate of change of the flame surface area than the longer phase of flame
surface production by stretch. The same mechanism operates as in the flame-plate
interaction, except that flame surface destruction is produced by mutual annihilation of
neighboring front elements and not by thermal losses.

quite similar to that associated with the flame-plate interaction in figure 6a. The presence
of harmonics of the fundamental frequency f = 150 Hz indicates that the pressure signal
is also periodic with an oscillation frequency corresponding to the flame oscillation
frequency which is also the forcing frequency. However the wave shape is nonlinear

5.3. Discussion
The qualitative analysis developed in the previous section is extended to infer
correlations between the sound radiation and the combustion dynamics. This is be
achieved by comparing time evolutions of the pressure fluctuation p′ and the light
emission I signals recorded during the three experiments. Results are respectively
displayed in figures 10 to 11 for the flame-plate (FP configuration), flame-flame (MF
configuration) and flame-vortex (VF configuration) interactions. The relative intensity
I/I– of the CH* natural flame emission signal recorded by the PM is plotted at the top of
these figures. The corresponding pressure fluctuations p′ recorded by the microphone
Micro. are also plotted at the bottom of these figures with the delayed time rate of
change dCH*/dt. This latter signal is obtained by post-processing the CH* signal. The
first step is a differentiation and a low pass-filtering. It takes into account the time delay
τ ∼r/c0 for the sound wave to cover the distance r between the compact flame and the
microphone M, with c0 taken equal to 343 m s−1. The resulting signal is then scaled to
get a signal dCH*/dt(t) with the same amplitude as the pressure fluctuation p′(t + τ) by
setting ρ∞/(4πr)(ρu/ρb−1)k = 1 in eq. (4). Owing to the combustion noise theory, these
quantities dCH*/dt(t) and p′(t + τ) plotted in figures 10 to 11 should be equal for a
homogeneous mixture maintained at a constant equivalence ratio. Except some differences
in the high frequency content, the signals obtained for high levels of velocity modulation
are well correlated for the three cases explored (figures 10a, 10b and 11a). It is in
particular shown that the phase corresponding to the largest negative pressure fluctuation
always corresponds to the maximum negative rate of change of the CH* signal.
Concerning the MF, phases corresponding the conditioned images of figure 7 are also
plotted in figure 10b as circles. These data were obtained by directly measuring the
relative flame surface area fluctuations A(t)/A– using the snapshots of figure 7 and by
assuming an axisymmetric configuration. During a cycle of oscillation, the flame light
emission level increases slowly when the flame surface increases up to its maximum
value. It then drops suddenly to its minimum value on a shorter time scale than during
the flame surface augmentation period. The maximum rate of decrease of the CH*
signal fixes the pressure fluctuation amplitude level in the three cases explored, showing
that flame surface destruction is the main mechanism leading to combustion noise
production. When coupled with a resonant acoustic mode of the burner, theses pressure
fluctuations are strong and robust enough to generate self sustained combustion
oscillations (Durox et al. (2002); Schuller et al. (2003); Durox et al. (2005)). The same
relation eq. (4) between far field pressure fluctuations and heat release variations holds
in these unforced configurations.
The last case involving a flame-vortex interaction with an “V”-flame (VF) slightly
differs from the two previous cases (FP and MF). Results not presented here show that
during self-sustained situations the level of velocity fluctuation at the burner outlet takes
lower values than the value used in the forced flow configuration. The time rate of
change of the light emission signal dCH* /dt and the corresponding pressure fluctuation
p′ are not well correlated anymore for self-sustained oscillations (Durox et al. (2005))
or in forced configurations for a low modulation level as in figure 11b. It is believed that
this phenomenon is due to the mixing layer between the jet and the surrounding air

The first flow interaction is that of a conical flame submitted to convective waves

the wrinkle travels towards the top of the flame, elements of neighboring fronts get
closer to each other and pinch off (Fig. 4-MF, instants c and d). This mutual annihilation

of this cyclic interaction is a flame surface variation Arms/A– = 0.19 of the same order

6. CONFINEMENT EFFECTS, NOISE AND INSTABILITIES
Most of the work on combustion noise has concerned radiation from unconfined flames.
This case is less dependant on the geometry because sound generation is not modified
by reflection from boundaries. It is also easier to examine unconfined flames with
optical techniques. However, in many applications combustion takes place in confined
environments and radiation takes place from the combustor inlet or exhaust sections.
Another important issue in these confined configurations is the interactions of sound
pressure waves with their sources. This generally involves two types of interactions:
•
The first correspond to weak coupled interactions manifested by enhancement of
sound radiation from confined systems by the presence of discrete peaks in the
radiated sound spectrum. A typical core noise spectrum measured in the far field of
an engine has the general form of an open flame spectrum, but with a superimposed
series of relatively sharp peaks corresponding to the resonant frequencies of the
engine (Mahan and Karchmer (1991)). Combustion under these conditions remains
globally stable and interactions between waves and combustion noise sources are
relatively weak. Pressures waves generated by the flame and reflected back towards
the combustion region from the boundaries may be scattered by the flame (Lieuwen
and Cho (2005)) and one expects that the turbulent combustion region to be only
weakly modified by acoustic perturbations in these regimes.
•
The second type pertains to strongly coupled interactions when acoustic energy
densities fed into some of the burner eigenmodes are high enough to destabilize the
reactive flow under some phase match conditions. The flow inside the chamber
then generally evolves into a self-sustained oscillations accompanied by strong
noise production inside the chamber (Candel (2002)) also leading to modifications
of the radiated spectrum in the farfield region. In these strongly coupled regimes,
acoustic fluctuations and large coherent structures of the reactive flow are syn-
chronized around a resonant frequency of the burner. The difference between these
regimes and the weakly coupled ones is that now pressures waves generated by the

7. COMPUTATIONAL COMBUSTION ACOUSTICS
Much of the current effort in the field of combustion noise is concerned with the
development of computational tools for the estimation of the noise radiated by flames.
Computational combustion acoustics (CCA) is in a naissant state and it borrows many of
its tools and concepts from two more mature domains: (1) Computational aeroacoustics
(CAA, see Mankbadi (1999); Tam (2006)) and (2) Computational Flame Dynamics
(CFD, see Candel et al. (2007)). The prediction of noise radiated by combustion raises two
major issues:
(1) It is first important to determine the noise sources in the flame region. As clearly
apparent from the previous sections this requires a precise modeling of the flame
dynamical properties. The determination of the noise sources has to be carried out
in a region of finite size which encloses the flame.

7.1. Combustion noise source modeling
Methods reviewed in this section are devoted to the determination of noise sources
inside and in the near vicinity of the combustion process. The simulation domain which
is envisaged has typically the size of the combustion chamber or of a few injector
diameters in the case of jet flames.
As in CAA, one can distinguish three general classes of methods for estimating noise
emission from turbulent flames. These classes have been mainly explored in aerodynamic
noise investigations pertaining to practical situations in ground and air transportation
and in industrial flow noise problems (Ozyörük and Long (1996); Tam (2006)).
•
Stochastic methods rely on a synthetic field of fluctuations to represent the instan-
taneous distribution of sources in the flowfield. The method has been explored in
CAA (Bechara et al. (1994); Bogey et al. (2002)). The method is attractive because
it does not require costly simulations and because it is applicable to complex
geometries. It is however not so easy to construct a suitable field of fluctuations
having the properties of real eddies in a combustion region. This is probably why
the stochastic method is apparently not considered in the current CCA effort.
•
Statistical methods are based on elaborate source models. The source characteristic
parameters like time and spatial scales, correlation function and source intensity are
usually deduced from a steady or unsteady state calculation of the flame based
on average Navier-Stokes equations. This scheme has been extensively explored
in CAA most notably in the case of free jets at subsonic or supersonic conditions
(Bailly et al. (1994, 1996, 1997), Bechara et al. (1995), Tam and Auriault (1999)).
Due to the reasonable computational cost associated with RANS and k −∈simu-
lations, this class of methods is now widely used in design applications mostly for
non-reactive situations. Statistical source modeling is not as advanced in the case
of combustion noise.
•
Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS) and Large Eddy Simulation (LES) constitute
the most novel methods of combustion source determination. In both cases, the
simulation provides instantaneous distributions of fluctuations in the flowfield, in a
region which includes the flame. In reactive DNS, investigations were initially carried
out in two dimensions and for flames with simple chemistry. Three-dimensional

has been included in some of these calculations. DNS has advanced the state of the
art in turbulent combustion modeling by providing a detailed view of the dynami-

inar cases, the improvement of computer capacities has led to new possibilities. For
simple geometries, it is now possible to simulate the flame dynamics in different
coupling situations. DNS thus constitutes a useful tool in the identification of noise
sources in simple laminar configurations.

7.4. Simulation of acoustic propagation
It has been shown that sources of combustion noise can be determined by making use
of current combustion LES tools. It remains to define methods for the propagation of
sound to the farfield. In contrast with combustion instability problems, where acoustic
propagation takes place in a finite size internal flow such as a gas turbine the sound
propagation has to be simulated over a large scale domain which covers at least a few
wavelength. A direct method consisting in calculating the reactive flow and the radiated
sound field at ounce would require higher order discretization schemes and clean
nonreflecting boundary conditions.

8. CONCLUSIONS
While combustion noise has received less attention than jet noise or rotating machinery
noise, progress has been accomplished in many respects. Sources of indirect combustion
noise identified in the early 70’s to result from coupling between convected
inhomogeneities and mean pressure gardients are now the subject of further evaluation
based on modern computational tools. Direct combustion noise sources also identified
during the same period have been studied in a series of detailed investigations relying on
perturbed flame configurations. The basic expressions providing the sound field in terms
of heat release fluctuations or flame surface area are now well validated in the premixed
flame case. These experiment have also provide a link between flame dynamics and noise
radiation. While the early correlations of the sound output of turbulent flames were
relatively imprecise some more recent efforts have provided more adequate results at least
for premixed turbulent jet flames. Much of the recent effort has concerned the development
of computational tools for combustion noise estimation. Initial results of this effort are
promising but this particular field of research is clearly less advanced than that of
computational aeroacoustics. One of the future challenges will be to devise reliable
computational tools which would be capable of reproducing well controlled experiments
on perturbed laminar flames. Another objective will be to develop the suitable code coupling
methodology which will provide noise radiation estimates and suitable spectral densities.
