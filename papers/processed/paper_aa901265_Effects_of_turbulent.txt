Abstract
Thermoacoustic oscillations of a turbulent ﬂame stabilized behind a bluff body in a duct are considered using
(1) a kinematic model of the response of the ﬂame to oncoming ﬂow velocity oscillations and (2) the n–τ model
of the inﬂuence of a localized heat source on longitudinal acoustic waves in a duct. The kinematic model well
known in the literature is extended to allow for ﬂame speed increase with the distance from the bluff body. Such
an increase is an important peculiarity of practical premixed turbulent ﬂames developing with the distance from a
ﬂameholder. For weak, harmonic, uniform ﬂuctuations of the oncoming ﬂow velocity, perturbations in the ﬂame
shape and in the total heat release rate are analytically determined. Calculated results show that the development
of turbulent ﬂame speed substantially affects both the gain and the phase of the ﬂame transfer function and, in
consequence, the stability of the ﬂame studied.

1. Introduction
Theoretical studies of combustion (thermoacous-
tic) instabilities are often performed by dividing this
complex problem into two parts [1–3]. First, the re-
sponse of a ﬂame to speciﬁed external perturbations
(e.g., ﬂow velocity oscillations) is investigated by re-
solving the structure of the combustion zone using a
more or less detailed combustion submodel. The goal
of such a study is to model the dynamics of the to-
tal heat release rate in the ﬂame under the inﬂuence
of ﬂow perturbations. Second, the effect of a time-

The former subproblem is often addressed by in-
voking a simple kinematic model of ﬂame oscilla-

propagation of an inﬁnitely thin interface (ﬂame sur-
face), which separates unburned gas mixture from
products. The speed, S, of the self-propagation of the
interface is considered to be a known quantity that
does not vary in space or time during the oscillations
if the equivalence ratio is constant. Accordingly, the
total heat release rate is assumed to be controlled by
the surface area of this interface.

The goal of this article is to assess the effects of
the ﬂame speed development on premixed turbulent
ﬂame oscillations. To show the importance of such ef-

Accordingly, to obtain analytical solutions, we con-
sider the simplest well-known model problem, i.e.,
ﬂame oscillations under the inﬂuence of uniform per-
turbations in the axial ﬂow velocity in the case of a
constant equivalence ratio [2,3,7,8]. Certainly, such
a model is quite simple and does not address some
important phenomena that can analytically be studied
even within the framework of the kinematic approach,
e.g., equivalence ratio oscillations [6,11] or axial ve-
locity perturbations convected by the mean ﬂow [9,
13] (the latter problem is relevant to thermoacoustic
instabilities driven by large vortical structures shed
from a ﬂameholder [1,15]). The use of this model
is justiﬁed by our primary goal of demonstrating the
effects of turbulent ﬂame speed development on ther-
moacoustic instabilities in a clear and simple manner.
For the same reasons (simplicity and clarity), we re-
strict ourselves to the case of V-ﬂames, although the
kinematic approach can also be applied to conical
ﬂames [2,9,13].
Our strategy consists of using a well-known sim-
ple model and extending it solely to address turbulent
ﬂame speed development, followed by comparison of
results of the original and extended models. In partic-
ular, ducted ﬂame oscillations were recently analyzed
by Dowling [3] using the kinematic model summa-
rized in Section 2.1. We extend her approach, allow-
ing the turbulent ﬂame speed to increase with the dis-
tance from ﬂameholder. A model of this process is
brieﬂy described in Section 2.2 and is discussed in
detail elsewhere [14]. The extended kinematic model
allows us to analytically predict the response of to-
tal heat release Q(t) in the ﬂame to weak, uniform,

2. Model
2.1. Kinematic model
We consider a conﬁned, premixed, turbulent ﬂame
stabilized in the wake of an axisymmetric center body
in a cylindrical duct and study the oscillations in the
total heat release rate in the ﬂame under the inﬂuence
of oncoming ﬂow velocity perturbations (see Fig. 1).
Within the framework of the aforementioned
kinematic model, the well-known G equation [16]
is invoked to simulate ﬂame dynamics. Following
Dowling [3], we consider the mean ﬂow to be one-
dimensional and independent of the radial coordi-
nate r. The inﬂuence of density variations in the ﬂame
on the ﬂow is neglected [2,3,9,13]. In the present
article, the latter simpliﬁcation is invoked to follow
the strategy outlined above, i.e., to introduce a sin-
gle extension of Dowling’s model solely to simulate
turbulent ﬂame speed development. Nevertheless, it
is worth mentioning that mean ﬂow accelerates sub-
stantially in a conﬁned ﬂame, and this phenomenon
can also be studied analytically [17].
The aforementioned assumptions allow us to sim-
ulate ﬂame oscillations using the simple equation

As discussed in Section 2.1, the kinematic ap-
proach used by us (i.e., Eq. (1) with S being a turbu-
lent ﬂame speed) appears to be valid if the turbulence
frequency spectrum is separated from the frequency
of the oscillations, i.e., ωT ≪2π. Under the con-
ditions of the calculations, this inequality holds if
Ω0 ≪17. Consequently, the focus of the following
discussion is placed on the range of low and moder-
ate frequencies.
In this range, ﬂame speed development reduces the
gain and, hence, the sensitivity of the ﬂame to up-
stream ﬂow perturbations. However, the development
also increases the phase difference between these
perturbations and heat release rate oscillations (see
Fig. 4b). Under certain conditions, the latter effect
can be of more importance than the gain reduction,
as demonstrated in the end of this section.
A decrease in the turbulent time scale acceler-
ates the ﬂame development and reduces the effects
discussed above as far as the functions Φ(Ω0) and
ϕ(Ω0) are concerned. However, the decrease in T
also affects the fully developed turbulent ﬂame speed
which is increased by both u′ and lt [14]. If the time
scale is decreased by increasing u′ with lt being kept
constant, then S0 is increased and Ω0 is decreased.
Both the decrease in T and the decrease in Ω0 result
in increasing gain and decreasing phase difference as
far as the functions Φ(ω) and ϕ(ω) are concerned.
However, if the length scale is decreased with u′ be-
ing kept constant, then the two effects (a decrease in
T and an increase in Ω0) counteract one another and
the transfer function F(ω′) can be weakly sensitive
to the length scale, as shown by the boldface lines in
Fig. 5. Here, ω′ = aω/U is a renormalized frequency.
The ﬁne lines in Fig. 5 show the effect of the
length scale on the transfer function as predicted by
the original kinematic model (constant ﬂame speed).
This effect is well pronounced and controlled solely
by the reduction in Ω0 by lt. The expression S0 ∝
u′(T /τc)1/4, which well approximates numerous ex-
perimental data [14], has been invoked to calculate
the effect of lt on Ω0(S0). Here, τc is a chemical time
scale, which has been kept constant in the calcula-
tions. Boldface lines show the effect of lt on F(ω′)
as predicted by the kinematic model with the devel-
oping ﬂame speed approximated with Eq. (15) (see
curve 4 in Fig. 2). The effect of lt on F is weakly
pronounced due to the counteraction of the effects of
T = lt/u′ and Ω0 on F, as discussed above.
It is worth emphasizing that the inﬂuence of tur-
bulence on ﬂame oscillations is not restricted to the
ﬂame speed increase with the distance from ﬂame-
holder and the dependence of the ﬂame speed on tur-
bulence characteristics such as u′ and lt. In addition,
turbulence strongly increases the mean thickness of
a premixed ﬂame as compared with the laminar case
and this phenomenon can markedly affect thermoa-
coustic oscillations, as discussed elsewhere [19].
Moreover, ducted turbulent ﬂames can substan-
tially be affected by vortex shedding from ﬂame-
holder [15]. Recently, the kinematic approach has
been extended to model this phenomenon by analyz-
ing the response of a premixed ﬂame to axial convec-
tive waves [9,13]. For this purpose, Eqs. (7) and (8)
have been solved analytically in the case of a constant
ﬂame speed S but variable velocity v = v0 exp(−ikξ)
either with the wavenumber k = ω/cu [9] or with
an arbitrary k [13]. The results reported by Schuller
et al. [9] and by Preetham and Lieuwen [13] show
that the ﬂame transfer function for such perturbations
differs substantially from the ﬂame transfer function
reported early for uniform perturbations [3]. In par-
ticular, the gain of the ﬂame transfer function for the
former perturbations can be larger than unity in a cer-
tain range of frequencies; i.e., a ﬂame stabilized be-

from negative to positive when ﬂame speed decreases.
The value of Im(ω) = 0 corresponds to the transition
from stable to unstable burning. Accordingly, the val-

Fig. 6b indicates that the critical condition (see cir-

ﬂame speed development can promote the instability
if 0.5 < s < 0.7 despite the reduction in the gain Φ
due to the development (see Fig. 4a).
4. Conclusions
The kinematic model of ducted ﬂame oscillations
has been extended to allow for ﬂame speed increase
with the distance from a ﬂameholder due to the devel-
opment of a premixed turbulent ﬂame.
For weak, harmonic, uniform ﬂuctuations of the
incoming ﬂow velocity, perturbations in the ﬂame
shape and in the total heat release rate have been de-
termined analytically.
