On Model Design of a Surrogate
Fuel Formulation
Calculations of evaporation characteristics (distillation curve, two-phase diagram, and
critical points) of surrogates are described in detail. The efﬁciency of some surrogate
blends, represented in literature, in reﬂecting the evaporation characteristics was ana-
lyzed. Based on the analysis, the chemical capabilities of surrogate models are not linked
to their abilities to reﬂect the phase-equilibrium properties of real fuel. It is shown that
model design of practical fuels must include the phase-equilibrium and distillation curve
calculations. A surrogate mixture was selected, which closely matches the boiling-point
curve and two-phase diagram for jet-A. Next, physical properties of reference fuel were
taken into consideration: combustion enthalpy, formation enthalpy, molar weight, ap-
proximate formula (carbon per hydrogen ratio), sooting tendency index, critical point,
two-phase diagram, and distillation curve. DOI: 10.1115/1.4000593
1
Introduction
Practical fuels, such as gasoline, kerosene, diesel, and so on, are
complex mixtures of several hundred individual species, the ma-
jority of which are distilled from crude oil. Therefore, only sim-
pliﬁed chemical kinetic models of practical fuels surrogate blends
or reference fuels can be used in the design of engine combustion
chambers. Standard rules for the construction of an input content
formula of a surrogate mixture are not yet developed, but the
general principles of such a construction are evident and require
the reference fuel to have the essential physical and chemical
properties of the real fuel. Here, under the physical properties we
understand density, fuel energy content, evaporation characteris-
tics boiling range, ﬂash point, and vapor pressure, thermal con-
ductivity, surface tension, viscosity, and so on. The chemical prop-
erties relate to the chemical composition, carbon per hydrogen
C/H ratio, ﬂow reactor concentration histories, ﬂame speeds,
ignition delays, sooting tendency, and so on. The components of
the surrogate fuel and their amounts or percentages input formula
of a surrogate fuel model are to be speciﬁed based on the list of
chemical and physical properties that need to be predicted. The
number of components in the input formula of the surrogate has to
be kept reasonably small usually three to ten. Generally, at this
stage of the model development we must do the ﬁrst step in the
model reduction and include in the surrogate mixture whenever
possible only the species that are essential for the representation
of physical and chemical properties of the modeled fuels.
Since one of the main aims of the combustion modeling is the
simulation of fuel heat release ﬂame speed and ignition delay
times and pollution formation, it is easy to determine the “chemi-
cal properties” required for the construction of the input surrogate
formula. The “physical properties,” most closely connected to the
chemical properties, are density and fuel enthalpy. These two
quantities are additive and do not inﬂuence remarkably the num-
ber of hydrocarbons in the reference fuel, as the density and en-
thalpy values of individual hydrocarbons involved in the practical
fuel combustion are quite similar 1.
Other physical characteristics, such as the evaporation and vis-
cosity, have a greater impact on the number of components in the
surrogate blend and are at the same time difﬁcult to predict. They
are very important for the precombustion processes in the cham-
bers with short residence times of the propellants and high efﬁ-
ciency requirements such as liquid propellant rocket engines or jet
engines. Due to the inﬂuence of surface tension and density gra-
dients on the fuel atomization and mixing, all other physical prop-
erties are more or less closely linked to these two. A large number
of surrogate fuel mixtures have been proposed recently, evaluated
in engines, in fundamental experiments, and studied numerically.
Most of those were developed for various jet fuels 1–12. These
models simulate the fuel oxidation chemistry and generally, suc-
cessfully reﬂect the combustion kinetic. Unfortunately, the capa-
bilities of the surrogate models to reﬂect the physical properties,
especially the evaporation characteristic, are considerably less
studied. The present work is aimed to elaborate criteria for the
determination of the input formula of the reference fuel model. It
is our ﬁrst step to establish the principles of model design of
surrogate blends.
This work is concentrated on the evaluation of evaporation
characteristics of reference fuel models. In the ﬁrst part of this
paper, we brieﬂy describe the boiling characteristics of hydrocar-
bons used in the surrogate blends. The second part is devoted to
the methods employed for the calculation of phase diagrams, criti-
cal points, and distillation curves of multicomponent mixtures.
The third part contains the thermodynamic analysis of some sur-
rogate blends presented in literature and the evaluated composi-
tion of a sample surrogate blend, which reﬂects the evaporation
properties of the aviation fuel.
2
Reference Fuel Evaporation Characteristics
The hydrocarbons of practical fuels are primarily parafﬁns,
naphthenes cycloparafﬁns, and aromatics. In Table 1 we summa-
rize the hydrocarbons used in different chemical models for gaso-
line, kerosene, and diesel 1–12. These species successfully rep-
resent hundreds of hydrocarbons in surrogate mixtures by
simulations of fuel oxidation. How do these species represent the
evaporation characteristics? What kind of criteria do we have to
evaluate these characteristics?
First, the requirements for the fuel volatility are traditionally
preset by the distillation curve temperature boiling-point TBP
curve—boiling temperature versus the volume of evaporated frac-
tion during distillation, which represents the distribution of spe-
cies by boiling temperatures. Quite often, fuel is characterized by
the maximum evaporated percent volume only for some boiling
temperatures instead of the distillation curve. The most important
distillation characteristics are the temperatures at the top end of
the range, as these provide a measure of the proportion of heavier
components, which correlate closely with aromatic contents in
particular. Heavier components in the fuel have more potential for
incomplete vaporization and combustion, which results in the in-
creased production of smoke or soot. Presetting lower end values

These parameters are not additive and are not easy to ﬁt. They
are calculated from the equation of state EOS, i.e., are linked
directly to the thermodynamic state functions. Ideally, for well-
controlled modeling, we have to approximate the thermodynamic
functions of a multicomponent real fuel to those of the simpliﬁed
surrogate blend. In what follows, we made an attempt to identify
tools to calculate thermodynamic equilibrium, i.e., two-phase dia-
grams and critical points, for multicomponent mixtures. Using
these tools we calculated the two-phase diagrams for some surro-
gates from literature and optimized the chemical contents of a
blend, which satisﬁes the thermodynamic properties of practical
fuels.
Calculating vapor-liquid equilibrium for mixtures, one faces
two main problems. The ﬁrst one is choosing an equation of state,
which can provide reliable results and at the same time can be,
possibly, simple and would not require additional calculations to
determine the equation parameters. The second problem is choos-
ing a mixing rule to evaluate EOS parameters for multicomponent
mixtures. For nonideal cases a big difference in molecular size,
high polarity, and so on simple mixing rules are not applicable.

While the behavior of functional groups is the key for chemical

K. It means the reference fuel model applied for the computa-

poorly correlate with each other. We can conclude that by the
reference model construction, the evaporation facilities of the ref-

tools we optimized the reference mixture with 12 components
proposed in Ref. 45, Table 7, to reproduce the phase-equilibrium

data, Fig. 10. During the optimization we reduced the number of
components in the initial formula of a surrogate 45 to ﬁve com-
ponents, which represent parafﬁns, naphthenes, and aromatics in
the mixture, Table 8. This mixture represents the evaporation
properties of kerosene: Figs. 13 and 14 show a good correlation
with the experimental data and calculated distillation curve and
phase diagram. Other essential physical properties presented in
Table 8 are in accordance with those of real kerosene. The chemi-
