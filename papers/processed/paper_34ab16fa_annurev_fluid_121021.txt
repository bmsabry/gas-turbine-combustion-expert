Abstract
Thermoacoustic instability is a flow instability that arises due to a two-way
coupling between acoustic waves and unsteady heat release rate. It can cause
damaging, large-amplitude oscillations in the combustors of gas turbines,
aeroengines, rocket engines, etc., and the transition to decarbonized fuels is
likely to introduce new thermoacoustic instability problems. With a focus on
practical thermoacoustic instability problems, especially in gas turbine com-
bustors, this review presents the common types of combustor and burner
geometry used. It discusses the relevant flow physics underpinning their
acoustic and unsteady flame behaviors, including how these differ across
combustor and burner types. Computational tools for predicting thermoa-
coustic instability can be categorized into direct computational approaches,
in which a single flow simulation resolves all of the most important length
scales and timescales, and coupled/hybrid approaches, which couple sepa-
rate computational treatments for the acoustic waves and flame, exploiting
the large disparity in length scales associated with these. Examples of suc-
cessful computational prediction of thermoacoustic instability in realistic
combustors are given, along with outlooks for future research in this area.

1. INTRODUCTION
Thermoacoustic instability is a flow instability that arises due to a two-way coupling between
acoustic waves and unsteady heat release rate. Its practical occurrence is within combustors,
where the unsteady heat release rate is associated with unsteady combustion, and hence it is often
termed combustion instability. It is invariably unwanted, as it often leads to large and damag-
ing flow and heat release rate oscillations, which can cause structural damage, excessive vibration
and noise, flame flashback, flame blow-off, and other deterioration in a combustion system’s
performance.
The combustors of gas turbines, aeroengines, rocket engines, domestic boilers, afterburners,
etc. can all exhibit thermoacoustic instability. The phenomenon first came to prominence due
to its occurrence in rocket engines, including the F-1 rocket engines for the Apollo missions
(Oefelein & Yang 1993). Over recent decades, it has been a major issue for gas turbine manufac-
turers (both land-based and aeroengines), as the use of lean premixed combustion for low NOx
emissions significantly exacerbates thermoacoustic instability problems (Mongia et al. 2003).
Whether thermoacoustic instability is a problem generally becomes apparent late in the design
and development process, making modifications for its mitigation difficult and costly (Poinsot
2017). The current need to decarbonize energy generation and aviation is driving a transition
toward carbon-free fuels, such as hydrogen and ammonia, as well as other sustainable fuels, such
as sustainable aviation fuel, with new designs of fuel injectors, burners, combustors, and cooling
systems potentially needed to accommodate these. New problems with thermoacoustic instability
are likely to arise; for example, the use of hydrogen in fuel can sometimes increase the propensity
to instability (Beita et al. 2021). However, the need for redesigns also creates new opportunities
for mitigating instability and a more urgent need for accurate computational predictions that can
help inform the design process.
Returning to the underpinning mechanism, thermoacoustic instability arises due to a two-way
coupling between acoustic (sound) waves and unsteady heat release. For example, background
sound disturbances incident on a flame will cause oscillations in the heat release rate—this may
be through local changes in the fuel–air ratio or hydrodynamic oscillations causing flame-front
movement (Candel 2002, Ducruix et al. 2003, Lieuwen 2003). Unsteady combustion is an effi-
cient acoustic source and in turn generates new acoustic waves, which propagate away from the
flame (Ducruix et al. 2003, Dowling & Mahmoudi 2015). These partially reflect from the bound-
aries of the combustor, arriving back at the flame where they further perturb the heat release rate.
Under some conditions, the resulting cycle will lead to oscillations of certain frequencies succes-
sively growing in amplitude—thermoacoustic instability. It is noted that intrinsic thermoacoustic
modes arise from a slightly different coupling mechanism that does not require acoustic reflection
from boundaries—the works by Hoeijmakers et al. (2014), Emmert et al. (2015), and Silva (2023)
provide more details on these. Thermoacoustic instability is typically linear, meaning that small
perturbations grow in time, eventually reaching levels that introduce nonlinear effects that limit
further growth. Self-excited oscillations occur, often in the form of limit cycle oscillations dom-
inated by a single oscillation frequency, although more complex nonlinear dynamics are possible
( Juniper & Sujith 2018).
The key flow phenomena are thus the acoustic waves and the flame’s unsteady heat release
rate, which depend upon acoustic propagation, acoustic reflection, turbulent reacting flow, vortex
dynamics, combustion chemistry, heat transfer, etc. These diverse flow physics span a very large
range of length scales and timescales. For example, the length scales relevant to the flame include
turbulent flame-front wrinkling and even smaller chemical reaction scales, while the acoustic
waves typically have wavelengths of the order of a meter or more. The flame-front chemical

It is for these reasons that achieving accurate thermoacoustic instability predictions for de-
signs of real combustors remains a challenging and expensive endeavor. Direct computational
approaches, in which simulations directly resolve all of the most relevant scales and flow physics,
are possible (Poinsot 2017). Alternative approaches instead apply separate computational treat-
ments to the two key phenomena—the acoustic waves and the flame—and then couple these. The
use of such hybrid/coupled methods has the benefit of allowing different computational tools
to be tailored to the relevant flow scales and flow physics, with potentially significant savings in
computational time.
This article builds on previous relevant reviews (Candel 2002, Dowling & Morgans 2005,
Candel et al. 2014, Poinsot 2017, Juniper & Sujith 2018) by focusing on the flow physics, tools,
and advances associated with moving predictive capability of thermoacoustic instability toward
increasingly realistic combustors, as well as advances that may allow mitigation of thermoacoustic
instability to be incorporated into new designs.
The behavior of acoustic waves within a combustor is dictated by its geometry, boundary
conditions, and temperature profile. Section 2 explores the different categories of geometry
used in practical combustors, along with their associated acoustic characteristics. This review
focuses on geometries relevant to gas turbine applications, but the key flow physics and in-
teraction mechanisms also apply to other applications. Section 3 then discusses the effect of
the burner, in terms of both the response of the flame’s unsteady heat release rate and the
burner’s acoustic characteristics. Combustor acoustic modes depend on combustor boundary con-
ditions, while the thermoacoustic stability is determined by the balance between acoustic energy
gain and acoustic energy damping. Section 4 discusses combustor boundaries and flow field in
terms of acoustic boundary conditions, temperature profile, and acoustic damping. For acoustic
damping, sources both inherent to the combustion system and deliberately introduced through
acoustic dampers are considered. An important aspect of work in the field over the last decade
has been the success of hybrid/coupled approaches for predicting thermoacoustic instability.
Section 5 presents examples in which thermoacoustic instability has been successfully predicted
using both direct computational tools and hybrid/coupled tools employing a range of different
approaches.
2. COMBUSTOR GEOMETRY
The combustor geometry informs the length scales and timescales over which acoustic waves
propagate and reflect back from boundaries to interact with the flame. Together with the com-
bustor boundary conditions and speed of sound field, it informs the frequency and acoustic mode
shape corresponding to any thermoacoustic instability.
Combustors sustain a mean flow, and the pressure fluctuations associated with their acoustic
waves generally remain small compared to the mean pressure, even during large-amplitude ther-
moacoustic oscillations. Furthermore, the effect of viscosity on the acoustic waves is generally
negligible. Small (linear) flow perturbations are then governed by the linearized Euler equa-
tions (Dowling & Stow 2003), which can be solved numerically, either as part of a full flow field
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) solution or as a separate linearized simulation. If some fur-
ther simplifications are made, it is possible to solve for the acoustic field more quickly but still
with good accuracy and potentially in a manner that retains more physical insight. There are two
main branches of approach for further acoustic simplifications.

flow but instead simplify the geometry and mean speed of sound field. The low frequencies also
imply that the spatial extent of the flame is short compared to the long acoustic wavelengths,
allowing the generation of acoustics arising from the flame’s unsteady heat release rate to be sim-
plified. This can be included either through the combination of a flame model and flame jump
conditions or through a combined burner transfer matrix approach, as discussed in Section 3.

fications and their implications for the acoustic fields in each of these combustor geometry types
are discussed in more detail in the following sections.

To incorporate the effect of the flame, it can usually be assumed that the longitudinal extent of
the flame is much shorter than the dominant acoustic wavelengths. This means that the change
in acoustic wave strengths across the flame can be taken to be spatially discontinuous. It can be
included either by the combined effect of a flame model and compact jump conditions for first-
order (linear) flow fluctuations across the flame or by using a combined burner transfer matrix
approach, as described in Section 3.
It is worth mentioning that another modeling approach, employing truncated Galerkin ex-
pansions (Culick & Yang 1995), can be used to model the acoustics. Such approaches account
for neither the mean flow nor the velocity jump across the flame, and errors may arise if trunca-
tion is applied after too few terms (Dowling & Stow 2003). However, they extend more naturally
to nonlinear acoustic regimes and hence are useful where this can be important, e.g., for rocket
engines (Culick & Yang 1995).
Even though the presence of flame unsteadiness can significantly affect the stability of ther-
moacoustic modes, it usually modifies the acoustic mode shape and frequency only slightly. Thus,
as long as the combustor length, mean temperature distribution, and acoustic boundary condi-
tions (which are discussed in more detail in Section 4) can be prescribed, the frequencies of the
possible thermoacoustic modes follow quite straightforwardly. However, predicting the stability
of a mode, as well as its oscillation amplitude if it is unstable, requires the flame response and the
acoustic damping to be prescribed; these are usually much more challenging and will be addressed
in Sections 3 and 4, respectively.

It is possible for more than one thermoacoustic mode to be unstable, although a single mode
is generally seen to dominate the thermoacoustic oscillations resulting from instability (Stow &
Dowling 2009, Li & Morgans 2015, Acharya et al. 2018, John et al. 2023). If the transverse di-
mensions of the combustor are large enough and the acoustic damping not sufficiently strong,
transverse thermoacoustic instabilities can occur, as is often the case in rocket engines (Culick &
Yang 1995, Urbano et al. 2016), afterburners (Rogers & Marble 1956, Nakaya et al. 2021), and
sometimes gas turbine combustors (Schwing et al. 2011, Berger et al. 2017). Recent interest has
emerged in the use of sequential combustors for their operational flexibility and ultralow NOx
emissions: They employ a secondary flame downstream of the main flame to shorten the high
temperature region. For these, thermoacoustic interactions remain similar, with the heat release
rate from both the main and the secondary flame zones contributing (Schulz et al. 2019, Xiong
et al. 2021, Choi & Kim 2023).
2.2. Can-Annular Combustors
Many modern heavy-duty gas turbines have a can-annular geometry. Multiple separate can com-
bustors are installed around a circumference, with a transition duct at the combustor outlets
linking to the relevant segment of the annular turbine inlet, as shown in Figure 1b.
Due to thermal expansion effects, a gap is usually left between the transition duct outlet and the
turbine inlet guide vane. This leads to acoustic cross talk between neighboring can combustors.
Even though the flame in each can is well separated from those in other cans, this cross talk can
result in acoustically coupled thermoacoustic modes. An example is the push–pull thermoacoustic
mode, where the pressure oscillations between neighboring cans are out of phase (Farisco et al.
2017, Moon et al. 2019), as illustrated in Figure 2e, where the cross-talk gap is denoted Lg. Several
recent studies have investigated the acoustic characteristics of the cross talk, for example, model-
ing it using Rayleigh conductivity–based hole models (von Saldern et al. 2021b), considering the
acoustically quasi-one-dimensional end annulus (Haeringer et al. 2021), and taking the effect of
mean gazing flow into consideration (Orchini 2022). The cross talk gives rise to both longitudinal
and circumferential modes, with clusters of modes very close in frequency occurring (Ghirardo
et al. 2019). Accounting for the nonlinear responses of the flames, these clusters may cause com-
plex and relatively slow mode interaction transients, with different modes able to synchronize (von
Saldern et al. 2021a).
When the system exhibits rotational symmetry, i.e., all combustors are identical, Bloch-wave
theory can be used to provide the boundary condition at the cross talk of a single can (Ghirardo
et al. 2019), reducing the system to be analyzed to just a single can. If a source of asymmetry
between cans is introduced, the full system needs to be analyzed. Such asymmetry can arise from
one can having a different flame response, different equivalence ratio (Moon et al. 2021), different
nozzle swirl distribution (Guan et al. 2023), or different can-to-can coupling strength (Buschmann
et al. 2023). A significant impact on the azimuthal pressure distribution can result (Ghirardo et al.
2019).
2.3. Annular Combustors
Combustors with a thin annular shape are widely used in both aeroengines and land-based gas
turbines. Typically, more than 10–20 burners are uniformly installed around the annulus, with the
aim of achieving a uniform temperature distribution at the turbine inlet, as shown in Figure 1c.
The combustor’s mean circumference (denoted Lc in the simplified geometries of Figure 2) is
typically much larger than its thin radial gap (denoted 1r in Figure 2), while its length (denoted
L in Figure 2) can vary from being much shorter to being of the same order as the circumference.

At low frequencies, thermoacoustic modes that involve acoustic propagation purely in the cir-

acoustically closed. A mode shape with purely circumferential variation is illustrated in Figure 2b.
For such modes, if the combustor is further acoustically decoupled from the plenum, simple mod-
els based on the Galerkin expansion can be used (Noiray et al. 2011, Ghirardo et al. 2016). When
the combustor and plenum acoustics are coupled, a quasi-one-dimensional linear model that ac-
counts for purely circumferential acoustic propagation (Bauerheim et al. 2014a, 2016) can offer
analytical expressions for mode stability and azimuthal mode degeneracy/splitting properties, ac-
counting for both uniform and differing burners around the annulus (Bauerheim et al. 2014b,
Poinsot 2017).
When the combustor’s longitudinal length is not negligible compared to the acoustic wave-
length or the acoustic boundary conditions differ from closed–closed, acoustic waves can vary
in the longitudinal as well as circumferential direction. Research on annular combustor geome-
tries has benefited from the development of lab-scale annular combustion rigs over the last
decade (Worth & Dawson 2013, Bourgouin et al. 2015b). The combination of longitudinal and
circumferential variations may lead to acoustic mode shapes that vary only in the longitudinal
direction, as shown in Figure 2a, mode shapes that vary in both the circumferential and lon-
gitudinal directions, as shown in Figure 2c, and even modes that involve a coupling between
circumferential and longitudinal components (Bourgouin et al. 2015a, Moeck et al. 2019, Orchini
et al. 2019, Yang et al. 2019a), as shown in Figure 2d. The presence of acoustic dampers or varying
burners around the annulus will lead to more complicated modal couplings. Two-dimensional
low-order network models (Stow & Dowling 2001, Evesque & Polifke 2002, Stow & Dowling
2009, Yang et al. 2019a), which apply wave solutions to the convected wave equation in a thin
annulus and allow for variations in both the longitudinal and circumferential directions (Dowling
& Stow 2003), can be used to capture these effects. Such models have been used to study the
effect of dampers (Stow & Dowling 2003, Yang et al. 2019b) and feedback control (Morgans &
Stow 2007, Illingworth & Morgans 2010) in annular combustors and have included the effects of
differing burners around the annulus (Morgans & Stow 2007) and flame nonlinearities (Stow &
Dowling 2004, 2009; Yang et al. 2019a). The effect of symmetry breaking resulting from differing
burners can lead to mode splitting of circumferential modes (Noiray et al. 2011, Bauerheim et al.
2014b).
Circumferential modes differ from other modes in that rather than having acoustic boundary
conditions at either end of their spatial domain, they instead have the requirement of periodicity
every 2π radians. This permits both standing and spinning modes, with the balance of these de-
termined by a parameter known as the nature angle (Ghirardo & Bothien 2018, Ghirardo et al.
2021). When the system is rotationally symmetric, such as when the burners are all the same and
a purely spinning mode is considered, Bloch-wave theory can be used to reduce the simulation of
the full annular combustor to a single segment (Mensah et al. 2016). An azimuthal mean flow, even
if small, causes a difference in the clockwise and anticlockwise wave propagation speeds and thus
significantly affects the nature angle (Bauerheim et al. 2015). The flame response to azimuthal
velocity perturbations can be nonzero for asymmetric flames, arising, for example, from a non-
negligible azimuthal mean flow. While the azimuthal flame response may be much smaller than
its longitudinal counterpart, it may also alter the nature angle (Li et al. 2019). Recent experimen-
tal results (Nygård et al. 2021) showed that an annular geometry with swirling flames generally
gives different flame responses to clockwise and anticlockwise acoustic forcing. This leads to the
heat release rate perturbation having a different nature angle to that of the pressure perturbation
and a nonzero heat release rate response at the pressure node of a perfectly standing pressure
mode.

3. BURNER GEOMETRY
Thermoacoustic instabilities depend not only on the propagation and reflection of acoustic waves
in the combustor, but also on how the flame generates and responds to acoustic waves. It is at the
burner (also referred to as an injector) that reactants are brought together, setting up conditions
for combustion within a desired spatial zone in the combustor. Burners are typically located at the
upstream end of the combustor (with sequential/staged combustors involving further sequences
of burners along the combustor). Their effect on thermoacoustic behavior occurs through both
the unsteady characteristics of the flame(s) associated with them and their acoustic characteristics.
These can be treated either in a combined manner or separately, as follows.
3.1. Combined Acoustic/Unsteady Flame Treatment of Burners
The combined effect of burner geometry and the effect of its subsequent unsteady flame on
the acoustics can be characterized through a burner transfer matrix, which relates acoustic wave
strengths upstream and downstream of the burner. Following the pressure wave notation of

3.2. Unsteady Flame Response of Burners

characterized by what is widely known as the flame model (Candel 2002, Lieuwen 2003). As shown
in Figure 3b, this typically links the velocity perturbation ahead of the flame to the global heat
release rate perturbation of the flame, although it can be extended to also include the effect of
equivalence ratio fluctuations on the heat release rate (Lieuwen 2003).

els such as the FDF is it possible to predict the final thermoacoustic oscillations resulting from
instability (Stow & Dowling 2009, Han et al. 2015, Laera et al. 2017a).
Flame models typically have the form of a low-pass filter combined with a phase lag. The
latter is linked to time delays with various physical roots: acoustic inertia (flames respond to
hydrodynamic/vortical disturbances resulting from acoustic excitation, which advect along the
flame front), mixing, transport, and sometimes evaporation delays (Candel 2002, Ducruix et al.
2003). While it is possible to define the FTF and FDF with respect to a range of different acoustic
variables (Gaudron et al. 2017), it is the upstream velocity perturbation that links most physically
to these effects. Interestingly, hydrogen enrichment of the fuel has been found to extend the band-
width of the flame response—the gain drop-off is pushed to higher frequencies (Æsøy et al. 2021,
Lim et al. 2021). This may at least partly explain why hydrogen-fueled combustors often exhibit
an increased propensity to thermoacoustic instability (Beita et al. 2021).
Swirl burners often exhibit additional dips in the gain of their flame response at certain fre-
quencies (Candel et al. 2014, Æsøy et al. 2022). There are currently relatively few data on flame
models for multiflame burners (Noiray et al. 2008); these hold promise for achieving stable low
NOx combustion of 100% hydrogen and so may receive increased attention in the future.
3.3. Acoustic Characteristics of Burners
Combustor thermoacoustic behavior can sometimes be decoupled from that of upstream com-
ponents such as the plenum (Schuller et al. 2012). Then the location of the burner outlet, i.e.,
where the reactants leave the burner and enter the combustion chamber, can be taken to form
the thermoacoustic domain’s upstream acoustic boundary, with this being characterized by the
flow contraction presented by the burner flow passageways. Most simplistically, this may be
approximated as a closed acoustic boundary, but more sophisticated treatments are possible.
If the combustor is not sufficiently decoupled from components upstream of the burner, which
in general comprise both the airstream and the fuel stream, then the upstream boundary will need
to shift upstream of these (Stow & Dowling 2001, 2009; Han et al. 2015; Laera et al. 2017a; Xia
et al. 2019). As the acoustic impedance of the airstream is typically much lower than that of the fuel
stream, often only the former is accounted for, via components such as air plenums. The acoustic
characteristics of the burner must be prescribed, with the simplest approach being to account

promise for better capturing and potentially exploiting the associated acoustic damping. Relevant
progress for the different burner types is briefly outlined below.
3.3.1. Flame-holder burners. Flame-holder burners typically stabilize the flame via a recir-

the potential to affect the acoustic characteristics. Dowling (1995) showed analytically that bluff-
body drag was likely to be thermoacoustically important above area blockage ratios of 25%. The
acoustic response of a simple bluff body—a circular disc surrounded by flow through an annular
aperture—was then considered in more detail by Schleicher & Howe (2013), who used an un-
steady drag term to account for the acoustic damping associated with vortex shedding from the
disc edge. Su et al. (2021) considered a circular bluff-body burner centrally located at the expan-
sion plane of a circular duct: Using a Green’s function and a vortex sheet model, they predicted
the acoustic reflection and transmission characteristics well compared to results from nonreacting
compressible large eddy simulations (LES).

3.3.2. Swirl burners. Swirl burners with multiple swirl passages (Candel et al. 2014) are ubiqui-

response beyond applying compact area changes. Su et al. (2019) simplified the geometry of a
swirler passage, with acoustic predictions comparing well to CFD. Their results confirmed that
the length of the passage affects the phase of the acoustic response and that acoustic energy is
mainly dissipated by the unsteady vorticity shed from the swirl vanes and the large-scale flow
expansion at the passage exit.
3.3.3. Multiflame burners. Multiflame burners (Durox et al. 2009) are plates with multiple
small holes; a flame front forms downstream of each hole. These multiple flame fronts may inter-
act. For such burners, models for the acoustic response of isolated holes with mean flow passing
through them—based on the work of Howe (1979)—can capture acoustic damping due to vor-
tex shedding. More recent work has found that short holes, where the mean flow that separates
upon entering the hole does not reattach within the hole (typically holes with a length less than
1.5 times their diameter), can generate rather than damp acoustic energy ( Jing & Sun 2000, Su
et al. 2015). Models that account for two-way acoustic–vorticity coupling can capture this (Yang &
Morgans 2016, 2017b), with some studies also linking to a purely hydrodynamic instability (Fabre
et al. 2020). For longer holes, accounting for the unsteady vortex sheets shed from the hole inlet
and outlet properly captures the acoustic response (Brokof et al. 2023). For multihole plates, in-
teraction between neighboring holes can affect the hole acoustics—particularly the reactance, i.e.,
the imaginary part of the impedance (Lee et al. 2007).
Although linking the aeroacoustic response of multiflame burner holes with thermoacoustic
stability is still in its infancy, it is a concept that may gain importance. The rapidly growing interest
in hydrogen as a fuel means that multiflame burners (which offer both flashback resistance in the
context of hydrogen’s fast flame speed and fast mixing of products for low NOx) may be used more
widely in the future (Beita et al. 2021).
4. COMBUSTOR BOUNDARIES AND FLOW FIELD
As discussed in Section 2, the thermoacoustic modes of a system depend upon the combustor
geometry and the acoustic boundary conditions. The thermoacoustic stability is then determined
by the balance between (a) the acoustic energy source arising from the unsteady heat release rate of
the flame and (b) the damping rate of the acoustic energy (Chu 1965, Dowling & Morgans 2005).
This balance is important both in determining the system’s linear stability and in determining
the nonlinear oscillation amplitude resulting from instability, the latter of which occurs when the
above acoustic energy source and acoustic energy damping terms are balanced.
The boundaries and flow field of a combustor affect both its acoustic boundary conditions and
its acoustic damping. The acoustic damping may arise from the intrinsic properties of the geome-
try and flow field (including the burner itself, as discussed in Section 3). It may also be augmented
by the addition of acoustic dampers as passive control methods for mitigating thermoacoustic
instability (Richards et al. 2006, Zhao & Li 2015, Lahiri & Bake 2017). It is worth noting that
since damping typically arises from acoustic-flow interactions, it is often naturally accounted for
in low-order network models (Gaudron et al. 2021), but it needs to be more carefully included in
Helmholtz solvers (Ni et al. 2017, 2018; Heilmann & Sattelmayer 2022), which by default neglect
mean flow effects.
4.1. Upstream Boundary
Combustion chambers in real engines typically sit between the compressor and turbine, with the
thermoacoustic domain boundary conditions set by these components. As discussed in Section 3,

the burner (Schuller et al. 2012), such that the burner outlet can be taken as the thermoacous-

flow contraction (as seen from downstream) presented by the burner flow passageways, with the
various options described in Section 3. When the thermoacoustic domain needs to extend up-

as choked (Dowling & Stow 2003). In cases where the domain is truncated between the compres-
sor and the burner outlet, it can be hard to choose how far upstream this should extend (Wolf et al.
2012).
4.2. Downstream Boundary
Lab-based combustors operating at atmospheric pressure typically have open downstream bound-
aries, but combustors operating at raised pressure have more complex downstreams, for example,
a nozzle that may or may not be choked. For gas turbines, the downstream end of the combus-
tor interfaces with the turbine, the first stator row of which is known as the nozzle guide vane
(NGV). The flow through this rapidly accelerates to an approximately choked condition, turning
as it does so. Acoustically, the reflection of acoustic waves is no longer the only source of boundary
acoustics, as the indirect noise (Marble & Candel 1977) arising from the acceleration of entropy,
compositional, and possibly vortical fluctuations may also need accounting for (Goh & Morgans
2013, Duran et al. 2014, Dowling & Mahmoudi 2015, Morgans & Duran 2016, Ihme 2017).
The downstream acoustic boundary condition can be approximated by analytical methods
that assume choked nozzle flows and account for both direct and indirect acoustic reflection.
Such models have progressed from those assuming one-dimensional, very low-frequency per-
turbations (Marble & Candel 1977) to those that extend to any frequency (Duran & Moreau
2013, Magri et al. 2016, Yeddula et al. 2022), including for annular configurations (Duran &
Morgans 2015). Analytical attempts to account for blade effects (Cumpsty & Marble 1977,
Mishra & Bodony 2013, Guzmán-Iñigo et al. 2021) exist, but approaches that push beyond the
zero frequency assumption remain immature for practical application. Experiments (Bake et al.
2009) and computations (Leyko et al. 2009, Brouzet et al. 2024) can be used to inform nozzle flow
boundary conditions as well as real blade passage (NGV) boundary conditions (Leyko et al. 2014).
4.3. Combustor Walls
The combustor bounding walls can affect both the flame and the acoustic waves in the combustor.
The mean and unsteady flame response can be affected by wall heat transfer and wall temperature
effects near the flame anchoring position (Agostinelli et al. 2021). For the acoustics, visco-thermal
damping effects at walls are generally negligible for the typical cross-sectional size of real com-
bustors (Li & Morgans 2016). The effect on the acoustics of heat transfer and an axially varying
temperature field is discussed in Section 4.5, and the effect of dampers embedded into or attached
to the combustor walls in Section 4.6.
4.4. Area Changes
Although the main combustion chamber often exhibits only gradual changes in the cross-sectional
area, the larger thermoacoustic domain, comprising the plenum, burner, combustor, etc., typically
involves a sequence of rapid area changes. When such area changes happen over a length scale that
is much smaller than the acoustic wavelength, jump conditions can be used to link the strength
of linear flow perturbations across this change. For area contractions, the flow changes are isen-
tropic, and conservation of mass and energy combined with the isentropic condition yields jump

At rapid area expansions, large-scale flow separation occurs and the isentropic condition must

unchanged across the area expansion interface (Ronneberger 1987, Dowling & Stow 2003). In a
recent study, Gaudron et al. (2023) confirmed the validity of this assumption for flow perturba-
tions as well as the mean flow, by comparing to simulations using the linearized Navier–Stokes
equations. The authors furthermore characterized the acoustic damping associated with the stag-
nation pressure loss, finding that the flow expansion damps acoustic energy, except for a very
few conditions involving a large Mach number when acoustic generation is possible. This implies
that acoustic modeling approaches that assume constant static pressure across an area expansion
implicitly account for an important source of acoustic damping.
While entropy noise is typically more relevant for flow contractions where the flow acceler-
ates, recent laboratory experiments on entropy noise (Rolland et al. 2017) have used a downstream
orifice plate, which first contracts and then expands the flow. For the flow expansion, the compact
isentropic model (Marble & Candel 1977) failed to accurately predict the entropy noise. Model-
ing by Yang et al. (2020) showed that the spatial extent of the recirculation zone caused by flow
separation was the main cause of this divergence. By applying the aforementioned assumption of
constant static pressure across the area change,entropy noise can be predicted well (Guzmán-Iñigo
et al. 2022).
4.5. Spatially Varying Flow Field
In real combustors, the flow field temperature and possibly the cross-sectional area often vary
in the axial direction, the former due to mixing with cooling flows and/or heat losses. Flow
field temperature changes directly alter the speed of sound and hence affect acoustic propaga-
tion and thermoacoustic modes. Acoustic modeling approaches based on the inhomogeneous
Helmholtz equation are able to account for axial variations in temperature and cross-sectional
area naturally (Laera et al. 2017a). Acoustic characterization via wave-based models can account
for these effects simplistically by slicing the combustor domain into smaller axial segments (Laera
et al. 2017b). There has also been significant recent progress in extending wave-based acoustic
modeling (i.e., for low-order acoustic network models) to account for axial variations in temper-
ature (Li & Morgans 2017, Li et al. 2018), cross-sectional area (Yeddula & Morgans 2021), and
heat transfer (Yeddula et al. 2022), including for annular geometries (Li et al. 2021).
4.6. Acoustic Dampers
The types of acoustic damper commonly added to combustion systems include Helmholtz res-
onators, perforated liners, and quarter-wave tubes (Richards et al. 2006, Zhao & Li 2015, Lahiri
& Bake 2017). These are all underpinned by holes sustaining a bias and/or grazing flow in order
to absorb acoustic energy. Vorticity generated by flow separation at the hole edges is convected
by the mean flow and finally dissipated into heat, absorbing the acoustic energy. Linear acous-
tic models are usually sufficient to predict the acoustic characteristics of such dampers (Eldredge
& Dowling 2003, Bellucci et al. 2004, Dupere & Dowling 2005). For holes where the dominant
flow is the bias flow through them rather than grazing flow, vortex-sound theory for thin circular
holes (Howe 1979) has been widely used to predict the acoustic characteristics, predicting acoustic
absorption/damping across all frequencies. However, for holes with similar length and diameter,
the flow that separates from the hole inlet edge does not reattach before the hole exit, and the hole

5. EXAMPLES OF THERMOACOUSTIC INSTABILITY PREDICTION
FOR REALISTIC COMBUSTORS
The prediction of thermoacoustic instabilities in realistic combustors has progressed over the last
two decades, targeting different levels of combustor and burner complexity and using different
prediction tools. The targeted combustor type varies from lab-scale single-burner combustors to
industrial-scale multiple-burner annular combustors, with the burners varying from bluff-body
stabilized burners through laboratory matrix burners to industrial swirl burners. The predic-
tion methods can be broken down into two main categories: (a) direct methods, which use
CFD, mainly in the form of compressible LES, to solve for thermoacoustic oscillations in a
combustion system, and (b) hybrid/coupled methods, which couple separate treatments for the
acoustic waves and the flame unsteadiness. The computational expense of hybrid/coupled meth-
ods is generally much lower than for direct methods, since the physics-informed characterization
of the acoustic waves is captured with fewer resources. Some examples of thermoacoustic pre-
dictions achieved and a summary of the combustor type and prediction method are shown in
Table 1.
For single-burner combustors, predicting the thermoacoustic mode frequencies requires re-
solving the acoustic propagation within the spatial domain along with the acoustic boundary
conditions at either end. The former is relatively straightforward, requiring knowledge of the
mean temperature and thus speed of sound; the latter can be obvious for some combustors but
commonly requires some consideration of the acoustic-flow interactions at the boundaries. In or-
der to go beyond predicting thermoacoustic modal frequencies to predicting the stability of these
modes, the linear flame response and the acoustic damping must be characterized with reasonable
accuracy. While it is possible to prescribe the linear flame response using theoretical or numerical
models, for realistic combustors it usually needs to be obtained from experimental measurements
or LES. The need for experimental or CFD characterization is especially true if the flame is long
enough to not be acoustically compact or is at elevated pressure. Due to the typical low combustor
Mach numbers, LES for the flame response can be incompressible, i.e., allowing density changes
due to temperature but not pressure—this exploits the fact that flames respond primarily to the
hydrodynamic fluctuations initiated by acoustic excitation (Candel 2002, Ducruix et al. 2003). In-
compressible LES has the benefit of allowing larger time steps and avoiding the need to deal with
the acoustic performance of the simulation’s boundary conditions.

SUMMARY POINTS
1. Thermoacoustic instabilities are caused by a two-way coupling between acoustic waves
and unsteady heat release rate.
2. Thermoacoustic instabilities are problematic for combustors in aeroengines, land-based
gas turbines, rocket engines, domestic boilers, etc., often arising late in the design and
development process.
3. There has been significant research activity over the last decade on modeling and un-
derstanding thermoacoustic instabilities in annular-shaped combustors, largely driven

by new experimental facilities. There is now growing research activity on modeling and
understanding thermoacoustic instabilities in can-annular geometries.
4. Fast and accurate computational prediction of thermoacoustic instability and ther-
moacoustic oscillations in real combustors remains challenging and computationally
intensive, largely due to the range of length scales, timescales, and flow physics at play.
5. Significant progress has been made over the last decade using coupled/hybrid approaches
for computational prediction of thermoacoustic instability. Such approaches combine
separate computational treatments for the acoustic waves and the flame. Successful
predictions extend to pressurized and industrially representative combustors.
FUTURE ISSUES
1. The move to decarbonize energy systems and aviation is driving a transition to new
fuels. Some of these will require substantial redesign of the burner and/or combustor.
This transition will likely bring new thermoacoustic instability problems but also new
opportunities to explore ways of mitigating thermoacoustic instability.
2. Can-annular combustor geometries are relevant to large/efficient gas turbines for en-
ergy generation. Further thermoacoustic characterization and understanding of these
are needed across experimental, modeling, and numerical tools.
3. Given that burner designs are likely to undergo quite significant changes in the context
of the transition to new fuels, it will be important to quickly develop an understanding
of both the unsteady flame response and acoustic damping mechanisms associated with
new burner designs.
4. Further understanding and accurate incorporation of the main acoustic damping
mechanisms into thermoacoustic instability prediction methods are needed.
5. Data-driven tools such as optimization remain relatively unexplored for mitigation of
thermoacoustic instability and may offer avenues for exploiting the high sensitivity that
thermoacoustic stability can exhibit to design changes.
