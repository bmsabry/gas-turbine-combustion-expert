This work describes new type of combustion instability for which the 3-way coupling between mixing,
ﬂame heat release, and acoustics is modiﬁed by local buoyancy effects. Measurements of fuel mixture
fraction are made for a non-premixed jet ﬂame in a combustion chamber to assess the dynamics of
mixing under imposed acoustic oscillations (22–55 Hz). Infrared laser absorption and phase resolved
acetone-planar laser induced ﬂuorescence are used to measure the fuel mixture fraction and then the
degree of fuel/air mixing is calculated by determining the unmixedness. Results show acoustic excitation
causes oscillations in the degree of fuel/air mixing at the driving frequency, which results in oscillatory
ﬂame behavior. This oscillatory ﬂame behavior couples to the buoyancy and this in turn affects the
mixing. Results also show that the mixing becomes less effective when the excitation frequency is
increased or when the ﬂame is present, compared to the non-reacting case. This work describes a key
coupling mechanism that occurs when buoyancy is a signiﬁcant factor in the ﬂow ﬁeld.

1. Introduction
Combustion instabilities are pressure oscillations in a combus-
tion chamber caused by coupling between thermo-acoustic and
ﬂuid-dynamic processes. Such oscillations can cause catastrophic
damage in extreme cases. The interaction between vortices, sound
waves and combustion heat release can lead to self-excited os-
cillations that can cause problems such as increased pollutant
production, uneven burning, and, possible structural damage. Any
unsteadiness in the process of combustion can be a source of
pressure and velocity ﬂuctuations [1,2]. Recently, environmental
concerns have led to the use of lean premixed (LP) combustion
schemes which reduce the amount of NOx produced by the com-
bustion system. The NOx produced through the ‘thermal or Zel-
dovich pathway’ caused by the high temperature inside the com-
bustors can be reduced by lowering the ﬂame temperature. These
lower temperatures can result in the occurrence of instabilities,
since combustion in these systems tends to occur near the lean
blow-out limits [3–5].
Several different studies have examined the physical phenom-
ena involved in this process. A study of the acoustic coupling be-

The objective of the present study is to examine the effect of
acoustic oscillations, which are sources of unstable dynamics inside
the combustor, on the fuel/air mixing. This work also examines the
link between ﬂame behavior and mixing behavior. The current ef-
fort builds on previous work [13] by focusing on the fuel–air struc-
ture upstream of the ﬂame front. To this end, two-dimensional
images of the fuel–air distribution are obtained by imaging the
acetone concentration. One-dimensional absorption measurements
are seed to examine the behavior of fuel mixture fraction.
2. Experimental conﬁguration
In this study, acetone was used as a fuel marker to visualize
the fuel/air mixing region [24,25] directly upstream of the ﬂame,
and this is supplemented with time-resolved data acquired with
an infrared absorption probe, as illustrated in Fig. 1.
2.1. Apparatus
A schematic view of the acoustic chamber is shown in Fig. 2.
The acoustic chamber is made of a 30-cm-diameter aluminum cen-
tral section which is 180 cm long and a stainless steel top that
extends 50 cm to each side. The top of the chamber houses the
loudspeakers and has a port to exhaust the product gases. The ex-
haust port is open to the atmosphere which creates an acoustically
open exit condition for acoustic wave resonance. The bottom of the
chamber is a closed-end acoustic boundary condition. The speak-
ers are 30 cm in diameter and they are powered by 1000 W power
ampliﬁers. A function generator (Wavetek 171) provides the input
signal.
The burner is a jet-injection burner with the ﬂame anchored
approximately in the middle of the quartz tube. The fuel consists
of 50% methane and 50% nitrogen with a trace amount of acetone
seed. Air is entrained into the jet as the jet ﬂuid moves through the
burner. The fuel jet speed is approximately 20 m/s at the outlet of
the 1.0-cm-diameter fuel port, yielding a jet Reynolds number of
10,000–20,000. The open-ended square cross-section quartz enclo-
sure is 5.72 cm wide on each side and 11.43 cm tall.

2.3. Acetone PLIF
Acetone PLIF is a widely used technique for measuring distribu-
tions of species concentrations [30–32] and for ﬂow visualization
purposes [33]. An intensiﬁed CCD camera was used for the PLIF
imaging, while a data acquisition board (NI PCI 6014) along with a
pressure transducer (PCB 106B50) was used to record the pressure,
laser intensity, and ICCD gating signals. The image is captured on
an intensiﬁed CCD camera with 300 by 225 pixels mapping to a
5.5 by 4.2 cm image plane area. Images are taken at random pres-
sure phases, with the camera gating signal being recorded by the
data acquisition system along with the pressure signal so as to en-
able appropriate post-processing.
The laser beam at 532 nm is used to pump a dye laser which
operates at 560 nm. The output of the dye laser is then frequency
doubled to 280 nm for excitation of acetone with the ﬂuorescence
signal expected in the range of 350–550 nm.

It can be seen in Fig. 4a that large values of temporal unmixed-
ness occur in the mixing layer, at normalized radial locations that
are 30–60% of the total radial distance. This is where shear-induced
mixing between the jet and the entrained air is expected to occur.
There is little variation in the overall structure of unmixedness dis-
tribution with respect to the varying acoustic forcing frequencies,
and all plots indicate the same general mixing layer structure. In
Figs. 4b and 4c, lighter shading indicates a region with a larger
values of unmixedness, thus marking the region of high fuel con-
centration ﬂuctuation. Further downstream from the jet nozzle
(Fig. 4b), the shear layer becomes wider. The low values of tem-
poral unmixedness in the core region (located between 0 and 30%
of the distance from the center), is due to the large and relatively
uniform fuel concentration. Cross sectional views cut from Fig. 4 at
longitudinal distances of 1–3 cm are shown in Fig. 5.
Fig. 5a shows the temporal unmixedness for non-reacting case
at three vertical heights above the eductor block while Fig. 5b
shows the same for reacting cases. There is little temporal variation
for mixing in the non-reacting case in both the radial and vertical
location. On the other hand, Fig. 5b shows a relatively strong peak
at the 50% radial location, and the peak slowly drops as the ﬂow
travels vertically upward.
This seems to be a direct result of buoyancy. Simple calcula-
tion based on a 2-D FLUENT model shows that, for reacting cases,
the ﬂow velocity in the downstream (upward) direction (oppo-
site to that of gravity), mass-averaged within the mixing zone, is
larger than the non-reacting ﬂow velocity in the same zone. This
zone spans from the centerline (0%) to about 60% of the radial dis-
tance to the outer edge (Fig. 6). The velocity distribution in Fig. 6a
shows that the high velocity core of the reacting ﬂow is widened
compared to that of the cold ﬂow, and this can be shown more
clearly in Fig. 6b. It is evident that for radial locations of 30% or
farther out, the mass-weighted velocity proﬁle is larger for the re-
acting ﬂow. The temporal unmixedness distribution pattern for the
reacting ﬂow shows that the shear mixing zone is formed approx-
imately in the vicinity of 30 to 60% of the radial distance which
agrees with Fig. 4. The proﬁle also agrees with the unmixedness
pattern seen in Figs. 4 and 5. So even in a system which should be
primarily a jet-driven ﬂow, the effect of buoyancy due to combus-
tion still appears to have a signiﬁcant impact.
This means that at the same vertical location, the distribu-
tion of unmixedness for the reacting ﬂow cases will resemble
the distributions at the lower vertical locations (which correspond
to earlier times) for the non-reacting cases. This is evidenced by
the fact that the non-reacting cases are mostly mixed by the
time the ﬂow reaches the imaging area while the reacting ﬂows
are still undergoing mixing. The characteristic time of the mix-
ing should be greater than that of the convection for the cur-
rent conditions. It also appears that the stabilizing presence of
the ﬂame due to gas expansion also slows down the mixing pro-
cess.

Combustion causes a region of irregular pressure ﬂuctuations
that is not seen in the non-reacting cases (see Fig. 10b) due to the
generation of sound waves by the irregular burning of the turbu-
lent ﬂame. This phenomenon is sometimes called the ‘1/ f error,’
and is observed in a wide variety of experimental work in the
ﬁeld of random processes. The amplitude of waves generated by
the turbulent burning of fuel is signiﬁcant and is due to ﬂuctua-
tions in the local equivalence ratio in the ﬂame area, which itself
is caused by irregularities in the fuel/air mixing upstream of the
ﬂame.

4. Conclusion
This work has demonstrated the existence and has character-
ized the behavior of a new combustion instability coupling mode
that relates weak acoustic forcing to several factors: mixture frac-
tion oscillations, ﬂame oscillations and buoyancy forces. The mode
is characterized by a 3-way coupling between the acoustics, mix-
ing, and ﬂame heat-release with the buoyancy acting to modify
the frequency and phase relationship of the coupling. The buoy-
ancy acts to accelerate the ﬂow ﬁeld and hence reduce the amount
of time available for mixing. This results in a less well mixed ﬂow
and stronger mixture fraction oscillations. Cold ﬂow tests cannot
identify this mode without changes to the testing and analysis
methodology.
The phase-resolved global unmixedness measurements indicate
the presence of the primary mode of oscillation, which occurs at
the same frequency as the excitation. Conversely, the unmixedness
oscillations are phase shifted from what is seen for the ﬂame base
behavior, and the amount of shift varies for different frequencies. It
is clear that the acoustic forcing causes a strong periodicity in the
mixing layer at the driven frequency for all cases. However, higher
harmonic frequencies are present only in the reacting ﬂow cases.
It appears that these oscillations in the unmixedness are then the
main cause of ﬂuctuations in local equivalence ratio in the ﬂame
region, which, in turn, induces the periodic ﬂame motion observed
in the previous work [12,13]. The periodic ﬂame motion creates
large values of local Rayleigh index which drives the acoustic ﬁeld.
The temporal unmixedness pattern reveals that the breakdown
of the fuel/air distribution structure is slower in the reacting ﬂow
cases because the ﬂow ﬁeld is modiﬁed by the buoyant acceler-
ation due to the high temperature of the hot product gases, re-
sulting in slower mixing due to the loss of mixing time and wider
variations in the mixing zone. In general, the mean ﬂow patterns
keep the same structure downstream of the mixing region and as
the ﬂow progresses downstream, subsequent mixing reduces the
variation in the distribution of fuel/air mixing in both reacting and
non-reacting cases.
