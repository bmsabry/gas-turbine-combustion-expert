Acoustic Damper Placement and
Tuning for Annular Combustors:
An Adjoint-Based Optimization
Study
Thermoacoustic instabilities pose a major threat to modern gas turbines. The use of
acoustic dampers, like Helmholtz resonators, has proven useful for the mitigation of such
instabilities. However, assessing the effect of acoustic dampers on thermoacoustic modes
in annular combustion chambers remains an intricate task. This results from the implicit
nature of the thermoacoustic Helmholtz equation associated with the high number of pos-
sible parameter values for the positioning of the dampers and their impedance design. In
the present work, the principal challenges of the effective placement and the design of the
impedance of acoustic dampers in annular chambers are discussed. This includes the
choice of an appropriate objective function for the optimization, the combinatorial chal-
lenges when dealing with different possible damper arrangements, and the numerical
complexities when using the thermoacoustic Helmholtz equation to approach this issue.
As a key aspect, the paper proposes a new adjoint-based approach to tackle these prob-
lems. The new algorithm establishes algebraic models that predict the effect of acoustic
dampers on the growth rates of the thermoacoustic modes. The theory is exempliο§¹ed on
the basis of a generic annular combustor model with 12 burners.

Introduction
Thermoacoustic instabilities pose a major threat to modern gas
turbines. These instabilities have negative effects on the opera-
tional range of the combustors and may severely damage the
machines [1]. Both passive and active damping methods may be
considered to tackle this issue. Usually, passive methods are used
because they feature high reliability at low costs. Such methods
are based on the use of acoustic damping devices like Helmholtz
dampers. It is very difο§¹cult to adapt these components after
machine installation. Thus, the availability of fast and accurate
mathematical design tools is essential for the development of new
gas turbines featuring a higher efο§¹ciency and greater lifetime.

of thermoacoustic instabilities [2βÄ™4], but their numerical costs are
too high to be used for design purposes. As an alternative, the
governing equations can be linearized and transformed to the fre-
quency domain. Thermoacoustic instabilities can then be modeled
using a thermoacoustic Helmholtz equation:

The Optimization Algorithm
Multiparameter perturbation theory provides the key ingredient
for an optimization algorithm to position and tune acoustic damp-
ers. Therefore, the problem can be split into two major steps,
namely positioning and tuning of the dampers. An illustration of
the scheme is displayed in Fig. 3.
First, the best positioning is computed. The optimization starts
with one classical iterative solution of the baseline conο§¹guration.
Solutions of the Helmholtz equation are the computationally most
expensive operations in this algorithm. Evaluations of the explicit
multiparameter sensitivity functions (22) are much faster. Thus,
assessing all considered positioning patterns with respect to an
objective function f in a brute force sensitivity study is feasible
when using the base-line solution as input for these asymptotic
computations. The value of the objective function is a quality
measure of the desired design goals. The function should assess
the damping rate of the considered modes and can contain penalty
terms accounting for design constraints. Because the argument of
the objective function can be expressed as a direct function chain
starting with the design parameters fp, continuing with the pertur-
bation parameters ep, and ending with eigenvalues xΟΑmΟû of the con-
sidered modes it can be explicitly written as

Conclusions and Future Work
An adjoint-based method for the optimum positioning and tun-
ing of multiple acoustic dampers has been derived. As a key
aspect, the method utilizes the truncation rule. Application to a
model problem indicates that this rule is applicable to solutions of
the thermoacoustic Helmholtz equation. The method also rests on
a multiparameter formulation of the ο§¹rst-order eigenvalue pertur-
bations. Even if not shown in this paper, it is possible to combine
the method with the Bloch wave methodology to exploit the dis-
crete rotational symmetry of the system in order to further acceler-
ate the computations [21]. To account for physical impedance
models, linearization of the involved perturbation operators will
be necessary. This together with a three-dimensional combustor
model will be the topic of future work in order to validate the
approach for real combustors.
The optimization algorithm could also be used to optimize
other entities than acoustic dampers. For instance, it could be uti-
lized to ο§¹nd the best fuel staging pattern if the effects are available
as ο§²ame response functions. Moreover, the multiparameter formu-
lation could help calculating uncertainty quantiο§¹cations like those
discussed in Ref. [22]. The main limitations of the framework
arise from the fact that the multiparameter perturbation is of ο§¹rst
order. Thus, a higher-order perturbation formulation would
improve the methodology. For instance, it would allow for global
optimization and larger step sizes. Feeding the results from the
tuning step back into the positioning step may also lead to
improvements of the algorithm. A general application for the pre-
sented perturbation theory could also be the acceleration of the
iterative algorithms for the solution of the Helmholtz equation.
Future work will address these topics.
