Influence of Layout Performance 
on Combustion Characteristics of 
the Combined Micromixing 
Flames at Different Dilution 
Ratios
Micromixing (MM) combustion technology represents a highly promising approach for 
achieving low-emission performance in gas turbine applications. As for six types of syngas 
with N2 dilution ratios (X) ranging from 0% to 50%, the experimental and numerical studies 
are carried out at different tube center distance (TCD) of 1.25, 1.5, 1.75, 2, 2.25, and 2.5D 
(D represents the nozzle exit diameter, D ¼ 10 mm). The in-depth discussions around the 
flowfield characteristics, flame structure and pollutant emissions are conducted. The results 
indicate that the numerical models show a good performance in flame structure and axial 
temperature distribution prediction. Syngas with lower X (X < 20%) sustains stable 
combustion under high flow rates; however, the associated NO emission exceeds regulatory 
standards. In contrast, higher X levels (X > 40%) result in diminished flame stability but 
achieve significant NOx reduction. Consequently, this study recommends a dilution ratio 
between 20% and 40% when implementing fuel-side dilution strategies to balance 
combustion stability and effective NOx emissions control. With the increasing TCD, the 
mutual influence and interaction between the adjacent flames gradually worsen, the flame 
height and high-temperature zone area significantly decrease, and NO emission rapidly 
drops. When TCD is higher than 2.25 D, the combustion efficiency across all dilution 
conditions falls markedly below 99%. The recommended TCD range in this study is set 
between 1.5 D and 2 D. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4069251] 
Keywords: micromixing combustion, dilution ratio, tube center distance, combustion 
stability, NO emission

1 Introduction
As a leading technology in low-emission coal utilization under 
carbon neutrality frameworks, the integrated gasification combined 
cycle (IGCC) demonstrates significant advantages [1–3]. In the 
IGCC, syngas serves as the primary fuel for gas turbine combustors, 
with combustible components predominantly H2, CO, and minor 
methane CH4 [4,5]. The inherent reactivity of hydrogen, charac­
terized by low ignition energy requirements, rapid flame propaga­
tion, and broad flammability limits, amplifies risks of auto-ignition, 
flashback, and combustion instabilities compared to conventional 
natural gas fuels [6–9]. Furthermore, syngas inherently contains 
diluents such as N2 and CO2, the concentrations of which vary 
considerably depending on the gasification process employed. 
These compositional differences directly impact combustion 
stability and pollutant emission levels. Recent research has focused 
on micromixing (MM) combustion technology to address these 
issues, demonstrating enhanced performance through reduced 
reactant mixing scales, compact flame dimensions, and improved 
premixing homogeneity at combustor outlets [10–12].
Micronozzle arrays are utilized in MM combustion systems to 
enhance fuel-oxidizer mixing efficiency, serving as an alternative to 
traditional single-nozzle configurations. By improving fuel–air 
homogenization in the premixing zone, these micronozzles reduce 
localized high-temperature zones [13] and shorten flame length. 
This configuration decreases fuel residence time, thereby reducing 
NOx formation [14,15]. Lopez-Ruiz et al. [16] demonstrated the 
theoretical viability of MM combustion for hydrogen-rich syngas in 
gas turbines. Experimental studies [17–19] further confirm that MM 
combustors can suppress NOx emissions to levels below 10 lL/L at 
15%O2. The novel MM nozzle configuration proposed here employs 
an annular jet design to optimize fuel–air mixing efficiency while 
minimizing pressure losses.
In addition, limited by the thermal load of an MM nozzle, the MM 
combustor usually employs a certain number of nozzles with some 
different layout criteria. For these criteria, the tube center distance 
(TCD) between the adjacent MM nozzles is one of the most 
important distribution parameters, which not only decides the 
overall MM burner size, but also has a remarkable influence on the 
flame-to-flame interactions. Understanding the impact of TCD on 
flame behavior is crucial, as it directly relates to the efficiency and 
stability of the combustion process. Reduced TCD enhances 
interflame heat recirculation, potentially improving flame stability, 
but may also intensify flame merging risks that elevate local 
temperatures and NOx formation. Conversely, increased TCD 
mitigates flame interactions but may compromise combustion 
efficiency due to reduced thermal feedback. This delicate balance 
necessitates quantitative characterization of TCD effects. Further­
more, TCD optimization is critical for achieving compact 
combustor design while maintaining operational flexibility across 
varying thermal loads. Several researchers have delved into this 
aspect, exploring various configurations and spacings to optimize 
the performance of MM combustors. Numerical investigations by 
Veetil [20] on perforated-plate combustion systems revealed crucial 
correlations between geometric parameters and flame dynamics. 
The computational analysis demonstrated that diminished hole-to- 
hole spacing effectively reduces both flame base curvature and 
liftoff distance from the burner surface, thereby improving 
combustion stabilization mechanisms. Research by Hindasageri 
[21] employed comparative analysis of inline versus staggered 
configurations under varying Reynolds numbers and TCD. Through 
experimental characterization of flame impingement phenomena on 
opposing plates, their study quantified thermal interactions between 
combustion zones and heated surfaces. Notably, increased flame-to- 
flame spacing was found to enhance heat flux transmission to the 
impingement plate, albeit with compromised lean blow-off stability 
thresholds. Abdelhafez et al. [22] analyzed the hydrodynamic and 
combustion interactions in oxy-methane micromixer burners, 
focusing on nozzle geometry parameters. Results demonstrated 
that increasing the interjet spacing had a destabilizing effect, as it 
impaired the mutual stabilization mechanism between adjacent fuel 
jets, thereby degrading the system’s overall flame anchoring 
capability. Similarly, Æsøy [23] observed that three hydrogen-rich 
flames exhibit strong similarities to the single flame when TCD is 2 
D, indicating minimal interaction between them at this spacing. 
However, when TCD reaches 1.15 D, a prominent interaction zone 
forms between the flames.
Despite these valuable insights, there is still a lack of deep 
understanding of the TCD rule as the fuel heating value changes due 
to different inert gas components and ratios in MM combustion 
technology. Further research is needed to explore the intricate 
relationship between TCD, fuel composition, and combustion 
performance, in order to fully harness the potential of MM 
combustors. Six types of syngas with N2 dilution ratios ranging 
from 0% to 50% are chosen in this paper to investigate the influence 
of the layout performance on combustion characteristics including 
the flowfield characteristics, flame structure and pollutant emissions 
by changing the TCD of 1.25, 1.5, 1.75, 2, 2.25, and 2.5 D through 
experimental and numerical simulation methods.

nozzles combine the characteristics of MM and “flame stability by 
bluff body” [14,24]. Figure 2 illustrates various aspects of the

the number of fuel injection holes is 6, the fuel hole diameter is

MM nozzles comprise seven of the aforementioned jet nozzles,

analyses are conducted at different TCD of 1.25, 1.5, 1.75, 2, 2.25, 
and 2.5 D. The planar laser-induced fluorescence of hydroxyl 
radicals camera captures the longitudinal OH signal from three 
adjacent nozzles that the laser passes through, as shown in Fig. 2(e).
2.2 Numerical Methods. The numerical simulations are con­
ducted using ANSYS FLUENT. The primary models employed include: 
the realizable k–e model for turbulent flow [25], the eddy- 
dissipation-concept (model for combustion, and the discrete 
ordinates radiation model coupled with the weighted sum of gray 
gas model for radiative heat transfer. Chemical kinetics are 
governed by the Williams reaction mechanism (18 species and 38 
reactions). The full multicomponent diffusion model was imple­
mented to resolve molecular diffusion effects, including preferential 
diffusion of lighter species [26]. For NO formation, the thermal and 
prompt reaction mechanisms are taken into account.
Figure 3 illustrates the geometry and cross-sectional view of the 
computational domain for the combined MM nozzles, which 
replicates the geometric parameters of the experimental burner. 
Fuel and air inlets were defined as mass-flow boundaries, while the 
outlet was configured as a pressure outlet. Air tubes, fuel tubes, and 
fuel holes were assigned no-slip adiabatic wall conditions, while 
remaining walls were treated as isothermal surfaces at 773 K, 
consistent with experimental temperature measurements. The axial 
distance from the nozzle outlet to the calculation domain outlet 
direction is denoted as Z, and the radial distance from the central axis 
to the wall direction is denoted as X.

3 Results and Discussions
3.1 Experimental and Numerical Data Comparison. Figure 5
shows the average OH distribution at the center section of the nozzle 
under typical operating conditions (TCD ¼ 1.5 D), where the 
presented OH values are normalized relative to the respective 
maximum concentrations measured experimentally and computed 
numerically. The results indicate that both the experimental and 
numerical simulations of OH distribution show obvious jet flame 
characteristics. To quantitatively describe the accuracy of numerical 
simulation in predicting OH distribution results, Fig. 6 shows the 
distributions of experimental and numerical OH normalization 
intensity (based on the maximum OH concentration) projected in the 
axial and radial directions [35]. The results show that the 
distributions of experimental and numerical OH in the radial (X) 
and axial (Z) directions are similar. Although a localized 
discrepancy near the nozzle outlet, where the simulation over- 
predicts the jet expansion rate, can influence high-temperature zone 
estimation and potentially enhance radical concentrations and NO 
formation via thermal dissociation and the Zeldovich mechanism 
[36–38], quantitative comparisons (see Figs. 14 and 23) demonstrate 
that the resulting errors in predicted NOx emissions at the 
downstream plane remain consistently below 10% across varying 
X and TCD. Therefore, despite this localized effect on the NO 
formation, the overall predictive capability of the simulation for the 
integrated OH distribution and downstream NO emission shows 
strong consistency with experiments and falls within acceptable 
engineering tolerances.
Figure 7 compares the measured and calculated axial temperature 
distributions at the typical case (TCD ¼ 1.5 D, X ¼ 0%). Mean­
while, the mean deviation (MD) of 72 K between experimental and 
numerical temperatures is calculated as the average of pointwise 
differences. While the MD reflects slight overpredictions, the 
average relative error (5.7%) and high correlation coefficient (0.98) 
confirm strong agreement in trends. In addition, the maximum 
difference is along the Z ¼ 35 D axial close to the combustion 
chamber outlet, which may result from strong heat dissipation due to 
the fact that the tail of the burner shell was not wrapped with 
insulation cotton in actual experiments. To sum up, the numerical 
models show a good performance in flame structure axial 
temperature distribution prediction, which can be used for the 
following studies of combined MM flames.

increase of X. When X is greater than 40%, the syngas contains a 
large amount of inert dilution gas, which accelerates the speed of 
fuel injection from each small orifice, increases the jet rigidity, and 
CO fills the entire annular cavity. As shown in Fig. 9, the J and CO 
concentration UI increase rapidly with the increase of X, and the

3.2.2 Flame Structural Characteristics and Temperature Dis­
tribution Performance. This paper initially delves into the TCD of 2 
D, aiming to investigate the influence of X on the combined MM 
flame structural characteristics. The OH radical, a key intermediate 
in hydrocarbon combustion, serves as a marker for the primary heat 
release zone and flame structure [39]. Figure 10(a) illustrates the 
contour of OH radicals in a longitudinal plane under varying X. It is 
evident that when the X is less than 50%, the jet flames remain 
independent, with no observable flame merging, and the root 
structure of single flame is V-shaped [40]. With the increase of X, a 
reduction in OH radical concentration is observed, accompanied by 
flame narrowing and a contraction of the reaction zone, which is 
attributed to the diminished intensity of the reaction generating OH 
radicals.
Notably, there is a prominent phenomenon of overlapping and 
merging among the whole combined MM flames when X reaches 
50%, and the flames are no longer anchored at the bluff-body, which 
is because adding a large amount of N2 dilution to the fuel side will 
significantly reduce the oxygen concentration in the mixture, 
thereby slowing down the combustion rate and reducing the 
intensity of combustion [41]. Figure 10(b) shows the temperature 
distributions of different X in a longitudinal plane. It can be seen that 
as X increases from 0% to 40%, the high-temperature zone gradually 
moves away from the nozzle outlet, and the heat release becomes 
more uniform. When X reaches 50%, the high-temperature zone is

4 Conclusion
The experimental and numerical studies are carried out at 
different TCD of 1.25, 1.5, 1.75, 2, and 2.25D for six heating value 
syngas varied from 11.70 to 5.85 MJ/Nm3 in this paper. The in-depth 
discussions around the flowfield characteristics, flame structure, and 
pollutant emissions are conducted.
(1) When the X is less than 50%, the jet flames are independent of 
each other, there is no flame association phenomenon, and the 
root structure of the jet flame is V-shaped. While there is a 
prominent phenomenon of overlapping and merging among 
the whole combined MM flames when X reaches 50%, and 
the flames are no longer anchored at the bluff-body. The 
variations in TCD would not significantly alter the overall 
flame morphology.
(2) With the increase of X, the fuel-air jet momentum ratio and 
the uniformity index of CO increase rapidly, the fuel-air 
mixing effect is optimized, a reduction in OH radical 
concentration is observed, accompanied by flame narrowing 
and a decrease in the reaction zone area, OTDF and 
combustion efficiency decrease, and the NO emissions 
rapidly drop from 33 to 0.5 lL/L at 15%O2. The optimal X 
range of 20%40% is recommended to balance stable 
combustion performance with NO reduction, aligning with 
practical combustion system requirements.
(3) With the increasing TCD, the mutual influence and 
interaction between the adjacent flames gradually worsens, 
the flame height and high-temperature area significantly 
decrease, and NO emissions rapidly drop. The recommended 
TCD range in this study is set between 1.5 D and 2 D.
(4) The recirculation zones increase with the increase of X or 
TCD, however, the negative impact of X or TCD on flame 
stability exceeded the positive effect of increasing the 
strength and area of the recirculation zone. Therefore, when 
evaluating combustion intensity, multiple factors need to be 
comprehensively considered to ensure the accuracy of the 
results.
