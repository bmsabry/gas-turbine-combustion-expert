Toward the Development  
of an RRQL System—Part I:  
Swirl Pattern Effect on  
Exhaust Emissions and 
Chemiluminescence Distribution 
for NH3–Air Premixed Swirl 
Flames
Ammonia (NH3) is a carbon–free energy source and hydrogen carrier, but its fuel–bound 
nitrogen can lead to significant nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions. Staged combustion 
strategies, such as rich–quench–lean, can achieve low NOx emissions. However, improper 
design may result in high NOx levels when operating with a rich head end without a sufficient 
post–flame relaxation time. Previous work has shown that a rich–relaxation–quench–lean 
(RRQL) configuration can minimize NOx emissions in the rich head end (Cole et al., 2024, 
“Rich Ammonia Flame Shapes and NO Relaxation: Facility Development and Character­
ization,” ASME Paper No. GT2024–122369). This study focuses on how different swirl 
geometries affect exhaust emissions and flame morphology in rich, premixed NH3–air 
flames, for the design of a rich relaxation head end of an RRQL combustor. Experiments were 
conducted using a modular swirl burner and measuring NOx, N2O, and NH3 emissions, and 
recording natural flame luminosity and NH2* and OH* chemiluminescence images. Swirler 
design affects emissions by influencing flame length (with shorter flames allowing for more 
postflame relaxation time) and flame–wall interactions (influencing NH3 and N2O 
emissions). A compact flame with minimal wall interactions allows for increased NOx 
relaxation, while minimal wall interactions prevent heat losses and instabilities, which 
minimize NH3 and N2O emissions at a richer equivalence ratio, potentially enhancing H2 
production during the relaxation phase without increasing overall primary stage NOx 
emissions, thereby improving system efficiency. Detailed spatial evolution of NOx, N2O, and 
NH3 emissions further supports the RRQL as a promising combustor design for NH3 
combustion, if the rich head end is designed properly. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4069796] 
1 Introduction
Ammonia (NH3) is gaining interest as a potential noncarbon 
containing energy source to contribute to decarbonizing the energy 
sector [1–3]. Its well–established production, storage, and distribu­
tion infrastructure—largely driven by applications in fertilization 
and refrigeration—positions it as a feasible alternative to fossil 
fuels. However, NH3 combustion presents significant challenges, 
including flame stability and emissions control. The slow flame 
speeds, low reactivity, and narrow flammability limits lead to 
stability and operability concerns. From an emissions perspective, 
the additional fuel–bound nitrogen oxides (NOx) penalty, NH3 slip, 
and nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions lead to environmental impact 
concerns. Different combustion schemes, including fuel blends, 
high swirl stabilization, and staged combustion strategies, among 
others, have been proposed by researchers as potential avenues to 
mitigate the reactivity and emissions challenges with NH3 [4–6].
Chemiluminescence studies for NH3–air flames can map radical 
intensities with flame shapes and may potentially be correlated with 
emissions trends in a reaction zone in a non–invasive manner. For 
example, chemiluminescence studies have shown that N2O and NO 
emissions have been correlated with NH2 radicals [7]. NH2 is a key 
species in NOx formation and reduction through HNO reactions, as 
well as NH3 oxidation through NHi pathways [8,9]. Additionally, 
OH* is known to accelerate NH3  decomposition, and NOx

Researchers at Cardiff University have extensively examined 
NH3–H2 and NH3–CH4 blends at ratios that yield comparable flame 
speeds to typical hydrocarbons. Pugh et al. studied single–stage 
swirling NH3–H2–air flames and found operability limits narrower 
than other fuels but with relatively good flame stability [5,12]. From 
an emissions perspective, they reported that rich equivalence ratios

2

Additionally, N2O emissions rose in extreme lean cases, whereas 
NH* and OH* were found to peak closer to stoichiometric lean 
cases.
Hayakawa et al. also extensively studied swirling NH3–air flames 
[13]. The increased swirl intensity enhanced reactant mixing but led 
to higher NOx emissions, which were attributed to higher flame 
temperatures. NOx emissions were minimized when NH* would 
peak under rich conditions. In contrast, OH* exhibited a maximum, 
and NOx levels were elevated under lean conditions.
Wang et al. studied how residence time and swirl intensity affect 
NOx emissions in premixed NH3–CH4–air swirling flames using a 
bespoke single–stage swirl burner at elevated pressures [14]. 
Increasing residence time, either by extending the combustion 
chamber or enhancing swirl, was found to advance chemical 
reactions and reduce NO2 and N2O emissions, with swirl showing 
greater efficiency in lowering NOx than increasing combustor 
length. However, increased swirl can slightly decrease combustion 
efficiency, as evidenced by increased CO under certain conditions. 
These findings provide insight into optimizing flame configurations 
to minimize NOx emissions without compromising performance and 
disagree with findings from Hayakawa et al. [13].
Cole et al. recently introduced the rich–relaxation–quench–lean 
(RRQL) strategy to minimize NOx emissions [15]. The relaxation 
stage takes advantage of relatively low equilibrium NOx emissions 
produced from rich NH3–air flames and utilizes long residence times 
and elevated pressures to kinetically relax NOx emissions toward 
equilibrium values [4]. The RRQL experimental facility demon­
strated NOx relaxation in the primary stage, which consisted of a 
swirling NH3–air flame in a modular swirl burner. A preliminary 
study of the effect of swirl number (Sg) on flame stability and shape 
is presented; however, the effect of swirl geometry on emissions was 
left for future studies. Several new 3D–printed modular swirlers 
were studied, examining the effects of Sg, number of vanes, and vane 
shape on flame morphology, NH
2 and OH* radical distribution, and 
averaged emissions at a single residence time. Finally, detailed axial 
and radially resolved emissions are compared between the standard 
swirler design [15] and the best performing swirler geometry.
2 Materials and Methods
2.1 Burner and Conditions. Experiments were conducted 
with a lab–scale modular burner, shown in Fig. 1. The burner 
consists of a modular swirler, and the swirler center body houses a 
hydrogen torch and spark ignitor which is used only to ignite the 
NH3–air mixture. This burner simulates the rich and relaxation 
sections of an RRQL NH3 combustor, and the reader can refer to Ref. 
[15] for further details. The burner is built to accommodate 
additional upgrades for pressurized lean stage combustion after 
the rich burn and relaxation stages. It has also been designed to 
accommodate a modular and interchangeable swirler, which is 
3D–printed stainless steel with diverse geometric swirl numbers (Sg) 
and swirler geometries. They are each either axial curved or straight

Table 1 shows that the five different swirlers were tested with 
various Sg and different numbers of vanes (8, 12, 16). Due to the 
change in effective area, the thermal power has been slightly 
adjusted between 0.75 and 0.85 kW to keep a constant nozzle 
velocity (U0) of 0.90 m/s. The nozzle velocity is calculated by 
considering mass continuity of the air–fuel mixture across the 
effective area of the swirler, and the swirler effective area is 
calculated to account for swirler vane blockage. Swirlers are named 
for their Sg (0.7 or 1.1), geometry of the vanes (straight or curved), 
and number of vanes, e.g., Sg0.7–S16 corresponds to a swirler with 
Sg ¼ 0.7 and 16 straight vanes.
The combustor liner consists of a 33 cm long quartz tube with a 
5.02 cm inner diameter that fits concentrically onto the burner and 
seals with an O–ring. The length from the dump plane to the top of 
the quartz is 26.5 cm. There is a blockage plate on the top of the 
quartz to prevent backflow or interactions with outside air. The long 
quartz tube is meant to allow NOx emissions to relax toward 
equilibrium with residence times around 100 ms post–flame for a 
5 cm flame, depending on the nozzle velocity and expansion ratio. 
As shown in Ref. [4], these values should enable the capture of the 
first order of magnitude NO relaxation at 1 bar and nearly the full 
approach to equilibrium at 10 bar.
Equivalence ratios (/) and the nozzle velocity are calculated in 
real–time using the mass flow ratios for the fuel and air circuits. The 
mass flow rates are metered using manual and electronic pressure 
regulators to control upstream pressures through critical orifices. An 
upstream pressure transducer and thermocouple in combination 
with a downstream pressure transducer allow the operator to ensure 
sonic flow for effective metering. An additional thermocouple is 
located upstream of the swirler to track the reactant temperature. The 
air and fuel flow control setup described leads to an accuracy better 
than 1% for the /. The nozzle velocity is defined using the reactant 
density, effective nozzle area, and reactant mass flow. The Reynolds 
number at the nozzle exit varies between 10,000 and 18,000, and it is 
most sensitive to nozzle velocity and varies only marginally with 
equivalence ratio due to similarities in kinematic viscosity for air 
and NH3.

2.3 Flame Imaging. Time–averaged broadband flame images 
were recorded with a digital single-lens reflex (DSLR) camera with 
an exposure time of 1/4 s, an aperture of f/16, and an ISO number of 
800. White balance was kept constant at 4000 K throughout the 
experimental campaign. Chemiluminescence images were recorded 
with a high–speed, intensified camera (FASTCAM (Tokyo, Japan) 
SA–X2/HiCATT intensifier) fitted with a UV lens (CERCOV
R 
(Concord, CA), 2178, f ¼ 150 mm, f/2.8). A 20 nm bandpass filter 
centered at 308 nm (Invisible Vision) was used for OH*, and a 10 nm 
bandpass filter centered at 633 nm (Andover Corp., Salem, NH, 
633FS10-50) was used for NH
2. It is acknowledged that several 
species may emit in this range, such as H2O*, NO

2 [19,20]. 
The PHOTRON FASTCAM software is used to record 300 frames for each 
data condition at 500 fps, which were then background subtracted, 
temporally averaged, and inverse Abel–transformed during post­
processing to map flame shapes across the swirlers under the varying 
swirlers and equivalence ratios listed in Table 2.
3 Results and Discussion
3.1 Flame Appearance. Figure 2 shows the time–averaged 
broadband flame images as a function of the / for all the swirlers 
tested. In general, the flames present the typical V–shaped 
morphology of a swirling flame, and the flame length increases as 
a function of the /. The Sg1.1–C8 swirler (first row) produces a 
compact flame with lobe–like structures when the flow interacts with 
the combustor liner walls. Generally, compact flames are desirable 
due to their increased NOx relaxation zones for a fixed geometry. 
However, in a preliminary study, flame instabilities and oppor­
tunities for improved emissions production [15] were found for this 
specific swirler. These lobes decrease by increasing the number of 
vanes (Sg1.1–C12), but they are still present. Using a swirler with 
straight (flat) vanes sharpens the lobe—like structures but produces 
a slightly more elongated flame (Sg1.1–S12). Increasing the number 
of vanes to 16 (Sg0.7–S16) produces a compact flame, and minor 
differences can be spotted for the two swirl numbers Sg0.7–S16 and

Figure 5 presents isocontours for inverse Abel–transformed OH* 
(black lines) and NH
2 (gray lines) as a function of / for all the 
swirlers tested. The solid lines represent isocontours of 40% 
maximum intensity, and the dashed lines represent isocontours of 
90% intensity. Overall, the NH

the outer side of the flame front, beginning at the swirler outlet and 
extending downstream. Looking at the 40% intensity isocontour, as 
/ increases, the NH

slightly, indicating a delayed stabilization of the flame in richer 
mixtures. Additionally, the length of the NH

increasing /, signifying enhanced radical production in the preheat 
zone. In contrast, OH* radicals are concentrated on the inner side of 
the flame front and are typically lifted, reflecting their association 
with higher temperature regions closer to the flame core. 
Furthermore, when analyzing the 90% isocontour line, the relative 
locations of NH
2 and OH* are consistent, where OH* is typically 
lifted on the inner edge of the flame.
Generally, with increasing /, the length of the radical isocontour 
decreases, which is particularly apparent for the swirl geometries 
with a visible lobe structure (Sg1.1–C8, Sg1.1–C12, and Sg1.1–S12). 
Furthermore, these swirl geometries with lobe structure present in 
the flame brush have a smaller area in the 90% isocontour for both 
radical distributions as opposed to the swirlers, which produce a 
more traditional inner recirculation zone flame shape (Sg1.1–S16, 
Sg0.7–S16). The key takeaway in terms of swirler geometry 
performance from the chemiluminescence images is the interaction 
of the lobe structures with the quartz liner. Regions of high OH* 
intensity, such as the 90% OH* isocontour, are located closer to the 
quartz liner, which can cause dramatic effects on NOx relaxation, 
NH3 cracking, and N2O emissions produced due to cold liner walls.

NOx, NH3, and N2O at 7.6 cm downstream from the flame, or 
80 ms from the flame brush, as a function of / for all the swirlers 
tested. It is observed that in general NOx decreases while N2O and 
NH3 increase as a function of /, trends that are well known in 
literature [10]. To better observe the impact of the swirler geometry 
on the optimal / (minimum NOx–NH3 tradeoff), a zoomed-in plot is 
also shown in Fig. 6 (bottom panel). It can be seen that Sg1.1–C8 has 
its sweet spot at a leaner / (see circle solid dot) at approximately 
1.11. This optimal spot shifts toward richer / (see red arrow) by 
increasing the number of vanes. The vane shape, curved versus 
straight, causes a slight shift in the optimal / toward the rich end, 
which can be compared with the Sg1.1–C12 and Sg1.1–S12 
geometries. Furthermore, increasing Sg also causes a slight shift in 
the optimal / toward the rich end, which can be compared with the 
Sg1.1–S16 and Sg0.7–S16 geometries. However, this shift is within 
the average error in /. Although not demonstrated within this test 
matrix, the Sg will affect flame stability and decrease the stable 
operability limits. Increasing the number of vanes to 16 in the 
Sg1.1–S16 produced the lowest NOx emissions at the richest sweet 
spot of 1.13 (see diamond solid dot) which could produce more H2 
for the second stage.
Generally, with improved flame shape and decreased liner 
interaction caused by slight modifications in swirler geometry 
such as the number of vanes and the vane geometry, the optimal / 
shifts to richer equivalence ratios. The flame shape and peak OH* 
concentrations show that with increased heat losses to liner walls, at 
the same / NH3 and N2O emissions double. NOx emissions see a 
slight improvement due to the increased NH3 slip, meaning that all 
the fuel–bound N2 is not being released in the primary stage.

4 Conclusions
An experimental study was carried out to understand the impact of 
the swirl pattern on the flame morphology and exhaust emissions 
of rich premixed NH3–air flames. Focus was on the characterization 
of the combustion zone through flame imaging and exhaust 
emissions. The main conclusions are:
(1) Swirler geometry can dramatically affect flame shape, peak 
radical concentrations, and emissions species for rich NH3 
flames.
(2) Elevated heat losses caused by swirler–liner interaction, 
indicated by regions of high OH* intensity, can cause the 
optimal / to shift closer to stoichiometric due to elevated 
NH3 and N2O emissions at richer / as shown by the Sg1.1–C8 
swirler configuration.
(3) This leaner / for Sg1.1–C8 suggests a potentially lower H2 
concentration for the second combustion stage, which could 
result in reduced overall combustion efficiency.
(4) The elevated N2O concentrations are likely attributed to heat 
losses through the walls, potentially exacerbated by the 
higher flame to wall interaction area produced by the lobed 
flame structures.
(5) Reduced NH3 cracking in the primary stage implies a higher 
contribution of fuel–bound nitrogen (fuel–NOx) to overall 
NOx production in the secondary lean stage.
These findings highlight the intricate interplay between swirler 
geometry, flame structure, and emission characteristics, under­
scoring the importance of careful optimization for minimizing 
emissions and maintaining combustion efficiency. As a result, we 
chose the Sg1.1–S16 swirler for further RRQL studies (Part II of this 
paper).
