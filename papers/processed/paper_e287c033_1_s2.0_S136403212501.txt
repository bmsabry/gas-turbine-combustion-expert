A B S T R A C T
Syngas, a hydrogen-rich fuel derived from coal and biomass gasification, offers a promising pathway towards 
sustainable energy systems by reducing emissions, enabling carbon capture, and integrating with advanced 
power plants such as gas turbines (GTs), internal combustion engines (ICEs) and industrial furnaces and boilers. 
This review comprehensively examines syngas combustion from basic principles to industrial applications, 
addressing critical gaps between fundamental research and practical implementation. Experimental and 
modeling studies of combustion characteristics, covering fundamental aspects (e.g., ignition delay times, laminar 
burning velocities and kinetic mechanisms) and turbulent combustion phenomena (such as turbulent burning 
velocities, flame structures and preferential diffusion) are systematically reviewed to clarify the effects of syngas 
compositions, particularly H2/CO ratios and diluents, along with operating conditions on syngas combustion 
processes. This work further evaluates syngas utilization in GTs, ICEs, industrial furnaces and boilers, empha­
sizing innovative strategies like flameless combustion, dual-fuel operation and homogeneous charge compression 
ignition to reduce NOx emissions and enhance stability. Major challenges such as flashback risk, thermoacoustic 
instabilities and NOx emissions related to syngas variability are discussed. In addition, this work emphasizes the 
importance of combustion control strategies, hybrid power configurations (e.g., IGCC), and system-level as­
sessments (e.g., TEA and LCA) in optimizing syngas-fueled combined heat and power systems. By integrating 
multidisciplinary insights, this review highlights the potential of adaptive combustion technologies and syngas/ 
ammonia cofiring to accelerate the transition to carbon-neutral energy.
1. Background and motivation
Coal remains the primary energy source for power generation in 
China, and this trend is expected to continue in the near future [1]. 
However, the extensive utilization of coal has resulted in significant 
environmental impacts, including degraded air quality, strained water 
resources and disrupted ecosystems. Moreover, coal combustion con­
tributes over 80 % of CO2 emissions from the power sector, raising 
global warming concerns [2]. Thus, the development of efficient, 
low-emission coal-based power generation technologies is imperative. 
Among these technologies, “clean coal” solutions, particularly IGCC 
systems, have attracted extensive attention. IGCC utilizes gasified coal 
as fuel, known as synthesis gas or syngas, offering cleaner power gener­
ation with substantially lower emissions compared to traditional 
coal-fired systems. Furthermore, IGCC provides a cost-effective pathway 
for CCS, supporting decarbonization efforts [3].
In parallel, interest is significantly growing in biomass-derived syn­
gas, which is produced from forest residues, agricultural waste and other 
biomass sources, and can achieve near net-zero CO2 emissions. The 
global energy potential of biomass is estimated at 200–500 EJ/year [4], 
with bioenergy projected to contribute 100–300 EJ/year, or roughly 25 
%–33 % of the global energy mix, by 2050 [5]. With abundant bioenergy 
reserves, China possesses significant potential for utilizing biomass as a 
renewable feedstock. Biomass gasification produces syngas as a valuable 
gaseous product and char as a solid byproduct [6]. In addition, coal and

Syngas is derived from coal, biomass and hydrocarbons via ther­
mochemical and emerging technologies. Conventional gasification 
processes typically involve partial oxidation and water-gas shift re­
actions, whereas non-catalytic methods require high temperatures 
(500–650 ◦C) to decompose feedstocks [7,8]. Catalytic gasification, 
employing alkali metals (Na, K, Ca), enhances H2 yield and reduces tar 
through electron-transfer or oxygen-transfer mechanisms [9–11]. 
Co-gasification of coal and biomass further improves carbon conversion 
and reduces emissions by promoting synergistic interactions [12,13]. 
Emerging methods include pCatalytic and eCatalytic CO2 reduction 
processes. Photocatalytic systems, such as CdS/W2N3 heterostructures, 
enable tunable CO/H2 ratios under visible light irradiation [14–18]. 
Electrocatalytic systems, using bimetallic catalysts like Cu/Ni(OH)2, 
optimize syngas selectivity through electrochemical pathways [19–21]. 
SCWG operates above 374 ◦C and 22.1 MPa, exploiting the reactivity of 
water to produce H2-rich syngas with minimal tar, aided by K2CO3 
catalysts [22–24]. Plasma gasification utilizes high-energy arcs for 
near-complete feedstock conversion but faces energy-intensity chal­
lenges [25,26]. Advances in catalytic stability, material science, and 
real-time process monitoring are critical for scalable, eco-friendly syn­
gas production.
A major challenge in syngas utilization is the considerable variability 
in its composition, which depends on the type of feedstock, synthesis 
method, and operating conditions. [27]. For IGCC powerplants, syngas 
is typically produced through entrained flow, fixed-bed and 
fluidized-bed gasifiers. Unlike natural gas (mainly CH4), coal- and 
biomass-derived syngas contains a complex mixture of CO and H2 as 
major components, along with trace amounts of CH4, CO2 and N2. The 
specific syngas composition varies with the type of feedstock and gasi­
fication conditions [28]. For instance, different feedstocks such as coal, 
lignite, peat and biomass exhibit H/C ratios ranging from 0 to 1.8 and 
O/C ratios ranging from 0 to 1.0, resulting in different LHVs for the 
produced syngas [29]. Syngas produced through air gasification typi­
cally has an LHV of about 5.0 MJ/Nm3, whereas oxygen gasification can 
produce syngas with an LHV of up to 12.0 MJ/Nm3. Additionally, the 
CO/H2 ratio can vary widely, ranging from 4:1 to 1:2. These composi­
tional variations require specialized burner design, particularly for 
H2-rich mixtures that may lead to flashback and combustion instability 
issues [30].
In addition to combustion stability, the distinct physicochemical 
properties of syngas significantly affect pollutant formation, especially 
nitrogen oxides (NOx) [31]. For example, coal-derived syngas often 
generates higher NOx emissions than natural gas under conventional 
combustion conditions [30], which highlights the need for tailored 
combustion strategies. Major GT OEMs, including General Electric, 
Siemens, and Mitsubishi Hitachi Power Systems, have already imple­
mented several commercial syngas IGCC powerplants. However, further 
optimization of syngas combustion remains pivotal for scalability [32]. 
Beyond power generation, coal- and biomass-derived syngas also serves 
as a precursor for H2 production and synthesis fuels, often referred to as 
“e-fuels”. Its integration with industrial and micro GTs, ICEs and in­
dustrial furnaces and boilers further highlights its versatility in 
advanced energy systems [33,34].
As illustrated and summarized in Fig. 1, syngas represents a prom­
ising energy carrier for driving clean and sustainable power systems 
through the conversion of coal and biomass into cleaner gaseous fuels, 
which can be directly utilized in GTs, ICEs, and industrial furnaces and 
boilers. Syngas CHP systems, such as IGCC and SOFC-based hybrid en­
ergy systems [35], offer pathways to significantly improve overall 
thermal efficiency. In addition, CCS as well as carbon recycling through 
photosynthesis and carbon reduction through eCatalytic or pCatalytic 
processes, have great potential for achieving deep CO2 reduction. 
Nevertheless, substantial research needs exist in syngas combustion 
from fundamentals to applications, largely due to the significant vari­
ability in syngas properties depending on feedstocks and synthesis 
methods. Moreover, comprehensive system-level assessments, including 
TEA and LCA, help evaluate the sustainability and feasibility of 
syngas-fueled CHP solutions.
While most previous reviews have focused on syngas production 
technologies [29,36–41] or basic combustion properties [31], this re­
view provides a broader and more integrated perspective, emphasizing 
the role of syngas as a key fuel in advanced hybrid energy systems aimed 
at high efficiency, low emissions and long-term sustainability.

Specifically, this work explores the entire spectrum of syngas combus­
tion from fundamental mechanisms to practical system-level applica­
tions, which is organized into three main sections. Section 2 discusses 
the fundamental combustion characteristics and reaction kinetics of 
syngas under varying conditions. Section 3 reviews recent experimental 
and numerical investigations on turbulent syngas flames, including 
advanced laser diagnostics and CFD simulations. Section 4 evaluates the 
use of syngas in practical systems such as GTs, ICEs, industrial furnaces, 
and CHP systems. This comprehensive review aims to guide future 
research and inform the development of syngas-based low-carbon en­
ergy technologies.
2. Fundamental combustion research
Understanding the fundamental combustion characteristics of syngas 
is crucial for its efficient application in ICEs, GTs, furnaces and boilers. 
Initial studies by Lee et al. [42] reviewed the LBVs and IDTs of syngas 
mixtures; this work further explores the latest studies on the funda­
mental combustion characteristics of pure syngas (H2/CO) and its blends 
with other gaseous fuels like CH4 or NH3, focusing on IDTs and LBVs. 
Additionally, it highlights advancements in kinetic models, emphasizing 
key elementary reactions, to improve combustion predictions and sup­
port the development of clean energy systems.
2.1. Fundamental combustion characteristics of syngas
2.1.1. Measurements of LBVs and IDTs
IDT and LBV are key parameters for evaluating fuel reactivity related 
to its fundamental combustion characteristics, which affect flame sta­
bility phenomena like blow-off and extinction. Accurate characteriza­
tion of these parameters is essential for designing reliable and efficient 
combustion devices like boilers and GTs [43]. The IDT and LBV mea­
surements of syngas and syngas/gaseous fuel mixtures can also provide 
insights into exothermicity, diffusivity and reactivity, and aid in the 
validation of kinetic models [44], which have been extensively studied 
under a wide range of pressure (Pu), temperature (Tu) and equivalence 
ratios (ϕ), as summarized in Table 1.
Experimental characterization of IDTs for syngas and syngas/ 
gaseous fuel mixtures has employed ST and RCM [45–62], while LBVs 
have been determined by BF, SPF, CSN, CTF, HFB and EHDC [84,
91–102]. Fig. 2 summarizes the operating condition ranges, with IDTs 
measured under pressure up to 30 MPa, and LBVs tested at Pu ≤5.0 MPa 
and Tu ≤700 K, respectively. However, LBV measurement remains very 
limited at high-pressure, normal temperature as well as normal pressure, 
high-temperature conditions, leaving a data gap in the upper-right re­
gion of Fig. 2(b). Additionally, the LBVs of syngas/ammonia mixtures 
are primarily measured at near-NTP conditions, highlighting the need 
for further experiments at elevated pressures and temperatures.
Discrepancies among different data sources remain another issue 
that requires attention. Fig. 3 compares LBVs for 50 %H2/50 %CO/air 
mixtures at NTP conditions (Pu = 1 atm, Tu = 298–303 K) and ϕ =
0.4–5.0. These values were obtained using multiple experimental 
methods, including SPF, BF, HFB and CTF. While the scatter in measured 
LBVs is relatively small on the lean side, it increases significantly under 
rich conditions, attributed to enhanced flame instability, localized heat 
losses and increased sensitivity to experimental conditions, such as 
buoyancy effects and local strain-induced variations.
2.1.2. Key factors influencing LBVs and IDTs
Several factors influence the IDTs and LBVs of syngas, including 
pressure, temperature, H2/CO ratio, diluents, and blending with other 
gaseous fuels. Fig. 4 summarizes the effects of these parameters as re­
ported in various studies. Overall, higher temperatures and H2/CO ratios 
enhance fuel reactivity of syngas, leading to decreased IDTs and 
increased LBVs, while elevated pressure and diluents ( H2O, N2, and 
CO2) tend to suppress reactivity. NH3 blending has little influence on 
IDTs but generally reduces LBVs, with the magnitude of the variations 
depending on syngas composition. These complex and sometimes

K´eromn`es et al. [49] for a (50 %H2/50 %CO)/O2/Ar mixture at ϕ = 0.5,

contrast, LBVs consistently decrease with increasing pressure, as

These findings reveal that both ignition and flame propagation behavior 
are affected by coupled thermal and chemical effects.

Increased CO content results in larger IDTs, highlighting higher reac­
tivity of H2 compared to CO. This is attributed to reduced H-atom 
availability and increased activation energy with more CO. Similarly, 
LBVs increase with H2/CO ratios due to faster H2 chemistry, though 
notable discrepancies between experimental and simulated results are 
reported at high H2/CO ratios [50,64–66,68,70,72,81,88].

2.2. Reaction kinetics in syngas combustion
2.2.1. Elementary reactions
Syngas combustion mechanisms are primarily composed of the H2 
and the CO sub-mechanisms. The reactivity of H2 is mainly controlled by 
the chain-branching reaction R1 and the pressure-dependent chain-in­
hibition reaction R2. R1 is critical for H2 combustion and other fuel 
oxidations at high temperatures (≥1000 K, depending on pressure) and 
has been extensively studied [103–105]. The “crossover” behavior of 
IDTs shown in Fig. 5 highlights the importance of these two reactions. 
Determining the rate constants of these reactions across a wide range of 
pressures and temperatures is thus crucial to predicting the fundamental 
combustion characteristics of syngas.

Reaction CO + OH = CO2 + H (R3) represents the oxidation of 
carbon monoxide (CO) by the hydroxyl radical (OH), which is an 
important reaction for the combustion of all hydrocarbon fuels and a key 
step in CO conversion in atmospheric chemistry. Studies have refined 
the rate constants through experiments and theoretical calculations, 
highlighting improved consistency across methods and updates in 
thermodynamic parameters. Wooldridge et al. [106] measured the rate 
constant for R3 using the ST, and Joshi and Wang [107] performed 
theoretical calculations at the CCSD(T)/cc-pvTZ level. Recent work by 
Wang et al. [108] improved rate constant determination for R3 based on 
simultaneous measurements of OH and CO time-histories in Ar-diluted 
CH2O/O2 oxidation, showing better agreement with previous studies, 
as shown in Fig. 8. Their results are consistent with the respective error 
bars, with minor differences attributed to updates in the formation 
enthalpy of OH.
Reaction CO + HO2 = CO2 + OH (R4) is particularly significant 
under high-pressure conditions. Various theoretical studies [65,
109–111] and experimental measurements [112–114] have been con­
ducted on R4. For instance, Allen et al. [109] used ab initio CISD cal­
culations based on unrestricted Hartree-Fock optimized geometries, 
while Sun et al. [65] employed MP2(full)/6-31G(d,p) geometry opti­
mization and transition state theory. A comparison of rate constants is 
presented in Fig. 9, where the results from Li et al. [111] show good 
agreement with those from You et al. [110] and Sun et al. [65], though 
lower than experimental data [112–114] by a factor of 3–211.
Reaction HCO + O2 = CO + HO2 (R5) plays an important role at 
lower temperatures and lean conditions. The rate constant of R5 exhibits 
a clear temperature dependence, with several experimental and theo­
retical studies [115–120] conducted on it. The results are compared in 
Fig. 10, showing a weak positive temperature dependence. Notably, the 
GRI-Mech. 3.0 model [121] underestimates the temperature depen­
dence, while Hsu et al. [117] found larger discrepancies at higher 
temperatures (≥1500 K).
2.2.2. Kinetic modeling
Several kinetic models have been developed to simulate syngas 
combustion, with some specifically for syngas [49,65,69,122–126] and 
others for hydrocarbons and oxygenates [121,127–129] incorporating 
syngas as a sub-mechanism. Notable models include the GRI 3.0 model 
[121], USC Mech II model [128], San Diego model [127] and NUIG 
model [129]. In addition to those general models, several kinetic models 
have been tailored for syngas combustion, as summarized in Table 2. 
Other kinetic models, such as one by K´eromn`es et al. [49] with 15 
species and 49 reactions, were specifically validated under 
high-pressure conditions (up to 70 atm), while Goswami et al. [69] 
updated the syngas kinetic model to address the stringent requirements 
of combustion systems like GTs. A comparative analysis by Jithin et al. 
[31] of the performance of those kinetic models [121,122,124–126,128]

3. Combustion diagnostics and turbulent combustion research
This section focuses on experimental and numerical investigations 
into turbulent flames of syngas, as well as syngas/low-carbon fuel 
blends. Advanced laser diagnostics and CFD tools are extensively uti­
lized in these studies.
3.1. Optical measurements of turbulent flames
3.1.1. Conical flames
Conical flames stabilized in BF burners are widely employed to 
evaluate the laminar flame speed (SL) and turbulent flame speed (ST) for 
syngas/air mixtures [68,131–134] based on optical measurements of 
flame topologies. Fig. 13 compares time-averaged PIV and OH-PLIF 
images for methane/air and syngas/air flames [135]. Methane flames 
exhibit a straight conical shape, whereas syngas flames feature a 
smoother top due to flame front wrinkling. Further investigations into 
the curved flame front structures of syngas flames under ambient and 
elevated pressures up to 1.0 MPa were conducted by Zhang et al. [136,
137], revealing pressure-dependent flame topologies. Moreover, Wang 
et al. [138] combined OH-PLIF imaging and CFD simulations to analyze 
flame structure, reaction rates and the critical conditions for tip opening, 
a phenomenon where the flame tip extinguishes due to excessive 
negative curvature, as shown in Fig. 14. It is demonstrated that tip 
opening occurs when the local Karlovitz number (Ka) reaches around 4, 
independent of H2 content or flow velocity. This universal threshold 
shows the local Ka as a good indicator for predicting the local extinction 
of negatively stretched syngas flames.
3.1.2. Swirl flames
GTMCs feature diverse geometries, including single- and dual-swirl 
burners. Representative designs include low-swirl injectors [139], 
single-swirl configurations (e.g., TECFLAM [140], TURBOMECA 
[141–145], PRECCINSTA [146,147]) and dual-swirl burners [148,149], 
as shown in Fig. 15. To investigate the flame dynamics of swirl flames, 
flame front behavior and large-scale vortex interactions affecting heat 
and mass transfer, simultaneous PIV/PLIF measurements are extensively 
utilized [150–156]. Chemiluminescence imaging, such as OH* chem­
iluminescence, is further used to resolve heat release oscillations [157]. 
Studies in swirl-stabilized flames demonstrate the impacts of fuel com­
positions and swirl intensity on velocity fields and flame shapes, as 
quantified via PIV, OH-PLIF and OH* chemiluminescence imaging 
[158–163]. For instance, Dam et al. [161] and Sayad et al. [162] 
employed PIV to analyze flashback and combustion-induced vortex 
breakdown in fully premixed syngas/air flames. Lee et al. [164,165] 
investigated thermoacoustic instabilities and emissions in partially 
premixed syngas/air flames, revealing correlations between fuel 
composition, flame shape and pollutant formation. Choi et al. [166] 
validated RANS simulations of premixed syngas/air dual-swirl flames 
using PIV results, showing reasonable agreement for velocity profiles 
but significant discrepancies in temperature profiles.
Despite these advances, syngas-related studies remain limited in 
GTMCs like TURBOMECA, PRECCINSTA and DLR dual-swirl burners 
[157,168,169]. Notably, Dulin et al. [169] conducted PIV and OH-PLIF 
measurements for methane and syngas flames under various pressures 
and temperatures, observing that syngas flames exhibit enhanced up­
stream penetration due to higher flame speeds and increased flashback 
susceptibility under elevated conditions, as shown in Fig. 16. Advanced 
laser diagnostics have also enabled detailed analysis of turbulent flame 
front wrinkling in syngas swirl flames. A single-swirl burner developed 
at the University of Twente [170] features an annular jet fuel supply

3.2.2. Turbulent flame structure
Understanding turbulent flame structure is crucial for accurate 
combustion modeling and combustor design. Syngas, with different 
features concerning LBV, mass diffusivity and Le compared to methane, 
exhibits distinct TCI [190]. Advances in laser diagnostic techniques, 
particularly PLIF, have significantly contributed to exploring these in­
teractions [136,137,190–192].
Non-premixed flames. Boyette et al. [191] investigated the effects of 
pressure and Re on the structure of turbulent non-premixed syngas 
flames. It was observed that the OH layer thickness decreases mono­
tonically with pressure, as local extinction is suppressed at increased 
pressures. However, flame front corrugations are highly sensitive to Re 
but relatively insensitive to pressure.
Premixed flames. Wang et al. [190] investigated Bunsen-type tur­
bulent premixed syngas (45.5 %CO/24.5 %H2/30 %CO2) using OH-PLIF 
at 0.5 MPa, as shown in Fig. 21. Based on the accumulated distribution 
function (ADF) of curvature radius shown in Fig. 22, syngas flames 
feature smaller fine flame cusps and more pronounced wrinkling due to 
intrinsic instabilities compared to methane flames. The wrinkling scale 
is influenced by turbulence, while the smallest wrinkles are governed by 
flame instability. Further studies by Wang et al. [192] and Zhang et al. 
[136] highlight that syngas flames exhibit higher flame surface density 
than methane flames, largely independent of pressure and H2/CO ratio 
when H2 content exceeds 50 %. These finer flame structures arise from 
increased flame instability at high H2 content.
3.2.3. Turbulent flame modeling
Recent advancements in syngas combustion modeling have 
improved predictions of flame behavior and pollutant formation in both 
non-premixed and premixed syngas/air flames. PDE is a crucial factor in 
hydrogen-rich flames and emphasizes the need for advanced models to 
capture species-specific diffusion behaviors.
Modeling of non-premixed flames. Cuoci et al. [193] evaluated 
EDM, EDC and SLF models for turbulent non-premixed coaxial jet 
flames. The EDC model was found to show the best prediction, with CFD 
post-processing accurately predicting the pollutant formation. Safer 
et al. [194] used the SLF model and Eulerian Particle Flamelet model to 
study NO emissions in non-premixed counter-flow syngas flames, 
finding that H2 enrichment reduces thermal NO formation, while higher 
pressure promotes it via the Zel’dovich mechanism. Fukumoto et al. 
[195] employ an extended EDC model within an LES framework to 
simulate a syngas jet flame, explicitly incorporating preferential diffu­
sion and finite-rate chemistry to better capture TCI. The results show 
that non-unity Le effects, particularly the high diffusivity of H2, signif­
icantly influence flame structure and species distribution.

combustion critically influences flame dynamics and stability. These 
effects, driven by disparities in molecular diffusion rates, alter local 
mixture states and flame structures, particularly in fuels like syngas. In 
hydrogen-dominated autoignition, PDE induces temporal scale shifts, 
which conventional flamelet models fail to capture [198]. In 
non-premixed flames, PDE leads to leaner equivalence ratio distribu­

predictions [199]. The magnitude of PDE is characterized by Le, a key 
parameter in species-specific diffusion behavior [200]. To address the 
limitations of traditional models assuming uniform Le [201], advanced 
approaches have emerged. The Igniting Mixing Layer flamelet model 
improves hydrogen autoignition predictions by refining transport 
equations and mixture stratification [198]. As compared in Fig. 23, the 
FGM model with consideration of PDE (FGM-PD), incorporating 
non-uniform Le, reduces laminar flame speed errors to 0.6 % and re­

Future work should extend PDE-integrated models to multifuel sce­

applications [202,203].
Analysis of turbulence-chemistry interactions. Wehrmann et al. 
[189] analyzed TCI by comparing turbulent flame speeds of pure 
hydrogen and syngas flames. The turbulent flame speed, normalized by 
the respective laminar value, evolves differently under varying condi­
tions. At constant flow fields and H2 content, syngas flames exhibit 
stronger flame-flow interaction but a smaller increase in TBV, indicating 
weaker thermo-diffusive effects. At a constant equivalence ratio, TBV 
decreases with increasing CO content. Under identical flow fields, the 
interaction is similar for both fuels, yet syngas shows a much lower TBV 
than pure hydrogen, while CO substitution slightly increases it.
4. Combustion techniques for applications
This section presents the advancements and challenges in syngas 
combustion across practical applications, including GTs, furnaces and 
reciprocating engines. Key issues include pollutant formation, flame 
stabilization and operational efficiency.
4.1. Gas turbine
GTs, widely applied in industrial power generation, are facing 
pressure to reduce CO2 emissions. Syngas, a viable alternative to con­
ventional fossil fuels, offers CO2 reduction potential but introduces 
complexities due to its composition, particularly the H2 involved. The 
high reactivity, rapid burning velocity and low ignition delay of H2 
significantly influence GT performance, raising concerns such as flash­
back [204] and thermoacoustic instabilities [169]. Research has focused 
on addressing these challenges and optimizing GT operation for syngas 
combustion.

4.1.1. Heavy-duty gas turbine
The use of syngas as an alternative fuel in heavy-duty GTs faces 
challenges due to the sensitivity of its fundamental combustion char­
acteristics to its composition. Besides, turbulent combustion is crucial to 
fuel/air mixing and enhances heat release, but the mechanical 
complexity of flow and chemistry poses challenges for numerical 
simulation and combustor optimization. This highlights the challenge of 
designing efficient and low-emission GT combustors.
Specialized software packages, such as GateCycle software, have 
been used to model heavy-duty GT performance under various syngas 
compositions [205,206]. Kim et al. [207] demonstrated that increasing 
turbine mass flow improves the power output and efficiency of an F-class 
commercial GT fueled by syngas, but blade overheating and compressor 
surge risks arise. He et al. [208] found that compressor air bleeding 
significantly enhances operation with low-calorific syngas.
Analytical models proposed by Chacartegui et al. [209,210] are used 
to study the combustion dynamics under off-design conditions for a 
syngas-fueled heavy-duty GT combustor, emphasizing the need for 
burner optimization to maintain stable, efficient and low-emission op­
erations. Risks of flame flashback and auto-ignition upstream of the 
premixing zone remain critical challenges to reliability and efficiency 
[211,212].
4.1.2. Micro gas turbine
Studies have highlighted the feasibility of using syngas in μGTs, with 
promising stability enhancement and emission reduction. Delattin et al. 
[213] tested a pressurized, slightly modified μGT combustor by 
gradually switching the fuel feed from 100 % natural gas to 100 % 
low-calorific syngas. Key performance parameters, including tempera­
ture profiles, pressure, emissions and flame imaging, were monitored 
and showed no issues regarding auto-ignition or dynamic/static com­
bustion instability. During the process, the flame anchoring position 
remained stable, and the NOx emission was reduced to 5 ppm due to the 
reduced temperature. Corrˆea et al. [214] studied the effect of syngas as a 
supplement to natural gas in a μGT system (Fig. 24) fueled by syngas 
derived from rice hull gasification, rich in CO2 and N2. When switching 
to a 50 % natural gas/50 % syngas blend, the system efficiency drops by 
about 13 %. However, turbine outlet temperatures remain steady across 
varying fuel compositions. Unlike the results from Delattin et al. [213], 
their findings presented increased NOx emission at low power outputs 
and higher CO emission at all conditions, emphasizing the impact of 
syngas on emission performance. Ashini et al. [215] experimentally and 
numerically compared the performance of natural gas, biogas, and 
syngas/biogas mixtures. Compared to biogas and natural gas, bio­
gas/syngas mixtures produced lower CO emissions but higher NO 
emissions than biogas (shown in Fig. 25). These studies demonstrate that 
while syngas can reduce overall emissions and maintain combustion 
stability, its integration in μGT systems requires addressing efficiency 
trade-offs and emission variances depending on power output and fuel 
composition.

4.2.2. Dual-fuel CI engine
CI engines, commonly referred to as diesel engines, operate by 
compressing air to a high pressure and temperature, which ignites the 
fuel upon injection. CI engine is widely used in heavy-duty vehicles, 
ships and industrial machinery due to its high thermal efficiency and 
fuel economy. However, traditional CI engines’ reliance on diesel leads 
to high NOx emissions. A promising mitigation strategy involves using 
syngas as supplementary fuel in CI engines, forming dual-fuel CI systems 
[216]. Many studies have explored the performance and emissions of 
diesel/syngas dual-fuel combustion.
Azimov et al. [221] investigated a pilot-ignited, supercharged, 
dual-fuel CI engine fueled by different types of syngas. This engine 
employed a PREMIER combustion mode (Fig. 27), a hybrid of SI and CI 
combustion that combines spark-assisted and spark-induced compres­
sion ignition. Increased H2 content in syngas leads to shorter combustion 
durations, elevated combustion temperatures and efficiency, and 
reduced CO and HC emissions, but increased NOx emissions. Ali et al. 
[222] explored very lean combustion in a syngas/diesel dual-fuel engine 
using numerical simulations. Diesel combustion predominantly occurs 
in premixed combustion mode. For syngas with high H2 content, com­
bustion is located in the piston bowl and squish region near the top dead 
center. For syngas with low H2 content, delayed flame propagation to­
wards the central region of the piston leads to unburned syngas emis­
sions, highlighting the influence of H2 content on combustion dynamics.
RCCI represents an advanced dual-fuel CI strategy for better com­
bustion control. In RCCI mode, the pilot fuel is injected early during the 
compression stroke [216]. Olanrewaju et al. [223] analyzed HRR and 
combustion phasing for a syngas/diesel RCCI engine. Higher syngas 
fraction increases ignition delays, delaying critical parameters such as 
the start of combustion, 50 % mass fraction burned, peak pressure and 
peak release rate. Overall, dual-fuel operation with syngas leads to 
reduced peak pressure and peak temperature compared to baseline 
diesel fuel.
4.2.3. HCCI engine
HCCI engines achieve combustion by compressing a uniform air-fuel 
mixture until auto-ignition occurs. By operating at lower temperatures, 
reduced NOx formation and enhanced combustion efficiency can be 
achieved [224]. However, challenges such as limited operating ranges, 
combustion control and high emissions of unburned products [225].
Bika et al. [226] tested multiple compositions of syngas in an HCCI 
engine under various intake temperatures. Increased CO fraction results 
in higher auto-ignition temperatures, requiring increased intake tem­
perature to maintain optimal conditions. Combustion efficiency, ranging 
between 83 % and 88 %, remains much lower than conventional CI and 
SI engines. This may be attributed to the inherently low combustion 
temperature of the HCCI engine, leading to more unburned fuel/air 
mixtures. Przybyla et al. [227] compared HCCI and SI engines fueled 
with identical syngas. The HCCI engine exhibits higher peak cylinder 
pressures but lower peak temperatures (Fig. 28), resulting in high CO 
emissions and lower indicated efficiency compared to the SI engine.
Efforts to improve HCCI operation have also extended into numerical 
simulations. Starik et al. [228] studied the effects of singlet delta oxygen 
and ozone on HCCI combustion of syngas, revealing that 1 % singlet 
delta oxygen or 0.38 % ozone addition enhances ignition intensity and 
combustion efficiency by up to 14 % while reducing CO and NO emis­
sions by 15 %. Yamasaki and Kaneko [229] experimentally and 
numerically 
studied 
the 
combustion 
characteristics 
in 
mock 
syngas-fueled HCCI engines, using Chemkin-Pro for reaction analysis.

content decreases faster than CO during ignition but requires more time 
for full oxidation. Combustion characteristics, including timing and 
duration, are proportional to the volumetric H2/CO ratios.
4.3. Industrial furnace and boiler
Industrial furnace, also known as direct heaters, plays a critical role 
in industrial processes like metal smelting, glass production and ce­
ramics firing. These systems increasingly incorporate advanced controls 
and heat exchangers to enhance energy efficiency and reduce emissions. 
The substitution of fossil fuels with syngas has emerged as a feasible way 
to reduce the carbon footprint of furnaces [230]. Cofiring syngas with 
natural gas has demonstrated significant potential for reducing emis­
sions and improving thermal efficiency [231].
4.3.1. Pure syngas combustion and cofiring
Habib et al. [232] studied methane cofiring with syngas in a 200 MW 
two-burner package boiler. The study found that increasing the H2 
content of syngas raises the boiler outlet temperature. However, the 
optimal emissions performance was observed at a CO/H2 ratio of 50:50, 
where thermal NO emissions are minimized and prompt NO levels are 
moderate. An increase in excess air from 5 % to 25 % effectively reduces 
CO and NOx emissions by 30 %, making the CO/H2 ratio of 50:50 ideal 
for emission control and equipment longevity.

4.3.2. Flameless combustion
Syngas combustion, particularly H2-rich blends, poses challenges 
like flashback and early autoignition due to the high reactivity of H2 
[234,235]. Flameless combustion offers a solution by using high exhaust 
gas and heat recirculation to suppress thermal NOx emissions and 
recover energy from high-temperature exhausts in furnaces [236]. Yepes 
et al. [234] studied the mixture of 30 % syngas and 70 % natural gas 
(SG-NG) in a semi-industrial regenerative furnace originally designed 
for pure natural gas. CFD modeling showed that syngas addition reduces 
furnace temperature due to increased recirculation and CO2 in flue 
gases. NOx emissions decreased by almost 50 %, attributed to reduced 
contributions from thermal, N2O and NNH formation pathways. The 
syngas addition also increases reactivity and shifts the reaction zone 
upstream. Kim et al. [237] examined CH4/H2/CO mixtures for flameless 
combustion under oxygen-enriched conditions, analyzing emissions and 
temperature profiles in a furnace. Stable flameless combustion was 
achieved when H2 or CO was added to CH4. While NO emissions increase 
with H2 addition, the dominant formation pathways shift, with the N2O 
intermediate route playing a central role under N2 dilution and H2 
enrichment.
4.4. Combined heat and power systems
4.4.1. IGCC powerplant
IGCC technology integrates coal or biomass gasification with 
combined-cycle power generation, offering a clean and efficient 
pathway for converting fossil fuels and biomass into electricity. How­
ever, applying commercial GTs in IGCC powerplants often deviates from 
their original design due to the distinct lower volumetric heat value of 
syngas. This requires a higher mass flow into the combustor, compli­
cating turbine operation and reducing performance potential under 
certain conditions [207]. Moreover, syngas composition impacts IGCC 
system design and operational parameters, prompting numerous studies 
on these challenges.

Numerical studies. Kim et al. [207] conducted simulations on GT 
performance in IGCC systems using syngas with varying heating values. 
It was observed that a lower heating value increases turbine mass flow, 
enhancing power output and efficiency. However, these conditions in­
crease the risks of compressor surge and blade overheating, requiring 
adjustments like reducing combustion temperatures and introducing 
nitrogen flow. Follow-up studies from Kim et al. [238] highlighted that 
optimizing the system integration degree improves performance but 
raises metal temperatures and reduces surge margins. He et al. [208] 
modeled the performance of a part-load syngas-fueled GT, revealing that 
air bleeding from the compressor can alleviate blade cooling challenges 
and surge margin issues, particularly for low calorific syngas.
Experimental studies. Experiments on GT model combustors and 
full-scale GTs validate syngas application in IGCC. Lee et al. [30] 
compared syngas combustion characteristics from the Taean IGCC plant 
(Korea) and the Buggenum IGCC plant (Netherlands) using scale-down 
combustors of GE7EA. Differences in syngas composition influenced 
flame stability and emissions, with the Taean syngas (lower H2/CO 
ratio) producing higher NOx but lower CO emissions than the Buggenum 
syngas. Samiran et al. [204] found that moderate CO-enriched syngas 
(CO/H2 = 1.2) offers superior flame stability and lower NOx emissions 
compared to CO-enriched syngas (CO/H2 = 3). Park and Lee [239] 
investigated combustion instabilities of H2/CO/CH4 flames in a 
scale-downed industrial GT combustor that features a partially premixed 
swirl combustion mode. Their findings revealed that H2 contents are a 
key driver of high-order instability modes, as the rapid combustion of H2 
affects the chemical time and flame temperature compared to CO and 
CH4. For full-scale GT validation, Park et al. [240] conducted compre­
hensive combustion tests in a syngas test facility, as shown in Fig. 30. 
These tests evaluated ignition, load transitions and fuel changeovers, 
demonstrating reliable performance across various syngas compositions 
and calorific values. Nearly 100 % combustion efficiency was achieved 
under different fuel/air ratios, though CO emissions slightly increase at 
higher loads due to thermal dissociation of CO2. These studies demon­
strate the adaptability and potential of syngas for stable, efficient 
operation in IGCC powerplant systems.
4.4.2. SOFC-based CHP systems
As summarized by Radenahmad et al. [35], integrating biomass 
gasification with SOFCs provides a promising route toward sustainable 
CHP systems. Studies have shown that biomass-derived syngas can 
enable SOFC-based CHP systems with high exergy and energy effi­
ciencies [241–243]. SOFCs demonstrate good fuel flexibility, accom­
modating syngas from invasive biomass sources and thus offering both 
renewable energy and ecological benefits. While natural gas-based 
micro-CHP systems have already been commercialized in Europe and 
Japan [244], biomass-fed SOFC systems remain largely at pilot 
demonstration stages [245]. Besides, the practical deployment is con­
strained by challenges in system integration and durability.
The key technical barriers are SOFC material robustness and the 
compatibility between gasifier output and fuel cell operation. Although 
conventional Ni/YSZ anodes offer high catalytic activity, they are 
vulnerable to sulfur poisoning and carbon deposition [246]. Alternatives 
such as sulfur-tolerant Ni/GDC [247] and Ni-free perovskites like 
LaSrCrRuO3 [248] show potential but require scalable manufacturing 
processes. Gas cleaning also remains a critical issue, i.e., cold gas 
cleaning reduces overall efficiency [249], while hot cleaning demands 
advanced high-temperature filtration technologies [250]. Additionally, 
the variability of biomass feedstock in terms of moisture, ash, and sulfur 
content further increases system complexity [251]. Hybrid systems 
coupling SOFCs with GTs can increase electrical efficiency from about 
60 % to 80 % [245], though precise thermal management is needed. 
Intermediate-temperature SOFCs (400–700 ◦C) offer improved opera­
tional flexibility but introduce new material challenges related to 
durability and performance at lower temperatures [252].
The market potential of biomass-SOFC CHP systems depends on 
advances in TRLs of each part [253], as demonstrated in Fig. 31. Their 
high efficiency and carbon-neutral operation position them for rural and 
off-grid applications [254]. Micro-CHP units (<50 kWe) could disrupt 
conventional grids, though commercialization is hindered by high cap­
ital and material costs [255]. Utilizing invasive biomass aligns with 
circular economy principles, while advances in modular gasification and 
SOFC manufacturing [254,256] are essential for broader adoption.

that integrating gasification processes such as fluidized bed boilers in 
existing CHP plants can improve efficiency and capacity but often leads 
to higher operating costs compared with natural gas, making the Lev­
elized Cost of Electricity less competitive without strategic optimization 
[257,258]. LCA studies emphasize that feedstock choice and energy

three biomass CHP systems (100 kWth–10 MWth) using bubbling fluid­

analysis, showed that larger-scale plants (e.g., 10 MWth) perform best 
economically due to economies of scale. However, feasibility remains 
sensitive to feedstock logistics and policy incentives such as carbon 
pricing. Overall, integrated TEA-LCA approaches are essential to achieve 
both cost competitiveness through syngas valorization and meaningful 
emission reductions of up to ~50 % compared with fossil fuels.
5. Concluding remarks and prospects
Syngas, derived from coal and biomass gasification, presents a ver­
satile and sustainable energy carrier with significant potential to reduce 
environmental impact while enabling efficient power generation and 
hydrogen and liquid fuel production. This review systematically exam­
ines syngas combustion from fundamental mechanisms to practical ap­
plications, highlighting its role in transitioning toward carbon-neutral 
energy systems. The key findings and challenges are summarized below.

(1) Fundamental combustion characteristics. Syngas combustion is 
strongly influenced by the H2/CO ratio, diluents and operating 
conditions. IDTs and LBVs depend sensitively on temperature, 
pressure and fuel composition. Kinetic models, such as those by 
K´eromn`es et al. [49] and Frassoldati et al. [123], can effectively 
predict major trends but require refinement for fuel-rich condi­
tions and complex blends (e.g., syngas/CH4/NH3). Future 
modeling must integrate multi-component fuel dynamics and 
high-pressure kinetics to improve predictive accuracy under 
practical conditions.
(2) Turbulent flame behavior and diagnostics. Laser-based diagnostics, 
such as OH-PLIF and PIV, coupled with CFD simulations, have 
provided valuable insights into turbulent flame structures and 
TCI. Swirl-stabilized and Bunsen-type flames reveal that H2 
diffusivity enhances TBVs but increases susceptibility to flash­
back and thermoacoustic instabilities. Discrepancies in temper­
ature predictions highlight the need for improved subgrid 
models. Continued development of diagnostic and simulation 
tools is crucial for optimizing combustor designs, particularly for 
syngas blends with low calorific value or high inert content.
(3) Applications in energy systems. In GTs, syngas facilitates low- 
emission operations through premixed and diffusion combus­
tion techniques, though flame instability and NOx formation 
remain challenges. In IC engines, dual-fuel and HCCI strategies 
improve efficiency and reduce carbon emissions but face con­
straints in ignition control and operational flexibility. Industrial 
furnaces and boilers benefit significantly from syngas through 
improved thermal efficiency and reduced NOx emissions, espe­
cially in technologies like cofiring and flameless combustion. 
Hybrid systems, including IGCC and SOFC-based CHP, particu­
larly when integrated with biomass gasification, show promising 
potential for efficiency promotion and CO2 reduction.
Looking forward, the contribution of syngas to global decarbon­
ization will depend on advances in renewable-powered gasification, 
hybrid energy integration and intelligent combustion control. Future 
directions and opportunities include. 
(1) Renewable syngas and fuel flexibility. Biomass- and waste-derived 
syngas can close the carbon cycle, while cofiring with e-fuels 
like ammonia or hydrogen can enhance energy density and flame 
stability. However, such blends may risk high NOx emissions 
through combined thermal- and fuel-NOx pathways [143,264], 
as well as thermoacoustic instabilities due to fuel reactivity var­
iations [145]. Future studies should focus on simultaneous con­
trol of combustion stability and pollutants, particularly by 
understanding how fuel reactivity shapes turbulent flame 
dynamics.
(2) AI-enabled combustion intelligence. Emerging approaches such as 
AI-driven kinetic modeling [265], spatio-temporal data recon­
struction [266] and advanced diagnostics will benefit syngas 
research. By integrating AI with multi-scale simulations and 
real-time sensing, adaptive combustion systems can be developed 
to optimize real-time performance and accelerate the develop­
ment of cleaner and more efficient energy conversion 
technologies.
(3) System-level analysis and perspectives. Integrated LCA and TEA are 
important to guide the future deployment of syngas technologies. 
These tools can identify the most sustainable feedstocks, quantify 
climate benefits and support investment under evolving carbon 
policies. Systematic evaluations will ensure that syngas-based 
hybrid systems achieve both economic competitiveness and 
deep decarbonization benefits.
Overall, collaborative efforts between academia and industry are 
essential to standardize the syngas production process, validate kinetic 
models under extreme conditions and deploy pilot-scale systems for 
carbon capture and utilization. By addressing these priorities, syngas 
can solidify its role as a cornerstone of sustainable energy infrastructure.
