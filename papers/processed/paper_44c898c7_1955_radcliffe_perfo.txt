INTRODUCTION 
Fuel is frequently injected into gas turbines through swirl jets. 
The principles of operation of all swirl jets are the same, but the 
variation of performance from one design to another is such that 
it is usually difficult for those not actively engaged in the 
manufacture of a particular type of jet to estimate the per- 
formance. 
Fig. l a  shows one arrangement of an atomizer. It consists of 
a main body and a plug. There are flats on the side of the 
atomizer so arranged that when the body is screwed into a fuel 
delivery line, the fuel has unrestricted access to each of the 
tangential ports, Fig. l b  shows another arrangement of the 
atomizer. In this instance the orifice plate and back plug make 
a push fit with the swirl sleeve. The three pieces when assembled 
fit into a body so arranged that fuel has access to the two swirl 
ports. The essential details of the atomizer are shown in Fig. lc. 
In brief, the arrangement is such that fuel enters the swirl 
chamber through two ports of diameter, p .  Each port admits fuel 
into the swirl chamber which may be regarded as a very short 
cylinder of diameter, D, and length, b, leading to a cone of 
included angle, 4. The fuel flows in a vortex in the chamber 
leaving through the final orifice of diameter, d. The flow 
orifice as one or more filaments of fuel. The atomization in such 
instances is poor.

CONCLUSION 
It is thus shown that the Power Jets type of atomizer can be 
designed to give the appropriate output for any fuel of low 
enough viscosity (circa 20 centipoises) and that the effect of 
varying the atomizer shape can be predicted. Similarly the 
atomizer spray particle size may be estimated when it is known 
that the air into which the fuel is sprayed has a pressure of 
1 atm. 
Further work on the effect of the combustion air pressure on 
atomization and work on the atomization by spill atomizers, 
especially for fuels of relatively high viscosity, will also be 
valuable.

The correlation of liquid properties with discharge had not 
been related to atomizers of ‘non-standard’ dimensions, and 
equation (2), which gave the correlation with design parameters, 
was of limited use since their interactions were not considered. 
In the ‘standard’ atomizer the spray angles were not given for 
the various flows through the atomizer. It was, therefore, difficult 
to ascertain the general effect of both liquid properties and 
atomizer dimensions. Theories of flow defined the theoretical 
spray angle as that at which the tangent to the curving conical 
liquid sheet attained its maximum value (Fig. 27). The author, 
and many other investigators, measured an apparent angle which 
was jnfluenced by the properties of the spray curtain, such as 
the diffuse boundary or the spatial distribution of the drops, 
instead of the theoretical angle. Such measurements were 
dependent on the ambient atmosphere and could be compared 
only under similar conditions. They also gave rise to anomalous 
results when compared with ideal theory. Thus, according to 
ideal theory, the spray angle decreased with increasing p2/2rd, 
and Figs. 11 and 12 were in agreement with that, but Fig. 13 
was at variance. That discrepancy could be explained by the 
reduced effect of air resistance with the nozzles of higher flow 
number, where the reduction in the sheet cone angle was more 
than counterbalanced by the increased momentum of the drops 
so that the apparent flow angle would increase. 
The author had stated that equation (5) for the drop size, 
which was based on Needham’s results (1946), should hold for

cone angle. Since the cone angle affected the drop size, and the 
author’s results showed a considerable variation in cone angle, 
the application of equation (5) could be an approximation only, 
and particularly so at low pressures.
