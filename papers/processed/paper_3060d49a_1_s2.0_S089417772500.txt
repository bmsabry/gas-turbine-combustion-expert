A B S T R A C T
Swirl combustion is a fundamental feature in modern energy conversion systems, particularly in gas turbines and 
aeroengines, where it enhances flame stabilization, mixing efficiency, and emissions control. The global shift 
toward low- and zero-carbon fuels, such as ammonia and hydrogen, introduces new challenges in swirl com­
bustion environments, including altered flow dynamics, temperature distributions, and chemical reaction 
pathways. In this context, laser-based diagnostic techniques have undergone significant advances, enabling non- 
intrusive, high-resolution, time-resolved measurements of velocity fields, scalar distributions, and reaction zone 
structures in swirl-stabilized flames. This review summarizes recent progress in major laser diagnostic methods, 
including high-speed particle image velocimetry, planar laser-induced fluorescence, laser absorption spectros­
copy, Rayleigh scattering, and Raman scattering based techniques. Developments in multiphysics and hybrid 
techniques are also discussed. Additionally, we emphasizes representative laser diagnostic advancements driven 
by high-temperature and low-carbon combustion technologies. The co-evolution of laser technology, diagnostic 
methods, and swirl combustion is discussed to show how advances in each area have accelerated progress in the 
others. Finally, the review outlines remaining scientific challenges, practical barriers to industrial application, 
and future research priorities, with emphasis on diagnostics for extreme conditions, AI-driven data analysis, and 
compact in-situ measurement solutions for next-generation swirl combustion systems.
1. Introduction
Swirling flames play a pivotal role in both fundamental combustion 
research and a variety of industrial applications. Their ability to enhance 
fuel–air mixing and stabilize combustion makes them essential in sectors 
such as power generation and aero-propulsion. The introduction of swirl 
generates complex flow structures, most notably recirculation zones, 
vortex cores and helical vortices, as illustrated in Fig. 1(a). These flow 
features, together with their interaction with the flame, are visualized 
through diverse cases, including the technically premixed methane–air 
[1], attached hydrogen [2], and high-pressure ammonia–air swirling 
flames [3] in Fig. 1. Such structures promote the entrainment and 
mixing of hot gases and reactive radicals with incoming fresh mixtures, 
thereby sustaining continuous ignition and stabilizing the flame [4,5]. 
These features are critical for achieving high thermal efficiency and low 
emissions in advanced combustion systems [6].
To meet increasing demands for performance and environmental 
compliance, combustion technologies in gas turbines and aeroengines 
are continually evolving. A central objective is to achieve higher com­
bustion temperatures and pressures to improve overall engine effi­
ciency. However, elevated temperatures also intensify the formation of 
nitrogen oxides (NOx), posing a significant environmental challenge. To 
mitigate NOx emissions while maintaining performance, modern sys­
tems increasingly adopt low-emission strategies, particularly lean- 
premixed combustion. This approach operates at low equivalence ra­
tios to suppress pollutant formation.
Several notable lean-premixed combustion technologies have 
emerged in this context, including dry low NOx (DLN) combustors, lean 
premixed prevaporized (LPP) systems, twin annular premixing swirler 
(TAPS) technology, and lean direct injection (LDI). DLN systems use 
staged fuel injection with precise air–fuel ratio control across multiple 
zones, employing advanced swirlers for flame stabilization [8–10]. LPP 
systems utilize engineered swirling flows to form distinct recirculation 
zones (primary, corner, and lip) that enhance mixing, improve flame 
stability, and reduce emissions [11–13]. TAPS technology features dual 
concentric swirlers operating at different equivalence ratios, a central

residence time to reduce flashback risk while still enabling low-emission 
combustion. Studies across these systems demonstrate that optimized 
fuel injection and controlled swirling flows significantly shape flame

Despite these advancements, lean-premixed flames are inherently 
more susceptible to thermoacoustic instabilities, self-excited oscillations 
driven by the interaction between heat release and pressure waves 
[5,20]. These oscillations, often resonant in nature, remain a focal point 
of combustion research due to their potential to cause structural damage 
and operational instability. Advanced diagnostic tools are essential to 
characterize and mitigate these dynamic phenomena, with laser-based 
techniques proving particularly effective in capturing the high-speed, 
complex behavior of swirling flames [21,22].
Another critical trajectory in combustion research is the integration 
of low-carbon and carbon-free fuels to address global decarbonization 
goals. Given the urgent need to reduce carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions 
from turbomachinery, several strategies are being explored, including 
the adoption of sustainable aviation fuels, development of zero-carbon 
fuel propulsion systems, improvements in thermal efficiency, and 
deployment of carbon capture, utilization, and storage technologies 
[23]. Among these approaches, carbon-free fuels, such as hydrogen (H2) 
and ammonia (NH3), present the most transformative potential for long- 
term combustion technology. These “novel” fuels exhibit significantly 
different physical and chemical properties compared to conventional 
hydrocarbons (e.g., natural gas, kerosene), resulting in unique com­
bustion behaviors under swirling flow conditions.
Hydrogen, for instance, is gaining significant attention due to its high 
reactivity and clean combustion profile. With a laminar flame speed of 
up to 3.51 m/s, approximately seven times that of methane (CH4), and a 
wide flammability range (4–75 % by volume), hydrogen enables stable 
and rapid combustion [24]. However, its low volumetric density and 
strong propensity for flashback, much higher than natural gas, pose 
design and safety challenges. Additionally, high-frequency pressure 
fluctuations (>1000 Hz) during hydrogen combustion can couple with 
the chamber’s acoustic modes, triggering thermoacoustic instabilities 
that may cause hardware degradation, such as nozzle erosion and liner 
failure [25,26].
Ammonia is another promising candidate for carbon-free energy, 
particularly in power generation and the marine sectors. Due to its low 
reactivity and narrow flammability range, ammonia combustion is 
prone to blow-off, requiring careful attention to flame stabilization. 
Extensive research has focused on understanding ammonia’s combus­
tion dynamics in swirling flows, with particular emphasis on flame 
stabilization and NOx formation [27,28]. Unique to ammonia is its fuel- 
type nitrogen, which can produce substantial NOx and nitrous oxide 
(N2O) emissions, the latter being a potent greenhouse gas with a global 
warming potential nearly 300 times that of CO2 [29–31]. Consequently, 
efforts to optimize ammonia combustion prioritize enhancing stability 
while minimizing pollutant formation.
The intricate flame behaviors introduced by these emerging fuels and 
advanced combustor designs underscore the need for advanced diag­
nostic tools. Laser diagnostics have emerged as indispensable in this 
context, offering non-intrusive, high-resolution measurements of key 
parameters such as temperature, species concentrations, and velocity 
fields [22,32,33]. Techniques like particle image velocimetry (PIV) 
[34–45], planar laser-induced fluorescence (PLIF) [38,46–60], Rayleigh 
scattering [61–64], spontaneous Raman scattering (SRS) [65–76], and 
so on have enabled detailed visualization of vortex–flame interactions, 
combustion instabilities, and pollutant formation mechanisms.This re­
view focuses on recent advancements in laser diagnostics for swirl 
combustion, highlighting high-speed imaging techniques, advanced 
spectroscopic methods, and multi-parameter measurement strategies 
that address challenges in high-temperature and low-carbon combustion 
technologies. To provide a structured overview of recent developments 
in this field, this review is organized into four main sections. Section 2
discusses the relationship between advancements in laser technology, 
diagnostic methods, and swirl combustion research, highlighting their 
co-evolution and mutual influence. Section 3 presents a detailed review 
of diagnostic progress, distinguishing between developments driven by

2. The interplay between laser technology, diagnostic method, 
and swirl combustion
The advancement of swirl combustion research is deeply intertwined 
with progress in laser sources and diagnostic technologies. This synergy 
manifests through two mutually reinforcing development cycles as 
shown in Fig. 2.
2.1. Co-evolution of laser sources and diagnostic capabilities
Progress in laser technology, particularly Nd:YAG solid-state lasers 
widely used in combustion diagnostics, continues to expand the frontiers 
of measurement capabilities. Key parameters such as output energy, 
repetition rate, pulse width, and wavelength tunability have been 
significantly enhanced [23]. Modern commercial Nd:YAG lasers can 
now deliver pulse energies from several to tens of joules. The emergence 
of diode-pumped solid-state lasers and fiber lasers has introduced sys­
tems capable of higher repetition rates and improved beam quality. 
Their repetition rates have advanced from early 10 Hz to kilohertz 
(kHz), tens of kHz, and even megahertz (MHz) levels [77], significantly 
enhancing the temporal resolution of measurements. In addition, diode 
and fiber seed-injection techniques [78,79] have transformed Nd:YAG 
lasers from unstable, broadband sources (linewidth ≈0.5 nm, timing 
jitter ≈ns) into precision instruments with ultra-narrow linewidths (<
0.001 nm) and exceptional timing stability (< 1 ps jitter). Femtosecond 
laser systems have likewise evolved from bulky dye-based setups (pulse 
energy in the nJ range, pulse duration ≈100 fs) to compact, high-power 
Ti:sapphire sources (pulse energy in the mJ–J range, pulse duration ≈
20–50 fs) and fiber-based systems (pulse energy in the µJ–mJ range, 
pulse duration ≈50–500 fs). Breakthroughs in Chirped pulse amplifi­
cation (CPA) [80,81], optical parametric CPA, and high-harmonic gen­
eration have further advanced commercially available ultrafast 
femtosecond lasers, enabling more precise and stable control over pulse 
characteristics.
These developments have directly enabled new diagnostic modal­
ities. For instance, the effectiveness of high-speed optical diagnostics is 
closely tied to advancements in laser technology. High-repetition-rate 
lasers have facilitated high-speed techniques up to 10 to 1000 kHz 
such as PIV [34,36,38–40,42,44,45], PLIF [46,48,50,52–55], filtered 
Rayleigh scattering (FRS) [61–64], spontaneous Raman scattering 
[69,70], and ultrafast diagnostics including femtosecond-based 
nonlinear Coherent Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering (CARS) [82–84]. 
The advanced laser diagnostics achieved higher temporal and spatial 
resolutions, improved measurement accuracy, and multi-parameter 
sensing.
Importantly, this development cycle 1 is bidirectional: the increasing 
demands of laser diagnostics, for better beam quality, stability, and 
tunability, have in turn driven innovations in laser technology. 
Together, laser development and diagnostic capabilities form a positive 
feedback loop.
2.2. Integration of laser diagnostics in swirl combustion research
The second cycle in Fig. 2 exists between laser diagnostics and tur­
bulent swirl combustion studies. As diagnostics improve, researchers 
gain unprecedented insight into the swirling flow, mixing, and reaction 
structures that characterize swirl-stabilized flames.
High-speed PIV and laser Doppler velocimetry (LDV) allow for time- 
resolved measurements of flow structures. Advanced techniques, such as 
time-resolved stereo PIV and tomographic PIV, reconstruct complex 
vortex dynamics in two-dimensional (2D-3C) [35,36,40,42,45] and 
three-dimensional (3D-3C) velocity fields [37,41,43]. Similarly, high- 
speed PLIF provides real-time visualization of fuel and reactive species 
[46,48,50,52–55], while OH-PLIF enables simultaneous mapping of 
radicals at the flame front and post-flame regions. Imaging multiple 
species PLIF simultaneously using one single [38,51,56–60] or multiple 
lasers [47,49,50] are also developed to examine turbulence-chemistry 
interactions. Emerging broadband Raman scattering techniques allow 
quantitative species measurements in hydrocarbon, hydrogen, and 
ammonia-based flames [65–68,73–76]. The transition of Raman spec­
troscopy from 1D line measurements to 2D spatial mapping facilitates 
the investigation of key scalar fields [71,72], such as scalar dissipation 
rates, critical for understanding flamelet behavior and extinction 
phenomena.
Conversely, the complex and unsteady dynamics inherent in swirl 
combustion, including ignition, blowout, flashback, extinction, and 
acoustic or plasma-coupled flame responses, demand high-fidelity di­
agnostics of velocity, temperature, species concentration, and heat 
release. Addressing these challenges continues to drive diagnostic 
innovation, reinforcing this second feedback loop.
2.3. Scope and focus of this review
Laser technology, diagnostic methods, and swirl combustion 
research are closely interlinked through these two cycles of mutual 
reinforcement. Each domain continues to push the boundaries of the 
others, resulting in accelerated progress across all three. While 
numerous reviews [22,77,85–87] have addressed technical details 
within each individual area, the focus of this work is on their synergistic 
evolution. Specifically, this review explores two key developmental 
pathways: the progression of conventional low-emission swirl combus­
tion and its reciprocal influence on laser technology and diagnostic 
methods. The rise of low-carbon swirl combustion systems and the 
emergence of novel diagnostics and laser tools tailored to these

3. Evolution and applications of laser diagnostics in swirl 
combustion
3.1. Developments driven by high-temperature combustion technologies
The development of high-repetition-rate laser diagnostics has been 
largely driven by the need to understand the complex dynamics of high- 
temperature combustion systems, particularly in swirling flames. In gas 
turbine combustors, dynamic processes such as flame stabilization and 
extinction, combustion instabilities, and pollutant formation pose sig­
nificant challenges for both experimental and numerical investigations 
[88]. Among these, thermoacoustic instability stands out as a key un­
steady phenomenon where interactions between acoustic waves, flow 
fields, and flame dynamics result in the formation of coherent periodic 
structures. These instabilities exemplify the intricate coupling mecha­
nisms in swirl combustion.
To capture and analyze such rapidly evolving phenomena, high- 
repetition-rate laser diagnostics have become essential. They enable 
time-resolved measurements that provide insights into the fundamental 
mechanisms governing flame response, vortex-flame interactions, and 
instability-induced structures. Advanced diagnostics have significantly 
deepened our understanding of these complex systems and offered 
pathways to optimize combustion performance both theoretically and 
practically.
3.1.1. Laser-based flow field diagnostics in swirling flames
Laser-based diagnostics are indispensable tools for resolving the 
complex fluid dynamics in swirl combustion. Techniques such as LDV 
and PIV have played foundational roles in characterizing velocity fields 
and coherent structures.
LDV provides point-wise velocity measurements by detecting the 
Doppler shift in scattered laser light. Its high temporal resolution makes 
it ideal for studying unsteady features in swirling flows. For example, 
Durox et al. [89] used LDV to obtain axial and radial velocity compo­
nents at various axial and radial positions in a swirl combustion cham­
ber. LDV allows to observe how changes in the swirl number (e.g., 
through variation of inlet guide vanes) affect the velocity field, flow 
structure, and flame dynamics (Fig. 3). Such measurements are critical 
for identifying jet penetration, recirculation zones, and validating 
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) models or complementing other 
diagnostics like chemiluminescence imaging [90].
PIV, in contrast, captures two- or three-dimensional velocity fields by 
tracking seeded particles over short intervals. Stereoscopic and dual- 
plane PIV configurations enable full three-component velocity vector 
measurements, which are particularly suited for analyzing complex, 
transient phenomena such as precessing vortex cores (PVCs) and recir­
culation zones [42,48,91]. Low-speed PIV (~10 Hz) can reveal average 
and fluctuating characteristics of swirling flows, while high-speed PIV 
(up to 100 kHz) captures transient structures in real time.
For instance, Sweeney et al. [25] showed that moderately swirling 
flames form compact recirculation zones comparable to those of pre­
mixed or stratified non-swirling flames, as shown in Fig. 4. The shape of 
the recirculation zone changes significantly with high swirl, extending 
farther downstream and entraining dilute products of combustion. Such 
insights were only made possible through the application of PIV.

Innovations such as regenerative amplification and multistage power 
amplification have enabled pulse repetition rates of tens to hundreds of 
kilohertz [77], supporting the development of high-speed PIV. These 
advancements allow researchers to capture velocity fields at unprece­
dented temporal resolutions (10–100 kHz) [48,88], uncovering features 
like vortex-induced flame roll-up, tip extinction, and dynamic in­
teractions between flow structures and flame fronts, as shown in Fig. 5. 
Analytical techniques such as proper orthogonal decomposition (POD) 
and wavelet analysis applied to time-resolved PIV or PLIF data further 
enable the extraction of dominant flow modes and their temporal evo­
lution, enhancing the understanding of coherent structure behavior 
[36,93,94].
Quantitative vortex analysis has also been promoted based on high- 
speed flow field measurement [95–98]. Wang et al. [95] utilized 50 kHz 
PIV and CH* chemiluminescence to investigate unsteady vortex ring 
behavior in thermoacoustically forced flames. They extracted parame­
ters such as core vorticity, circulation, and convective velocity of the 
outer vortex ring (OVR) to identify mechanisms of vortex formation and 
detachment, and their influence on flame heat release (Fig. 6). These 
studies confirmed that vortex behavior, while altered by acoustic exci­
tation, adheres to fundamental vortex dynamics principles.
The central recirculation zone (CRZ) is an essential feature in 
swirling flames, including the blue whirl [99,100], which emerges due 
to vortex breakdown. Wang et al. [101] recently analyzed the ISL of 
swirling flow, and found that the boundary between the cases with and 
without CRZ, as shown in Fig. 7, collapses to a single line for all test 
cases, yielding a critical Reynolds number of 424 ± 11. This highlighted 
that CRZ formation is closely tied to the strength of the inner shear layer 
(ISL) and the viscosity of burned gases, adding depth to the current 
understanding of CRZ mechanisms.
Synchronized measurements such as PIV/CH2O PLIF have revealed 
how acoustically induced vortices modulate flame front fluctuations and 
heat release rates [55,102]. Ren et al. [103] identified two distinct 
vortex–flame interaction regimes in V-shaped premixed flames: weak 
interaction in lean flames (interrupted by tip extinction), and strong 
interaction near stoichiometric conditions, where vortex strength syn­
chronizes with heat release rate. Wang et al. [54] investigated phase- 
averaged vortex cores and CH2O fields of a centrally staged 
combustor, as shown in Fig. 8. They found that PVC in the pilot flame 
drives coherent heat release rate (HRR) fluctuations, while the outer 
helical vortex (OHV) contributes to additional HRR via flame tip 
stretching and roll-up. The PVC affects the primary heat-release process 
by dictating the convective transport of the reactants upstream of the 
flame front. The second growth of OHVs, attributed to thermal expan­
sion, plays a critical role in flame dynamics.
For 3D swirling flame dynamics, Zheng et al. [104,105] developed 
dual-plane stereoscopic PIV (DP-s-PIV) technology to reconstruct 3D 
acoustically induced vortex structures and explored how excitation 
frequency governs the formation of inner and outer vortex rings. As 
shown in Fig. 9, their results, in conjunction with further 50 kHz PIV and 
CH* imaging [95], demonstrated that acoustic frequency modulates 
vortex behavior and heat release intensity. Most recently, long-duration, 
kilohertz-rate planar PIV was employed by Wang et al. [54,106] to study 
the 3D structures of PVC and OHV and their impacts on stratified 
swirling flames. The reconstructed vortical structures of the primary 
recirculation zone (PRZ), lip recirculation zone (LRZ), PVC, and OHV 
are clearly shown in Fig. 10. Their work revealed how different vortex 
structures, PVC through reactant transport and OHV through flame 
stretching, regulate combustion behavior. Phase-conditioned analysis 
with respect to both acoustic signals and vortex phases offered a 
rigorous approach to resolving local flame dynamics.
Advancements in laser diagnostics, especially high-speed PIV and its 
technical variants, have transformed our ability to resolve and interpret 
the dynamic flow fields in swirl combustion. These tools have revealed 
the role of recirculation zones (e.g., PRZ, LRZ) and coherent vortex 
structures (e.g., PVC, OHV, vortex rings) in flame stabilization, extinc­
tion, and heat release modulation. The integration of time-resolved, 
quantitative vortex dynamics with advanced vortical analysis con­
tinues to provide new insights into the fundamental physics governing 
swirling flow.
3.1.2. High-speed species measurement techniques in swirling flames
Simultaneous measurement of flow fields and key chemical species 
in combustion flames is crucial for investigating combustion chemistry, 
flame structure, and flame-flow interactions. As such, many studies have 
integrated flow diagnostics with species measurement techniques to 
observe both fluid dynamics and chemical evolution in real time 
[42,48,55,88,102,107,108].
Various diagnostic techniques are available for detecting combustion 
species, including chemiluminescence, laser-induced fluorescence (LIF), 
laser absorption spectroscopy (LAS), Raman scattering, and so on. Each 
method has specific advantages and limitations, depending on experi­
mental requirements and species of interest. Among these, PLIF is 
particularly well-suited for capturing species distributions in high- 
speed, transient combustion environments, due to its high spatial reso­
lution, non-intrusive nature, and strong sensitivity.

High-speed PLIF has yielded critical insights into swirl combustion 
by capturing transient phenomena such as mixing, ignition, flame sta­
bilization, and vortex interactions. For example, Boxx et al. [109] used 
combined OH-PLIF and stereo-PIV at 10 kHz to investigate flame dy­
namics in a lean premixed gas turbine combustor. They observed strong 
local periodicity and global fluctuations in velocity and heat release 
(~364 Hz), though no clear evidence of a PVC was found. St¨ohr et al. 
[48] combined OH and acetone PLIF with PIV at 10 kHz to study tran­
sient mixing and combustion in a lean partially-premixed swirling 
flame. Transport and mixing of fuel is visualized by adding 9 % acetone 
to the methane. Fuel–air mixing visualized via acetone fluorescence 
showed that PVC-enhanced mixing helped stabilize the flame, as shown 
in Fig. 11. Sulfur dioxide PLIF at 20 kHz [110] revealed vortex–flame 
interactions where vortex circumferential velocities deviated from the 
main flow, inducing flame contour curling and shedding.
Simultaneous stereoscopic PIV and OH-PLIF at 10 kHz has also 
improved understanding of flame–flow coupling, which is essential for 
low-emission combustor design [42]. Steinberg et al. [107] explored 
self-excited thermoacoustic oscillations in a gas turbine combustor using 
OH-PLIF, PIV, and OH* chemiluminescence at 5 kHz. They highlighted 
the phase interaction between PVC and acoustic modes as a key driver of 
instability, as shown in Fig. 12. If the two oscillations are in phase, 
pressure fluctuations can be amplified to create a combustion instability.
High-speed OH/CH2O PLIF has also revealed pockets of formalde­
hyde enclosed by OH at flame tips, offering clues on flame stabilization 
mechanisms. Liu et al. [108] employed simultaneous 20 kHz PIV, 
acetone PLIF, and CH2O PLIF to investigate V-shaped and lift-off flame 
transitions in a model gas turbine combustor. As shown in Fig. 13, they 
studied the intermittent transitions between the anchored V-shape flame

the equivalence ratio or forcing amplitude can re-anchor lifted flames, 
offering guidance for stable, low-emission burner design.
PLIF techniques are being extended to high-pressure, reactive, two- 
phase flows for evaluating novel low-NOx liquid-fueled combustors. 
For instance, Legros et al. [111] combined OH, NO, and kerosene vapor 
PLIF with PIV to examine droplet–flame interactions in a lean-premixed 
kerosene combustor. The single-shot distributions of kerosene mole 
fraction, OH radical, and kerosene temperature for three operating 
conditions are shown in Fig. 14. Their work underscored the influence of 
droplet size on flame anchoring and species distribution. They high­
lighted the importance of droplet size on the mutual predictions of the 
aerodynamic field, the structure of the flame, flame anchoring, and 
species distribution.

3.1.3. Advances in high-speed temperature diagnostics
Accurate temperature measurement in swirling flames is critical for 
understanding temperature distribution and combustion characteristics. 
One of the distinguishing features of laser-based temperature di­
agnostics is the diversity of available techniques, each offering unique 
capabilities. Methods such as LAS, filtered Rayleigh scattering (FRS), 
CARS, and two-color PLIF have been successfully employed to probe 
temperature fields in swirling flames.
LAS is a widely used non-intrusive technique capable of providing 
quantitative, species-specific measurements of gas temperature, pres­
sure, composition, and velocity. It typically employs near- or mid- 
infrared tunable diode lasers or broadband sources such as interband 
cascade lasers and quantum cascade lasers (QCLs). A comprehensive 
review of LAS using these light sources can be found in [85,117]. This 
section focuses on LAS applications in swirling flame temperature 
measurements.
LAS is a calibration-free and temporally resolved diagnostic tech­
nique; however, it inherently provides line-of-sight averaged tempera­
tures. Since swirling flames exhibit strong spatial non-uniformity and 
temporal fluctuations, advanced strategies such as scanning lasers or 
tomographic systems with multiple beams and detectors are required to 
obtain spatially resolved temperature fields. For example, Liu et al.

Ongoing advancements in laser diagnostic techniques have signifi­
cantly enhanced the spatial and temporal resolution of temperature 
measurements in swirling flames, enabling detailed characterization of 
thermal fields that are critical for optimizing combustor design and 
elucidating complex flow–flame interactions. Each temperature diag­
nostic method offers distinct advantages and inherent trade-offs. LAS 
delivers high temporal resolution and excellent species selectivity, but 
provides only line-of-sight averaged measurements. Rayleigh and 
Raman scattering techniques offer high spatial resolution and localized 
measurements, yet demand optically clean environments and complex 
experimental configurations. Two-color PLIF facilitates rapid two- 
dimensional imaging of temperature fields but relies on tracer calibra­
tion and may suffer from limited accuracy under certain conditions.
3.2. Innovations driven by low- and zero-carbon fuel combustion
The rapid advancement of low-carbon combustion technologies, 
particularly those utilizing hydrogen and ammonia, has become a sig­
nificant driver in the evolution of combustion diagnostic techniques. 
Among these, laser-based methods have gained prominence for their 
capability to detect and quantify key quantities in swirl combustion 
systems, enabling better understanding of combustion dynamics and 
supporting the development of low- and zero-carbon gas turbine and 
aeroengine technologies.
While the transition to alternative fuels has limited impact on flow 
field and temperature measurement techniques, it introduces substantial 
changes in combustion chemistry and the resulting product species. 
Accordingly, this section focuses on how novel fuels influence the 
composition of swirl combustion products and, in turn, how this drives 
the advancement of diagnostic techniques for nitrogen-containing 
species.

In parallel, nitrogen oxides (NOx) emissions remain a central concern

with significant environmental implications. To investigate the effects of 
these nitrogen-containing species on ignition behavior, flame stability,

developed. These include LAS, LIF, and Raman scattering, tailored to 
provide accurate and time-resolved measurements of trace nitrogen 
species under high-temperature, high-pressure conditions.
Among the most prominent techniques for detecting nitrogen- 
containing species in combustion environments, TDLAS has shown 
considerable efficacy in measuring trace concentrations of NH3, NO, and 
NO2. The absorption line strengths of some species, e.g., NH3, NO, H2O, 
CO, CO2, from 1 to 10 μm are shown in Fig. 20. Weng et al. [132]
explored ultraviolet (UV) absorption spectroscopy of NH3 within the 
190–230 nm range under high-temperature conditions, observing a 
decrease in absorption cross-section as temperature increased from 295 
K to 1570 K. Li et al. [133] developed a mid-infrared TDLAS system 
using QCLs targeting NH3 absorption near 1103.45 cm−1 in CH4/NH3 
swirl combustion. Their system successfully detected NH3 emissions 
ranging from 26 to 738 ppm, as shown in Fig. 20, with emissions 
increasing alongside fuel NH3 content and equivalence ratio. Another 
study utilized a linear calibration-free wavelength modulation spec­
troscopy approach with QCLs at ~ 5.2 µm (NO) and ~ 6.25 µm (NO2), 
enabling in situ, non-intrusive, and quantitative detection even under 
substantial H2O interference, achieving ppm-level sensitivity in gas 
turbine exhausts [134].
LIF has also become a key technique for visualizing reactive nitrogen 
species in flames. While LIF has been extensively applied in laminar and 
turbulent jet flames for ammonia and ammonia-blended combustion, its 
application in swirl combustion remains limited due to the inherent 
complexity of turbulent flows. Brackmann et al. [135] measured OH, 
NH, and NO LIF in laminar premixed ammonia-air flames, using the 
resulting data to validate several detailed kinetic models. Alekseev et al. 
[136] revealed that peak NO formation occurs at ~ 50 % NH3 fuel 
content in NH3-dimethyl ether flame using NO-PLIF. Wang et al. 
[57–59] developed a series of PLIF systems capable of simultaneous 
multi-species imaging (e.g., NH/NO [57], OH/NO [59], NH3/NH/NO 
[58]) using a single dye laser. Dai et al. [60] extended this approach to 
measure NH3/NH2/NH with a single dye laser system. Other studies 
have employed two or more laser systems to simultaneously measure 
combinations such as NH3/NH/Rayleigh [56], NH3/NH/OH [137], NH/ 
OH [138], and NH/NO/Rayleigh [139]. These efforts, particularly those 
splitting a single laser beam to excite different species, have provided 
valuable insights into flame structures and pollutant formation 
dynamics.
In swirling flame environments, Okafor et al. [140] implemented 
simultaneous OH/NO-PLIF imaging using two Nd:YAG-pumped dye la­
sers in a micro gas turbine combustor fueled by CH4–NH3–air mixtures. 
Their results show spatial correlations between high OH and NO in­
tensities in fuel-rich zones (see Fig. 21), suggesting that NO formation is 
promoted by OH radical production. An optimal equivalence ratio (Φ =
1.30–1.35) was identified for minimizing NOx emissions, which 
depended on the NH3 fraction. The two-stage rich-lean burning flames of 
methane-ammonia combustion produced lower NOx emissions than 
NH3-air combustion due to the higher flame speeds that suppressed 
secondary NOx formation.
Wang et al. [3] applied PLIF techniques to investigate NO and OH 
formation in premixed NH3/CH4-air flames at 2 bar. To avoid NH3 ab­
sorption near 226 nm (commonly used for NO), they selected an alter­
native excitation wavelength around 236 nm for the NO (0–1) band. As 
shown in Fig. 22, the PLIF images display rapid NO formation imme­
diately downstream of the reaction zone, with minimal variation in NO 
intensity above the nozzle. Increased residence time enhanced reaction 
completion, while higher swirl numbers significantly reduced NO, NO2, 
and N2O emissions, more effectively than increasing combustor length.

secondary combustion stage. Thus, using hydrogen-enriched ammonia

Plasma-assisted combustion is another emerging technique to sta­
bilize low-carbon flames and reduce pollutant emissions. Low-carbon 
fuels such as ammonia and biogas mixtures pose significant combus­
tion stability challenges due to their low reactivity or slow kinetics. 
Laser diagnostics applied to plasma-assisted flames have revealed that 
non-equilibrium plasma discharges can significantly enhance combus­
tion by increasing radicals (e.g., NH2) production and reducing NOx 
emissions [145–147].
For instance, Tang et al. [148,149] utilized a plasma torch driven by 
low-power microsecond repetitively pulsed discharges (μRPDs) to sta­

emissions of excited species such as OH, N2*, H*, and O* under plasma 
activation. The plasma markedly enhanced flame anchoring and 
extended the lean blow-off limit from ~ 0.6 to ~ 0.4–0.5 over Reynolds 
numbers ranging from ~ 3000 to 7500.
Hydrogen-enriched lean premixed combustion is prone to induce 
high-frequency thermoacoustic instability. Kushwaha et al. [150]
explored these phenomena in a swirl-stabilized gas turbine combustor 
using high-speed OH* chemiluminescence, OH-PLIF, and stereo-PIV at 
10 kHz, as shown in Fig. 26. Extended proper orthogonal decomposition 
revealed phase-synchronized flame surface structures and HRR oscilla­

sights into flame–flow coupling mechanisms. Æsøy et al. [151]
examined the effect of hydrogen enrichment on the nonlinear response 
and saturation of premixed flames stabilized by a bluff-body using high- 
speed OH-PLIF at a rate of 10 kHz [152]. The OH-PLIF images are 
processed to extract the phase-averaged flame surface density and flame 
curvature, as shown in Fig. 27. They observed that high levels of 
hydrogen increase the amplitude response to acoustic forcing, leading to 
higher saturation amplitudes. Substituting natural gas with hydrogen in 
gas turbines increases the risk of much higher limit-cycle amplitudes if 
self-excited instabilities occur. Aguilar et al. [153] investigated the in­
fluence of hydrogen on the stability of a perfectly premixed combustor. 
It demonstrates that the effect of premixed hydrogen on thermoacoustic 
instabilities can be predicted using well-established methods, e.g. low- 
order network model.

3.3. Practical challenges in implementing laser-based diagnostic 
techniques in industrial swirl combustor environments
While laser-based diagnostics have become indispensable tools for 
exploring swirl combustion in controlled laboratory settings, their 
translation to industrial combustors remains highly constrained. Prac­
tical applications, especially in aeroengines and gas turbines, introduce 
severe environmental, structural, and operational limitations that can 
significantly impair diagnostic accuracy, reliability, and longevity. This 
section provides a comprehensive overview of the key barriers hindering 
practical implementation and the technological strategies developed to 
address them.

accuracy at high pressures due to spectral line broadening and intensi­

3.3.2. Optical signal attenuation and interference
Swirling flames in practical combustors often involve staged com­
bustion, diffusion-mode burning, and complex fuel–air stratification, all 
of which produce significant optical interference. In particular, fuel-rich 
zones and kerosene sprays generate soot particles and oil droplets that 
absorb or scatter incident laser beams, drastically reducing signal 
quality in techniques such as PLIF, Raman, and Rayleigh scattering 
[166]. Chemiluminescence from the flame and glowing surfaces in­
troduces further background noise, masking weak signals. This is espe­
cially problematic for diagnostics requiring high sensitivity and low- 
noise detection, such as spontaneous Raman scattering or FRS [73,75].
To overcome these challenges, several advanced strategies have been 
proposed. Pulsed and burst-mode lasers improve signal-to-noise ratios 
by increasing instantaneous energy delivery. Structured light tech­
niques, polarization filtering, and multi-wavelength detection enhance 
signal separation from background interference [167,168]. FRS lever­
ages narrowband atomic absorption filters (e.g., iodine vapor cells) to 
suppress unwanted light [169]. Spectral deconvolution algorithms 
disentangle overlapping signals from particles, soot, and flame emis­
sions. Backscatter or off-axis collection geometries minimize the influ­
ence of intense forward-scattered light from particles.
In addition, combustion byproducts tend to contaminate optical 
windows over time, reducing transmittance and causing beam distor­
tion. In particle-based diagnostics like PIV (Particle Image Velocimetry) 
or LDV (Laser Doppler Velocimetry), seeding with metal oxides (e.g., 
TiO2 or Al2O3) is common. These particles often deposit on optical 
windows, rapidly degrading image quality. Solutions include the use of 
purge air curtains, anti-soot coatings, self-cleaning windows, and time- 
gated imaging, all of which aim to reduce contamination and prolong 
optical clarity.
3.3.3. Optical access constraints in realistic combustor geometries
One of the most significant limitations in practical combustors is the 
lack of adequate optical access. Unlike laboratory test rigs, real engines 
feature compact and highly integrated structures. Multi-layered 
combustor walls, designed to accommodate thermal expansion, cool­
ing airflows, and mechanical stresses, offer limited space for installing 
optical windows.
Even when optical ports are included, their size is typically con­
strained by structural and thermal considerations. Small-aperture win­
dows result in either insufficient signal throughput or unsafely high laser 
energy densities. In addition, thermal gradients can lead to window 
deformation, coating degradation, and alignment drift.
To address these issues, two primary strategies have emerged. On the 
one hand, optically accessible model combustors are designed to repli­
cate real combustor geometry and operating conditions while incorpo­
rating large viewing windows for diagnostics like PIV, PLIF, and FRS 
[88,170]. Although these platforms offer controlled environments, they 
often compromise realism in terms of turbulence intensity, heat flux, and 
wall boundary conditions. On the other, miniaturized diagnostic inte­
gration involves embedding small windows or optical probes directly 
into engine components. Sapphire or fused silica windows, combined 
with active cooling via air-film systems, provide a durable interface for 
line-of-sight techniques like TDLAS or LAS. Additionally, endoscopic 
probes employing fiber-optic bundles or micro-lens arrays allow de­
livery and collection of light in confined geometries [171]. However, 
fiber-based systems must be engineered to withstand extreme thermal 
and mechanical loads.
While relatively simple techniques like LAS can be adapted to con­
strained environments, more complex diagnostics, such as spontaneous 
Raman scattering, Rayleigh scattering, and CARS, require precise 
alignment, large optical apertures, and high collection efficiencies, 
making them much more difficult to implement in practical systems

3.3.4. Calibration and quantification challenges
Calibration is essential for signal quantification in PIV, PLIF, FRS, 
SRS, and CARS measurements. Nonlinear techniques such as CARS 
require accurate thermodynamic models and databases that account for 
temperature- and pressure-dependent molecular behavior. Reliable 
calibration and accurate quantification remain major challenges in real 
combustor environments. In laboratory conditions, known reference 
gases or flow fields can be used for in-situ calibration of temperature, 
species concentration, and velocity. In contrast, real engines combustor 
offer limited space and accessibility, making it difficult to insert cali­
bration targets or introduce controlled reference gases. Spontaneous 
radiation from hot walls and flames interferes with weak diagnostic 
signals, particularly in scattering or fluorescence-based methods. These 
interference signals increase the difficulty of removing background 
noise, environmental effects, and the influence of other species or 
particles.
To address these issues, miniature calibration cells and reference 
burners are being developed to provide localized calibration environ­
ments within confined systems. Spectral separation technologies, such 
as narrowband filters and polarization-based light suppression, enhance 
signal integrity. Furthermore, artificial intelligence (AI)-based calibra­
tion or quantification methods are gaining increasing attention. Ma­
chine learning models can be trained to identify and filter out 
background noise and interference signals caused by spontaneous ra­
diation from hot surfaces and flames, improving signal quality [172]. 
Training datasets generated from high-fidelity CFD simulations can be 
used to optimize inversion algorithms for diagnostic techniques like 
CARS, improving measurement accuracy without the need for physical 
calibration targets. With their rapid prediction capabilities, AI methods 
can perform estimations in microseconds, supporting real-time

4. Conclusions and future directions
4.1. Summary of key developments in laser diagnostics for swirl 
combustion
The field of laser-based diagnostics for swirl combustion has un­
dergone transformative progress in recent years, particularly in response 
to the global demand for cleaner, low-carbon energy technologies. As 
combustion systems transition from traditional hydrocarbon fuels to 
low- and zero-carbon alternatives such as hydrogen and ammonia, un­
derstanding the complex multiphysics phenomena, such as turbulent 
flow interactions, heat release dynamics, flame stability, and pollutant 
formation, has become increasingly critical.
Laser diagnostics, including LAS, PIV, PLIF, Rayleigh scattering, 
Raman scattering, and so on, have emerged as indispensable tools for 
non-intrusively probing combustion processes with high spatial and 
temporal resolution. Advances in laser technology and high-frame-rate 
detectors have revolutionized high-speed diagnostics, greatly deep­
ening our understanding of swirl combustion dynamics. The integration 
of multiple diagnostic techniques has enabled simultaneous measure­
ments of velocity fields, intermediate species (e.g., OH, CH2O, NO), and 
temperature distributions, offering unprecedented insight into flame 
structure, blow-off mechanisms, flashback behavior, and thermoacous­
tic instabilities. These experimental datasets also play a crucial role in 
validating computational fluid dynamics models of combustors.
In low-carbon combustion research, advanced diagnostic techniques 
have provided compelling evidence of the unique challenges associated 
with fuels like ammonia, including low flame speeds, narrow stability 
limits, and elevated NOx emissions. Approaches such as hydrogen 
blending, plasma-assisted combustion, and swirl stabilization have been 
extensively investigated using high-speed diagnostics to understand and 
mitigate these issues. Similarly, the application of time-resolved and 
volumetric laser diagnostics has shed light on complex blowoff and 
flashback dynamics in ammonia-hydrogen-enriched flames, revealing 
critical mechanisms that govern flame anchoring, propagation, and 
instability.
Despite significant progress, challenges remain in applying these 
advanced diagnostic methods to high-pressure, real-engine conditions 
due to limitations in optical access, signal attenuation, and system 
complexity. Future efforts should focus on improving the robustness and 
portability of diagnostic systems, enhancing multi-dimensional and 
machine learning-assisted data analysis techniques, and expanding 
measurement capabilities to include additional species and reaction 
intermediates.

The co-evolution of swirl combustion research and laser-based di­
agnostics has demonstrated a strong symbiotic relationship, and ad­
vancements in one domain often catalyze progress in the other two.
