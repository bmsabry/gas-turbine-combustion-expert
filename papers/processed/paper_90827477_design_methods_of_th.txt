Design methods of the ABB ALSTOM POWER gas
turbine dry low emission combustion system
H Alkabie
ABB ALSTOM POWER UK Limited, PO Box 1, Waterside South, Lincoln LN5 7FD, UK
Abstract:
This paper describes the conceptual design and aerothermal approach of the ABB
ALSTOM POWER UK Limited (ABB ALSTOM POWER) dry low emission (DLE) combustion
system directed towards all current and future power ranges. A set of parameters was employed as a
common criterion to design the company’s combustion system across the product range. The
parameters made it possible to scale the combustion system to operate at any required ﬁring temperature
without the penalty of achieving the low emission targets. The logic and sensible air mass mapping and
testing management based on a basic knowledge of the aerothermodynamics of the combustion system
and its integral parts produced reliable operation and engine results.
The combustion system aerothermodynamic design considers the constraints of maintaining
combustor speciﬁcations while introducing a reliable ultra-low NOx concept with minimal structural
change to engine design. Furthermore, the ignition loop, piloting and stability margins were issues
continuously assessed through a deﬁned application programme to examine their impact on engine
operability at various load conditions.
A main radial swirler injector is utilized for both gas and liquid to produce a fuel lean mixture for
combustion at full-load operating conditions, while a pilot fuel injector guarantees a local fuel-rich zone
to sustain stability at low power conditions. The carefully planned switch-over between the pilot and
main swirler provides smooth operation and low emission across the load range conditions.
The company combustion system relies upon the principle of aerodynamic variable mixing with a
single vortex generator burner. The partial premixing process takes place at the radial inﬂow swirler
slots before the fuel–air mixture is introduced into a prechamber and thereafter into the combustion
chamber. For vane passage injection the liquid fuel atomization should be at an optimum and the vane
passage and prechamber length set to provide the vaporization period. The impingement cooling
scheme aided by a thin layer of thermal barrier coating (TBC) achieved an excellent combustor wall
temperature at all operating conditions and across the DLE engines product range. The scheme made it
possible to direct a large amount of air towards the swirler to achieve the lean premix combustion.
Substantial CO emission reduction at part load was achieved by variable guide vanes (VGV)
modulation for the single-shaft engines. This action inhibits NO formation without slowing the CO
oxidation process to a frozen condition. The variable mixing regime approach and accurate air
management has demonstrated an ultra-low NOx emission of 12–15 and 32–35 ppmv (corrected to 15%
O2) at the full-load condition while operating on gas and distillate-2 fuel respectively. Moreover, the
system exhibited low CO and dynamics oscillation capability for both gas and liquid fuel operation.
At the present time, the one-dimensional analysis has been proved to be a reliable tool for combustion
system designers to produce a preliminary design conﬁguration. The particular choice for a commercial
computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) code to satisfy various criteria of the swirling combusting ﬂow is
proving to be difﬁcult due to constraints regarding solver and/or methods limitation.
The results obtained from the high-pressure air facilities, engine beds and ﬁeld trials represent a true
technological achievement through collective engineering design efforts towards emission and
pollution control for small- and medium-range gas turbine engines.

design, aerothermodynamics, combustion, DLE, gas turbine, premixing, emission, NOx

INTRODUCTION
Over the past 50 years, development in gas turbine
technology has changed step by step towards less
pollution and more reliable risk-free components. None-
theless, combustion engineers still continuously face
design challenges for higher efﬁciency, better energy
management and conservation, especially with the outcry
about gradual deterioration in the air quality and the
discovery of an ozone layer hole. Advancements in the
conceptual technological approach are still a red alert
priority for engineers and scientists in order to meet rising
demands for stringent current and projected emissions
regulations. ABB ALSTOM POWER UK Limited met
the challenges in the environmental aspects by forming
an advanced technological
team of multidiscipline
engineering skills to concurrently work together to
spearhead the tasks. The team has not relaxed the more
conventional requirements, such as reliable ignition, run-
up efﬁciency, stability and pattern factor, but has
concentrated on the wider range of a turn-down ratio
implied by current and future legislation to achieve better
combustion efﬁciency at part-load conditions. Conse-
quently, the combustion chamber size and/or better
aerodynamic structures inside the combustion chamber
may be dictated by pollution constraints.
It was also a design brief that higher turbine tem-
peratures than previously experienced would be em-
ployed to improve performance. Such an advancement
has to include customer expectations with regard to
improvement in reliability, durability and fuel ﬂexibility,
and a lower purchase cost should not be compromised.
Cannon et al. [1] proposed a general design speciﬁca-
tion for industrial dry low NOx (DLN) that should
incorporate the following features:
(a) machine retroﬁttable,
(b) adaptable to the full industrial range,
(c) maximize design simplicity (for cost, enhanced life

The Company combustion team broadly concluded from
past experiences that the main objective should be to
adopt the process of a dry lean premixed combustion
system in line with the majority of industrial gas turbine
manufacturers. Also, a system with no mechanical
moving parts should be employed to control air and/or
fuel and achieve the required reliability and cost con-
sideration. The stabilizing mechanism should be one that
imposes a rapid, intense mixing regime with high inten-
sity of heat release in order for the generated NOx and CO
to be controlled in the shortest possible residence time.
It is a known fact that NOx emission can be reduced by
using lean well-mixed primary zones, but the level of
reduction is closely related to the quality of the improved
fuel and air mixing [2, 3]. Low emissions combustion
systems have been demonstrated using radial swirler
passages as a means to partially premixing fuel and air for
both gaseous and liquid fuels [4–6]. In a previously
investigated radial inﬂow swirler [7], the radial passages
have been carried through a bend into an annulus around
a central fuel injector, thus becoming a form of axial
swirler but with the beneﬁt of a greater passage and
mixing time. The complexity of the system provides a
very hard task to stop any premature ignition due to a
locally high residence time zone that could lead to
ignition.
Conventional combustor designs have a very poor
mixing in the primary zone to give a wide stability range.
This results in very large temperature gradients that
generate large NOx concentrations, as shown in the
traverses of Heitor and Whitelaw [8]. Future engines,
with higher turbine entry temperatures, will require more
air in the primary zone and better fuel–air mixing. The
concept of utilizing the passages of radial inﬂow swirler
for partial mixing offered low NOx emissions without
using fully premixed fuel and air. Previous work demon-
strating an ultra-low NOx primary zone for gas turbine
combustor applications was investigated by Alkabie [6].
Alkabie and Andrews [4, 5, 9–13] have examined three
different modes of fuel injection: central radial outwards,
radial swirler passage and peripheral radial inward injec-
tions. The fuel injection in the vane passages of radial
swirlers improved the fuel and air mixing compared with
the central injection. The internal gas composition
measurements showed that for the passage fuel injection
the fuel and air mixing was good quite close to the
swirler, i.e. a low NOx level was achieved in a short
residence time, and that this was the key to the low NOx
emissions. Also, the swirling jet in the extended pre-
chamber of the swirler exploited the centrifugal mixing
effects due to the density differences between burnt
(vortex central core region) and unburnt gases to enhance
the mixing even further. The results showed that passage
fuel mixing could give the required NOx emission with a
reasonable stability margin and it was better than the
peripheral or wall injection [4, 12].
The present dry low emission (DLE) radial swirler
utilizes the beneﬁt of the passage fuel injection method,
which offers enough residence time for the initial partial
fuel–air premixing in the passages before introducing the
mixture into the prechamber. The geometrical size of the
prechamber (e.g. length, diameter) may affect the
swirling ﬂow vortex breakdown.
The investigation carried out by Andrews, Alkabie and
co-workers [4–6, 9–16] demonstrated that with more fuel
holes located in the upstream region in the passages of the
swirler and other stabilizing mechanisms, an improve-
ment in the mixing process could be achieved. In this way
more fuel–air uniformity could be achieved to eliminate
any local rich zone that may cause a rise in local reaction
temperature and hence more thermal NOx emission via
the Zeldovich mechanism. This is in total agreement to
what Miknus, Appeleton, Heywood and Hicks [17, 18]
have indicated. Their investigation suggested that as the
mixing parameter(s) approaches the zero value the peaks
of the fuel–air distribution approach the mathematical
means of the mixture and low NOx emission is achieved.
In previous investigations [10, 12], a prechamber
length of 40 mm, having the same diameter as the swirler
exit, was enough to introduce more mixing length and to
delay the breakdown of potential swirling core mass ﬂow
caused by the expansion and imposed pressure re-
distribution. The NOx level exhibited was in the range
of 1–3 ppmv (corrected to 15% O2) using the atmospheric
rig facility [4, 10]. The present work adopted a similar
approach to achieve rapid mixing and stability. Further-
more, the choice of the entry and exit proﬁles of such an
extended swirling ﬂow piece proved to be the critical
parameter in the aerodynamic stability of the system. The
combustion system can only react to what the upstream
regions impose on it. The ﬂame anchoring mechanism
can be disrupted by the imbalance between the rate of
removal of products from the recirculation zone and the
rate of burning [19].

DESIGN METHODS
The geometry of the stabilizing mechanism can inﬂuence
the combustion characteristics of the system. The entry
leading edge blockage and ﬂow separation occurring in
some geometry inﬂuences the pressure drop and hence
the intensity of the combustion zone turbulence. The
scale of the turbulence may also be inﬂuenced by other
integral parts of the ﬂame holder. The residence time
within the recirculation region can inﬂuence the tem-
perature and mixture ratio limits beyond which combus-
tion cannot be sustained. In lean combustion systems

Therefore, the ﬂame stabilizer design must satisfy the
following basic requirements:
1. The stabilizer drag coefﬁcient and total blockage
including the pressure drop due to heat addition must
not exceed an acceptable level. Higher values may
lead to loss in overall engine performance.
2. The ﬂow regime imposed by the stabilizer must
provide ignition interconnection where multiple in-
dependent areas of ﬂow recirculation are generated,
i.e. to allow ﬂame propagation from one recirculation
zone to another during the initial light-up and run-up to
load condition.
3. The velocity and blockage created by the stabilizer and
any of its integral parts must result in a combustor
entrance velocity that is sufﬁcient to preclude ﬂash-
back under all operating load conditions.
4. The hot product residence time resulting from the
aerodynamic structure imposed by the stabilizer
should be adequate to produce a steady and stable
source of high-temperature gas and active chemical
species under all operating conditions.
5. The stbailizer must be self cooling when heat is
transferred to it by the burning gases due to radiation,
convection or conduction.
6. The stabilizer must permit future expansion without
major impact on other parts of the combustion
chamber and engine casing.
There are other requirements related to the hot parts
life and the acceptable pattern factor for the nozzle guide
vanes (NGV). However, it is self-evident that the success
or failure of a gas turbine is determined by careful and
skilful engineering of the combustion can and the
transition piece. The combustion system must deliver a
desirable temperature distribution at the turbine inlet. The
turbine nozzle is the hottest part of the gas turbine. Its life
is limited by thermal fatigue and oxidation; therefore,
temperature gradations must be minimized in both the
circumferential and radial directions. There are other
elements that contribute to the temperature proﬁle such as
fuel quality, engine air or fuel maldistribution, i.e. can-to-
can variation and air leakage.
The DLE combustion system design work and progress
was born by examining other workers’ past experiences
and published works. In addition, the Company utilized
in-house technology advancement and the pre-commis-
sioning development work to satisfy the above criteria.
The current DLE main design feature and principal
combustion approach may be summarized as follows:
(a) lean burn,
(b) radial swirler stabilized ﬂow with pilot assistance for
turn-down,
(c) programmable main/pilot splitter valve in the control
system with minimum pilot fuel feed at full-load
operating condition,
(d) variable mixing with passage fuel injection regime,
(e) retroﬁttable replacement for the conventional diffu-
sion ﬂame combustor,
(f) impingement cooled with effusion cooling on selec-
tive parts.
The Company’s main combustion design parameters
were always evaluated before any design release, either
experimentally or mathematically, through one-dimen-
sional analyses or whenever practically possible through
CFD work. The quality of the assessment of these parts is
represented by the level of aerothermodynamics perfor-
mance of the combustion system on the development bed
and ﬁeld trial. The Typhoon DLN combustion system, as
schematically illustrated in Figs 1a and 2, encompasses
six reverse ﬂow tubular combustors connected by means
of ﬁxture upstream and downstream sections by the
effusion-cooled transition duct to the centre casing. A
lean premix main burner is employed to reduce ﬂame
temperature and hence suppress NOx production. Further-
more, for ignition and ﬂame stability, particularly during
load rejection, a central pilot injector is fully integrated
and utilized.

COMBUSTION SYSTEM TEST LOGIC
Figure 3 illustrates the ABB ALSTOM POWER logic
ﬂow diagram for testing the combustion system. The full-
scale combustor tests are used to verify the ignition, run-
up efﬁciency, emissions and heat transfer analysis at
realistic simulated and/or engine operating conditions.
Geometrical calibration is under continuous assessment
to ensure that the design intent has been met. By far the
most important aspect in the DLE combustion system
development work is engine air management. Air
percentage and placement will almost guarantee a
healthy combustion process and engine performance. It
becomes common practice for the company combustion
team to identify the air mapping for each of the DLE
conﬁgurations.
The process starts by identifying how much air is
passed by the compressor discharge shroud. This value
may be derived from a performance programme that
identiﬁes the overall engine speciﬁcation. However, the
combustion engineer is constrained by the latter design
criteria requirements in order to achieve a certain
commercial target in the market place.
From the one-dimensional analyses, the operating
swirler and primary zone Mach number can be identiﬁed
for the ﬁrst iteration and hence the combustor diameter
and length calculated. A typical industrial reference
Mach number for the primary zone region should not be
>0.047, based on the combustor diameter. With a given
fuel speciﬁcation, the reaction temperature can be
calculated together with the combustion velocities and
other aerothermodynamic parameters can be identiﬁed.
All other procedures are iterative that may be modiﬁed
and ﬁne tuned using atmospheric and high-pressure air
facility rig results.

PRESSURE DROP AND MACH NUMBER
The ﬂow ﬁeld through a radial inﬂow swirler has a very
complicated structure and is dependent on the geome-
trical shape of the swirler [6]. The ﬂow suffers separation
at the swirler entry which causes a reduction in the open
ﬂow area. This is why such a type of aerodynamic
stabilizer has a low discharge coefﬁcient. The level of
turbulence imposed on the combustion domain controls a
large part of the rate of fuel–air mixing and the
subsequent rate of ﬂame propagation. However, the level
of turbulence is controlled by the combustion system’s
burner pressure drop and blockage.

inﬂuencing the residence time and hence the emission
concentration. It can be employed as a combustion
chamber scaling parameter. The relationship between the
total overall pressure loss and primary zone Mach

NOx AND CO EMISSION RESULTS
Fuels such as natural gas and gas oil contain no or small
amounts of nitrogen. Therefore, NOx formation in the
combustion chamber is caused by a minor contribution
from fuel nitrogen, being largely due to the oxidation of
nitrogen from the combustion air via the Zeldovich
process and the prompt or N2O intermediate mechanism.
The maximum production of NO ends within a residence
time of 2 ms for stabilizers such as the radial swirler
employed for the present work. The time of 2 ms
represents the relaxation time needed for a nitrogen
molecule to go from an excited state to an equilibrium
one. Moreover, the reaction rate for NOx formation at
postcombustion temperatures must be very slow. This
was also demonstrated [12] utilizing similar passage fuel
injection by traversing a 140 mm i.d. combustor on the
atmospheric test rig.
Figure 7 demonstrates typical NOx and CO emissions
for the Tornado (Fig. 7a), Tempest (Fig. 7b), Typhoon
(Fig. 7c) and Cyclone (Figs 7e and f) engines as a func-
tion of engine power output. Also, shown in Fig. 7d is the
emission of the Tornado twin-shaft engine as a function
of corrected power output. The length and diameter of the
combustion chamber and hence the mean residence time
plays little or no part in the NO concentration. However,
the deterioration in the mixing regime during part-load
operation plus assistance of the pilot fuel produced high
values of mixing parameters (see Fig. 6) and hence a
rapid increase in the NOx level.
In a practical engine, it is hard to maintain the same
mixing parameter s without varying the geometry of the
combustion system. There are three conditions that may
exist for high CO production:
1. The cool wall of the combustor quenches the hot
products that slow or freeze the CO depletion rate.
2. The rapid mixing between hot products with cold
gases may be worsened by the shorter residence time.
3. Pockets of unmixed fuel and air escape the hot
combustion zone.
For the present work, the ﬁrst reason may be the
dominant factor that contributes to the ﬁnal CO level at
a part-load engine condition. However, the majority
could be attributed to the unmixedness and the escape of
the unburned pockets from the primary zone region to
cause high CO and UHC emissions. There are optimum
zone operating conditions for minimizing CO levels
across the engine power range, as will be discussed later.
For the present conﬁguration, the optimum means that the
engine would operate near the full-load design equiva-
lence ratio fPZR design.

CFD SUPPORT FOR DLE COMBUSTION
Over the last few years, the use of the computer as an
engineering tool has increased due to better computa-
tional software and facilities in terms of computer access,
storage, speed and relative cost. There has also been a
substantial increase in the use of computational ﬂuid
dynamics (CFD) in gas turbine combustor applications. It
is important to note that the CFD applications for gas
turbine combustors are driven not only by the potential
cost and time savings but also by the ability to provide
additional insight into complex problems that do not lend
themselves to analytical solutions and may be too costly
and time consuming to pursue experimentally.
The use of an effective tool to describe the internal
ﬂow ﬁeld of the combustion system employed for ABB
ALSTOM POWER engines was always considered part
of its technology programme. The particular choice for a
commercial CFD code to satisfy various criteria of the
swirling combusting ﬂow had proved to be difﬁcult due
to the many constraints regarding solver and/or methods
limitation.
STAR-CD CFD and other
codes were
employed to perform the necessary parametric compari-
sons for the combustion system.
A validation programme is currently undergoing
rigorous consideration to ﬁnd the means and methodol-
ogy to improve CFD combusting ﬂow analyses. Some
feasibility studies involve the evaluation and comparison
of two-dimensional with three-dimensional numerical
methods. The saving would be in the scale of 1/24 h of
CPU and man time. Figure 15 illustrates examples of the
current CFD work carried out to give an insight into the
current DLE combustor swirl ﬂow ﬁeld and temperature
distribution. Figure 15a illustrates the STAR-CD numer-
ical solutions for the velocity ﬂow ﬁeld ratio to the
combustor mean approach velocity of the Tempest
combustion system, while Fig. 15b demonstrates the
two-dimensional simulation of the DLE combustor using
CFX-Task ﬂow. The latter code utilizes a turbulent ﬂame
speed based combustion model. The process is still
undergoing considerable validation to prove its viability
as a tool for the practical domain.

CONCLUSION
1. The full DLE burner assembly has demonstrated
reasonable pressure loss and aerodynamics resistance
to ﬂashback to meet the constraints associated with
the Company engines retroﬁt programme and the new
design.
2. Fuel injection through the passages of the radial in-
ﬂow swirler improves fuel and air mixing exten-
sively. The bulk mean residence time was much less
than the spontaneous ignition delay across the engine
ranges. The partial premixing of the fuel and air aided
by the delay in the vortex breakdown of the swirling
ﬂow, together with the aerodynamic shear layers, all
contributed to establishing a well-stirred reactor.
3. The new method of a variable mixing regime
utilizing the three zonal areas of radial passages,
prechamber and the shear layer imposed by the
swirler ﬂow ﬁeld has demonstrated a ﬁnal low mixing
parameter approaching the fully premixed system.
One-dimensional analysis estimated the mean resi-
dence time of 42 ms. This is the relaxation time
needed for a nitrogen molecule to go from an excited
to an equilibrium state and hence the reaction rate for
NOx formation at postcombustion temperatures must
be very slow.
4. Better ignition characteristics and stability margins
were achieved by the current pilot system. This is
attributed to sufﬁcient unmixedness generated by the
pilot in the stabilizing swirling shear layer to give a
considerable extension of the premixed stability
limits imposed by the radial swirler. The ﬁne-tuned
programmed pilot schedule proved its adequacy
across the engine power range. However, pilot fuel
ﬂow is contributing to the rise of NOx emissions due
to the deterioration of the local regional mixing
parameter. It is paramount that the pilot/main swirler
fuel schedule should utilize a rapid actuating pilot
valve through fully integrated engine control system
logic to accommodate any load range including the
load shed condition.
5. The Company DLE combustion system exhibited
low NOx emissions of 15–20 ppmv (corrected to 15%
O2) across all engines ranges on development test
beds and ﬁeld trials. This is achieved with natural gas
while operating a minimum pilot fuel split ratio at
full-load conditions.
6. Ultra low NOx emission 440 ppmv (corrected to
15% O2) is achieved by the ABB ALSTOM POWER
DLE combustion system while operating at full
thermal power with a minimum pilot fuel split using
distillate-2 fuel.
7. The impingement cooling scheme aided by a thin
layer of TBC was shown to give sufﬁcient cooling
effectiveness for the combustion chamber even
though the coolant mass ﬂowrate was reduced to
420 per cent of the total mass delivered by the
compressor to the combustion system. The maximum
combustor wall temperature exhibited by the system
was 4800°C for various thermal loads and turbine
entry temperatures.
