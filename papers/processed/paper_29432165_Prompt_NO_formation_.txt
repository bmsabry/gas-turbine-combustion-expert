Prompt-NO formation in methane/oxygen/nitrogen ﬂames
seeded with oxygenated volatile organic compounds:
Methyl ethyl ketone or ethyl acetate

Abstract
In the present work, CH and NO proﬁles are determined using laser-induced ﬂuorescence (LIF) measurements
in eight low-pressure laminar ﬂames of CH4/O2/N2 containing various amounts of methyl ethyl ketone or ethyl
acetate with respect to the equivalence ratio. Relative CH LIF signals are calibrated using cavity ring-down spec-
troscopy (CRDS), while NO LIF calibration is performed in the burned gases of NO seeded ﬂames. Temperature
measurements are obtained using a coated Pt/Rh thermocouple and serve as temperature proﬁles input for Chemkin
modeling. Volatile organic compound (VOC) submechanisms, previously validated upon major and intermediate
species proﬁles measured using sampling techniques, are incorporated into the GDF-Kin 3.0_NCN for the CH4
oxidation. This mechanism is adjusted and validated to predict the effect of VOCs seeding on CH and NO for-
mation. It takes into account not only the CH and NO proﬁles but those previously measured. When methane is
replaced by either MEK or EA, the NO mole fraction in the burned gases and the CH peak value are found to
jointly decrease, indicating that NO is mainly formed according to the prompt-NO mechanism. According to the
kinetic analysis, the VOC impact on NO formation is demonstrated. It is shown that CH radical formation, through
the C1 sequence, mainly involves the CH3 radical, which is formed with the same propensity by one molecule
of methane, MEK, or EA. Due to the methane replacement by VOC while the equivalence ratio is maintained
constant, we found that the CH peak value decrease follows the total fuel volumetric ﬂow rate decrease, yielding,
an NO decrease in the burned gases.

1. Introduction
Nowdays, tremendous efforts are made to pre-
vent pollutant emissions in the atmosphere. Volatile
organic compounds (VOCs) are known to be pre-
cursors of ozone formation in the troposphere un-
der UV radiations in the presence of NOx. VOCs

are mainly formed from either transportation emis-
sions or solvent industries. Different ways of degra-
dation of VOCs are generally proposed in order to
prevent their release in air: catalytic oxidation or ther-
mal degradation. Thermal degradation requires a rela-
tively high level of temperature (700–1700 K), which
means fuel consumption and NOx formation. This
process presents the main advantage that it works
continuously and is able to treat a large quantity of
compounds without restrictions about their nature,
unlike the catalytic processes. Two different ther-
mal processes are proposed for industrial applica-
tions; regenerative [1] or recuperative [2]. In order
to better overcome NOx formation, it is necessary to
have a better knowledge of the VOCs’ kinetic chem-
istry.
Only a few studies have been performed on the
inﬂuence and/or effect of oxygenated compounds on
NOx formation. In a well-stirred reactor at a temper-
ature lower than 1450 K, it has been shown that in the
presence of oxygenated compounds (involving differ-
ent kinds of C–O bonds), NO reduction by urea can
be obtained not only at a lower temperature but also
within a wider temperature range [3]. The efﬁciency
of the NO reduction was shown to depend on the ad-
ditive but was found to be similar for methyl acetate
and propylaldehyde (both C3 species, respectively
CH3COOCH3 and C2H5CHO). In a small-scale re-
cuperative incinerator, Salvador et al. [2] conducted
a parametric study to improve the operating condi-
tions of industrial devices. The VOCs are added to the
heated air ﬂux before entering into a burner supplied
with natural gas. Different parameters were consid-
ered. Only ethyl acetate was examined in this study.
They showed that NOx emissions decrease when the
ethyl acetate ﬂow rate in air increases while the nat-
ural gas supply to the burner decreases.
The present work aims to propose a detailed ki-
netic model for the oxygenated VOCs’ oxidation in
the presence of N-species in order to predict NO for-
mation in low-pressure methane/O2/N2 ﬂames seeded
with VOCs. Two oxygenated compounds, containing
the same number of C-atoms and H-atoms, represen-
tative of ketones and esters were chosen: methyl ethyl
ketone (MEK), which is commonly used in paint and
adhesive manufacture and application, and ethyl ac-
etate (EA), which is commonly used as a solvent, no-
tably in some segments of the printing industry. The
detailed kinetic scheme is based on two kinds of ﬂame
structure studies performed in our laboratory. The ﬁrst
one was devoted to the fulﬁllment of a kinetic mecha-
nism describing methyl ethyl ketone (CH3COC2H5)
and ethyl acetate (CH3COOC2H5) oxidation [4,5].
Experiments were conducted in low-pressure laminar
ﬂames seeded with either MEK or EA using probe
sampling techniques [6,7]. The second one, dedicated
to NO formation in natural gas ﬂames, made it pos-
sible to propose a new detailed kinetic mechanism,
GDF-Kin 3.0_NCN [8]. Experiments were performed
in natural gas/air ﬂames and focused on the quantita-
tive measurements, using laser induced ﬂuorescence
(LIF), of two major species involved in the prompt-
NO mechanism: CH and NO [9].
In this work, several methane/O2/N2 ﬂames see-
ded with either MEK or EA have been stabilized
and analyzed in terms of NO formation by measuring
NO and CH radicals by laser diagnostics. During this
work, ﬂames have been analyzed at a relatively lean
equivalence ratio with respect to fuel consumption.
Meanwhile, NO formation will be limited. The initial
detailed kinetic scheme has been modiﬁed to better
predict experimental CH and NO proﬁles. Modiﬁca-
tions were performed with respect to species proﬁles
previously measured by GC techniques. It will be
shown that oxidation in the presence of oxygenated
VOCs leads to NO diminution.
2. Experimental setup
Experiments were performed in different lami-
nar, premixed ﬂames stabilized at 5.3 kPa (40 Torr).
The homemade 63-mm-diameter burner (the plate is
equally perforated with 800-µm-diameter holes) is the
one previously used for studies of ﬂames containing
VOCs [6,7]. The inner side is ﬁlled with small balls
to better stabilize the ﬂame. The burner is vertically
mobile in a stainless enclosure. The setup (burner, en-
closure, gas supply lines) was heated at 353 K. The
pressure was controlled via a 0–13.3 kPa pressure
gauge. The facility is equipped with four optical ac-
cesses for the laser diagnostics.
The mixture composition was controlled via mass
ﬂow meters. The liquid VOCs are preheated at 353 K
in an enclosed stainless device in order to work with
the vapor phase. Several ﬂames have been studied.
In a ﬁrst series where MEK has been added to the
mixture, the mixture equivalence ratio is close to
0.78 and the N2/O2 ratio is equal to 3.26. In a sec-
ond series where EA has been added to the mix-
ture, the equivalence ratio is close to 0.85 and the
N2/O2 ratio is equal to 3.10. The N2/O2 ratio value
varies only by 5% between the two series, which does
not affect the temperature proﬁles. A problem in the
mass ﬂow controller calibration procedure was high-
lighted during the data process, explaining the dif-
ference of the equivalence ratio between the MEK
and the EA seeded ﬂames. In order to have a stan-
dard ﬂame for modeling, the study was completed
with a CH4/O2/N2 ﬂame, hereafter called the refer-
ence ﬂame. Premixed gas compositions are given in

3. Temperature proﬁles
The temperature proﬁles, corrected for radia-
tion, were determined using a 100-µm-diameter wire
coated Pt-6%Rh/Pt-30%Rh thermocouple. The ther-
mocouple was placed parallel to the burner surface.
More details about the method and the radiation
losses correction were given previously [6,10]. For
example, at 20 mm above the burner in the reference
ﬂame, the temperature was determined to be 1680 K.
Taking into account the radiation losses, the tempera-
ture was corrected to be 1890 K. The global accuracy
of the temperature measurements is estimated to be
about 5% along the temperature proﬁle. Repeatabil-
ity of the temperature measurements under each set
of ﬂame conditions was better than ±5 K.
4. Laser diagnostics
The laser system consists of a frequency-doubled
Quantel Nd:YAG laser pumping a dye laser. Wave-
lengths around 317 nm for the CH radical excitation
were provided by doubling the fundamental dye ra-
diation (obtained with a mixture of dyes: Rh640 and
DCM) pumped with 532 nm. For the NO excitation,
wavelengths around 225 nm were obtained by mixing
the frequency-doubled output of the dye laser (con-
stituted by a mixture of Rh590 and Rh610) with the
residual infrared radiation of the YAG laser. The re-
sulting FWHM linewidth was around 1 cm−1. Dye
laser wavelengths were systematically calibrated with
the LIF-excitation spectra using the LIFBASE soft-
ware [11].
Species (CH and NO) proﬁles were locally mea-
sured by LIF. The laser beam was focused be-
fore entering into the burner enclosure (focal length
500 mm). The laser energy ﬂuctuations were moni-
tored by a postﬂame photodiode. The LIF signal was
collected at f/4 by a two-lens system and focused
on the entrance slit of a 0.25-m monochromator, pro-
viding a high degree of rejection of background ra-
diation. The entrance slit was parallel to the laser
axis, and the output slit was adjustable to provide a
bandpass adapted to the ﬂuorescence band under in-
vestigation. The ﬂuorescence spectra were collected
with a 1.5-ns rise-time Philips XP2020Q photomul-
tiplier tube. The ﬂuorescence and laser intensity sig-
nals were simultaneously resolved and averaged over
500 pulses and stored by a digital scope (LECROY
9354A, 8-bit, 500-MHz bandwidth, 1-GS/s sampling
rate). LIF signals were measured at their temporal
peak to minimize the effect of quantum yield vari-
ation along the ﬂame on N(J ′′) determination. The
LIF signal being proportional to the rotational pop-
ulation N(J ′′), conversion into total density num-
ber has been completed by dividing the signal by
the Boltzmann fraction fB(J ′′,T ), easily calculated
using the experimental temperature data. Compari-
son with modeling data being based on the species
mole fraction, this last one is derived from the to-
tal density and the ideal gas law. The speciﬁc con-
ditions of the relative LIF measurements are given
below.
The relative species proﬁles in each ﬂame were
measured by LIF. Thus, the peak LIF signals obtained
in the different ﬂames have been linked to each other
by comparing their LIF intensity to that obtained in
the reference ﬂame (see Table 1), for which a direct
calibration has been undertaken. The “linking” proce-
dure has made it necessary to change the ﬂame con-
ditions between ﬂame (i) and the reference ﬂame and
to compare the peak LIF signals. That procedure has
been repeated several times to limit the uncertainty.
The standard deviation was less than 3%. The calibra-
tion of the LIF signals obtained in the reference ﬂame
was performed in the zone where the species concen-
tration is maximal. So it was performed at the peak
value for the CH calibration and at 20 mm above the
burner for the NO calibration.

4.1. CH measurements
CH LIF measurements were performed using
the predissociative C2Σ+ ←X2Π(0,0) state near
315 nm, according to an excitation/detection scheme
previously used in the lab [9,12]. The doublet P1(10)/
P2(10) near 317.3 nm was selected for the CH exci-
tation because it is characterized by a weak variation
of the ratio fB(J ′′,T )/T in the ﬂame temperature
range. CH measurements at low pressure are weakly
affected by quenching variation because of the predis-
sociative feature of the excited state. In order to reject
all the interferences due to the ﬂame emission, the
ﬂuorescence was collected in the entire Q-branch at
314.7 nm with a narrow bandwidth of 3.0 nm selected
by the monochromator.
The calibration step was performed by cavity ring-
down spectroscopy (CRDS) measurements in the ref-
erence ﬂame. The laser beam was shaped with a sys-
tem of a 100-µm pinhole aperture and two 100-mm
focal lenses in order to match approximately the
TEM00 transverse mode of the CRD cavity. The
390-mm-length CRD cavity consisted of two high-
reﬂectivity mirrors (radius curvature 250 mm, R =
99.7% at 317 nm). The CRD signal was recorded
using the same PMT and scope, both as in LIF mea-
surements, and processed in order to measure the
decay time of the CRD cavity. The measurements
were performed at the CH-peak by scanning the laser
around the P1(8) and P2(8) lines (on resonance). The
CRD lifetime was found to be 400 ns with the laser
tuned off resonance and decreased to about 335 ns
with the laser tuned on resonance (with the P1(8)
line) at the CH peak location. From the measured
lifetimes on and off resonance, one obtains the inte-
grated absorptivity according to the procedure pre-
viously described [13]. The P1(8) and P2(8) lines
have been preferred because they are separated one
from the other, unlike the doublet P1(10)/P2(10), for
which a deconvolution is required to determine the
integrated absorptivity. From the integrated absorptiv-
ity of the lines P1(8) and P2(8) and considering the
absorption constant across the ﬂame diameter (one-
dimensional ﬂame), the absolute number density of
the excited rotational level (J ′′ = 8) was determined.
Knowing the temperature at the measured location
(T = 1725 ± 100 K), the CH-peak mole fraction was
determined as being 2.7 ± 0.2 ppm in the reference
ﬂame.
As previously pointed out [9,14], due to the fact
that the CRDS is a line-of-sight absorption technique,
inhomogeneities along the laser beam in the entire
ﬂame diameter have to be taken into account, since
they lead to underestimation of the concentration on
the burner axis. Luque et al. [14] have shown, using
planar LIF, that corrections could reach 20%. Sim-

4.2. NO measurements
NO-LIF measurements in ﬂames have been per-
formed by excitation of the A2Σ+ ←X2Π(0–0)
band (Q1(23) or Q2(27) lines) near 225.6 nm.
The ﬂuorescence collection was achieved using the
A2Σ+ ←X2Π(0–2) band at 246 nm with a band-
width of 8 nm selected by the monochromator.
The excitation range is possibly disturbed by O2
Schumann–Runge bands. Caution has been taken
to prevent this background signal by recording the
off resonance signals [15]. In the presence of MEK
in the premixed gases, the off resonance (at 225.45
nm) proﬁles show an increase around the ﬂame front
zone (Fig. 1). This effect was negligible in ﬂames
containing EA. The background signal in the pres-
ence of MEK may be attributed to acetone emis-
sion and/or ﬂuorescence, which was shown to be
present in the ﬂame front of MEK-containing ﬂames
from previous gas chromatography (GC) measure-
ments [6]. The background signal in the ﬂame 3-
MEK could reach 55% of the total signal collected
at 246 nm. It was subtracted on the basis of on–
off measurements. Finally, the relative NO proﬁles
were determined from the difference between the
on and the off resonance signals in each ﬂame, i.e.,
the off-resonance-corrected LIF signal. The LIF sig-

NO. When NO was added in the premixed gases, the
off-resonance-corrected LIF signal (LIF(on–off)(total

gas (native NO + added NO). Fig. 2 shows that
the off-resonance-corrected LIF signal 20 mm above
the burner depends linearly on the NO added in
the reference ﬂame indicating negligible reburning.
The native NO was determined according to the in-
verse of the slope of the quantity ((LIF(on–off)(total
NO)/LIF(on–off)(native NO)) −1) as a function of the
added NO. In the reference ﬂame, at 20 mm above the
burner, the native NO was found to be 11.6 ppm with
an accuracy of 15%.

5. Modeling
5.1. Methodology
The premix code of the Chemkin-II package
[18,19] was used during this work in order to perform
modeling computations. Kinetic analysis is essen-
tially based on the rate of production and consump-
tion. The rate of production/consumption of each
species is systematically computed using the appro-
priate subroutines in the Chemkin package (CKQYP,
CKCONT) [19]. Following the Chemkin formalism,
for each species k and each reaction i, the rate of
progress is given by Rk,i = νk,iqi, where νk,i is the
stoichiometric number relative to species k in reaction
i, and qi is the net rate of reaction i. The summa-
tion of Rk,i over the I reactions is the production
rate of species k. For species k, the involved reactions
are sorted out in respect to their maximum absolute
value. Their sign indicates if the species is consumed
or produced. Then they are rearranged to determine
the species ﬂux when the same species are involved
in both hands. The rate of progress depending on the
species concentration and the temperature depends on
the location as well. So, in order to better account for
this variation along the burner axis, we choose to con-
sider the species molar ﬂux by integrating the rate of

5.2. Initial mechanism
The detailed kinetic scheme devoted to NO forma-
tion prediction in methane/air ﬂames containing VOC
(MEK or EA) requires three submechanisms: (i) for
the methane oxidation, (ii) for the VOC oxidation, and
(iii) a full description of nitrogen chemistry.
The VOC submechanisms (for methyl ethyl ke-
tone and ethyl acetate oxidation), proposed by Decot-
tignies et al. [4] and Gasnot et al. [5], involve 156
reactions and 30 species. They both involve propanal,
acetone, acetaldehyde, ethanol, DME (dimethyl et-
her), and methanol oxidation submechanisms. The
MEK oxidation involve a methyl vinyl ketone (MVK)
oxidation submechanism, while the EA oxidation in-
volve acetic acid and vinyl acetate oxidation sub-
mechanisms. The VOC submechanisms were initially
validated in conjunction with the GRI2.11 mechanism
[20] for the methane oxidation. Full details concern-
ing their validation were given in [4,5]. Hereafter this
mechanism will be called the VOC-GRI2.11 mecha-
nism.
The recent detailed mechanism GDF-Kin 3.0_
NCN proposed by El-Bakali et al. [8] appeared to
offer a good compromise for points (i) and (iii). It
has been fully validated for natural gas oxidation in
the presence of N species. It takes into account the
new route of NCN contribution in the prompt NO for-
mation. This model involves 883 reactions and 121
species.
In this work, the GRI2.11 mechanism was re-
placed by the GDF-Kin 3.0_NCN one. In the case of
duplicate reactions (for example, as for the methanol
or acetaldehyde oxidation submechanisms), the ones
given in the GDF-Kin 3.0_NCN were adopted. The
resulting mechanism, hereafter called the initial mech-
anism, has been associated with thermodynamic and
transport databases initially considered. The reaction
number affected to each reaction refers to the origi-
nal order of the reactions of the GDF-Kin 3.0_NCN
followed by the VOC submechanisms (starting from
reaction 884).
5.3. Validation of the mechanism
The initial mechanism has been tested on the ba-
sis of the comparison between the experimental and
the predicted proﬁles of CH radical and NO. Mean-
while, caution has been paid to preserve the initial
overall good agreement between the experimental and
the modeling proﬁles (according to the VOC-GRI2.11
mechanism) of the major species involved into the ox-
idation process of both methane and VOC. As a ﬁrst
step, the initial mechanism was tested according to the
previous experimental data measured in low-pressure
methane/O2/N2 ﬂames containing different amounts
of either MEK or EA [6,7].
The comparison between the experimental species
proﬁles and those simulated showed that acetone
was absolutely not predicted according to the ini-
tial mechanism. Decottignies et al. [4] showed that
acetone is mainly produced by the recombination
reaction between CH3 and CH3CO radicals (956)
CH3COCH3 = CH3 + CH3CO. It appeared that the
rate constant of the reaction (244) CH3CO + M =
CH3 +CO+M, initially selected according to Wilk et
al. [21] in GDF-Kin 3.0_NCN, led to a very fast con-
sumption of the CH3CO radical, inhibiting acetone
formation. So we introduced the value, ﬁrst selected
in the VOC submechanisms [4,5] according to the
recommendations of Baulch et al. [22] and recently
conﬁrmed by ab initio calculation [23]. Finally, in or-
der to improve the acetone prediction by the model,
the rate constant of the reaction (956) proposed by
Sato and Hidaka [24] was selected.
Modiﬁcation of the methane oxidation due to the
replacement of the GRI2.11 mechanism by the GDF-
Kin 3.0_NCN one affects principally the C2H6 and
C3H8 simulated proﬁles. Based on the experimen-
tal proﬁles reported by Gasnot et al. [6,7] and the
present ones (for CH and NO), few rate constant ad-
justments were proposed, as reported in Table 2. The
involved reaction rate constants (except for reactions
(244), (956), (967), and (985)) were initially esti-
mated by analogy with the unsaturated hydrocarbon
species according to Warnatz [25] for H-abstraction

is formed in the ﬂame front area where the CH radical
is present according to the prompt-NO mechanism.

indicating negligible contribution of the thermal-NO
mechanism. Nevertheless, it is also noteworthy that
the NO production starts upstream from the CH radi-

According to kinetic considerations, NO is formed in
the reaction zone while the experimental and simu-

the reaction zone close to the burner surface. This
might be due to preferential species diffusion towards
the cold burner surface.

very low CH peak and NO mole fraction (a few ppm).

6.3. Discussions
Considering the good agreement between exper-
imental and simulated proﬁles, modeling has been
used to analyze the CH formation pathways in or-
der to understand the VOC impact on the CH forma-
tion and consequently on the (prompt) NO formation.
CH radicals being mainly formed through the C1 se-
quence, the CH3 pathways are ﬁrst detailed and com-
pared under four different ﬂame conditions, ref/MEK
and 2-MEK, and ref/EA and 2-EA, in order to high-
light the VOC contribution to the CH formation via
the CH3 route.
6.3.1. CH3 rates of production and consumption
The rates of production of the CH3 radical were
calculated according to the detailed kinetic model
proposed in this work, under different ﬂame condi-
tions. Comparison between the pathways in seeded
and nonseeded ﬂames has made it possible to char-
acterize a speciﬁc CH3 route of formation when VOC
is added in the gas.
In methane/O2/N2 ﬂames, the analysis has shown
that the CH3 radical (well known to be formed by H-
abstraction of CH4 reactions) is mainly produced ac-
cording to only three major steps: (39) CH4 + OH =
CH3 + H2O, (40) CH4 + O = CH3 + OH, and (41)
CH4 + H = CH3 + H2. According to the CH3 molar
ﬂux, these three reactions are responsible for 95% of
the total amount of CH3 production.

mixed gases, new steps are responsible for the CH3
radical formation: (965) CH3COCH2 = CH2CO +
CH3, (−57) CH3 + CH3 = C2H5 + H, (912) C2H5-

(910) C2H5COCH3 = CH3 + C2H5CO, and (934)
CH3CHCOCH3 = CH3CHCO + CH3. These reac-

6.3.3. NO formation
Recently, the NCN route in prompt-NO forma-
tion has been proposed as the new route instead
of the classical HCN route [28,29]. A few authors
have replaced the reaction CH + N2 = HCN + N
by CH + N2 = NCN + H in kinetic models. El-
Bakali et al. [8] found that the theoretical rate constant
of the reaction CH + N2 = NCN + H proposed by
Moskaleva and Lin [28] led to an important under-
estimation of prompt-NO formation. El-Bakali et al.
[8] found that a rate constant (k(cm3/mols) = 5.1 ×
1011 exp(−13,600/RT )) led to satisfactory agree-
ment between experimental and simulated results.
However, the kinetic rate constant of this reaction
still requires validation. Full discussion of the rate
constant value choice and validation in the GDF-Kin
3.0_NCN mechanism is proposed by El-Bakali et al.
[8]. It has not been changed in the present version of
the mechanism. The ability of this mechanism to pre-
dict NO formation has been conﬁrmed in the present
work in various lean VOC-containing ﬂames exhibit-
ing very low concentrations of CH and NO without
any change of the nitrogen chemistry.
Williams and Fleming [30] recently proposed to
include the C2O route in the prompt-NO formation
in order to take into account the stoichiometry and
fuel type effect on the NO formation. So the pos-
sible role of C2O as precursor has been considered
here because of the propensity of VOC oxidation to
form C2O. As shown in Fig. 5, from CH3CHCOCH3
radical, CH3CHCO is mainly formed with CH3, lead-
ing to CH2CCO and then to C2O. Due to MEK, the
maximum value of C2O concentration can reach 100
times the value simulated without the MEK added,
keeping constant the equivalence ratio. In the pres-

ence of EA in the premixed gas, the maximum value
of C2O density is close to the one simulated in the
CH4/O2/N2 ﬂame, keeping constant the equivalence

the one initially postulated in the GDF-Kin 3.0_NCN

7. Conclusions
LIF measurements of CH radicals and NO in low-
pressure CH4/O2/N2 ﬂames containing either MEK
(methyl ethyl ketone) or EA (ethyl acetate) have been
performed in order to examine the VOC seeding effect
on the NO formation. CH–LIF signal (using a pre-
dissociative state of CH: C2Σ+ ←X2Π(0,0) with
a collection bandpass limited to the Q-branch) was
calibrated using CRDS measurements. NO–LIF sig-
nal (using the A2Σ+ ←X2Π state by excitation of
the (0–0) band and collection of the (0–2) band in
order to minimize the background perturbation) was
calibrated using NO seeded procedure. Special at-
tention has been paid in order to take into account
background effects [15,16] and possible NO reburn-
ing effects [17]. Relative NO–LIF signals were sys-
tematically determined from the difference between
the on and the off resonance signals. During the cal-
ibration procedure, the same attention was followed
by recording the LIF signals (on/off resonance) in the
burned gases for different amounts of NO added in
the ﬂame.
Measurements along the ﬂame have been realized
for different ﬂame compositions with a close to con-
stant total volumetric ﬂow rate. In a ﬁrst series where
MEK has been added to the mixture, the mixture
equivalence ratio is close to 0.78. In a second series
where EA has been added to the mixture, the equiva-
lence ratio is close to 0.85. Temperature proﬁles have
been measured using a coated Pt/Rh thermocouple.
A detailed mechanism for both MEK and EA ox-
idation has been validated according to CH and NO
proﬁles. This mechanism consists of MEK and EA
submechanisms previously proposed by Decottignies
et al. [4] and Gasnot et al. [5]. These submechanisms
initially incorporated with the GRI2.11 mechanism
(for CH4 oxidation) were validated under intermedi-
ates proﬁles measured using GC sampling techniques.
In order to take into account the N2 chemistry, the
GRI2.11 mechanism [20] was replaced by the GDF-
Kin 3.0_NCN one, recently proposed in our Lab [8].
Few rate constant values have been adjusted or up-
dated in order ﬁrstly to maintain the initial good
agreement between experimental and simulated pro-
ﬁles for the major and intermediate species, and sec-
ondly to improve the agreement between experimen-
tal and simulated species proﬁles of CH and NO.
When CH4 is substituted by either MEK or EA in
the ﬂame, CH-peak values decrease similarly to the
NO mole fraction in the burned gases, indicating that
NO is mainly formed according to the prompt-NO
mechanism. Considering the overall good agreement
between experimental and simulated proﬁles, a mod-
eling study has been performed in order to understand
the VOC impact on CH radical and NO formation.
According to the C1 sequence, analysis of the CH3
radical formation, mainly based on species ﬂux deter-
mination, made it possible to show that ﬁrst the CH3
radical is consumed independent of its source of for-
mation; second one molecule of VOC (MEK or EA)
has the same ability for CH3 formation as one mole-
cule of methane (even if the considered VOC contain
four times as many C-atoms than CH4). The CH-peak
values decrease is close to the total fuel volumetric
ﬂow rates diminishing when methane is substituted by
a VOC, which is consistent with the fact that a mole-
cule of VOC presents the same propensity to the CH3
formation as a molecule of CH4.
Since the NO formation follows mainly the prompt-
NO mechanism, the CH-peak values decrease implies
that the NO mole fraction diminishes in the burned
gases. So, the VOC oxidation leads to less NO release
than a CH4-based ﬂame in respect to the equivalence
ratio.
