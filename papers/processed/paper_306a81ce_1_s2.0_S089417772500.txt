A B S T R A C T
Swirl cup airblast fuel injectors are critical components in modern low-emission gas turbine combustors. Un­
derstanding the underlying physics of the interaction between fuel spray and complex swirling airflow is crucial 
for optimizing injector performance. This study investigates the influence of Venturi outlet angles (15◦, 25◦, and 
35◦) on airflow field and spray atomization dynamics. Advanced optical diagnostics, including high-speed 
shadowgraph, Phase Doppler Particle Analyzer (PDPA), Particle Imaging Velocimetry (PIV), and Planar Mie 
scattering (PMie), were employed to quantify flow-spray interactions under controlled fuel flow rates (2.0–4.0 
kg/h) and 3 % relative air pressure drop. Results demonstrate that the Venturi outlet angle significantly mod­
ulates primary atomization. Increasing the angle from 15◦to 35◦reduces liquid film length by 69.2 % due to 
enhanced gas–liquid shear stress. Moreover, larger angles amplify central toroidal recirculation zone (CTRZ) 
reverse velocity (−1.2 to −6.8 m/s), intensifying droplet entrainment and reducing Sauter Mean Diameter (SMD) 
by 30.9 %. These findings highlight that Venturi angles > 25◦optimize atomization by balancing shear-driven 
breakup and recirculation-enhanced mixing, providing critical insights for designing fuel injectors with 
improved combustion stability and emission performance.
1. Introduction
Modern gas turbine combustors increasingly adopt lean combustion 
strategies (e.g., Lean Direct Injection (LDI) and Lean Premixed perva­
porated (LPP)) to meet stringent emission regulations [1]. Currently, 
swirl cup airblast fuel injectors have become predominant in gas turbine 
combustors, due to their superior atomization and homogeneous 
fuel–air mixing capabilities before combustion [2,3]. Establishing a 
detailed physical understanding of the fluid dynamics in the swirl cup 
airblast fuel injectors, particularly the breakup of liquid sheets and the 
formation of sprays in swirling flows, is crucial for advancing the 
development of next-generation, low-emission gas turbine combustors 
[4].
The atomization process within swirl cup airblast fuel injectors in­
volves multiscale physical phenomena spanning from the near field 
(primary atomization) to far field (secondary atomization) [5]. This 
complex sequence includes: (1) liquid sheet formation at the pressure- 
swirl atomizer [6], (2) spray-wall interactions and film formation 
[7,8], (3) rim destabilization through air shear [7], (4) primary droplet 
generation via Rayleigh-Taylor instabilities [9] (5) subsequent droplet 
fragmentation through aerodynamic secondary breakup [10]. (6) Sec­
ondary breakup, drop collisions and coalescence [11,12], (7) spray in­
teractions with turbulent eddies, cluster formation, drop heat transfer 
and evaporation [13]. Recent studies have systematically examined 
critical operational and geometric parameters influencing spray dy­
namics. Key operational factors include including airflow velocity[14] 
and liquid fuel mass flow rate [15],as well as fuel injector geometric 
parameters including geometric swirl number (SN) [16], swirling flow 
directions [17], Venturi [18], flare [19], swirler angle [20], etc.
Candel et al. [21] Ying et al. [22] Jeng et al. [23] investigated the 
effects of the operation parameters on the spray characteristics. The 
studies concluded that swirling flow is characterized by the Central

Toroidal Recirculation Zone (CTRZ). The dimensions of the CTRZ are 
regarded as a crucial factor in determining the topological structure of 
the spray. Due to the presence of the CTRZ, there is a significant gradient 
in velocities between liquid droplets and the gas flow, leading to notable 
momentum exchange between the airflow and spray droplets. All 
droplets of various diameters move downstream within the spray cone, 
with smaller droplets recirculating upstream within the spray cone, and 
larger droplets decelerating downstream. Bokhart et al. [24,25] focused 
their research on the spray characteristics of different fuels under high- 
temperature and high-pressure conditions. The characteristics studied 
included droplet size and diameter. The results showed that under high- 
temperature and high-pressure conditions, the injection pressure has 
little effect on droplets size.
Regarding the influence of injector geometric parameters, numerous 
scholars have conducted extensive research. Fu et al. [26] studied the 
effect of confinement on swirling flow within a swirl cup. Their findings 
suggest that confinement affects the dimensions and intensity of the 
recirculation zone. Billant et al. [27] explored the correlation between 
vortex breakdown and the SN. Their study revealed that vortex break­
down occurred when the SN reached a critical threshold. Hadef et al. 
[28] examined the influence of swirling flow directions, particularly the 
effects of co-swirl and counter-swirl on droplet behavior. The research 
shows that the counter-swirler configurations promote a higher level of 
turbulence, which enhances fuel–air mixing and leads to a more even 
spray distribution. Kumar et al. [29] studied the impact of co-swirling 
and counter-swirling flows on droplet distribution and size, finding 
that the shear forces between the primary and secondary swirling flows 
are more pronounced in the counter-swirling case, thereby enhancing 
secondary atomization and improving spray uniformity. Merkle et al. 
[30] investigated how co-swirling and counter-swirling flows affect the 
lean stability limits. The findings suggest that the counter-swirler 
configuration enables a higher thermal load and a broader operational 
range compared to the co-swirler configurations. Rajamanickam et al. 
[31] investigated the effects of swirling flow direction and flare angles 
on droplet behavior and the flow field. Their study demonstrates that 
variations in swirling flow direction and flare design significantly alter 
the flow field’s topological structure and subsequent dynamics, noting a 
decreased axial extent of the CTRZ with counter-rotating swirls, sug­
gesting a reduction in tangential velocity. Lin et al. [32] explored the 
effect of the Venturi on non-reactive flows, concluding that a Venturi 
with reduced swirling strength can effectively mitigate combustion in­
stabilities in lean fuel regions. Ateshkadi et al. [33] used Planar Laser- 
Induced Fluorescence (PLIF) to assess the Venturi’s impact on atomi­
zation and fuel distribution, revealing that the Venturi’s structure plays 
a crucial role in the liquid film’s breakup process and the subsequent 
droplet distribution, with the Venturi confining the spray to a more 
focused area than cases without it. Jeung et al.[34] investigated the 
influence of the Venturi tip angle on the flow field and atomization 
process. Their research indicates that the Venturi tip angle has a pro­
nounced effect on the position and form of both internal and external 
recirculation zones, which in turn impacts the spray breakup charac­
teristics. A converging Venturi tip angle intensifies the velocity gradient 
between the primary and secondary swirlers, resulting in a decrease in 
the Sauter Mean Diameter (SMD). Fang et al.[35] applied the BP neural 
network to predict SMD as a function of geometry, condition parame­
ters. The result shows that the BP neural network is a novel and effective 
way to predict the SMD of droplets generated by a swirl cup airblast fuel 
injector. Han et al.[36] found that adjusting the fuel injector position 
modifies the fuel spray pattern’s structure, with moving the injector 
downstream lessening the aerodynamic influence on the nozzle and 
leading to an increase in SMD at the spray center.
The poor atomization quality adversely affects kernel generation and 
flame propagation, ultimately leading to deteriorated ignition perfor­
mance. [37,38] The flame propagation speed in the annular combustor 
is influenced by atomization quality and droplet size distribution. As 
atomization quality improves, it becomes easier for ignition to 
propagate between adjacent injectors. This effect is particularly pro­
nounced near the lean blowout limits[39]. In the meantime, an over 
misty spray can intensely increase the flame speed, which increases NOx 
emission and narrows the combustor’s operating equivalence ratio 
range. Liu et al.[40] examined the influence of spray characteristics on 
flame structures and combustion stability, highlighting the critical role 
of spray quality in maintaining combustion stability during ignition and 
near lean blow-out (LBO) conditions. Colby et al.[41] studied swirl cup 
injection and observed that larger droplets tend to traverse the main 
recirculation region rapidly with short residence times, whereas smaller 
droplets experience longer residence times, contributing to the stabili­
zation of the lean flame.
Despite numerous research have explored the flow field, spray 
characteristics, and combustion performance of swirl cup airblast in­
jectors [14–34]. A comprehensive understanding between Venturi 
structures and complex swirling fields remains elusive. Prior works 
primarily focused on swirl number [16], flow direction [17,21–24], and 
flare angles [19,24]. Venturi outlet angle – a key geometric control 
parameter for shear layer dynamics – remains quantitatively unchar­
acterized. While Jeung et al. [34] noted its influence on recirculation 
zones, systematic quantification of its impact on liquid film breakup (e. 
g., thickness, length) and droplet entrainment is absent. Moreover 
Existing studies [19,26,33] relied on isolated measurements (e.g., PLIF 
for fuel distribution or PDPA for droplet size). No prior work integrates 
simultaneous diagnostics for film dynamics (shadowgraph), flow fields 
(PIV), and droplet statistics (PDPA/PMie) – essential for decoupling 
primary/secondary atomization mechanisms modulated by Venturi 
angles.
To address this gap, the current study utilized an array of advanced 
laser-based diagnostic techniques, including Phase Doppler Particle 
Analyzer (PDPA), high-speed Particle Image Velocimetry (PIV), and 
high-speed Planar Mie scattering (PMie), high-speed shadowgraph, 
combined with three Venturi outlet angles (15◦, 25◦, and 35◦) configu­
rations to quantify key parameters such as SMD, droplet velocity, air 
flow velocity, and spray angle, film thickness. The research aimed to 
discern the effects of the Venturi outlet angles on spray characteristics 
and the dynamics of droplets under different the Venturi outlet angles. 
This further aimed to refine the swirl-spray coupling model and provide 
engineering guidance for optimizing low-emission combustion systems.
The structure of the paper is as follows: Section II details the geo­
metric parameters of the swirl cup airblast fuel injector and the exper­
imental methodology, including the diagnostic tools employed. Section 
III presents a discussion of the findings on the interaction between 
swirling flow, atomization, and droplet size or spatial distribution, based 
on experimental data and theoretical analysis. The paper concludes with 
a summary of the key findings and conclusions in Section IV.
2. Experimental apparatus and methodology
2.1. Swirl cup airblast fuel injector configuration and atomization test rig
As shown in Fig. 1, the swirl cup airblast fuel injector consists of four 
key components, which are pressure-swirl atomizer, Venturi, inner 
swirler, and outer swirler. The pressure-swirl atomizer is located at the 
center of the swirl cup airblast fuel injector, which produces an 80◦
hollow cone spray with 28 μ m measured SMD. It operates at a pressure 
of 0.7 MPa and delivers a fuel flow rate of 5.68 L/h. The Venturi has 15 
mm diameter component that directs the airflow and influences spray 
breakup dynamics. The pressure swirl atomizer is installed 11.5 mm 
from the Venturi exit. The inner swirler comprises eight elliptical 
tangential channels, this component induces a central toroidal recircu­
lation zone (CTRZ) through its swirling airflow. The outer swirler fea­
tures ten radial blocks, this component generates a swirling jet zone 
(SJZ) that interacts with the CTRZ. During operation, fuel droplets 
produced by the pressure-swirl atomizer collide with the Venturi’s inner 
surface, forming a thin liquid film. The intense swirling airflow at the

2.2. Optical diagnostic methods
Four advanced laser-based techniques (PDPA, High-speed PIV, High- 
speed shadowgraph, and High-speed PMie) were employed to quantify 
spray characteristics and flow dynamics.
Fig. 2 illustrates the measurement areas for PIV, PMie, and shad­
owgraph techniques, as well as the positions measured by PDPA. The 
PIV and PMie measurement areas are two identical rectangular regions 
of 80.0 × 70.0 mm2, which are symmetrical about the swirl cup airblast 
fuel injector center line. The high-speed shadowgraph technique was 
employed to capture the overall breakup within the Venturi with a field 
of view encompassing 16.0 × 7.0 mm2. Additionally, for the breakup at 
the edge of the Venturi, a more focused field of view was utilized, 
measuring 7.65 × 4.2 mm2, respectively. In the PDPA measurements, 
the radial distribution of the spray is sampled at 2 mm intervals, until 
reached the radial boundary. These measurements are carried out at 
eight axial positions (z = 5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60 mm). Taking the 
outer swirler exit diameter, d=15 mm as the normalization parameter, 
the radial and axial positions are in the range ofr/d = 0.00 ~ 1.33 in the 
radial direction andz/d = 0.33 ~ 4.00 in the vertical direction.
PIV and PMie systems were used to capture the 2-D flow field ve­
locity distribution and spray angle of the swirl cup airblast fuel injector 
configuration. 527 nm dual-pulsed laser (2000 Hz, 30mJ/pulse) with a 
cylindrical lens system (1 mm waist thickness) was used to illuminate 
the PIV flow tracing particle and fuel droplet. The scattered light from 
the PIV flow tracing particle or fuel droplet was collected by a Nikon 
105 mm f/2.8 lens and delivered to a laser synchronized highspeed 
camera operated at 2000 Hz in double frame mode for 1 s imaging 
duration. A spatial resolution of 7.2 pixels/mm was obtained with the 
field of view. A bandpass filter of 532 ± 10 nm filter was mounted in 
front of the lens to reject the scattered light from the surrounding 
environment. For the PIV measurement, the airflow was seeded by 
Ethylene glycol–water liquid particles with a mean diameter of 2 μm. By 
calculating the particle characteristic stop time scale (τp) and fluid me­
chanical time scale (τf) under all test conditions, the Stokes number 
(Stk = τp/τf) is much less than 0.5, indicating the particles are able to 
track the airflow.[44] Adaptive PIV with cross-correlation technique 
and multipass decreasing interrogation area size was used to construct 
the vector field from the set of raw images. The interrogation window 
sizes were progressively decreased from 64 × 64 to 32 × 32 pixels using 
a multi-pass algorithm. The velocity measured by the PIV was validated 
by LDV in a turbulent jet flow. The difference between the velocity 
measured by PIV and that measured by LDV was less than 10 %. Ac­
cording to the equipment manual, the accuracy of the PIV system for 
airflow velocity measurement is ± 1 %. By using the root-sum-of- 
squares (RSS) method to account for uncertainty propagation, the 
total uncertainty of the airflow velocity measurement was calculated to 
be 1.01 %.
The high-speed shadowgraph system is outfitted with a powerful 
100 W light-emitting diode (LED) flash lamp, specifically designed for 
back-illuminating the liquid film to enhance the visibility of the breakup 
process. This system is complemented by a high-speed camera capable of 
capturing the intricate details of film disintegration. To obtain clear 
images of the liquid film breaking up at the Venturi rim, the camera was 
set to operate at a staggering frequency of 20,000 Hz, with an ultra-short 
exposure time of 1/6, 300,000 s, ensuring the capture of even the fastest 
dynamics. For optimal imaging, the camera was equipped with both a 
0.9 × telecentric lens (62901 Edmund optics) and a 2.0 × telecentric 
lens (63731 Edmund optics), which allowed for a versatile field of view 
and achieved resolutions of 0.0260 pixels/mm and 0.0102 pixels/mm, 
respectively, ensuring detailed and precise data collection. By using the 
RSS method to account for uncertainty propagation, the total uncer­
tainty of liquid film thickness measurements obtained via the high-speed 
shadowgraph system was calculated to be 2.03 %.
The PDPA was used to measure the droplet size and velocity. It has 
three semiconductor lasers (488.5, 514.5, and 532 nm) operated at 40 
MHz frequency shift. The transmitting optics split the laser into two 
beams, focused to a 500 mm focal length. The receiving optic with a 750 
mm focal length was positioned in the forward scattering direction. The 
laser emitted by the transmitting optics is scattered by droplets and then 
enters the receiving optics. Subsequently, the received optical signal was 
then transferred to the PDPA processor to be converted to an electrical 
signal and sent to the computer. The measured SMD from PDPA was 
validated by measuring the size of traceable particle size certified 
reference materials (GBW(E)120138). The difference between the PDPA 
result and the reference materials’ size were in a range of 9 %. The PDPA 
system was capable of measuring droplets within a size range of 1–500 
μm, constrained by a 5 μm aperture filter, and had a velocity sensitivity 
of 0.1 m/s. The measurement accuracy of the PDPA system for droplet 
size is 3 %. By using the RSS method to account for uncertainty propa­
gation, the total uncertainty of liquid film thickness measurements was 
calculated to be 3.02 %.

3. Result and discussion
3.1. Flow and spray characteristics
Fig. 3 displays the time-averaged flow fields in the central plane for 
Venturi outlet angles of 15◦, 25◦, and 35◦under ΔPa = 3%. The red line 
is an isoline with an axial velocity of zero, representing the Central 
Toroidal Recirculation Zone (CTRZ) boundary.
From the Fig. 3, it can be observed that the flow fields of all con­
figurations exhibit an axisymmetric hollow cone-shaped structure, with 
two distinct flow zones identified in the flow field: The CTRZ occupies 
the central region with reversed axial flow, sustained by adverse pres­
sure gradients. The Swirling Jet Zone (SJZ) forms a hollow conical shear 
layer at the edge of the swirler, characterized by high axial velocity 
components. Additionally, for all configurations, the maximum velocity 
magnitude (~25 m/s) occurs at the Venturi exit and the velocity 
magnitude decayed to < 15 m/s atr/d = 1.5 downstream due to viscous 
dissipation and momentum redistribution. This momentum redistribu­
tion arises from flow expansion in the unconfined region, where the 
conservation of axial momentum flux drives radial spreading. The 
resultant pressure recovery establishes the CTRZ through flow reversal 
near the axis.
Notably, the Case 1 configuration (θ = 15◦, as seen in Fig. 3(a)) 
demonstrates divergent inner swirl flow patterns that intensify mo­
mentum exchange between inner and outer swirl streams. Compared to 
Case 1, Case 3 exhibits a reduction in the SJZ angle and a 46 % decrease 
in the CTRZ length (fromr/d = 0.74 to 0.4). This CTRZ contraction arises 
from the steep expansion associated with the larger Venturi outlet angle, 
which promotes early flow separation, a sharp increase in the axial ve­
locity gradient, and the formation of a strong shear layer. Additionally, 
the reverse flow within the CTRZ is markedly intensified, with the peak 
reverse velocity increasing dramatically from −1.2 m/s in Case 1 to 
−6.8 m/s in Case 3. The enhancement in reverse velocity arises from the 
increase in the Venturi outlet angle, which leads to an enlarged outlet 
cross-sectional area. This geometric change results in a decrease in the 
average axial velocity, while simultaneously increasing the local ve­
locities and the kinetic energy within the shear layer, thereby enhancing 
the recirculation velocity.

quantification of breakup thickness across all Venturi outlet angles and 
fuel flow rates.

3.3. Limitations and Future work
While this study clarifies the role of Venturi outlet angles in modu­
lating spray atomization dynamics, it has inherent limitations that 
should be acknowledged.
First, the experimental scope is currently focused on the liquid film 
breakup process near the Venturi outlet. Upstream stages of the liquid 
film evolution chain—including droplet impingement on the Venturi 
wall, initial film formation, and its transient motion along the wall­
—remain insufficiently explored. These upstream processes directly in­
fluence film thickness and stability at the outlet, and their interplay with 
Venturi angles requires systematic investigation to fully understand at­
omization mechanisms. Second, all experiments were conducted under 
ambient temperature (25 ◦C) and pressure conditions. In real engine 
combustors, injectors operate in high-temperature (>500 ◦C) and high- 
pressure (>1 MPa) environments, where fuel properties (e.g., viscosity, 
surface tension) and airflow dynamics (e.g., density, velocity) differ 
significantly from ambient conditions. The generalizability of the 
observed Venturi angle effects under such extreme operating conditions 
needs further validation.
Future work will address these limitations by: (1) integrating high- 
speed imaging of upstream film formation stages to establish a com­
plete liquid film evolution model; (2) conducting experiments in a high- 
pressure/temperature combustion test rig to verify the Venturi angle 
optimization strategy under engine-relevant conditions. These efforts 
will enhance the practical applicability of the findings for low-emission 
gas turbine combustor design.
4. Conclusion
Swirling flow and spray interactions of the swirl cups airblast fuel 
injectors were meticulously investigated through experimental 
methods. Various sophisticated laser-based diagnostics, including PDPA, 
high-speed PMie, high-speed PIV and high-speed shadowgraph were 
employed to measure critical parameters such as, airflow velocity, film 
breakup length, fuel distribution, and droplet Sauter Mean Diameter 
(SMD). This study elucidates the critical role of Venturi outlet angles in 
modulating spray atomization dynamics within swirl cup airblast fuel 
injectors. The key findings can be summarized as follows:
Increasing the Venturi outlet angle from 15◦to 35◦significantly 
enhanced gas–liquid shear stress, reducing the liquid film length by 69.2 
% and improving primary atomization.
Larger Venturi angles amplified the reverse velocity of the central 
toroidal recirculation zone (CTRZ) from −
1.2 m/s to −
6.8 m/s, 
intensifying droplet entrainment and secondary breakup through 
enhanced turbulence.
Optimal atomization performance (30.9 % reduction in Sauter Mean 
Diameter, SMD) was achieved at angles > 25◦, demonstrating a critical 
balance between shear-driven liquid film breakup and recirculation- 
driven droplet dispersion.
These findings highlight Venturi outlet angle as a critical geometric 
parameter for optimizing injector design, providing actionable insights 
to improve combustion stability and emission performance in low- 
emission gas turbine combustors.
