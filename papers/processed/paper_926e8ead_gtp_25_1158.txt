Flame Transfer Function and 
Emissions of a Piloted Single Jet 
Burner: Influence of Hydrogen 
Content
The next generation of gas turbines must enable partial or pure hydrogen combustion to 
decarbonize electricity production. In this respect, jet burners have received increased 
attention in research and industry as a viable solution for combustors functioning over a 
wide operational range, as they are well-suited for stabilizing flames with high propagation 
speeds. In this work, a generic piloted single-jet hydrogen burner is developed and 
characterized experimentally. The jet burner is operated under lean and dry conditions to 
keep NOx emissions low. These operating conditions are generally prone to thermoacoustic 
instabilities. The flame shape, NOx emissions, and the acoustic response of the flame are 
characterized while varying the hydrogen concentration and the strength of a pilot injection 
stage. The measurements of the emissions demonstrate that the jet burner is well-suited for 
the combustion of natural gas blends and pure hydrogen with low NOx. The mean flame OH* 
chemiluminescence images display a decreasing flame length with increasing hydrogen 
share and a strong dependence on the pilot strength. Last, the heat release rate response is 
assessed based on measured flame transfer functions for different fuel blends. We 
demonstrate that the injector used in this study is nonstiff. To assess the stiffness of the 
injector, a full flame transfer matrix is measured for selected cases, and a comparison of the 
two methods is provided. [DOI: 10.1115/1.4069115] 
Keywords: flame transfer function, jet flames, hydrogen 
1 Introduction
The increasing demand for electricity in our society requires 
permanent technological innovation in the power sector. Facing the 
challenges of climate change, the share of renewable energy 
resources, such as wind and solar power, needs to increase. The 
contribution of these renewable sources to the global power sector is 
however intermittent. This demands the co-existence of flexible 
power plants capable of promptly compensating for fluctuations in 
the grid. The gas-turbine technology is well suited for fulfilling this 
task. Modern gas turbines are highly optimized regarding pollutant 
emissions and reliable operation, but they still primarily rely on the 
combustion of fossil fuels, especially natural gas. These fuels are 
typically combusted in swirl-stabilized combustors that allow for 
lean premixed combustion. Within the context of a sustainable 
future power sector, fossil fuels in the power sector have to be 
replaced by greener, carbon-free fuels. To promote this transition, 
established gas turbine combustion concepts need to be adapted, and 
new concepts need to be developed to allow for the 
efficient combustion of alternative sustainable fuels, such as green 
hydrogen.
Although modern swirl burners achieve very low emissions and 
reliable operation, they come with certain drawbacks, including (i) a 
rather high-pressure loss in connection with complex geometries, 
(ii) inherent hydrodynamic instabilities related to precessing vortex 
core [1,2], and (iii) the sensitivity to thermoacoustic instabilities [3]. 
For swirl flames, the thermoacoustic feedback is driven by the phase 
relation of fluctuations in heat release rate, velocity, pressure, and 
swirl, whereby swirl fluctuations are unique to this type of flow 
[4–6]. Besides the advantage of aerodynamic flame stabilization, the 
typical flow field in a swirl combustor is prone to vortex breakdown- 
induced flashback because of the large variety of (positive and 
negative) flow velocities [7,8]. This type of flashback occurs 
primarily along the central recirculation zone. Additionally, 
boundary layer flashback along the combustor wall and mixing 
section can occur due to the declining flow velocity [9,10]. To 
investigate this flashback phenomenon in detail, jet burners have 
been used in connection with different types of natural gas/hydrogen 
fuel blends up to pure hydrogen premixed with air [11]. It was shown 
that the tip temperature of the injector, the degree of confinement, 
the burner material, and the static pressure have a significant

Jet burners rely on a simple turbulent pipe flow, with a 
characteristic uniform flow velocity profile of high momentum, 
connected to a combustion chamber. Thanks to their high bulk 
velocities of around 100 m/s, jet flames are highly flashback resistant 
[14], and the resulting flow field in the combustion chamber can 
become independent of the Reynolds-number, which leads to a 
certain robustness against mass flow fluctuations [15]. Because of all 
these characteristics, jet burners are considered well-suited to 
combust highly reactive fuels with high burning velocities, such as 
hydrogen. There exist already concepts to combust fuel blends with 
high hydrogen contents such as swirl-stabilized combustion with 
high amounts of steam [16], so-called micromix burners with pure 
hydrogen diffusion flames [17], and multijet-bruners [18]. Dry low- 
emission technologies based on swirl combustors are currently 
limited to fuel blends with around 60 vol:% hydrogen content [17]. 
The application of multijet-burners, as in Ref. [18], is limited to 
hydrogen concentrations of around 50 vol.%. For this concentration, 
the range of NOx emissions is below 25 ppm corrected to a dry basis. 
The current dry premixed-burner concepts are not capable of 
reliably handling fuels with a high hydrogen share. To further extend 
the operational range of gas turbines to fuels with high hydrogen 
share, a novel generic design is investigated in this paper.
In this respect, we developed a generic piloted single hydrogen jet 
burner (POShyDON) that allows for a detailed investigation of the 
dynamics and stability of jet flames with thermal powers up to 
150 kW. This jet burner involves a pilot stage and a main nozzle 
where preheated and premixed fuel-air mixtures with a hydrogen 
content of up to 100 % are provided. The burner design is inspired by 
the FLOX
V
R burner [19], whose ability of stabilizing flame fronts in 
single-nozzle burner configurations with or without a pilot has been 
discussed in detail [14,20]. Besides flame stabilization, the response 
of the flame to acoustic perturbations plays a major role in the design 
process of a combustor [3]. This response can be described by the 
relation of incoming acoustic perturbations to heat release rate 
disturbances, which is typically expressed as a flame transfer 
function (FTF). Recent results by Ref. [21] show that hydrogen 
enrichment of nonswirling flames leads to shorter flames with higher 
cutoff frequencies in the FTF. Accordingly, hydrogen flames appear 
to be more susceptible to thermoacoustic oscillation at higher 
frequencies compared to pure natural gas flames.
Whereas flame response and FTF have been thoroughly explored 
for laminar flames on a laboratory scale [22] and turbulent swirl 
flames with high thermal power [23,24], there exists a certain lack of 
data for turbulent premixed jet flames closer to industrial power 
scales. With the POShyDON jet burner setup, a broad range of 
operating conditions can be investigated by assessing the FTFs with 
the acoustic methods, and by investigating the flame shape with 
optical measurements. The main focus of this study is the 
characterization of the FTF and NOx emissions for different 
premixed jet flame configurations, including the variation of 
hydrogen-natural gas ratio and pilot flame conditions.
This paper starts with a description of the experimental setup, 
including the POShyDON-jet burner facility, the applied measure­
ment techniques, and the chosen operating conditions. Subse­
quently, results on emissions and FTFs are shown for hydrogen 
blends with and without a pilot. We then investigated the stiffness of the 
injector by measuring flame transfer functions for selected conditions. 
A detailed discussion on the obtained results concludes the paper.

4 Results
In this section, we report the results of the heat release response 
and the emissions of the POShyDON burner as the hydrogen content 
of the fuel is varied. The burner is investigated with and without pilot 
flow. For the cases with a pilot injection, the mass fractions are 
chosen to be PAR ¼ 5% and PFR ¼ 10%. Preliminary studies were 
conducted to understand the influence of these parameters. The 
PAR ¼ 5% is chosen to generate a sufficient penetration depth into 
the main jet. The PFR ¼ 10% is chosen in order to generate a pilot 
flame close to stoichiometric conditions. The total air mass flow of the 
system is kept constant to _mair ¼ 220 kg/h for all measurements. For 
each case considered, the fuel mass flow is calculated by using Eq. (2).
4.1 Mean Flame Shape. The mean flame shape is assessed 
based on OH* chemiluminescence images. The local heat release 
rate is proportional to the local OH* chemiluminescence for 
perfectly premixed conditions, which is valid in our setup in the 
absence of acoustic forcing. This is also the case with a pilot 
injection because the pilot is perfectly premixed. Figure 3 displays 
the mean OH* chemiluminescence fields as the hydrogen content 
varies, with and without a pilot injection.
For the pure natural gas case without pilot fuel injection, the flame 
is detached comparably far from the burner front plate, resulting in a 
long flame with a widely distributed heat release rate. Using the pilot 
injection stage for the same conditions results in a flame stabilized 
considerably closer to the burner outlet, with a reduced flame length. 
The length reduction is due to (i) a concentration of the heat release 
rate closer to the flame root and (ii) a possible recirculation effect 
assumed to be created by the injection of the eight jets in crossflow 
penetrating the main jet. By increasing the amount of hydrogen in 
the fuel composition, the flame length is drastically shortened, and 
the flame tends to attach closer to the burner outlet. The presence of 
the pilot alters the mean flame shape: the higher the hydrogen 
concentration the more pronounced the mean flame shape changes 
when the pilot is turned on. In particular, the pilot jets concentrate a 
major share of the heat release rate close to the nozzle outlet: the 
higher the hydrogen concentration the more pronounced this effect 
is. This implies a lower residence time, which in turn affects the NOx 
levels in a nonlinear fashion [30].
4.2 Influence of the Hydrogen Content on the Creation of 
Nitric Oxides. In this section, we discuss the results of the measured 
NOx emission levels. For a better understanding, a simulation of the 
reaction pathways is also performed by using the Cantera software.
4.2.1 Experimental Measurements of NOx. Figure 4 shows the 
measured NOx levels as the H2 concentration varies for well- 
premixed conditions, with and without pilot. The reported values are 
corrected on a dry basis as given in Eq. (4). The filled circles with a 
dotted line represent the configuration without pilot and the circles 
with a solid line describe the NOx emissions as a function of H2 for 
the piloted flame. The error bars denote two standard deviations.
The nonpiloted flame generates rather low and constant NOx 
emission values for H2  20%. With increasing hydrogen content, 
the emission values grow gradually and reach a maximum at 
NOx,corr ¼ 5.2 ppmvd for H2 ¼ 60%; for pure hydrogen combustion 
the emissions slightly decrease. In comparison, for the piloted flame, 
the NOx emissions show a linearly increasing trend between 0 and 
60% hydrogen content. The emissions peak at NOx,corr ¼ 6.3 ppmvd 
for H2 ¼ 60%; for higher hydrogen shares, the emissions steeply 
decrease and eventually fall below the value of the nonpiloted flame 
for pure hydrogen.

increased NOx creation. This increase is likely due to the (locally) 
richer fuel injected through the pilot stage. The pilot injection is

4.2.2 Numerical Investigation of the NOx Formation Path­
ways. To gain a better understanding of the NOx emissions, Cantera 
[32] simulations are performed with the GRI3.0 mechanism. These 
calculations estimate the contribution of various reaction pathways 
toward the formation of NOx for varying hydrogen content in the 
fuel–air mixture [33]. A perfectly stirred reactor (PSR) is assumed 
for the jet burner without pilot. The PSR assumes the gases to be 
perfectly mixed. The mass flowrate through the reactor is set 
constant. For each fuel blend, the same fuel compositions and 
equivalence ratios are used as in the experiments. Natural gas is 
approximated with pure methane and corrected as described in 
Eq. (4). The reactor inlet temperature is matched to the experimental 
one, and the pressure is set to atmospheric pressure. For each fuel 
mixture, a typical residence time is estimated from a characteristic 
length of the main reaction zone and the jet bulk velocities at the 
burner outlet. A threshold of 50% is chosen to characterize the length 
of the main reaction zone based on the OH* intensity maps shown in 
Fig. 3. This residence time estimation gives the highest residence 
time for the H2 ¼ 60% case of tres ¼ 1.8 ms and the lowest for the 
pure hydrogen case with tres ¼ 1.1 ms. For the pure natural gas case, a 
realistic residence time could not be deducted from the mean OH* 
intensity images due to the strongly detached and nonstationary 
flame resulting in a widely distributed OH* chemiluminescence 
intensity. For conditions with low hydrogen content, the results do 
not match as closely. This deviation may be caused by the flame’s 
slightly detached shape, which causes the residence time to be 
overestimated with the used method.
In Fig. 4, the simulated NOx values are plotted against the 
experimental values. Although the absolute values show some 
deviation from the experimental results, the emissions level and the 
qualitative behavior are captured well. As in the experimental 
measurements, a peak in NOx is found at H2 ¼ 60% in the Cantera 
calculations.
To understand the mechanism behind this peak, a pathway 
analysis of the formation of NOx as the hydrogen content increases is 
performed [33]. The results are shown in Fig. 5 where the left axis 
displays the relative contribution of the four main pathways for NOx 
formation; the right axis displays the absolute NOx level. The 
contribution of the thermal path is small compared to the other 
pathways, which is expected since the outlet temperature of the PSR 
is kept approximately constant by means of the constant thermal 
power set in the experiment. The variation of the simulated outlet 
temperature of the PSR is comparably small with 635 C for the 
different fuel variations. For reference, the mean PSR outlet 
temperature for all performed simulations is 1500 C. The variation 
of the thermal path is due to the variation in residence time, with 
H2 ¼ 60% corresponding to the highest residence time. The prompt 
path’s contribution decreases with increasing hydrogen share, 
vanishing at pure H2 combustion. This is because the prompt path 
relies on the formation of hydrocarbon CHi radicals, which are 
reduced with increased hydrogen share [34]. The absolute 
contribution of the NNH and N2O paths to the NOx formation 
increases with increasing hydrogen content, counteracting the decay 
of the prompt path. These counteracting effects lead to the peak at 
H2 ¼ 60%. The NNH and N2O paths are the most prominent NOx 
formation pathways for the pure hydrogen cases. A dedicated 
simulation of the conditions including the pilot injection is not 
performed. This is due to the missing information on the flow field 
and the mixing properties induced by the pilot jets.

For swirl-stabilized flames, it was shown that two mechanisms 
characterize the flame response: (i) swirl fluctuations and (ii) vortex 
roll-up due to Kevin-Helmholtz instabilities [35]. For the jet burner 
at hand, the flame response is, obviously, driven by the second 
mechanism. In fact, the shape of the FTF curves is comparable to the 
model developed by Ref. [36] describing the impact of vortex roll-up 
due to the Kelvin–Helmholtz instability. The increase in the phase 
values as well as in the gain values for higher forcing frequencies is 
due to a second mechanism overlaying the velocity fluctuations, 
which may be induced by the Kevin–Helmholtz instability. These 
two counteracting mechanisms overlay constructively and destruc­
tively, leading to rising and falling gain and phase values [37]. 
However, the analysis of this second mechanism requires a 
dedicated investigation of the complex reacting flow field which is 
beyond the scope of this study.
A characteristic time delay sc for perturbations reaching the flame 
can be estimated by the ratio of the flame length and the bulk flow 
velocity at the flame root [38]. Based on this time delay, a rough 
estimate of the cutoff frequency of the absolute value from the FTF 
can be derived by taking the reciprocal time delay: fc ¼ 1=sc. This 
results in an estimate of the cutoff frequency of fc  248 Hz for the 
natural gas flame response, which qualitatively matches the 
measured data in Fig. 6. With higher hydrogen content, the cutoff 
frequency increases, as the flame length is reduced. For H2 ¼ 100%, 
we estimate fc  647 Hz. This is not within the measured range, and 
a direct comparison with the experiments is not possible.
The gain of the FTF decreases for frequency below 200 Hz with 
increasing hydrogen content, suggesting that, for this burner 
configuration, the hydrogen-enriched flames are less prone to low- 
frequency thermoacoustic instabilities. Especially for the FTFs with 
a hydrogen content above H2 > 60%, the gain is lower than one for 
all frequencies below 200 Hz. For the phase values, below 200 Hz a 
flatter slope can be observed with increasing hydrogen content. This 
is consistent with the fact that the length of the flame shortens with 
increasing hydrogen content (see Fig. 3), resulting in shorter time 
delays for the flame response. These results are in agreement with 
those of Ref. [39], where the FTFs of a perfectly premixed lab-scale 
burner as a function of the hydrogen content are reported.
In our measurements, the phase of the FTFs in the low-frequency 
limit converges toward a negative value, close to −p. This indicates 
that the fuel injection is not stiff [40]. For a stiff injection, the 
assumption holds that the fuel injection is decoupled from the 
acoustic forcing, and the acoustic fluctuation does not interact with 
the fuel supply. In the following, the nonstiffness is characterized by 
measuring the whole flame transfer matrix for selected cases.

5 Conclusions
In this study, we have proposed the use of a piloted single jet 
burner, POShyDON, to stabilize the combustion of fuel blends 
spanning from pure natural gas to pure hydrogen. The burner 
concept is conceived to enable the next generation of gas turbines to 
flexibly combust blends of hydrogen-rich fuels. The burner 
characteristics have been investigated under atmospheric conditions 
for differing fuel blends and pilot injections.
We have demonstrated that the POShyDON burner can operate at 
100% hydrogen at dry conditions with low NOx formation. For the 
emissions, we showed that fuel blends with a higher amount of 
hydrogen generally result in increased emissions, although these 
levels decrease when moving from hydrogen-rich to pure hydrogen 
combustion. Furthermore, it was shown that the addition of a pilot 
injection stage slightly increases the NOx production. By consid­
ering time-averaged chemiluminescence data, we have shown that 
the flames stabilize further upstream when the pilot injection stage is 
turned on. With a higher hydrogen content, the flame length reduces 
and anchors closer to the burner outlet.
Last, the heat release dynamic response to acoustic perturbations 
was characterized. The measured FTFs display an increased cutoff 
frequency and shortened time lag with increasing hydrogen content. 
For low frequencies, the gain of the FTFs is dampened with 
increasing hydrogen content. The phase of the FTFs converges 
toward −p, which indicates that the fuel injector is not stiff. This 
was assessed by measuring the full FTMs for selected cases. It was 
observed that the T21 element of the FTM is not zero, implying a 
nonstiff injector. However, the T11 element is frequency dependent, 
and the T12 is nonzero, which suggests that the low Mach number 
assumption is slightly violated. A reconstruction of the FTF from the 
T22 element of the FTM reveals certain differences to the FTF 
measured with upstream forcing only which implies an influence of 
the pressure fluctuations on the heat release dynamics. As a 
consequence, the estimate of the FTF of our burner with a nonstiff 
injector with one (upstream) forcing is inaccurate, and the full FTM 
should be measured instead.
