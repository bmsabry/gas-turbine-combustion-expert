Abstract. To investigate the mechanisms leading to sustained thermoacoustic oscillations in swirl
ﬂames, a gas turbine model combustor was equipped with an optically accessible combustion chamber
allowing the application of various laser techniques. The ﬂame investigated was a swirled CH4/air
diffusion ﬂame (thermal power 10 kW, global equivalence ratio φ = 0.75) at atmospheric pressure
which exhibited self-excited thermoacoustic oscillations at a frequency of 290 Hz. In separate experi-
ments, the ﬂow velocities were measured by laser Doppler velocimetry, the ﬂame structures and heat
release rates by planar laser-induced ﬂuorescence of CH and by OH chemiluminescence, and the joint
probability density functions of the major species concentrations, mixture fraction, and temperature
by laser Raman scattering. All measurements were performed in a phase-locked mode, i.e., triggered
with respect to the oscillating pressure level measured by a microphone. The results revealed large
periodic variations of all measured quantities and showed that the heat release rate was correlated with
the degree of mixing of hot products with unburned fuel/air mixtures before ignition. The thermal
expansion of the reacting gases had, in turn, a strong inﬂuence on the ﬂow ﬁeld and induced a periodic
motion of the inner and outer recirculation zones. The combination of all results yielded a deeper
understanding of the events sustaining the oscillations in the ﬂame under investigation. The results
also represent a data base that can be used for the validation and improvement of CFD codes.
Key words: thermoacoustic, swirl ﬂames, laser diagnostic, validation measurements
1. Introduction
Thermoacoustic instabilities are often encountered in modern gas turbine (GT)
combustors and pose a severe problem for further NOx reduction by applying ultra-
lean combustion concepts [1–4]. Periodic pressure oscillations are driven by the
combustion process, which in turn is subject to pulsations that can be sustained
by a resonant feedback mechanism coupling pressure and heat release [5, 6]. The
physical and chemical mechanisms driving the instabilities are based on a complex
interaction between combustor geometry, pressure, ﬂow ﬁeld, mixing, chemical
reactions, and heat release, and are not understood well enough yet. Because the

problems could not be solved fundamentally so far, combustion instabilities have
been reduced or eliminated in industrial burners by passive or active control mech-
anisms [7, 8]. On the other hand, large efforts are currently being undertaken in the
development of numerical simulation tools in order to predict unsteady combustion
behavior so that improved GT combustors can be designed with their help. How-
ever, a deeper understanding of the complex interactions involved in combustion
instabilities is now and in the near future based on experimental research using
advanced measuring techniques with high temporal and spatial resolution.
The German Aerospace Center (DLR) has established a lab scale “standard
burner” for swirling CH4/air diffusion ﬂames at atmospheric pressure that is close
to a practical GT combustor but with large windows for optical measurements.
Several laser techniques have been applied in order to gain insight into processes
leading to the instabilities and to provide an experimental data base which can
be used for the validation of numerical simulations. The investigated ﬂame had
a thermal power of 10 kW, a global equivalence ratio of φ = 0.75, and it exhib-
ited thermoacoustic oscillations with a dominant frequency of 290 Hz which were
recorded by microphones. This instability was very well reproducible enabling the
comparison of experiments that were performed at different times. Laser Doppler
velocimetry was used to measure the ﬂow velocities simultaneously with the pres-
sure signal from a microphone in order to correlate velocities and pressure level.
Planar laser-induced ﬂuorescence (PLIF) was applied to measure two-dimensional
CH structures in the ﬂame and laser Raman scattering to determine the major species
concentrations, temperature, and mixture fraction. These measurements were trig-
gered with respect to the phase of the pressure pulsations in the combustor plenum,
enabling phase-resolved measurements. In addition, OH∗chemiluminescence was
detected as a measure for the heat release rate.
Although the instantaneous ﬂame behavior was dominated by turbulent ﬂuctua-
tions,measurementsofquantitiesaveragedatﬁxedphaseanglesrevealedsigniﬁcant
periodic variations of the ﬂame, especially in the near-ﬁeld. The heat release rate
varied by more than ±20% during an oscillation cycle and was accompanied by
pressure variations. At the inlet, the mean ﬂow velocities also varied by more than
20% in magnitude. The corresponding distributions of mixture fraction, species
concentrations, and temperature provided an insight into the chain of events during
an oscillation period.

2.2. MEASURING TECHNIQUES
The axial and radial velocity components were measured simultaneously with the
pressure signal from the plenum to be able to correlate the velocity data and the
pressure ﬂuctuation. The LDV system consisted of a bidirectional optic component
from DISA/DANTEC (DISA 55X) and an Ar+laser (INNOVA, Coherent) using

278

two different laser wavelengths (λlaser = 488 and 514.5 nm). A frequency shift of
40 MHz was applied for both directions. Using focusing lenses with f = 300 mm,
the probe volumes were about 60 µm in diameter and 1.0 mm in length. SiO2
particles with a diameter of 0.8 µm were seeded as scatterers into the air ﬂow. For
the simultaneous detection and analysis of the photomultiplier signals, two Dantec
burst spectrum analyzers were used (BSA enhanced, 57N20 and 57N35). At each
measuring location, typically 10,000–15,000 velocity data were recorded with the
center frequency being adapted at each measuring position. To correlate the velocity
signals to the oscillating pressure level, the positive slope of the pressure signal has
been recorded via a third BSA (57N35), thus logging the pressure periodicity.
In a post processing step the velocity data were sorted according to the pressure
ﬂuctuation into 36 intervals per period (resulting in a ±5◦resolution) by comparing
the arrival time of each velocity signal with the corresponding time of the pressure
signal. Phase-resolved velocity measurements were performed at h = 1 and 5 mm,
within radial intervals of r = 1 or 2 mm. At heights h > 5 mm, measurements
were performed without phase-resolution. The uncertainty of the measurements is
less than 1% for the mean values.
The ﬂame structures were visualized by planar laser-induced ﬂuorescence of
CH. The tunable frequency-doubled laser radiation of a pulsed optical paramet-
ric oscillator (Spectra Physics GCR 290 and MOPO 730) was formed to a light
sheet of approximately 50 mm height and irradiated vertically into the ﬂame. The
excited ﬂuorescence was collected at 90◦by a camera lens ( f = 50 mm, f /0.95,
Canon) and, after spectral ﬁltering, detected by an intensiﬁed CCD camera (Roper
Scientiﬁc). The laser pulse duration was 5 ns, the temporal detection gate of the
image intensiﬁer was 200 ns. The CH radicals were excited on the Q1(7) line of the
B2 −X2 (ν′ = 0, ν′′ = 0) band at 390.3 nm [11]. For the suppression of laser
scattered light and background radiation, a ﬁlter combination of a KV418 and a
short pass ﬁlter KP450 was used in front of the camera and only the ﬂuorescence
from the A state around λ ≈430 nm was detected. This state is efﬁciently populated
by collision-induced electronic energy transfer from the B to the A electronic level
[12, 13]. In ﬂames, CH radicals are formed at high temperatures on the fuel-rich
side of the reaction zone and have a short lifetime (≈10 ns). Thus, high CH concen-
trations can be interpreted as a marker for the fuel-consumption layer of the reaction
zone and, with some restrictions, as a qualitative measure for the heat release rate
[14, 15]. The chemiluminescence of OH∗was measured by an intensiﬁed CCD
camera equipped with an UV achromatic lens ( f = 100 mm, B. Halle Nachf.) and
an interference ﬁlter centered at λ = 312 nm. OH∗is an intermediate species in
CH4/air combustion [16] and is regarded as a good measure for the heat release rate
[17, 18].
For the pointwise quantitative measurement of the major species concentrations
(O2, N2, CH4, H2, CO, CO2, H2O) and the temperature, laser Raman scattering was
applied separately from the other measurements. The radiation of a ﬂashlamp-
pumped dye laser (Candela LFDL 20, wavelength λ = 489 nm, pulse energy

Ep ≈3 J, pulse duration τp ≈3 µs) was focused into the combustion chamber
and the Raman scattering emitted from the measuring volume (length 0.6 mm, di-

species were detected by photomultiplier tubes in the exit plane of the spectrograph.
The species number densities were calculated from these signals using calibration
measurements and the temperature was deduced from the total number density
via the ideal gas law [19, 20]. The simultaneous detection of all major species
with each laser pulse also enabled the determination of the instantaneous mixture
fraction [21]. At each measuring location within a pattern of roughly 50 points,
covering the heights h = 5, 10, 20, 30, and 40 mm, 400 single-pulse measurements
were performed from which the joint probability density functions (PDFs) were
established. At heights h ≥40 mm phase-dependent variations were smaller than
the measurement accuracy and no phase-locking was applied for measurements
above h = 40 mm. The measurement uncertainty due to systematic errors is typ-
ically 3–4% for the temperature and mixture fraction, and 3–5% for the species
mole fractions of O2, CH4, CO2, and H2O. The single-pulse uncertainties, includ-
ing statistical errors due to photon statistics, are 4–5% for temperature and mixture
fraction and typically 5–10% for the mole fractions, depending on the number
density [22, 23].
All pulsed measurements were performed in a phase-locked mode, i.e., triggered
with respect to the oscillating pressure signal measured by the microphone in the
combustor plenum [10, 24]. The phase angle α = 0◦was deﬁned at the pressure
minimum in the plenum and measurements were performed at steps of 45◦± 5◦.
The pressure curve was not exactly sinusoidal but exhibited a slight asymmetry in
the form of a slightly faster pressure decrease than increase. However, for simplicity
the pressure curve will be treated as a pure sine curve in the following. The signal
from the microphone in the combustion chamber was not used for the triggering
because it exhibited a poorer signal-to-noise ratio. The results displayed in this
paper are phase-resolved mean values, i.e., averaged at distinct phase angles.

3.4. MIXTURE FRACTION AND TEMPERATURE
Figure 6 shows radial proﬁles of the mixture fraction f , the CH4 mole fraction,
and temperature T at h = 5 mm for the four different phase angles discussed
above. H = 5 mm was the lowest measuring height for the Raman measurements
because for h < 5 mm the optical access was restricted. It can be seen from the
CH LIF distributions in Figure 5 that the ﬂame zone starts around this height, more
speciﬁcally, reactions may occur at r = 0–15 mm, but not for r > 15 mm. The
mean mixture fractions cover a range between f ≈0.03 (φ ≈0.5) and f ≈0.07
(φ ≈1.3). These values are quite close to the overall equivalence ratio of φ = 0.75
and demonstrate the fast mixing achieved by this nozzle conﬁguration. The highest
f -values are seen at r ≈7.5 mm, i.e., roughly at the position where CH4 is injected.
During an oscillation cycle, considerable variations of f occur with high values at
α = 225◦and low values at α = 45◦. The corresponding proﬁles of CH4 reach a
maximum mean mole fraction of X ≈0.09 around r = 7.5–10 mm. Signiﬁcant
variations of CH4 concentrations are observed on the ﬂame axis with the highest
mole fraction at 135◦when the reverse ﬂow in the IRZ is slow. A quite surprising
result is that the CH4 distribution extends so far radial outside: in a non-pulsating
ﬂame with a very similar ﬂow ﬁeld, the CH4 mole fractions were below 0.01 for
r ≥18 mm [26]. This is an indication that the pulsations generate an additional
mixing process which is already active below h = 5 mm. The lower frame of
Figure 6 displays the corresponding T -proﬁles. The largest phase-angle-dependent
variations are seen in the IRZ where T varies by roughly 800 K. A comparison with
Figure 3 shows that the temperature and the axial velocity on the axis are correlated:
at the highest reverse ﬂow velocity, the temperature is maximal. That means, that
at α ≈315◦hot gases are transported at a high ﬂow rate into the central air nozzle.
It could not be measured how deep the gases penetrate into the nozzle, however, it
is clear that they ﬁnally mix with air and re-enter the combustion chamber together
with the air ﬂow. In the radial region of the downstream ﬂow of the injected gases
(r ≈5–21 mm at h = 5 mm) the α-dependent temperature variations are on the
order of 300 K with a maximum at α ≈135◦, i.e., phase-shifted by ≈180◦compared
to the variations in the IRZ. The temperature changes in this region are not primarily
due to combustion but to transport and mixing processes, i.e., an α-dependent feed
of hot gases from the IRZ and the ORZ to the fresh gases. To illustrate this in
more detail, the scatterplots of Figure 7 display single-shot results at h = 5 mm,
r = 15 mm for four different phase angles. Each symbol represents the result of a
single-shot Raman measurement, the solid line shows for comparison the adiabatic
equilibrium state, and the cross indicates the mean value of the single-shot results.

increases signiﬁcantly with T and as a result the reaction and heat release rates are

4. Conclusions
A gas turbine model combustor for swirling CH4/air diffusion ﬂames at atmospheric
pressure has been presented which offered full optical access for the application of
various laser techniques. The characteristics of a ﬂame with self-excited thermoa-
coustic oscillations of f = 290 Hz have been investigated using phase-resolved
LDV, 2D LIF, laser Raman scattering, and chemiluminescence measurements. The
instantaneous ﬂame behavior was dominated by strong turbulent ﬂuctuations, how-
ever, periodic variations became clearly visible by averaging the measured results
at distinct phase angles.
The ﬂame burnt lifted and fuel, air, and exhaust gas were partially premixed
before ignition. The region of the main heat release was restricted to h ≈10–20 mm
and mixing and combustion were completed at h ≈25 mm. The largest phase-
dependent variations occurred in the inner and outer recirculation zones with a
pronounced axial movement of the IRZ (large variation of u) and radial movement
of the ORZ (large variation of v). During an oscillation cycle, the heat release rate
changed by more than ±20%, especially in the outer region of the ﬂame (r ≈15–
30 mm). These variations of heat release were not well correlated with changes of
the mixture fraction except for r ≥25 mm at the periphery of the ﬂame. The results
revealed that the temperature of the unburned fuel/air mixtures at h = 5 mm, i.e.,
at the beginning of the ﬂame zone varied in phase with the total heat release. This
temperature variation was due to a periodic change of the transport of hot products
from the recirculation zones to the inlet ﬂow and hardly to ﬂame reactions. The
oscillations of the heat release rate were accompanied by periodic changes of the
thermal expansion, swirl intensity, and pressure, which in turn inﬂuenced the ﬂow
ﬁeld. The variations were correlated in such a way that temperature, heat release,
and transport sustained a periodic oscillation at f ≈290 Hz.
