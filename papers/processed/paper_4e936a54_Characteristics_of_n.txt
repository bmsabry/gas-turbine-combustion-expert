Turbulent Nonpremixed Jet Flames

The formation of oxides of nitrogen, NOx, in turbulent nonpremixed ﬂames is experimentally analyzed making
use of velocity, temperature, and major species concentration measurements in jet propane ﬂames. The analysis
considered the contributions of Zeldovich (thermal) and Fenimore (prompt) mechanisms at different locations
of attached vertical ﬂames with Reynolds numbers of 20,400 and 37,600. The analysis of NO mass formation
rates suggests that prompt mechanism dominates NO formation throughout the ﬂames, with peak formation
rates occurring in the rich ﬂame region, at locations r/x approximately equal to 0.08, where r and x are the radial
and axial coordinate, respectively. NO concentrations are shown to decrease with Reynolds number, as the gas
residence times are reduced, although mass ﬂow rates of NOx per unit mass ﬂow rate of fuel are independent
of Reynolds number.

INTRODUCTION
It is well known that strategies to reduce NOx
emissions from combustion of fuels containing
no bound nitrogen, as is the case of most
commercial gaseous fuels, are primarily depen-
dent on thermal (Zeldovich), prompt (Feni-
more), and nitrous oxide (N2O) intermediate
mechanisms [1, 2]. It is also known that in
nonpremixed combustion of hydrocarbon fuels,
the ﬁrst two mechanisms dominate the process
of NO formation, whereas for nonhydrocarbon
systems only thermal mechanism is generally
active. In general, Zeldovich mechanism is dom-
inant in lean to stoichiometric laminar premixed
ﬂames, but Fenimore mechanism can be domi-
nant under conditions where Zeldovich NO is
small [3–7].
The complexity of the process and the various
questions typically raised in the modiﬁcation of
practical systems, [8], suggest that future emis-
sion regulations will probably not be met by
existing burners and that costly additional fuel
reburning end-of-pipe arrangements may be
required, unless improved designs are available.
It is, therefore, desirable to understand the
relative importance of NO formation mecha-
nisms, including its mean rates of formation in
ﬂames, so that the analysis of simple jet ﬂame
provides a suitable testing environment.
A typical example of the analysis of NO
formation mechanisms has been reported for
laminar opposed-ﬂow diffusion ﬂames by Drake
and Blint [9]. Based upon experimental and
calculated values for CH4/N2 ﬂames, the au-
thors concluded that Fenimore mechanism is
expected to dominate NO formation in laminar,
as in turbulent hydrocarbon diffusion ﬂames.
Also, the associated numerical/experimental re-
sults of Smooke et al. [5] indicated that prompt
production path is the dominant formation
route of NO in axisymmetric laminar diffusion
ﬂames. Atreya et al. [6], on the analysis of
radiating diffusion ﬂames, suggested that Feni-
more NO initiation mechanism becomes more
important for low-temperature ﬂames. In a re-
cent experimental study, Meunier et al. [7]
reported that Fenimore mechanism is the dom-
inant route for NO formation in a turbulent
propane diffusion lifted ﬂame, and that resi-
dence time is the dominant physical parameter
associated with NOx scaling for the ﬂames ana-
lyzed. Moreover, the importance of the interac-
tion between instantaneous fuel trajectories and
local ﬂame characteristics on emissions has
been demonstrated with a multifuel low-NOx
burner with air and fuel staging [8].
Although comparatively more general infor-
mation on NOx production in nonpremixed
ﬂames can be found in Turns [10], extended
control of this process still requires further
analysis, through simple combusting environ-
ment as that considered in this work.
This paper discusses results obtained in tur-
bulent
nonpremixed
attached
jet
propane
ﬂames, with Reynolds numbers in the range of
20,400 to 37,600, evaluating the relative impor-
tance of Zeldovich and Fenimore mechanisms
on NO formation. The analysis is based on the
evaluation of the relative contribution of each
of the formation rate mechanisms, utilizing ex-
perimental data of ﬂame time-resolved temper-
ature and mean concentration of major chemi-
cal species obtained at several radial locations
in the ﬂames.
The remainder of the paper includes four
sections, as follows. The next section describes
ﬂow conﬁguration and experimental methods.
Then, the necessary background for analysis of
NOx formation rates is presented and the sub-
sequent section presents and discusses sample
results. The last section summarizes the main
conclusions of the work.
FLOW CONFIGURATION AND
EXPERIMENTAL METHOD
The analysis of NO formation rates in this paper
is derived from detailed measurements in at-
tached vertical turbulent jet ﬂames of propane
in still air (see [11] and [12] for details). The
burner consists of a round nozzle with an exit
internal diameter of D 5 5 mm and boundary
conditions typical of a fully developed pipe ﬂow.
Commercial grade propane (C3 $ 92%) was
used as jet ﬂuid in mass ﬂow rates of 0.65 and
1.2 g/s, resulting in jet ﬂames with Reynolds
numbers of 20,400 and 37,600, and Richardson
numbers of 1.5 3 1024 and 4.1 3 1026. Visible
ﬂame length is 1.10 and 1.50 m, respectively

Spectrometric measurements at 1870 nm H2O

nature of propane ﬂame 8 and 12 burner diam-

for Re 5 20,400 and 37,600 [12].
Velocity measurements were obtained with a
single component dual-beam laser-Doppler vel-
ocimeter based on an argon-ion laser light
source, and operating in the backscatter mode.
Time-resolved temperature measurements were
obtained with bare ﬁne wire thermocouples
made of Pt:Pt/13%Rh wires with 0.040 mm
diameter. The output of the thermocouple was
digitally compensated over two decades and the
results showed that the energy-containing fre-
quencies of the ﬂames analyzed were negligible
above 750 Hz, which is a value smaller than the
frequency response of the system. Results for
mean temperature and the variance of temper-
ature ﬂuctuations are accurate within 3% and
20% of the measured values, respectively, as
demonstrated by Caldeira-Pires and Heitor [11]
and Caldeira-Pires [12], and are not repeated
here. These two works present a complete de-
scription of this technique, including an assess-
ment of main sources of errors. Namely, Cal-
deira-Pires and Heitor [11] reported maximum
thermocouple radiation heat losses of 6% of the
compensated temperature value.
Mean concentrations of CO, CO2, O2, NOx,
and unburned hydrocarbon (UHC) were mea-
sured by gas sampling and subsequent on-line
analysis, using an L-shaped water cooled probe
with an oriﬁce 1.5 mm in diameter. The analysis
was performed on a wet basis with a ﬂame
ionization detector for UHC and a chemilumi-
nescence analyzer for the NO, and on a dry
basis with infrared analyzers for O2, CO2, and
CO, as described by Heitor and Moreira [16].
The results are expected to be close to density-
weighted averages, within 610% of the mea-
sured values, and were shown to be independent
of the suction velocity, but sampling was not
performed for x/D . 50, because of probe
blockage with particulate soot. The measuring
accuracy was assessed through the evaluation of
the integral of the mean mixture fraction over
the area of each radial proﬁle and from the
calculated ratios between the measured and
stoichiometric C/H and N/O proportion, which
have shown a maximum deviation of 3% from
stoichiometry [12].

Zeldovich NOx formation rate are up to three
orders of magnitude lower than those calculated

dominant formation route of NOx in the jet
ﬂames studied herein, whereas the Zeldovich
NOx mechanism plays a minor role in total NO
production. This fact was already mentioned in
the literature for laminar diffusion ﬂames [5, 6]
and turbulent lifted jet ﬂames [7].

CONCLUSIONS
The characteristics of NOx formation are ana-
lyzed in propane nonpremixed turbulent jet
ﬂames, for Reynolds numbers of 20,400 and
37,600. The main conclusions may be summa-
rized as follows.
Based on the formulae presented by Røkke et
al. [18, 19], there is no relevant source of NO
other than that based on Fenimore’s mechanism
in the ﬂame regions investigated here.
The absolute temperature levels, together
with appropriate combination of UHC, N2, O2,
and H2O concentrations, are responsible for
prompt NO formation. The evidence is derived
from the fact that near the longitudinal axis of
the ﬂame and at x/D $ 20, temperature is still
relatively high (.700°C) and there exist en-
trained N2 and some entrained unreacted O2,
such that prompt NOx is formed.
For each longitudinal station, NO formation
rates are maxima near the local temperature
peaks, but NOx concentrations are maxima for
smaller radii, due to turbulent transport toward
the center of the ﬂame as the ﬂow proceeds
downstream.
At similar axial stations in the ﬂame, NO
concentrations increase by decreasing the Reyn-
olds number, due to increased gas residence
times. Also, NO mass ﬂow rates increase with
the Reynolds number, although for a given axial
station in the ﬂame, mass ﬂow rates of NOx per
unit mass ﬂow rate of fuel are independent of
the Reynolds number.
