RESEARCH ARTICLE
Characteristics of ﬂow from an oxy-fuel burner with separated
jets: inﬂuence of jet injection angle
Touﬁk Boushaki • Jean-Charles Sautet
Received: 28 February 2008 / Revised: 7 November 2009 / Accepted: 15 November 2009 / Published online: 20 December 2009
 Springer-Verlag 2009
Abstract
This paper presents an experimental study of
ﬂow development and structure on a separated jet burner in
reacting and non-reacting ﬂows. Effects of deﬂection jets in
an aligned conﬁguration of three round jets are empha-
sized. The idea is based on the conﬁnement of a central jet
of fuel by two side jets of oxygen to improve mixing, to
control ﬂame stability, and to reduce pollutant emissions.
The ﬁelds of mean velocity and ﬂuctuation intensity were
measured using Particle Image Velocimetry. The deﬂection
of jets has a considerable effect on the dynamic behavior
and on the ﬂame characteristics. Results showed that the
deﬂection of jets favors mixing and accelerates merging
and combining of jets to a single one. Measurements in
reacting ﬂow showed a high inﬂuence of combustion on
dynamic ﬁelds. Compared to non-reactive case, in com-
bustion, larger radial expansion and higher velocity were
observed, particularly, above the stabilization point of the
ﬂame.
1 Introduction
Growing environmental concerns and the optimization of
performance of industrial furnaces require the development
of new generations of burners and improved combustion
technologies. Manufacturers are turning to a new type of
burners where fuel and oxidizer are injected separately.
Design of these burners requires the knowledge of mech-
anisms controlling the stabilization of ﬂame and the
production of pollutants such as nitrogen oxides. The
present paper reports the results of an experimental
investigation of ﬂow issued from burner designed by three
aligned separated jets. The mixing and the dynamic ﬁeld
for both reacting and non-reacting ﬂows are studied.
Multiple jets are used in wide variety of engineering
applications, for example, thrust augmenting ejectors for
VTOL/STOL aircraft, jet engine/rocket combustors, and
industrial gas burners. It is well known that the multiple
jets have many advantages over single jet, such as better
mixing and noise reduction (Raghunathan and Reid 1981).
Many investigations have been reported in the literature
regarding the structure and development of non-reacting
multiple jets (Tanaka 1974; Tanaka and Nakata 1975;
Krothapalli et al. 1980; Pani and Dash 1983; Simonich
1986; Yimer et al. 1996; Moawad Ahmed et al. 2001). On
the other hand, few studies of reacting ﬂow in multiple jet
conﬁgurations are conducted, let alone in oxy-fuel com-
bustion. Menon and Gollahalli (1988) have studied the
interaction of multiple jet ﬂames of propane, resulting from
2, 3, and 5 nozzles subjected to a cross ﬂow. Leite et al.
(1996) were interested in the inﬂuence of jet number, the
spacing between jets, and tube diameters on the length of
an acetylene ﬂame. Lenze et al. (1975) have studied the
mutual inﬂuence of three and ﬁve jet diffusion ﬂame, with
town gas and natural gas burners. Their measurements
concern concentrations, ﬂame length, and ﬂame width in
free and conﬁned multiple ﬂames.
Combustion with oxygen is characterized by a higher
adiabatic ﬂame temperature, a higher ﬂame velocity, a
lower ignition temperature, and a wider ﬂammability range
than is the case in combustion with air (Baukal and Gebhart
1997; GEFGN 1979; Perthuis 1983; Ivernel and Marque
1975). Oxy-fuel burners have been adopted in a wide range
of industrial furnaces to improve productivity and fuel

Use of oxy-fuel burners with separated jets opens
interesting possibilities in NOx reduction and modularity
of ﬂame properties (lift-off, topology, ﬂame length) (Sautet
et al. 2006; Boushaki et al. 2007). For separated jet burners,
the principle is based on the geometrical separation of its
nozzles. This design makes it possible to have a high
dilution of reactants by combustion products, a large ﬂame,
and a homogeneous temperature in the ﬂame. This con-
ﬁguration is highly three-dimensional and asymmetrical.
Various parameters govern the ﬂow from this type of
burner like the number of jets, the form of nozzles, the
spacing between the jets, the exit velocities, etc. In previ-
ous papers by the authors (Boushaki et al. 2007), the
characteristics of ﬂames in burners with separated jets are
studied with burner parameters such as exit velocities,
separation distance between jets and angle of injection. It is
interesting to note that the inclination of jets makes it
possible to have a better stability of ﬂame and above all a
signiﬁcant reduction in NOx (Boushaki et al. 2008). The
present paper considers the dynamic behavior of three jet
interactions in more detail by varying angle of the side
oxygen jets.
This study investigates the dynamic ﬁeld on a burner
with 25 kW power composed of three jets, one central jet
of natural gas and two side jets of pure oxygen. The
parameter of control consists in inclining the side oxygen
jets toward the central jet of natural gas. The velocity
measurements were carried out using particle image
velocimetry (PIV) in non-reacting ﬂow as well as in reacting
ﬂow inside the combustion chamber.
2 Experimental method

In the case of reacting ﬂow, the oxy-fuel ﬂames develop in
a conﬁnement that models the industrial furnace. The lat-

Experimental device for PIV measurement requires the
basic elements used in laser tomography, i.e., a laser sheet
that illuminates the zone of ﬂow studied, a CCD camera, a
PC for data acquisition, and a control unit for synchroni-
zation (Fig. 2). The laser used is double-pulsed Nd-YAG
(Big Sky CFR200 Quantel) with a wavelength of 532 nm
and a frequency of 10 Hz. Laser energy is adjustable and
can be increased up to 150 mJ per pulse with pulse duration
of 8 ns. The laser sheet is formed by a ﬁrst divergent cyl-
inder lens, which spreads out the beam then by second
convergent spherical lens, which focuses the sheet (500 lm
of thickness in the test section). The signal of Mie scattering
emitted by particles is collected perpendicularly using a
CCD camera FlowMaster of Lavision (12-bit dynamic and
1280 9 1024 pixels resolution) with a 50-mm lens F/1.2
Nikkon. An interferential ﬁlter at 532 nm is placed in front
of the camera lens to reject visible light at wavelengths
other than 532 nm. Time delay between the laser pulses was
ﬁxed at 10 ls. For each operation condition, up to 400 pairs
of instantaneous images were collected.
The seeding particles for PIV measurements are olive
oil particles (*3–4 lm in diameter) in non-reacting ﬂow
and zirconium oxide (ZrO2) particles (*0.5 lm in diam-
eter) for reacting ﬂow. For olive oil particles, the seeding
system is an atomizer based on Venturi principle to pro-
duce ﬁne particles (Fig. 3a). It allows distribution of par-
ticles with more or less homogeneous sizes and in
sufﬁcient amount. In the reacting ﬂow, the injection of
ZrO2 particles is performed using a seeding system made
in our laboratory. The system consists of tubes equipped
with porous plates, placed at the bottom at the level of exit
gases (Fig. 3b). The gas passes through the porous med-
ium and drags a certain quantity of particles providing a
uniform seeding. Concentration of particles is controlled
by valves through the gas ﬂow rate in the line of seeding.
The particles must be dried ﬁrst, in order to limit
agglomerates. Particles can modify the characteristics of
the ﬂow if they are introduced in high quantity and can
even blow out the ﬂame. Hence, the measurements were
taken with seeding rates relatively low in order not to
perturb the ﬂow. The criterion assuring a good track of
ﬂow by particles is respected here, since the Stokes
number obtained in the case of our experiments is much
lower than unity (St  1). The Stokes number is deﬁned
as the ratio between the response time of particles (tp) and
a time characteristic of the ﬂow (tf). For example, tp 
0:015 ms in the case of oxygen and oxide zirconium par-

The software used for data acquisition and correlation
processing was Davis of Lavision (DaVis 6.2.2). The size

3 Results and discussion
3.1 Initial conditions of velocities
When injectors are inclined, the shape of nozzles changes
becoming elliptical rather than round. Therefore, the proﬁle
of velocity for inclined jet at the exit nozzle is quite dif-
ferent to the one of a straight jet. That is why results of the
exit velocities are provided; they are very useful for
numerical studies. Figure 4 shows the proﬁles of mean
velocities near the burner exit (z = 3 mm) in the non-
reacting ﬂow. For the side jets, the longitudinal mean
velocity, U, decreases with the angle of the oxygen jets;
however, the transversal velocity, V, increases as a result of
the deﬂection of injectors. The proﬁles represent well the
velocity evolution of the ﬂow near the exit of nozzles. This
was veriﬁed by two ways: ﬁrst from the calculation of ﬂow
rate by the velocity proﬁles and second by LDA velocity
measurement which the proﬁles are quite agreed with the
PIV results (see Fig. 5a). Table 1 summarizes the initial
velocity conditions in the jet axis for both ﬂuids (natural
gas and oxygen) as a function of jet angle. It is shown that

3.2 Velocity ﬁelds
Figure 6 shows an example of instantaneous velocity ﬁelds
achieved by PIV (taken among 400 instantaneous ﬁelds)
for oxygen jet angles 0, 10, and 20, with the longitudinal
velocity in color scale. The highly three-dimensional ﬂow
is observable especially when the jets are inclined, since
the ﬂow is characterized by a discontinuous aspect of
streamlines. For the central jet, the axial velocity is roughly
constant, and the radial velocity is nearly zero in the near
nozzle ﬁeld. More downstream, the side jets affect the
central jet, and its radial velocity is no longer zero.
Regarding the side jets, except in the case h = 0, an
incline of velocity vectors is observed, due to the deﬂection
of injectors, and then the impact of these vectors in the
internal mixing layers with the central jet. In the far ﬁeld,
vortices appeared in the region of the mixing layer between
the jet and the ambient air (for h = 20). This is clearly a
two-dimensional representation of a three-dimensional
phenomenon. At the merging zone of three the jets, some
discontinuities in the velocity values are noticed charac-
terizing the three-dimensionality, particularly when the
side jets are inclined. This is due to the transverse ﬂow of
jets and the elliptical shape of inclined nozzles, as it was
shown in the paper of Gutmark and Grinstein (1999) where
the entrainment rate and the mixing for elliptic jets are
more signiﬁcant compared to the round jets.

tions indicate a very different behavior according to the jet
angle (h) and the axial position of ﬂow (z). In the case

layers of the side jets. These ﬂuctuation peaks diminish

combined region of ﬂow. From z = 75 mm, there are only
two ﬂuctuation peaks corresponding to the characteristics of
a single jet. Further downstream, the turbulence peaks dis-
appear with the jet angle, and the maximum of ﬂuctuations
is located in the ﬂow center.

3.4 Velocities along the jet axis
Figure 12 shows the mean velocities along the centerline
of the jets (Ucl and Vcl) for the angles 0, 10, 20, and 30
on the central (NG) and side jets (Ox). The centerline
corresponds to the maximum values along the jet. For the
central jet, this maximum value locates at the center of
nozzle; whereas, for the lateral jet, the maximum value is
deviated, because the jet is inclined as shown in Fig. 12d.
For the straight jets (h = 0), the axial velocity (Ucl)
follows a classical decrease for the central jet (Fig. 12a)
and the side jet (Fig. 12b): ﬁrst, a very slight decrease to
around 26 mm corresponding to the potential core of the
jet, then a high decay of Ucl which corresponds to the
merging of mixing layers, and ﬁnally a slow decay up to
65 mm for the natural gas jet and 75 mm for the oxygen
jet. Compared to a single jet, the centerline velocity decay
in the conﬁguration of three jets is almost the same in the
ﬁrst zone of ﬂow (up to z = 25 mm). For greater than
25 mm, the decay is slightly slower for the case of a
multiple jet, as shown in Fig. 12a. The same results were
found by Moustafa and Rathakrishnan (1993) previously.
This may be explained by the results of Moustafa (1994)
which found a higher entrainment of the ambient ﬂuid in
the case of triple jet conﬁguration. This is true if the exit
velocity is almost the same for the three jets, like our
conﬁguration. However, if the exit velocity of the central
jet is much higher than the one of side jets, for example,
the central jet decelerates, and side jets accelerate.
Therefore, the centerline velocity decay of the central jet
is faster. It is important to note that the velocity decay in
multiple jet conﬁgurations is highly dependant to the
spacing between the jets and exit velocities. Because
the difference of velocity between the jets is very low in
the present conﬁguration, there is no attraction of jet by
other as shown in the conﬁguration studied by Yimer et al.
(2001). Tanaka and Nakata (1975) examined the effect of
velocity ratio between the jets in a conﬁguration of two-
dimensional plane jets. They observed three types of ﬂows
according to the velocity ratio: dominant central jet,
dominant side jets, and instable state.

velocity proﬁle changes and Ucl varies with the jet angle
(Fig. 12). A decrease in the length of potential core and
therefore a fast decrease in the longitudinal velocity in the
ﬁrst zone of the ﬂow in particular for the central jet are
noted. The decrease in the velocity (Ucl) is more important

4 Conclusions
An experimental study of development and structure of
ﬂow from an oxy-fuel burner with separated jets was
investigated in reacting and non-reacting ﬂows. Measure-
ments by PIV technique enabled the characterization of the
behavior of jets and the distribution of velocity ﬁelds.
Results show that the inclination of side jets toward the
central jet improves the mixing and thus accelerates the
merging, and then the combining of jets where velocity
proﬁles become uniform to form a single jet proﬁle. The
deﬂection of injectors induces a transversal velocity in the
ﬁrst zone of ﬂow increasing with the jet angle. Along
the ﬂow, this velocity decreases, since the side jets impact
the central jet, and the ﬂow forms a single jet. The axial
velocity along the centerline of jets (Ucl) follows an
ordinary evolution for the straight jets (h = 0), whereas it
changes behavior with the deﬂection of jets (h [ 0).
Indeed, the potential core length decreases, and therefore
the longitudinal velocity decreases fastly in the ﬁrst zone of
the ﬂow with the jet angle, in particular for the central jet.
After the ﬁrst region, the Ucl proﬁle presents a plateau (for
10) and an increase in velocity (for 20 and 30) due to the
union of jets leading a slight acceleration of ﬂow at the
beginning of the combined zone of jets. The results for
the turbulence intensity highlighted the zones of high tur-
bulence in the outer (ambient air-oxygen) and in the inner
(oxygen-natural gas) mixing layers. The inner zones of
