TAPS –A 4th Generation Propulsion Combustor Technology for Low Emissions 
 
Hukam C. Mongia* 
GE Aircraft Engines, Cincinnati, Ohio
ABSTRACT 
This paper describes a low-emissions combustion system 
that uses a Twin Annular Premixing Swirler (TAPS) 
arrangement to further reduce NOx emissions without 
adversely affecting other conflicting design requirements 
of propulsion engine combustors.  Two combustion 
systems have been developed using the test rigs: a single 
annular combustor scaled for the CFM56 engine size, 
Tech56/CFM SAC TAPS; and a Dual Annular TAPS for 
the GE90 size engine, DAC TAPS.  Both the DAC and 
SAC TAPS give approximately 50% reduction in the 
landing/takeoff (LTO) NOx compared to current rich-
dome product combustors. The Tech56/CFM TAPS 
combustion system has successfully completed engine 
demonstration 
including 
emissions, 
performance, 
operability and endurance.  
 
INTRODUCTION 
The turbopropulsion combustion design and technology 
community effort started in the middle and late sixties 
for the reduction of gaseous and smoke emissions. The 
first major NASA sponsored initiatives, Experimental 
Clean Combustor Program, ECCP and Pollution 
Reduction Technology Program, PRTP1-4 have been 
followed by several industry and government funded 
technology programs including the most recent High 
Speed Civil Transport (HSCT), Advanced Subsonic 
Transport (AST) and Ultra Energy Efficient Technology 
(UEET) initiatives of NASA. 
The Sixth Meeting of the Committee on Aircraft 
Noise May 11, 1981 recommended publication of  
Environmental Protection Annex 16 Volume II Aircraft 
Engines. The Second Meeting of the Committee on 
Aircraft Engine Emissions, June 30, 1981 introduced 
Volume II relating to control of smoke and gaseous 
emissions which was adopted by the First Meeting of the 
Committee on Aviation Environment Protection (CAEP), 
March 4, 1988, denoted as CAEP1. The details are 
provided 
in

Recent document of ICAO/CAEP/Working 
Group 3 (WG3-WP3/16,ICAO/CAEP/WG3, 14-15 May 
2002, Dallas, Texas) states: 
CO2 and NOx emissions are important for global climate 
change.  Any future ICAO/CAEP action (and technology 
goals) must consider fuel efficiency (CO2 reduction) and 
simultaneous decrease of NOx emissions. Reducing NOx 
is important because of the climatic effects of the ozone 
produced at flight altitudes.  
An atmospheric science paper presented by the 
UK at the recent ICAO/CAEP Working Group 3 
meetings indicates that NOx from aircraft is much more 
important to global warming than CO2.  Previous 
assessments have suggested that for aircraft, the effect of 
NOx is potentially equal to that of CO2. 
Highlights of a recent GAO study, GAO-03-
252, presented to the Chairman, Subcommittee on 
Aviation, the US Congress House Committee on 
Transportation and Infrastructure lists: 
“Aviation related emissions of nitrogen oxides, which 
contribute to formation of ozone, have been of particular 
concern to many airport operators. A federal study at 19 
airports estimated that by 2010, aircraft emissions have 
the potential to significantly contribute to air pollution in 
the areas around these airports. ------- GAO recommends 
that the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) develop 
a strategic framework that addresses the need for 
information on the extent and impact of emissions, 
identifies reduction options, establishes goals and time 
frames for achieving needed reductions, and defines the 
roles of government and industry in developing and 
implementing reduction programs.”

optimizing fuel-rich mixer, e.g., swirl cup, and extending

it was important to recognize where we have been and 
what should we set as our target for next -generation low-

70% reduction from CAEP2 levels, Equation 2. As

another 20 margin on the top of it, i.e., LTO NOx value 
of 32 g/kN.  Similarly for 40 OPR, the corresponding

In summary, one can conclude based on 
historical data that typical jumps in NOx reduction 
technology have been between 10-20% and several years

the other hand, for 40 OPR engines, one needs to 
develop the NOx technology in three steps (Fig. 5b)

NOx emissions starting from BACT.

TAPS Mod 2 produces two co-annular swirling 
jets produced respectively by pilot and main mixer. Each 
of the two swirling streams could be the result of multi-
swirler arrangement designed specifically for achieving 
specific design objectives. For example, the pilot uses a 
high flow number simplex pressure atomizer surrounded 
by two co-rotating swirlers that help produces spray 
quality suitable for starting and low-power operation 
along with the flow field characteristics appropriate for 
meeting the design requirements for ignition, starting, 
lean flame stability, combustion efficiency etc. The 
degree of mixedness of the flowfield influenced mostly 
by the pilot design features needs to be matched properly 
with these requirements in addition to providing NOx 
low enough to meet 50% and 30% CAEP2 requirement. 
The design features of the main mixer and the pilot have 
evolved over the years as shown typically in Fig. 7; Fig. 
6 shows one of these designs where the main mixer 
consists  of a cyclone or radial inflow swirler, a cavity, 
and fuel injected by discrete transverse jets. More 
discussion follows under the section entitled Future 
TAPS Activities. 
 
The pilot swirling jet(s) controlled initially by 
the fuel nozzle geometry interacts with the main swirling 
jet(s) separated suitably by a step height to provide 
thermal environment required to meet combustor’s 
critical design requirements. All the combustor air except 
cooling air required for the dome and liners passes 
through the pilot and cyclone swirlers. Fuel staging 
between the pilot and main is done within the fuel nozzle 
by means of “controlled pressure fuel nozzle” logic 
which allows predetermined discrete flow splits that vary 
from 100% through the pilot at low-power to 5-10% at 
the maximum power. 
  
TAPS combustion system aero design and 
development process relied extensively on systematic 
use of rig (single module, sector and full-annular test 
rigs) and engine testing. The state-of-the-art CCD-based 
design tools8 provided insignificant pre-test insight 
during the development phase of TAPS; however, they 
were useful for providing qualitative understanding of 
the test results. 
Combustion system testing included documenting data 
for gaseous emissions, smoke, combustion efficiency, 
pressure drop, profile, pattern factor, dome and liner 
temperature levels at critically selected points on the 
dome and liners, ignition, flame propagation at simulated 
sea-level to altitude operation, and lean blow-out at 
selected design points. 
The Tech56/CFM TAPS SAC combustion 
system (Fig. 8a) was designed to fit within the spatial 
envelope of the CFM56-7B engine combustion system 
(Figure 3a); like wise for the GE90 DAC TAPS (Fig. 8b) 
consistent with that of the GE90-94B envelope, Fig. 8c. 
The photographs of the combustors and fuel nozzles for 
the SAC and DAC TAPS are shown in Figures 9a and 
9b.

TAPS TEST RESULTS 
 
Extensive rig testing of both SAC and DAC 
TAPS has been conducted since 1999 in addition to 
engine testing of the SAC TAPS combustion system 
including the development control system. These results 
have been very encouraging in meeting expectations in 
regard 
to 
pressure 
drop, 
combustion 
efficiency, 
dome/liner wall temperature levels and gradients, 
combustor exit temperature quality including profile and 
pattern factor, lean blowout, ignition and flame 
propagation at critical design points in FAR simulated 
environment 
limited 
by 
test 
facility 
capabilities. 
Thorough testing of the Tech56/CFM SAC TAPS 
development engine testing have identified areas for 
further development including the occurrence of benign 
cracks on the dome, fuel nozzle shrouds and locally 
behind the igniter; all these are considered fixable as part 
of SAC TAPS product development effort. Engine 
ground starts (ignition, flame propagation, engine 
response to throttle push and chop) limited to minimum 
engine inlet temperature of approximately 15F, and the 
response in the hailstorm have been considered 
acceptable. However, the SAC TAPS engine has not 
gone through any flying test bed demonstration for 
operability including windmill start, and starting under 
extreme cold air and fuel conditions. 
  
Combustor exit temperature quality, exhaust 
smoke, liner wall temperature levels and gradients are 
generally superior to that of rich-dome SAC/DAC and 
lean DAC combustors. The gaseous emissions results are 
presented in Figures 11 and 13.  
Since the SAC TAPS emissions data is from a 
development engine using the ICAO recommended 
procedure for measuring engine emissions, a back to 
back to comparison with the CFM56-7B rich SAC and 
lean DAC is straight forward as shown in Fig. 11. For the 
CFM56-7B 27000 lb thrust rating, the SAC TAPS 
system gave 46% and 22% lower NOx compared to its 
counterpart rich-dome SAC and lean-dome DAC, 
respectively. HC emissions are essentially comparable

Extensive full-scale rig database of the GE90 
DAC TAPS was used to develop emissions correlations 
as a function of Pn, Exp(T3/b) and FARc for each of the 
fuel staging modes. The data was taken for simulated 
engine operating conditions with T3, P3 and fuel/air ratio 
(FAR) as high as 1170F, 350 psi and 0.037, respectively. 
The rig  was however run for the engine operating 
conditions (P3, T3 and Wa3) required to get data for 7% 
and 30% power points which are  the ICAO idle and 
approach conditions. These data along with correlations 
were used to calculate LTO NOx, HC and CO emissions 
of the AST DAC TAPS cycle as well as GE90-94B DAC 
TAPS, which are compared with engine data shown in 
Fig. 13. The GE90-94B DAC TAPS gives 54% lower 
NOx compared to the rich-dome DAC, the current 
product combustor and simultaneously CO and HC 
emissions are comparable with the product engines. 
 
FUTURE TAPS ACTIVITIES 
 
As summarized in the last section, the original 
design intent (nested flame whose strength can be varied 
throughout the operating envelope), the underlying 
functionalities, and feasibility of TAPS as a viable low-
NOx emissions combustion system concept has been 
clearly demonstrated for both SAC and DAC application. 
It has shown that 50% NOx reduction over current 
product combustors can be achieved without adversely 
impacting 
other 
design 
requirements 
including 
performance, operability and durability. There are still 
other challenges ahead, e.g., operability, fuel nozzle 
purging without causing plugging/deposition, product 
cost and weight for finding optimum engineering 
solution as part of product development; however, for 
technology the focus should be how much further NOx 
reduction can be achieved by refining the basic TAPS 
concept. Some of the refinements identified in Fig. 7 
have the potential to reduce TAPS NOx by additional 
50% (2X challenge) and 75% (4X challenge). These 
advances are necessary if we were to achieve step 2 
(identified in Fig. 5a), and steps 2 and 3 of Fig. 5b, if we 
ever want the combustion products for 30 and 40 OPR 
engines to have 30% CAEP2 NOx capability with 20% 
margin. 
 
There are several technology enablers that need 
to be identified and developed during the next 10 years 
for potential enhancements to the 1st generation TAPS 
combustion 
systems 
tested 
to-date 
for 
potential 
application in Tech56/CFM and GE90 engine families. A 
relative comparison of 2X and 4X reduction in NOx 
emissions index for 30 and 40 OPR engines over the 
current SAC and DAC TAPS and LEC is given in 
Figures 13a and 13b for the four ICAO LTO points of 
generic 30 and 40 OPR engine families. 
 
SUMMARY 
 
A brief overview of the low-NOx emissions 
technology and product evolution of the last 30 years 
was given leading to the rationale for starting with 
formulation of a new strategy for developing an 
affordable 
ultra-low 
emissions 
propulsion 
engine 
combustion system based on Twin Annular Premixing 
Swirler (TAPS) with a near-term intention being “Lean 
DAC Emissions with SAC Simplicity”. 
The main objective of the 1st phase TAPS effort 
for potential application in Tech56/CFM and GE90 class 
engines of SAC and DAC TAPS has been successfully 
concluded; the former through an extensive engine 
demonstration, the latter only through a full-scale 
annular combustor tested under simulated engine 
operating conditions.  
 
Approximately 50% reduction in LTO NOx 
over the current product combustors was achieved 
without adversely impacting other design requirements 
including performance, operability and durability etc. 
Further near-term TAPS technology development effort 
is planned to continue to make it more attractive for new 
centerline engine combustors; in addition to reducing 
NOx by additional 50% and 75% in order to quantify the 
TAPS entitlement levels.
